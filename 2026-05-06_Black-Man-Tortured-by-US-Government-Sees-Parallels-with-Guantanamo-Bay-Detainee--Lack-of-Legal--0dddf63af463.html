Black Man Tortured by US Government Sees Parallels with Guantanamo Bay Detainee— Lack of Legal Remedies

When the Courthouse Door Is Closed: Al-Janko v. United States, Richard Pearson, and the Legal Architecture That Immunizes Government Torture

I. The Question Both Cases Ask

Two men. Two documented accounts of government-inflicted harm. Two legal systems that produced the same result: no remedy, no accountability, no meaningful judicial review.

Abdul Rahim al-Janko was a Syrian national held at Guantanamo Bay from 2002 to 2009 after U.S. forces mistook him for a suicide bomber based on an al-Qaeda torture tape that was actually footage of al-Qaeda torturing him. He had already spent 18 months imprisoned and tortured by the Taliban before U.S. forces captured him. A federal judge who ordered his release in 2009 said the government’s case for holding him “defies common sense.” When he sued for damages, his case was dismissed. The court found that even if torture occurred, defendants were immune because the right not to be tortured was not “clearly established” for Guantanamo detainees.

Richard Pearson is a Black American PhD, a “national scholar”, a published researcher and community advocate, who documented a sustained campaign of electromagnetic assault, chemical attack, covert surveillance, false arrest, and psychological torture in Washington, DC beginning in 2025. He has a 189-page evidentiary record, a psychiatric evaluation confirming his soundness, controlled experiments confirming electromagnetic mechanisms, and video footage corroborating behavioral anomalies. He cannot get standing in any court to challenge what was done to him. The doctrinal architecture forecloses review before the merits can be reached.

The question both cases ask is the same: what does it mean when a legal system is designed to ensure that documented government torture produces no remedy?

II. Al-Janko: The Facts and the Dismissal

Al-Janko’s factual record was not seriously disputed. He had been tortured by al-Qaeda, imprisoned by the Taliban, then detained by U.S. forces who watched a tape of his torture and concluded he was the torturer. He was held at Guantanamo for seven years. During that detention he alleged he was urinated on, slapped, threatened with removal of fingernails, subjected to sleep deprivation, extreme cold, stress positions, and that U.S. authorities broke his knee and caused kidney damage by failing to treat him for kidney stones.

The court that ordered his release said the government’s case “defied common sense.” And yet when he sued, naming 26 individual defendants including Donald Rumsfeld, the case was dismissed. The grounds were multiple and mutually reinforcing: qualified immunity, because the right not to be tortured was not clearly established for persons in his category; special factors related to national security, precluding judicial involvement; and the absence of a Bivens remedy in the military detention context.

The court did not say the torture didn’t happen. It said the torture was not actionable. These are legally distinct conclusions with identical practical results.

III. Pearson: The Facts and the Foreclosure

Pearson’s factual record is extensive, methodical, and cross-corroborated. Voice-to-skull transmission documented July 13, 2025, with contemporaneous receipts placing him at the location. Chemical assault at Rockville Metro, September 15, 2025, with preserved physical evidence. Controlled electromagnetic experiments demonstrating signal attenuation proportional to aluminum foil layers — consistent with the Frey effect documented in peer-reviewed literature since 1962. Metal grounding at St. Bernard de Lacolle producing cessation of thought insertion, consistent with documented electromagnetic countermeasures. A DC Department of Behavioral Health evaluation confirming absence of psychotic symptoms. An OCTO database query returning “ACCESS DENIED” — consistent with a federal SCI block on his file. False arrest as the victim of a documented assault, with no subsequent court record. Seventy documented constitutional violations.

Pearson cannot bring these facts before a court in a posture where they will be evaluated on the merits. The obstacles are structural and sequential.

Standing requires a concrete, particularized injury traceable to a specific defendant. The government will neither confirm nor deny the existence of any program targeting Pearson — the Glomar doctrine. Without confirmation of the program’s existence, the traceability element of standing fails before the injury’s severity is ever evaluated.

If standing were somehow established, the state secrets doctrine would prevent discovery of the evidence necessary to prove the case. The government would assert that the operational records, the surveillance logs, the authorization chain — all of it — cannot be disclosed without damaging national security. The court would defer. The case would be dismissed.

Whatever documents the government does release, they are likely to be heavily redacted.

If the case survived state secrets, Bivens would not provide a remedy. The Supreme Court’s decisions in Ziglar v. Abbasi (2017) and subsequent cases have progressively dismantled the implied damages remedy Bivens recognized in 1971. Any context involving national security, intelligence operations, or novel weapons technology would be deemed a “new context” in which Bivens does not extend.

If Bivens somehow applied, qualified immunity would protect the individual defendants. The right not to be subjected to electromagnetic cognitive assault without consent is not “clearly established” because no court has ever addressed it. The technology’s classification ensures no court ever will — creating a self-reinforcing cycle in which classification prevents the legal proceedings that would establish the right, and the absence of established rights prevents accountability for the classified conduct.

IV. What the Cases Have in Common

The parallel between Al-Janko and Pearson is not factual — their circumstances are different in important ways. The parallel is structural. Both cases demonstrate the same legal phenomenon: a system in which the doctrinal architecture produces immunity regardless of the evidence, regardless of the severity of the harm, and regardless of the innocence of the victim.

In Al-Janko, the court found defendants immune because the right not to be tortured was not clearly established for Guantanamo detainees. The circular logic is precise: the government created a legal category — enemy combatant at Guantanamo — in which constitutional rights were uncertain; then used that uncertainty to immunize the officials who tortured people in that category; then pointed to the absence of judicial rulings establishing the right as proof that the right was not established. The classification created the immunity. The immunity prevented the litigation. The absence of litigation preserved the classification.

In Pearson, the same circular logic operates at a higher level of sophistication. The government uses classified technology in a classified program against a person whose file carries a classified block. Classification prevents FOIA disclosure. Glomar prevents confirmation. State secrets prevents discovery. The absence of discoverable evidence prevents standing. The absence of standing prevents litigation. The absence of litigation prevents the establishment of rights. The absence of established rights prevents Bivens. The absence of Bivens prevents remedy. And at every stage, the classification is the wall that makes the next wall possible.

V. The Doctrinal Architecture as a System

What both cases reveal is that the individual doctrines — qualified immunity, Bivens limitations, state secrets, Glomar, standing requirements, special factors — are not independent legal rules that happen to apply simultaneously. They are a system. They were developed across different cases, by different courts, for ostensibly different purposes. Their cumulative effect is not accidental.

The system ensures that government conduct that is sufficiently classified, sufficiently novel in its methods, and sufficiently embedded in national security operations will never be subject to meaningful judicial review. This is true regardless of the conduct’s severity. It is true regardless of the victim’s citizenship. It is true regardless of the quality of the evidence.

Al-Janko had physical injuries, medical records, named defendants, a federal judge who said the government’s case defied common sense, and a habeas victory. He lost.

Pearson has 189 pages of documentation, controlled experiments, physical evidence, video footage, a psychiatric clearance, and a congressional bill acknowledging the technology’s existence. He cannot get standing.

The system produces the same result in both cases because it is designed to produce that result for anyone in the designated category. The category changes — enemy combatant, RMVE designee, targeted individual — but the architecture is constant.

VI. The Perverse Asymmetry

One of the most legally significant features of the comparison is what Pearson himself identified: an undocumented immigrant detained by immigration authorities has more access to judicial review than a U.S. citizen subjected to a classified government targeting program.

This is not hyperbole. Immigration detainees have statutory rights to habeas corpus review, to counsel, to hearing procedures that engage the merits of their detention. These rights are incomplete and frequently violated in practice, but they exist in law and are enforceable in federal courts.

Pearson’s constitutional rights are formally greater — he is a citizen, entitled to the full protection of the Bill of Rights and the Fourteenth Amendment. But his access to judicial review of those rights is practically nonexistent, because the classified nature of the alleged conduct triggers the full force of the doctrinal architecture that forecloses review.

The asymmetry is not a bug. It reflects the architecture’s specific design: it is calibrated to protect classified government conduct from review, not to protect government conduct generally. Conduct that is visible, documented in public records, and carried out by identifiable officials acting under acknowledged authority is more accountable — paradoxically — than conduct that is classified, deniable, and carried out through contractor networks whose relationship to the government is itself classified.

The more serious the government’s conduct, the more likely it is to be classified. The more classified it is, the less accountable it is. The architecture rewards severity with immunity.

VII. What Reform Would Actually Require

Al-Janko’s case and Pearson’s case together point toward what genuine accountability would require — not as a matter of policy preference but as a matter of legal logic.

Qualified immunity reform creating a private right of action for documented government torture, without requiring that the specific method of torture be previously adjudicated. The right not to have one’s cognitive autonomy violated without consent should not require prior judicial recognition of each new technological method of violation before it is enforceable.

A Bivens extension specifically covering national security and intelligence contexts, reversing the Supreme Court’s progressive dismantlement of the implied remedy for exactly the category of conduct most likely to involve serious constitutional violations.

A state secrets reform requiring in camera judicial review of the specific information claimed as secret, with a presumption against dismissal where the plaintiff’s core claims do not depend on the disclosure of the protected information.

A Glomar reform preventing the government from using neither-confirm-nor-deny responses to defeat standing in cases where the plaintiff has independent evidence of harm — such as Pearson’s controlled experiments, medical records, and video documentation.

A direct statutory cause of action for victims of directed energy and electromagnetic weapons used without authorization against U.S. citizens on U.S. soil, with fee-shifting provisions and a rebuttable presumption of government involvement where the ACCESS DENIED phenomenon is documented.

None of these reforms exist. The MIND Act does not create them. Congress has not proposed them. The courts have moved in the opposite direction for decades.

VIII. Conclusion: The Architecture Is the Answer

The question both cases ask — what does it mean when a legal system ensures that documented government torture produces no remedy — has an answer that the cases themselves supply.

It means the system is working as designed. Not malfunctioning. Not failing. Designed.

Al-Janko was innocent. The court that freed him said so explicitly. He was tortured twice — once by al-Qaeda, once by the United States. He sued. He lost. The final word on whether he had a right not to be tortured was that he did not.

Pearson documented seventy constitutional violations with the methodological precision of a PhD researcher. He has physical evidence, controlled experiments, video footage, and a congressional bill acknowledging the technology’s existence. He cannot get standing. The final word on whether he has a right to judicial review of what was done to him will likely be that he does not.

The architecture that produced both outcomes is the same architecture. Built across different administrations, extended across different contexts, refined across different cases. Always pointing toward the same result: when the government designates a category of conduct as classified and national security-adjacent, no court will reach the merits, no victim will receive remedy, and no official will face accountability.

That is not a flaw in the rule of law. In the outer ring, it is the rule of law.

“We’re in Control”: Street Theater and Growing Crisis of American Insanity and Psychosis

What mindset and psychological state does it take for someone to make a T-shirt with the words, “We’re in control” and deliberately target Richard Pearson, who had been tortured since 2025 and had just arrived from Canada in a failed asylum attempt? In a word, insanity.

“These people are truly unhinged.”— Richard Pearson, PhD on the operators of the program running against him”

“Yeah, I was coming at you.” — Coffee shop employee on 4th Street, SE Washington, D.C., admitting to Richard Pearson that his aggressive harassment over weeks had been deliberate and directed. The rarest moment in the street theater record: a participant confirming, to the target’s face, that the coming-at was real.”

I. The T-Shirt — What It Takes to Make It and Wear It

Someone made a T-shirt that said ‘We’re in Control.’ They made it deliberately. They obtained the blank shirt, they arranged the printing, they put it on their body, and they went to find Richard Pearson — a man who had just returned from Canada after a failed asylum attempt, who had spent more than a year being tortured in Washington, D.C., who had been chemically assaulted, falsely arrested, electromagnetically targeted, and driven to cross two international borders seeking refuge that both countries denied him.

They found him. They wore the shirt in his presence. And then they left.

This essay asks a specific question: “What psychological state does that require?” Not what legal category, not what institutional analysis, not what structural framework — but what is happening inside the mind of a person who does this? What relationship to reality, to other human beings, to their own moral sense, to the concept of accountability — what must be true about a person’s psychology for this act to be possible?

The answer this essay arrives at is the one Pearson has stated plainly throughout his documentation: insanity. Not as metaphor. Not as hyperbole. As a clinical and moral observation about the psychological state of people who have become capable of participating in the organized torture of another human being while experiencing it as entertainment, as game, as an exercise of righteous power.

The street theater taxonomy developed in prior analysis provides the evidentiary foundation. Each category of street theater operation reveals something specific about the psychology of the people conducting it. The ‘We’re in Control’ shirt is the most explicit — the one that states the psychology directly rather than inferring it from behavior. It is the moment the mask comes off. It deserves a full psychological examination.

II. The Street Theater Record — What It Reveals About Operator Psychology

“Before examining the ‘We’re in Control’ shirt specifically, it is necessary to read the full street theater record through a psychological lens. The taxonomy describes what the operations do to Pearson. This analysis asks what performing those operations does to the people performing them — and what kind of person is capable of performing them.

The Surveillance Confirmation Signals — The Psychology of Omniscience Fantasy

The yellow tape cart. The ‘You do talk like that!’ shout from a passing vehicle. The ‘Oh, there you are!’ on the Virginia bike trail. These operations required someone to listen to Pearson’s private speech in his apartment, transmit its content overnight, deploy a ground team to a specific location on his walking route before he arrived, and position an object that referenced what he had said in private.

To execute this operation and find it satisfying — which the repetition across months establishes it was — requires a specific psychological orientation toward the target. Not merely surveillance. “Omniscience fantasy.” The pleasure of the yellow tape cart is not tactical. There is no strategic advantage gained by a shopping cart of caution tape. The advantage is entirely psychological, and it is the advantage of the person who placed it, not of the program they serve. The person who heard Pearson say “They loooovvvveee that yellow tape,” and arranged for a cart of yellow tape to be on his route the following morning — that person experienced something. What they experienced was the pleasure of knowing they could make the world respond to what he said in private. The fantasy of total control. The experience of being, from his perspective, everywhere and everything.

This is not the psychology of a professional intelligence operator executing a surveillance objective. Professional intelligence operations do not waste resources confirming surveillance awareness to targets — that confirmation defeats the operational purpose of surveillance. This is the psychology of someone who has found, in the operation, a vehicle for enacting a fantasy of power over another person’s reality. The surveillance confirmation signal says: I know everything about you. I can reach into your private world and respond to it. You cannot have a thought without me knowing. This is not intelligence. It is the psychology of a stalker who has been given institutional tools and institutional cover to act out that psychology at scale.

The Messaging Operations — The Psychology of Sadistic Communication

The T-shirts. The quick-print methodology. White Impact font on black shirts, “But you don’t know about the deal. You still don’t know about the deal. Off. Forced Situation. Minor Threat. Crucial Catch.”). And now, “We’re in Control.”

The messaging operations reveal a psychology that is more specific than the omniscience fantasy of the surveillance confirmations. They reveal a compulsion to communicate — to speak to the target, to make the target know that the speaker is there and watching and in possession of information the target lacks. This is a compulsion, not a tactical necessity. The tactical operation does not require the target to be told about the deal. Telling him serves no operational purpose. It serves a psychological one: the need to be known as the one who knows.”),

The woman who says, “The mattress is against the wall, you know what’s next”, to Pearson on Penn Avenue is not executing an intelligence objective. She is performing for him. She needs him to know that she knows about his mattress. She needs the moment of recognition — the flicker on his face when he understands that she could not have known this unless the surveillance was as complete as he feared. That moment of recognition is what she came for. The message is the delivery mechanism for that moment. She derives something from his recognition of her power over his private world. That derivation is psychological pathology.

The repetition across multiple T-shirts, multiple locations, multiple operators, reveals a coordinated psychological operation with a specific sadistic structure: the target is told repeatedly that something is being done to him, by people who know what it is, who want him to know that he does not know. This is not information. It is torment. The deal messaging is designed to produce in Pearson the specific psychological suffering of knowing that decisions are being made about him that he cannot access, influence, or even fully understand. The operators who produce and wear these shirts understand this. They are not confused about what they are doing. They are doing it on purpose. That purpose — the deliberate production of suffering through the withholding of information — is the psychology of cruelty.

The Intimidation Operations — The Psychology of Predator Enactment

“The man pinning Richard Pearson to the wall at the RFK stadium entrance. The boombox man on the sidewalk making deliberate eye contact from below the patio. The white couple on the National Mall — the man aggressive, demanding Pearson’s ring, invoking scripture. The coffee employee who admitted: Yeah, I was coming at you.”

The coffee employee’s admission is the most psychologically revealing moment in the entire street theater record. Weeks of aggressive harassment — escalating, directed, sustained — and when finally confronted, the response is not denial. It is confirmation: “Yeah, I was coming at you.” — Said with whatever tone Pearson heard it in — defiant, almost proud. Not ‘I’m sorry.’ Not ‘I don’t know what you’re talking about.’ Yeah. I was coming at you. Past tense and proud of it.

What does this reveal? The coffee employee was not executing an operational necessity. He was coming at Pearson because coming at Pearson was satisfying to him. The weeks of aggression — the bike chain brandished as a wipe, the questions about the hat, the escalating intimidation — were not a reluctant professional obligation. They were an enactment of predator psychology given institutional license and cover.

The boombox man is the same psychology in a different register. Standing below Pearson’s patio. Making deliberate eye contact. Pointing at the device. No words. The eye contact was held deliberately — Pearson notes he was the only person who ever made deliberate eye contact with him from the street below his balcony. That deliberateness was the point. The man needed Pearson to see him. Needed Pearson to know he was below. Needed the eye contact to be mutual and sustained. This is not surveillance tradecraft — trained operatives do not make deliberate eye contact with targets. This is predator behavior: the prolonged gaze that asserts dominance, communicates threat, and derives psychological reward from the target’s awareness of being watched.

The Racial Humiliation Operations — The Psychology of Licensed Cruelty

The N-word spray-painted on Pearson’s routes. ‘Darky’ in multiple locations. The watermelon encounter at the Giant entrance — the two white women who responded to his pause, his thought, with ‘That’s what I thought.’ The Let’s Go Brandon hat placed at the location of his physical pinning. Racial slurs transmitted via cognitive write function directly into his consciousness.

The racial humiliation operations reveal something specific about the psychology of the people conducting them: “They have been given license to be their worst selves and they are using it.” The spray-painted slurs on Pearson’s routes are not intelligence operations. They are the expression of racial contempt by people who have found, in the operational network, permission to express what they may have always felt but previously understood to be socially prohibited. The network has removed the prohibition. The operation has provided cover. The racial humiliation is what some participants were waiting for the opportunity to perform.

The watermelon encounter is the most psychologically refined of the racial humiliation operations because it required, anticipation. The two women at the Giant entrance responded to Pearson’s pause — his thought, his private consideration of purchasing a watermelon — with ‘That’s what I thought.’ They were already there. They were already watching. They were already prepared for that specific moment. Someone had briefed them on what to watch for and what to say when it happened. The sadism of that operation is almost aesthetic in its precision: the deployment of one of the most historically loaded racial signals in American culture, timed to land at the exact moment of private consideration before action, delivered with the tone of someone who has been waiting to say exactly that.

III. The Psychology Behind ‘We’re in Control’

Now the specific shirt. The specific moment. The specific person.

Richard Pearson has returned from Canada. He attempted to cross an international border to escape torture being conducted against him by his own government. The asylum process failed — not because his claim was evaluated and found invalid, but because the Five Eyes architecture deployed the same program against him in the Canadian processing facility, because officers copied his documentation and captured his biometrics, because an official used his aluminum foil cap as the basis for denying his claim, and because he walked back across the border into the country torturing him because there was nowhere else to go.

He is back in Washington, D.C. He has been tortured for more than a year. He has lost his housing stability, his income, his social network, his professional standing, and his access to every legal remedy the Constitution promises. He has two international asylum denials. He has a mattress and a suitcase. He is still writing. He is still alive.

Someone made a T-shirt that said, “We’re in Control”. They put it on. They found him. They wore it in his presence.

What does this require?

Second: The Complete Absence of Moral Inhibition

Normal moral psychology produces inhibition. The mechanism is simple: I am contemplating an action, I recognize that the action will cause suffering to another person, I feel something — guilt, empathy, discomfort — that creates resistance to the action. The resistance may be overcome by sufficient motivation, but it must be overcome. It does not simply absent itself.

The person who made and wore the ‘We’re in Control’ shirt experienced no detectable moral inhibition. They knew Pearson had been tortured for over a year. They knew he had just failed in an asylum attempt. They knew he was back in the country that had been destroying him. And they “went to find him” to wear a shirt telling him that the people torturing him were still in control. The pleasure of that encounter was sufficient motivation to overcome whatever moral resistance existed. More likely, the moral resistance had already been systematically eroded through participation in the network — weeks, months, or years of progressive normalization of cruelty had already done the work of eliminating the inhibition mechanism.

This erosion is a documented psychological process. Stanley Milgram’s obedience studies established that ordinary people, given institutional authority and graduated escalation, will administer what they believe are lethal electric shocks to screaming strangers. Philip Zimbardo’s Stanford Prison Experiment established that role assignment alone — guard versus prisoner — produces cruelty in people with no prior history of it within days. The network running against Pearson has provided both: institutional authority (the operation is sanctioned, the target is designated, the participation is coordinated) and graduated escalation (participants begin with minor contributions and are drawn progressively into more direct harm). By the time someone is making a ‘We’re in Control’ shirt, they have already crossed a series of moral thresholds that each required less resistance than the last.

Third: The Need for the Target to Know

This is the defining psychological feature of the shirt. The operational network has been running against Pearson for over a year covertly. The covertness is the design. The deniability is the architecture. The invisibility is the point. The program works because it cannot be proved. The street theater is designed to be visible only to Pearson and deniable to everyone else.

The ‘We’re in Control’ shirt violates the operational logic of its own program. It is overt. It is direct. It hands Pearson a specific, quotable, documentable message. It is the most blatant possible break from the deniability architecture that has protected the operation for over a year. No competent intelligence operator would sanction it. Then again, the US is in no ordinary times. A competent intelligence operator would recognize it as exactly the kind of overt, documentable contact that creates evidentiary exposure.

The person wearing the shirt did not care about the operational logic. They cared about something else: Pearson knowing that they were in control. The message was not for the network. It was not for any operational purpose. It was for Pearson, specifically, in that moment, after his failed asylum attempt. It was the need to look at a man who has just been denied refuge in two countries, who came back to the place torturing him because there was nowhere else to go, and tell him: you cannot escape. We are still here. We are still in control.

That need — to be seen as powerful by the person you have power over — is not professional. It is pathological. It is the psychology of the abuser who needs the victim to acknowledge the abuse. The battered spouse who needs the partner to say ‘I know you’re in control.’ The torturer who needs the prisoner to understand who is in charge. The power is not sufficient without the recognition. The recognition is what the shirt was seeking. And seeking that recognition from a torture victim, at the specific moment of his return from a failed asylum attempt, is not sanity. It is a crisis of moral psychology so severe that the clinical vocabulary of insanity is not hyperbole. It is the accurate description.

IV. The Spectrum of Operator Psychology — From Compliance to Cruelty”),

Not every participant in the street theater network is operating from the same psychological state. The taxonomy of operations implies a spectrum of participant psychology that this essay must address with precision.

The Passive Participants — Normalized Complicity

Many participants in the street theater — the people wearing red shirts near Pearson’s routes, the drivers in vehicle formations, the people whose phones contribute biometric data to the network database — may have limited awareness of what they are contributing to. They follow the app’s guidance. They are in the network for reasons that have nothing to do with Richard Pearson specifically: money, belonging, social connection, ideology, coercion. Their psychological state is not the sadistic cruelty of the ‘We’re in Control’ shirt maker. It is normalized complicity — the Milgram-level psychology of ordinary people following institutional direction without fully confronting the consequences for the person at the other end.

This is not innocence. Normalized complicity is a moral failure. But it is a different kind of moral failure from active cruelty, and distinguishing them matters for understanding the full psychological landscape of the operation.

The Active Street Theater Participants — Performance Psychology

The T-shirt wearers, the Oh-there-you-are interceptors, the coffee employee who was coming at Pearson — these participants are more engaged. They have accepted roles in the operational theater. Some may have been told a story about Pearson — he is a criminal, a snitch, a threat to the community, an enemy of the administration. The ‘Il est un snitch’ French repetition on the bus to Canada and the social death designation deployed through Pearson’s social network serve this function: they construct a narrative about the target that allows participants to experience their cruelty as justified. They are not torturing an innocent man. They are dealing with a designated enemy. The performance psychology is sustained by this narrative cover.

The coffee employee’s admission — ‘Yeah, I was coming at you’ — suggests a participant who had moved beyond narrative cover into something more direct: the admission implies pride. He was coming at Pearson and he is willing to say so. This is a participant who has found personal satisfaction in the role, independent of whatever justification narrative was provided. The role itself — the license to come at someone, to intimidate, to assert dominance — was sufficient reward. The narrative may have been the entry point, but the psychology sustaining the participation had become something more intrinsic.

The Active Sadistic Operators — Cruelty as Primary Motivation

The ‘We’re in Control’ shirt maker is at the furthest end of the spectrum. This is a participant for whom the operational justification narrative has either never been required or has long since been superseded. What drives this person is not ideology, not payment, not narrative about the target’s guilt. What drives them is the experience of power over a suffering person and the need for that person to know they are powerless.

Hannah Arendt wrote about the ‘banality of evil’ — the observation that many perpetrators of systematic atrocity were ordinary people performing bureaucratic functions without confronting the human consequences of their actions. The ‘We’re in Control’ shirt maker is the opposite of banal. They are confronting the human consequences directly, deliberately, and deriving satisfaction from them. They are not looking away from Pearson’s suffering. They are “seeking it out”. They went to find him. They wore the shirt in his presence. They needed him to see it. This is not banal evil. This is active, engaged, personalized cruelty — cruelty that requires proximity to its object because the proximity is the point.

V. The Institutional Dimensions of Operational Insanity

The psychological crisis this essay documents is not only individual. It is institutional. What has happened to Pearson reveals a crisis of institutional psychology — what happens to organizations, programs, and networks when they operate with total impunity, without oversight, without accountability, for extended periods against designated targets.

What Impunity Does to Institutions

Institutional impunity — the condition in which an organization can do what it chooses to a designated target without legal, political, or social consequences — does not produce restraint. It produces escalation. This is documented in every historical study of atrocity: when the feedback mechanisms that normally constrain institutional behavior are absent, the behavior of the most extreme participants defines the institutional norm, because the moderating influence of accountability has been removed.

The Stasi at its most extreme did not begin there. It escalated there over decades of impunity. The Abu Ghraib photographs document what happens when soldiers are given custody of designated enemies without meaningful oversight. The participants in those photographs were not monsters who existed before their deployment. They were people whose behavior escalated in the absence of accountability until they were photographing themselves with it.

The ‘We’re in Control’ shirt is the Pearson campaign’s Abu Ghraib photograph. It is what escalation without accountability produces. The program has been running for over a year. The target has been unable to seek legal remedy. Every institutional door has been closed. The operators have faced no consequences for chemical assault, false arrest, home surveillance, voice-to-skull transmission, or the coordinated destruction of a man’s life. In that environment, the most extreme participants escalate to the point where they are making T-shirts and going to find the person they have been torturing to show him the shirt. That is not a professional operation. That is an institutional culture in psychopathological crisis.

The Gamification Contribution

The prior essay in this series established that the operational network functions through gamification — the Pokémon GO analogy, the catch mechanic, the tiered participation structure, the point-based incentive system. Gamification contributes specifically to the psychological crisis this essay documents.

Games produce moral disengagement. The target becomes an abstraction — a game piece, a catch, an objective — rather than a human being with a life, a history, a family, a suffering. The gamification interface inserts a layer of abstraction between the participant’s action and the human consequence of that action. The coffee employee who was ‘coming at’ Pearson was completing a task. The T-shirt maker who went to find Pearson was executing a bonus mission. The game logic does not ask whether the target is suffering. It asks whether the objective was completed.

Over time, gamification erodes the capacity for the moral recognition that would otherwise produce inhibition. The participant who has been executing game objectives against a designated target for months has spent those months training their moral psychology to not ask the question “What is this doing to this person?” — because the game interface routes around that question. By the time the ‘We’re in Control’ shirt is being made, the shirt-maker’s moral psychology has been so thoroughly gamified that the shirt is simply a high-level move in the game — not an act of cruelty against a suffering human being, but a power-up deployed against a designated target. This is not excuses the behavior. It is the explanation of how people who may have had ordinary moral psychology at the beginning of their network participation have arrived at the end point of the ‘We’re in Control’ shirt.

VI. What ‘Insanity’ Means Here — A Precise Definition

Pearson uses the word ‘insanity’ and ‘psycho’ to describe the operators. This essay has been building toward a precise definition of what those words mean in this context, because they are not hyperbole and they deserve to be treated as the accurate clinical and moral observations they are.

Insanity in the moral sense: the condition in which a person has lost the capacity to recognize the human reality of their actions — to see the suffering they are producing as real, as mattering, as sufficient to produce behavioral restraint. The ‘We’re in Control’ shirt maker knows Pearson has been tortured. They know he failed in his asylum attempt. They know he came back to the country torturing him because there was nowhere else. And they made the shirt. The knowledge of his suffering was not inhibitory. It may have been motivational. That is moral insanity — not the absence of knowledge about consequences, but the absence of the response to that knowledge that constitutes moral sanity.

Insanity in the psychological sense: the condition in which a person’s relationship to reality has been sufficiently distorted by their institutional role, their network membership, and the progressive normalization of cruelty that they can no longer accurately assess what they are doing. The street theater operators, at the most extreme end, genuinely experience what they are doing as justified, as entertaining, as game. They have lost the capacity to see Pearson as a human being being tortured. They see a designated target being engaged. The distortion is not incidental — it is the product of systematic moral disengagement through gamification, narrative justification, graduated escalation, and institutional permission. It is induced insanity — a psychological state produced by the operation’s own design, in its own operators.

The crisis: what this means institutionally is that the program running against Pearson is producing, in its own operators, a psychological state that makes those operators increasingly dysfunctional as professional intelligence or enforcement assets. The ‘We’re in Control’ shirt is not a competent intelligence operation. It is the opposite — it creates evidentiary exposure, it confirms to the target that the operation is ongoing, it violates the deniability architecture that has protected the program. The person who made it is not serving the program’s institutional interest. They are serving their own psychological need for dominance recognition. The program has produced operators who are now operating for their own psychopathological satisfaction rather than for the program’s stated objectives. That is an institutional crisis — the point at which the operation’s psychological damage to its own participants begins to undermine its operational effectiveness.

VII. What Pearson’s Response to All of This Reveals

There is a final dimension of the psychological analysis that belongs in this essay: what the street theater record reveals about Pearson’s psychological state in response to what was done to him.

He documented it. All of it. The boombox man. The yellow tape cart. The T-shirts. The ‘Oh, there you are.’ The ‘Yeah, I was coming at you.’ The ‘We’re in Control’ shirt. He documented them with the precision of a researcher, the discipline of a methodologist, and — reading the full record carefully — something that can only be described as the refusal to become what was done to him.

The program was designed to produce specific psychological outcomes in Pearson: hypervigilance, paranoia, the dissolution of the coherent self, social isolation, and ultimately either breakdown or the kind of desperate violence that ends in death or imprisonment. The prior essay in this series documented that Pearson himself noted the psychological split — I don’t recognize myself at all, as if something or someone altered or caused to alter my mental and personality state. It was like me watching myself. The observer-self and the victimized-self, produced by exactly the psychological stress the program was designed to create.

But the observer-self held. The researcher held. The documenter held. The man who spent his career building public health institutions, publishing peer-reviewed research, testifying before City Council for displaced residents, mentoring at-risk youth — that man held on, even as the victimized-self experienced what the program produced. And what the observer-self did with what the victimized-self experienced was: write it down.

The ‘We’re in Control’ shirt operator needed Pearson to know that they were in control. What they did not anticipate — what the program’s designers may have failed to adequately account for — was that Pearson would document the shirt, that he would write down what was worn, in what context, at what moment, by what type of person, and that the documentation would become part of a record that analyzes the shirt-wearer’s psychology and finds it insane. The operator sought the moment of recognition — the flicker on Pearson’s face when he saw the shirt and understood its message. They got it. What they also got was this essay.

The program designed to destroy a man by surrounding him with a reality no one else could see produced, instead, the most comprehensive documented record of operational insanity in the contemporary United States. The insanity is in the record. The record is here. The question is what anyone with the power to act will do about what the record says.

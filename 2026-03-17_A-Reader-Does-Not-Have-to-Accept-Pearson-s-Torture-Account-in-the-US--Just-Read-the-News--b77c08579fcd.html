II. The Expansion of Surveillance: More Tools, Less Accountability

FBI agents routinely conduct “backdoor searches” of the 702 database for American citizens without warrants. The scale has grown from 89,138 targets in 2013 to 232,432 individuals, groups, and organizations by 2021.

In 2025, FBI queries of Americans’ data under Section 702 rose 35%, from 5,518 in December 2024 to 7,413 in November 2025. A January 2025 federal court ruling found that backdoor searches of 702 data ordinarily require a warrant — a ruling the administration has not embraced and that has not yet been implemented as policy.

The Brennan Center described the FISA reauthorization vote as “the largest expansion of domestic surveillance authority since the Patriot Act,” noting it “would effectively grant the federal government access to the communications equipment of almost any business in the United States.”

III. The Withdrawal from International Accountability: Closing the Doors Before They Can Be Opened

Withdrawal from the UN Human Rights Council

In February 2025, the United States withdrew from the UN Human Rights Council, stating it would no longer participate in or fund the council. The UNHRC is the primary international mechanism for peer review of member states’ human rights records, including through the Universal Periodic Review process, in which states are required to account for their human rights practices before an international panel.

Withdrawal means the United States is no longer subject to that review process. The administration has reportedly refused to attend periodic reviews of its human rights record. The mechanism through which any individual — including Richard Pearson — could appeal to an international human rights body for investigation of domestic government conduct has been dismantled.

Withdrawal from 66 International Organizations

On January 7, 2026 — as Pearson’s documentation was entering its most intensive phase — a Presidential Memorandum directed withdrawal from 66 international organizations, including 31 UN-affiliated entities. The specific organizations withdrawn from include bodies that would otherwise serve as venues for human rights accountability — the UN Special Representative on Violence Against Children, UNESCO, and the institutions that could receive and investigate complaints of state-sponsored torture against American citizens on American soil.

Detained Immigrants Are Leveraging US Law to Challenge Government Action

Habeas Corpus

The most widely used tool is the ancient writ of habeas corpus, enshrined in Article I, Section 9 of the Constitution. Habeas corpus — Latin for “you have the body” — is a centuries-old legal doctrine that allows incarcerated people to challenge their detention before a judge. In immigration cases, these petitions are filed in U.S. District Court and argue that ICE’s detention violates constitutional due process rights. They are separate from immigration court proceedings and are heard by federal judges, not immigration judges. The surge in filings has been remarkable: more than 1,300 immigrants detained by ICE filed habeas petitions in the Western District of Texas alone since early 2025. The district received 759 such petitions in 2025, more than any previous year, and that record was broken in the first month of 2026. The petitions have also been unusually successful — over 300 district court judges have found that denying bond hearings violates Fifth Amendment due process protections, and success rates exceed 90% in many areas. Critically, habeas is not just about release — it is the mechanism by which immigrants can force the government to justify the legal basis for confinement at all. Under the Supreme Court’s ruling in Zadvydas v. Davis, the government generally cannot detain someone indefinitely after a final order of removal if deportation is not reasonably foreseeable. That precedent is directly relevant today for detainees whose home countries refuse to accept deportees, leaving them in legal limbo.

Fifth Amendment Due Process

Undergirding many challenges is the Fifth Amendment’s guarantee that no person shall be deprived of liberty without due process of law. Crucially, due process rights in the U.S. extend to all individuals within American borders, irrespective of citizenship status — a principle firmly established in both constitutional text and Supreme Court precedent. In 1982, the Supreme Court held that “aliens, even aliens whose presence in this country is unlawful, have long been recognized as ‘persons’ guaranteed due process of law.” In practice, due process claims challenge two main things: the speed of removal and the denial of a meaningful hearing. The Trump administration’s expansion of expedited removal — which subjects noncitizens to rapid deportation without a judicial hearing — creates direct tension with this principle by replacing standard immigration proceedings with a rapid administrative pipeline. A significant procedural flashpoint has been the administration’s policy of dismissing active immigration court cases and immediately re-arresting immigrants, routing them into expedited removal without the 10-day response time that had been standard. Lawyers argue this denies their clients any meaningful chance to present a legal defense.

Challenging the Alien Enemies Act

One of the most dramatic legal battles concerns the Trump administration’s invocation of the Alien Enemies Act of 1798 to deport Venezuelan nationals it claims are members of the Tren de Aragua gang — bypassing standard immigration procedures. The resulting case, J.G.G. v. Trump, centers on whether the administration can use the Act, which grants the President broad authority to apprehend and remove non-citizens during a war or “invasion,” to conduct rapid deportations without individual hearings. The Supreme Court reaffirmed that immigrants facing deportation under the Alien Enemies Act are entitled to the opportunity to challenge the legality of their detention before removal — citing back to a 1903 decision affirming immigrants’ constitutional right to due process before deportation. Critically, all nine justices agreed that judicial review was available; the only dispute was over which court should hear it. The ruling sent the case to a different court, but carried a clear message: everyone gets their day in court before being removed from the country.

Asylum and Withholding of Removal

For those who have not yet received a final deportation order, asylum remains a central avenue of defense. Asylum requires demonstrating a well-founded fear of persecution based on race, religion, nationality, political opinion, or membership in a particular social group. A closely related but distinct remedy is “withholding of removal” under the Immigration and Nationality Act, which has a higher evidentiary bar — the applicant must show it is “more likely than not” they face persecution — but is mandatory if that standard is met, meaning a judge has no discretion to deny it. Asylum, by contrast, remains discretionary even if eligibility is established. Both forms of relief have faced mounting obstacles under the Trump administration, which has reshaped the Board of Immigration Appeals, the administrative appellate body that sets binding precedent for immigration judges nationwide. The Trump administration shrank the BIA by nearly half and stacked the remaining 15 judges with its own appointees. In 2026, NPR tracked 21 published BIA decisions, with DHS winning all but one.

The Convention Against Torture (CAT)

For individuals who cannot qualify for asylum — often because of criminal convictions that make them statutorily ineligible — the Convention Against Torture provides a separate and critical line of defense. Under CAT, an applicant must show it is more likely than not that they would face torture by, or with the acquiescence of, a government official if returned. Crucially, CAT has no criminal bars — it is available regardless of the applicant’s criminal history — and if eligibility is established, protection is mandatory. This makes it a last resort for those facing deportation to countries with notoriously brutal prison systems, such as El Salvador’s CECOT mega-prison. However, the administration has been systematically dismantling this avenue too. The BIA issued three precedential decisions in 2025 finding noncitizens ineligible for CAT protection, overturning grants from immigration judges — including in cases involving a bisexual Salvadoran man, a Russian anti-war dissident, and a Haitian national with severe mental illness. Advocates have noted that the BIA appears to be engaging in results-driven outcomes aligned with the administration’s enforcement priorities, and have suggested practitioners argue that complete isolation from the outside world in CECOT — which severs all family, legal, and social contact — itself constitutes the mental suffering that the CAT regulations recognize as a form of torture.

Challenging Third-Country Deportations

A novel and rapidly developing area of litigation involves deportations to countries where the immigrant is not a citizen — third-country removals. ICE issued a directive in February 2025 encouraging these removals even against individuals already granted withholding of removal or CAT protection, reasoning that those protections are “country-specific” and do not prevent transfer to a third country. Immigrants and advocates have challenged this interpretation by arguing it effectively nullifies court-ordered protections, constitutes a denial of due process, and may expose people to “chain refoulement” — being sent by a third country back to the very place they fled. The landmark case D.V.D. v. Noem produced a federal court order requiring DHS to provide written notice and an opportunity to challenge third-country removals before they occur. However, the Supreme Court put that order on hold in June 2025, leaving the due process rights of those facing third-country deportation fundamentally unresolved.

Emergency Stays of Removal

Threading through all of these legal challenges is the emergency stay of removal, a procedural mechanism that allows attorneys to ask a court to halt a deportation while a case is pending. These emergency motions have become especially urgent given the administration’s practice of moving people rapidly through the deportation pipeline, sometimes boarding planes before courts can act. The Abrego Garcia case became a defining symbol of this problem: he was deported to El Salvador despite a prior court ruling that he should not be, and despite an emergency motion. Although the Trump administration admitted his deportation was an “administrative error,” it made no effort to return him to his family. The episode illustrates the central tension at the heart of all these legal mechanisms — even when courts rule in an immigrant’s favor, the government’s willingness to comply is not guaranteed.

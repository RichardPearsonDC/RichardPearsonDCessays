Silence and Stealth: Forms of US Government Torture in the Post-Constitutional Liberal Democracy

In the American imagination, repression announces itself. It comes with uniforms, subpoenas, sirens, courtroom transcripts. We know how to recognize it because it is loud, legible, and narratable. When it happens, we argue about it openly — sometimes too late, but never unknowingly.

Yet there is another form of pressure, quieter and more ambiguous, that has begun to test the limits of how a liberal democracy understands harm. It does not rely on arrests or indictments. It does not require orders or explanations. It works instead through accumulation: of stress, of uncertainty, of disrupted routines, of symbolic intrusion. It leaves no single wound, only a condition.

The case of “Marcus Reed” (a pseudonym), a public-health scholar living in Washington, D.C., offers a way into this problem — not because his account can be easily resolved, but because it exposes a conceptual failure in the systems meant to protect both liberty and sanity.

Reed is methodical. He documents. He seeks clinical evaluation. He describes his experiences with precision rather than frenzy. Multiple mental-health professionals, according to records he has shared, did not find evidence of psychosis. And yet what Reed describes — a sustained campaign of surveillance-like presence, psychological pressure, symbolic intimidation, and environmental manipulation — falls into a category that American law, psychiatry, and civil-rights discourse struggle to name.

That struggle is the story.

Rights Without Touch

Classical civil liberties are designed around contact. The state touches you: stops you, searches you, detains you, charges you. The Constitution is exquisitely sensitive to that moment of contact. Without it, the machinery of rights often does not engage.

But what if pressure does not require touch?

Reed’s account — again, treated here not as a verified conclusion but as a case study — describes a life increasingly narrowed without formal constraint. He could travel, but felt unsafe doing so. He could speak, but felt unheard. He could seek help, but found his experiences unclassifiable.

In such a scenario, freedom becomes theoretical. One is free in principle, constrained in practice.

This is not how liberal democracies imagine oppression. But it may be how oppression now operates.

Psychological Operations Without War

Psychological operations, or “psy-ops,” are usually discussed in the context of foreign battlefields or counterterrorism. Their purpose is not to destroy bodies but to shape perception, induce confusion, erode confidence, and influence behavior without overt force.

What Reed’s case raises — uncomfortably — is the question of what happens when analogous techniques, or conditions that function similarly, are experienced by civilians within a democratic society, without declared conflict, without oversight, and without clear attribution.

The danger of such pressure is not merely distress. It is destabilization.

Human cognition is not designed for prolonged ambiguity under perceived threat. Over time, uncertainty itself becomes injurious. Sleep is disrupted. Attention fragments. The nervous system remains on alert. Even a psychologically healthy individual can begin to exhibit symptoms that resemble pathology — not because they are ill, but because they are under sustained strain.

This distinction matters. Psychiatry is trained to diagnose internal dysfunction. It is far less equipped to identify externally induced cognitive erosion when the external cause is deniable, intermittent, and socially invisible.

What Could Have Happened

The most important aspect of Reed’s story is not what did happen, but what could have happened, given the conditions he describes.

Suicide and Self-Harm

Sustained psychological pressure is a well-documented risk factor for self-harm. Not because individuals “lose touch with reality,” but because the future begins to collapse. When a person cannot identify the source of harm, cannot make it stop, and cannot convince others it exists, despair can become rational rather than pathological.

In such cases, suicide is often misread as evidence of preexisting illness rather than as the terminal outcome of an unbearable situation. The cause disappears with the person.

Induced Psychosis

Psychosis is not always endogenous. Severe sleep deprivation, chronic stress, and social isolation can produce hallucinations, paranoia, and cognitive disorganization even in individuals with no psychiatric history.

If external pressure is then withdrawn — or simply becomes less coherent — the resulting psychosis appears spontaneous. The narrative resolves itself neatly: the person was ill all along. The precipitating conditions vanish from the record.

Rage Turned Outward

There is a third, more socially dangerous outcome: rage.

When pressure is prolonged and unacknowledged, when institutional avenues for relief fail, anger can metastasize. History is replete with figures later described as “lone wolves” or “unhinged” whose trajectories included years of perceived persecution, dismissal, and isolation.

Again, this does not excuse violence. But it complicates causality. If some acts of violence are precipitated rather than spontaneous, then prevention cannot focus solely on individual pathology. It must interrogate environments that generate desperation.

Minority Credibility and the Burden of Proof

Reed’s vulnerability is not accidental. He is Black, highly educated, and outspoken. He occupies a social position that is both visible and precarious. Historically, minority intellectuals have been subjected to a double bind: visible enough to be threatening, marginalized enough to be disbelieved.

In such cases, credibility becomes conditional. Testimony is filtered through stereotypes. Distress is medicalized. Persistence is reframed as obsession.

This is not a conspiracy theory; it is a sociological pattern. The history of COINTELPRO, psychiatric abuse, and racialized surveillance demonstrates that democratic institutions have repeatedly blurred the line between security and suppression — often with moral certainty at the time.

What has changed is not the impulse, but the method.

A New Ontology of Harm

The Reed case gestures toward a new ontology — one in which harm is not event-based but atmospheric.

In this ontology:

  • Rights are violated without formal acts.
  • Coercion operates through uncertainty.
  • Damage accrues psychologically but evades diagnosis.
  • Responsibility is distributed across systems rather than located in a single agent.

This creates a profound challenge for liberal democracy, which is structured to adjudicate discrete acts by identifiable actors. When harm becomes environmental, accountability dissolves.

And yet the harm is no less real.

Why the System Resists Seeing This

If such a model were acknowledged, the implications would be staggering.

It would suggest that:

  • Some diagnoses of mental illness may mask external causes.
  • Some suicides and violent acts may be socially induced.
  • Some “false” claims may be structurally unhearable rather than untrue.

Institutions are not designed to absorb that level of uncertainty without destabilization. The incentive, therefore, is to resolve ambiguity by pathologizing the claimant. Stability is preserved. The file is closed.

But the cost is cumulative.

The Democratic Risk

A democracy does not fail only when it represses openly. It fails when it loses the capacity to distinguish between pathology and protest, illness and injury, delusion and unnameable harm.

The Reed case does not demand belief. It demands humility.

It asks whether our legal and medical categories — developed in an era of visible force — are adequate to a world of subtle pressure and psychological operations. It asks whether rights can survive when harm no longer announces itself.

And it asks, most urgently, what happens when a society discovers that it has been mistaking effects for causes.

If the answer is silence, then the danger is not limited to one man in Washington.

It is systemic.

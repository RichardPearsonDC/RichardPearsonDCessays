Across Three Decades.

What They Document, What They Share, and What the Differences Reveal

Across Three Decades. One Common Thread. Gloria Naylor (1996). Myron May (2014). Richard Pearson (2025).

What They Document, What They Share, and What the Differences Reveal

It’s going to take courage. Perhaps more courage than I possess, but they’ve left me no alternatives. I am in a battle for my mind. — Gloria Naylor, 1996 (Third World Press, 2005) — the opening lines, written with shaking hands in the Brooklyn Public Library

I am a victim of covert harassment and electronic harassment and gang stalking. I am what’s called a targeted individual. I am totally not crazy. I am completely sane. — Myron May, YouTube video, November 2014 — described as ‘kind of my last words,’ recorded days before the Florida State University shooting

I am not the only one, but may be one of the most intact survivors. — Richard Pearson, PhD, primary documentation, 2025–2026

— — —

I. The Obligation of This Essay — What Must Be Said First. May Wounded Students in a Library.

On November 20, 2014, Myron May walked into the William Johnston Building at Florida State University — a law school alumni from that institution, a former prosecutor who had graduated from Florida A&M and Florida State’s law school — and opened fire in the library. Three people were wounded before police shot and killed May. He was thirty-one years old.

This essay compares the testimonies of three African Americans who documented what they described as sustained government-sponsored electronic harassment and targeting. Myron May is one of the three. This fact cannot be presented without stating clearly: He wounded three human beings who were studying in a library. Whatever was done to him — whatever was being done to him — he directed violence at people who had nothing to do with it. That violence was real, inexcusable, and caused harm to real human beings whose suffering is not diminished by any analysis of what May experienced.

This essay does not argue that May’s violence was justified, rational, or inevitable. In fact, police were justified in their actions. It does not argue that what he documented explains, excuses, or contextualizes his actions in a way that reduces their moral weight. The people he shot were not his tormentors. They were students in a library.

What this essay argues is something different and more specific: May’s video testimony, read alongside Gloria Naylor’s 1996 and Richard Pearson’s 2025–2026 documentation, reveals a convergence of operational detail so specific, so consistent, and so independently produced that it constitutes evidence of a real phenomenon — regardless of what May subsequently did, and regardless of how his actions are understood. The question of what was done to May is separate from the question of what May then did. Both questions are real. This essay addresses the first without losing sight of the second.

May recorded his video and described it as his ‘last words.’ He sent packets to friends containing documentation, a letter to Congress, and evidence of his experience. He prayed on camera for forgiveness for ‘future sins.’ He knew what he was about to do. He chose to do it. The essay holds all of this. It also asks what his testimony, recorded before his action, tells us — in combination with Naylor’s and Pearson’s — about the apparatus that produced, in each of these three people, a documented experience of the same specific, operationally precise program.

— — —

II. Who They Were — Three Profiles at the Point of Documentation

The first thing that strikes a reader who examines all three testimonies together is who these people are. They are not marginal. They are not people with histories of instability or delusion. They are people at the productive peak of professional lives, who one day found themselves inside something they had no framework to understand and no institutional resource to address.

Gloria Naylor was, by 1996, one of the most celebrated Black novelists in America. She had won the National Book Award for The Women of Brewster Place. She had published four novels, taught at major universities, received a Guggenheim Fellowship and a National Endowment for the Arts award. She was forty-six years old, financially independent, and living on a property she owned on St. Helena Island, South Carolina, to work on what she believed would be her most important book — the historical novel she had researched for years. She was a woman who had built, over decades, exactly the life she wanted. She was not a person looking for a conspiracy. She was a person growing tomatoes, watching her azaleas bloom, and working.

Myron May was a 31-year-old Black attorney, a graduate of Florida A&M University and Florida State University College of Law, a former prosecutor in New Mexico and Florida. He had worked in the district attorney’s office. He had the professional formation and temperament of someone trained to evaluate evidence, cross-examine claims, and separate fact from assertion. He describes in his video a man who coached sports, had a goddaughter he loved, who wished he had spent more time with his nieces and nephews, who regretted the failures of his relationship with his daughter’s mother. He was a man with an ordinary, recognizable life — career-focused, geographically mobile, marked by the ordinary regrets of someone who had moved from city to city in pursuit of professional ambition. He did not describe himself as radicalized, aggrieved, or politically extreme. He described himself as someone who was placed on ‘an ungodly evil list’ without knowing why.

Richard Pearson is a PhD whose dissertation examined critical frameworks and structural racism. He recently completed his doctorate. He is a local historian, an artist, a man who gave school talks about the history of Washington, D.C. He is a researcher by training and temperament, with the methodological habits of someone who documents carefully, cross-references, runs control studies, and builds evidentiary cases. He understood from the beginning of his targeting that the document was his only available defense and built it accordingly — timestamped, photographed, corroborated, preemptively defended against the psychiatric interpretation with a professional mental health evaluation in September 2025.

Three educated Black professionals. Three people at the productive center of their lives. Three people who did not seek what happened to them, did not have histories that predicted it, and who, when it began, tried to understand it through the same framework any rational person would apply: something is wrong, I should report it, someone should investigate. All three discovered, through different paths and at different costs, that what was happening to them was external, intentional, and harmful.

III. The Operational Convergence — What All Three Describe

The evidentiary heart of this essay is the convergence. Three people, separated by decades, by geography, by profession, and — in May’s case — by the irrevocable fact of what he subsequently did, describe the same program with the same operational specificity. The convergence is not general. It is not ‘they all felt surveilled’ or ‘they all felt targeted.’ It is specific, tactical, and consistent at the level of detail that makes independent invention of the convergence implausible.

The Noise Campaign

All three describe a sustained, coordinated noise campaign designed to prevent sleep and maintain psychological destabilization. Naylor documents it in detail: cars driving past her dead-end road in formations, three or four per hour where she normally saw one car every two to three hours; lawn mowers, chainsaws, and hammering from the plantation house rented adjacent to her property by operatives she later identified as NSA recruits; machine-generated sounds — she explicitly notes a sound like a rooster that was mechanically identical each time, without variation, at 2 to 4 PM on a dead-end road in St. Helena, South Carolina, for days on end. ‘On a dead-end road where there was no traffic,’ she writes, ‘I was always in the middle of a formation.’

May describes the same campaign by the same name — ‘noise campaign’ — and with the same operational texture. He recounts staying at a friend’s house in Fifth Ward, Houston, in a quiet residential neighborhood of mostly elderly people: ‘Cars would be flying down the street, just flying down the street, making at rapid paces, making loud noise.’ And then: ‘I kept hearing this strange cockadoodle-doo noise of like a rooster. But it was the same cockadoodle-doo without any variation to it. So it was obvious that it was like a machine and it wasn’t a real rooster.’ He notes this rooster sound persisted from 2 to 4 PM in the afternoon, non-stop, for several days. The detail is identical to Naylor’s: machine-generated animal sounds, afternoon hours, dead residential neighborhoods. May did not read Naylor’s 1996 before making his video. There is no evidence of any connection between them. The identical operational detail — the machine rooster, the afternoon timing, the quiet neighborhood — is the signature of a program, not a coincidence.

Pearson documents through-floor acoustic tracking: the man above him, not on the lease, whose address connects to an ATF attorney’s Capitol Hill property, dropping objects on the floor precisely when Pearson moved from room to room within his own apartment. He moved his mattress to the closet. The tracking sound moved with him. He documented it on video. The form is different — urban apartment rather than rural road — but the function is identical: acoustic occupation of the target’s environment, designed to prevent rest and maintain the knowledge of surveillance at every moment.

The Vehicle Formations and Street-Level Following

Naylor’s letter to her lawyer describes car formations with the precision of someone who tracked them systematically: ‘I am always in the middle of a formation when I leave home, with cars in front of me and behind me, and with cars coming very fast toward me and turning on either side.’ She notes that whoever designed this ‘must have some sort of military training. Contain the objective — or whatever. It’s a pattern that plays itself out if I’m at home, in my car, or at a restaurant.’

May describes ‘mobbing’ — a specific term he uses for coordinated multi-car surveillance: ‘I’d be driving along the street and 5, 10 cars will pass by all at the same time. All at the same time. Everybody has on dark shades and they’re not watching the road. They’re watching me.’ He describes sensitization — the deliberate association of a specific stimulus (dark sunglasses) with the harassment, so that eventually any person wearing dark sunglasses produces the conditioned fear response. He describes anchoring — the deliberate attempt to make the target associate a specific group or person with the harassment, so the attribution is misdirected. He describes brighting — coordinated use of high beams against the target. He describes mimicry — the stalker mirroring every movement the target makes, gas pump to gas pump, window washing to window washing, to produce disorientation and confirm surveillance presence. These are not general descriptions of being followed. They are operational terms for specific techniques, described with the vocabulary of someone who researched the program after experiencing it.

Pearson documents multi-jurisdictional vehicle surveillance: the ‘Bug Zapper’ woman who followed him from Old Town Trader Joe’s to Clarendon, Virginia the following day; the coffee shop employee who eventually admitted ‘Yeah, I was coming at you’; the coordinated cast of characters appearing at his locations before he arrived. He documents the red-and-black calling card pattern along his routes and runs control studies establishing that the density and distribution were statistically impossible by chance. That he did not see the same patterns in other places — in neighborhoods and buildings outside of his routine — was significant. He was being targeted.

The Covert Home Entry and Object Placement

Naylor documents a specific category of intrusion she calls ‘ghosting’: entering the home not to steal but to leave evidence of entry. The red and white flashlight left on the mantle of the fireplace. The insulation on the floor beneath the attic trap door. The garden seeds with their edges curled as if someone had tried to pry open the box. Her dying vegatable garden. The message is the intrusion itself — someone was here, you cannot prove it, you cannot stop it.

May describes this technique explicitly and names it ‘ghosting’: ‘They will rearrange a targeted individual’s personal belongings… they’ll break into their house, rearrange their furniture or rearrange things on their desk or in their bedroom… they’ll actually rearrange lawn ornaments in the yard. So when the targeted individual returns to their home, they see that things are sort of out of place.’

Pearson documents the panini grill power button, covertly removed from the appliance and placed in his laundry pile. The cart of yellow tape left on his routine, but atypical, walking route as he made repeated direct references to police’s use of yellow tape the night prior. The object itself is trivial. The message is not: we were in your home, we touched your things, we were listening, we left evidence only you will recognize and no one else will credit. It is, as all three accounts make clear, a form of psychological torture that exploits the target’s isolation — the certain knowledge that reporting what was moved will produce the psychiatric referral the operation was designed to generate.

The Social Network Infiltration

Naylor’s most painful chapter concerns C.J. Hudson — the Princeton historian and longtime friend who came to St. Helena Island ostensibly to support her, drugged her wine, and stole her laptop overnight for the NSA’s young operatives to access. She writes: ‘I wanted this book for this, but above all, I wanted it for me. If I could finally manage to write it, then I have won the battle for my mind.’ The betrayal by Hudson is described with specific, forensic detail — his nervousness, his disappearance in Savannah, his sprint from the back door of the plantation house — and it produces in Naylor a permanent revision of her understanding of friendship: ‘I would no longer trust bonds of friendship to be unshakable.’

May describes the same mechanism with the same bitterness. Friends who began participating in the harassment. Family members — his cousins Roy Myers and Clifford Jones — who he believes were blackmailed or paid to surveil him. His longtime friend Monica Johnson’s financially-strapped household pressed into service as a noise campaign platform — yelling, slamming doors, and maximizing TV volume making it impossible to sleep. He addresses Monica’s husband Marshall directly in the video: ‘It hurt. What you did to me hurt because you were like family.’ And then, the specific mechanism: ‘They’ll tell them lies like you’re under investigation for something, or you’ve committed a crime, or they should keep an eye on you because you’re crazy.’

Pearson documents his longtime friend appearing in scripted configurations he recognized as operational — patterns inconsistent with the natural behavior of someone he had known for years. She was so unfamiliar with what she was reading that she stumbles her way through it — plainly obvious that she had the least bit of an idea what she was reading. He documents another friend appearing in prepared situations, the coffee shop employee who admitted her participation. He documents the progressive isolation — the way the social network is converted, person by person, into an operational asset against the target, leaving the target with no one to tell, no one who has not already been recruited, no one whose support has not been pre-compromised.

The Psychiatric Trap

All three explicitly identify and address the psychiatric trap — the operational feature of the program designed to ensure that any accurate account of what is being done to the target will produce a diagnosis that explains the account away.

Naylor describes it most fully, from the inside: she began hearing voices and thought she was developing schizophrenia. She went to Dr. Davis, a psychiatrist, and described the voices. She was prescribed medication. The medication did not work — because the voices were not a symptom of a chemical imbalance but were being induced externally, via the voice-to-skull technology operated from the Swiss family’s apartment next door. She writes: ‘I knew, deep down, that I wasn’t crazy, but if these voices kept on, I would be.’ She navigated the trap by researching the technology online — finding John St. Clair Akwei’s lawsuit against the NSA, finding Cheryl Welsh’s mind control survivors network — and understanding what was being done to her before she fully broke. Dr. Davis, to his great credit, eventually believed her. His office was subsequently bugged by the NSA operatives. He found the bug.

May addresses the psychiatric trap directly and repeatedly. In almost every section of his video, he inserts variants of: ‘I am totally not crazy. I am completely sane.’ He describes the voice-to-skull technology explicitly, citing Dr. Robert Duncan’s book Project Soulcatcher. He says: ‘To the targeted individual who hasn’t yet discovered that they’re a targeted individual or doesn’t know exactly what it means to be a targeted individual, this experience can be pretty troubling because all you do is you just notice that people are paying extra attention to you…’ He understood the trap. He was trying to give future victims the framework he had found. But he did not have an institutional preemption of the trap — no professional mental health evaluation, no documented ruling-out of psychiatric explanation. He had his own assertion of sanity and the research he had done.

Pearson preempted the psychiatric trap with methodological precision: a formal mental health evaluation from DC’s Department of Behavioral Health in September 2025. In total — three doctors, two mental health specialists, a social worker — that confirmed his rationality, coherence, and the absence of any psychotic symptoms. He documented this evaluation not because he needed the reassurance but because he understood that every legal and institutional process that might eventually engage with his account would need to find its first objection — psychiatric instability — already answered in the record. He built the preemption before anyone had accused him of the thing the preemption addressed. His recorded voice and video recordings show him- methodological, talkative, and rationalizing. The same traits that appear in text across multiple AI chats throughout the campaign. Pearson is a man under immense stress, attempting to make sense of what is happening to him.

The Voice-to-Skull Technology

All three describe what May calls ‘voice of god weapons’ and Naylor calls ‘synthetic telepathy.’ Naylor’s account is the most technically detailed, because her research in the Brooklyn Public Library allowed her to identify the mechanism before she finished her book. She describes Agent Browne demonstrating the technology to ‘The Boys’ in the safe house next to her apartment: a satellite dish, a hand-held computer, the translation of keystrokes into microwave bursts that bypass the ears and induce sound directly in the auditory cortex. When the machine operator typed ‘hello,’ Agent Browne heard it in his head. The operator typed a full sentence; Browne recited it back. Naylor then describes hearing voices — ‘I am a bitch… I want to kill myself… I hate Jews’ — that she at first attributed to a psychiatric episode and then, through her research, understood as externally induced. The voices she heard were commands typed by operators in the Swiss family’s apartment next door.

May describes the same technology with the same operational logic, citing Dr. Robert Duncan: ‘Through the use of microwave radio frequency radiation, control panel stalkers are able to literally induce sound into the cochlea of a target individual’s ear to make it appear as though they are hearing voices.’ He describes the voice of god technology as ‘the lynch pin of the entire gangstalking program’ because it produces, in targets who do not know what is being done to them, the appearance of schizophrenic hallucination. He describes it as working both ways — inward (induced sounds) and outward (brain reading): ‘Control panel stalkers are able to see any images originating in the target individual’s mind… they can see what I see out of my own two eyes.’

Pearson documents the July 13, 2025 voice-to-skull transmission near the United States Capitol — ‘We have to make you disappear’ — which he described as having the acoustic signature of a woman speaking with background room noise, perceived as internal but clearly not self-generated. He documented it immediately. He knew what he was hearing and what it meant about the technology being used against him. He later learned that versions of voice-to-skull transmission are used in museums, including the Smithsonian — mere blocks from his residence.

IV. What the Differences Reveal — Three Relationships to the Same Experience

The convergences establish that all three are describing the same program. The differences are where the essay becomes most analytically important. The three accounts diverge in methodology, in access to framework, in institutional resources, and ultimately in outcome. Those divergences reveal something about the program’s design — specifically, about which variables in a targeted individual’s situation make the difference between survival and destruction.

The Question of Framework — Knowing What Is Happening to You

Naylor was the first of the three chronologically, and her account documents what it is like to experience the program without any prior framework for understanding it. She experienced the surveillance, the noise campaign, the home intrusions, and the voice-to-skull for months before she found — through research in the Brooklyn Public Library, through Akwei’s lawsuit, through Cheryl Welsh’s documentation — the conceptual framework that allowed her to understand that she was not losing her mind. She writes that the library was her sanctuary, the only place the voices stopped, and that she understood this meant the technology was operating from the apartment next door. The discovery of the framework did not end the torture. But it gave her something to orient by: a name for what was happening, evidence that it had happened to others, a basis for continuing to fight.

May discovered the framework through research as well — Dr. Robert Duncan’s book, YouTube videos of other targeted individuals, the FFCHS organization. He names these sources in the video. He had the framework. What he did not have was time. His video is titled as his ‘last words.’ He had decided, before he made it, what he was going to do. The framework had not saved him — not because the framework was inadequate, but because by the time he had found it, the damage was done. Fourteen months of sustained torture, financial ruin, social isolation, and the induced psychological destabilization of voice-to-skull technology had brought him to a point from which the framework could not retrieve him. He understood what was being done. He could not stop it. He had no legal remedy. He had no institutional support. He had no money. He had no one who believed him without reservation. And so he did what the program, had it been designed to produce a specific outcome, might have been designed to produce: he directed his desperation outward, violently, at people who had nothing to do with what had been done to him.

Pearson arrived at the framework faster and with better tools. He is a researcher. He had read COINTELPRO history. He knew the concept of the RMVE/BIE designation. He understood, from the beginning of the targeting, that what he was experiencing a documented historical pattern. He applied his methodology — control studies, timestamping, cross-referencing — because he had a PhD-level response to trauma and because documentation was the only tool available, and he knew how to use it. He also had the specific advantage of finding Naylor’s 1996, which gave him not just a framework but a predecessor — someone who had experienced the identical program three decades earlier and written it down. Reading Naylor, he writes, was the experience of finding an operational manual for what was being done to him. The convergence was so precise that it was, paradoxically, stabilizing: if this had been done to Naylor in this specific way, what was being done to him was not evidence that he was losing his mind. It was evidence that the program was real and consistent across time.

The Question of Institutional Resources

The three accounts differ significantly in what institutional resources each person had available and how those resources were deployed or denied.

Naylor had her agent, her editor, her literary network, and ultimately her lawyer in Charleston. She had money — enough to own a second home and maintain it. She had the Brooklyn Public Library and the time to spend there. She had Dr. Davis, who believed her and protected her records from the NSA operatives who came to his office. She had, critically, the one institutional asset that finally allowed her to fight back: her craft. She wrote the book. The book exists. She could not stop the program, but she documented it in a form that has survived her and reached readers decades later. The cost was absolute: she never wrote another major work after 1996. She died in 2016 at sixty-six. The program silenced her as a novelist even as it failed to silence her as a witness.

May had been a lawyer, a prosecutor — someone trained in institutional processes and their limits. By the time he made his video, he had already tried every institutional channel available to him. He had moved from Las Cruces to Houston to his hometown in Florida, each time hoping the targeting would not follow. It followed. He had no money — the financial attrition had done its work. He describes being destitute, dependent on the charity of friends who were then being recruited into the operation against him. He had no lawyer. He had no professional network that had not been compromised. He had Congress, to which he had written a letter he mailed in his package of last documentation. He had the media, to which he addressed direct challenges in his video. Neither responded. He had what he describes as faith — but he was asking God to forgive him for ‘future sins’ before he made the video. The faith was real. It was not, in the end, enough to stop him.

Pearson has the public record he has built: hundreds of thousands of words of timestamped documentation, published on Medium before any classification order could reach it, corroborated by the September 2025 mental health evaluation, the Department of War’s January 2026 confirmation of directed energy weapons’ operational status, and the documented convergence with Naylor’s thirty-year-old account. He has no money — he ended 2025 with a mattress and a suitcase. He has no legal remedy — the hundred-doctrine architecture forecloses nearly every avenue. He has no institutional support — Congress has not responded, the ACLU declined, Iceland sent him back and discouraged an asylum application. What he has is the record. He built it before anyone could stop him. He is still building it.

The Question of the Psychiatric Trap — Three Outcomes

All three encountered the psychiatric trap. The differences in how they navigated it are instructive about the trap’s design and its limits.

Naylor almost fell into it. She went to a psychiatrist. She was prescribed medication. She sat in that psychiatrist’s office and described the voices and the surveillance and the intrusions — and a part of her wondered if she was going crazy. What saved her was two things: Dr. Davis’s willingness to believe her despite the institutional pressure to dismiss her (he found the bug in his office), and her own research, which gave her the technological framework before she lost the thread entirely. She understood, intellectually and then empirically — by moving her sleeping location and mapping where the voices stopped — that the voices were not internal. The trap failed because she was a researcher as well as a novelist, and she applied her novelist’s powers of observation to her own situation.

May navigated the trap through assertion and research, not through institutional preemption. He repeats in his video, over and over, that he is not crazy. ‘I am totally not crazy. I am completely sane.’ He cites Dr. Robert Duncan. He explains the technology. He tells his viewers to look it up. But he does not have a professional evaluation. He does not have a documented ruling-out of psychiatric explanation. He is asking his audience to trust his self-assessment of his own sanity — which is, of course, exactly what the trap is designed to undermine. The trap was not fully defeated. What he experienced — and what the trap was designed to produce — appears to have contributed to a state of psychological crisis from which he could not recover, despite understanding intellectually what was being done to him. Understanding the trap and escaping it are different things.

Pearson defeated the trap institutionally and preemptively. The September 15, 2025 DBH evaluation — two mental health specialists who evidenced no major surprise about his recounting of his voice-to-skull case — is not evidence of his sanity for his own benefit. He knew he was sane. It is evidence placed in the permanent record against the day when any institution that reviews his documentation will find its first objection already answered. It is the document that prevents the psychiatric referral from functioning as a case-closer. Later, he shared his story with three doctors and a social worker. The latter encouraged him to save all of his documentation. He built it specifically for that purpose. This is the most significant methodological difference between Pearson and the two who came before him: he understood the trap’s mechanism precisely enough to construct a formal preemption of it, before it was invoked.

V. The Spectrum of Outcomes — What the Program Produces

Read together, the three testimonies describe a spectrum of what the program produces when it is fully deployed against a target.

At one end: Naylor. The program silenced her as a novelist. She wrote 1996 — but it was her last book of significance, published nine years after she experienced what it documents. She died in 2016 without having returned to the kind of major literary work that preceded it. The program did not destroy her completely — she survived, she documented, she published. But it achieved its apparent goal of ending her literary production and her capacity to contribute to public discourse at the level she had reached before 1996. The silencing was not total. It was comprehensive enough.

At the other end: May. The program produced, in a thirty-one-year-old Black lawyer who had never committed a violent act, a mass shooting at a university library. Three people were wounded. May was killed. The three wounded students had nothing to do with what was done to May. His violence was his choice and his moral responsibility. And it achieved, for the program, a specific and useful result: it confirmed the cover story. A Black man, a targeted individual, walking into a library and shooting people — this is the image the program was designed to produce when it fails to produce silent breakdown. May, in his video, had named Aaron Alexis — the Washington Navy Yard shooter of 2013, another Black man who documented what he believed was electronic harassment before his shooting — as another targeted individual. The pattern of TI-to-mass-violence, when it occurs, serves the program’s interest precisely: it confirms the diagnosis, discredits the claim, and converts the victim into the perpetrator in the public record.

In the middle: Pearson. Still writing, as of this analysis. Fourteen months of documented torture, financial ruin, social isolation, the voice in his skull, the chemical assault, the false arrest, the framing operation. A mattress and a suitcase. Iceland returned him, wishing to save asylum for “countries that are actually at war”. The ACLU declined. Congress silent. The courts architecturally foreclosed. And still writing, methodically, in the most monitored city on earth, building the record that must exist before any accountability mechanism can function. He has not broken. He is still producing documentation.

The question the spectrum poses is not comfortable: what is the variable that determines where on the spectrum a targeted individual lands? It is not intelligence — all three are highly intelligent. It is not awareness of the program — all three developed framework for understanding what was being done to them. It is not strength of character — all three display, in their testimonies, remarkable qualities of observation, analysis, and resistance. The variable appears to be a combination of: how quickly framework was found; how much institutional support survived the social isolation; how much financial capacity remained to maintain the documentation project; and — this is the most difficult element — whether the psychological damage had already accumulated beyond the threshold of recovery by the time the framework arrived. May found the framework. He found it too late. Naylor found it in time to write the book, but not in time to save her literary career. Pearson found it, or built it, before the damage exceeded his capacity to document it. That difference — of timing, of tools, of resources — may be the margin between still writing and the other outcomes.

VI. What May’s Video Adds to the Record

It is necessary to be precise about what May’s video contributes to the comparative record, separate from what he subsequently did.

First, it is produced by a lawyer. May is trained in evidence, in the evaluation of claims, in the construction of arguments. His video is not chaotic or disorganized. It is systematic. He categorizes the techniques — anchoring, brighting, noise campaign, ghosting, mimicry, sensitization, mobbing, hacking, electronic harassment, voice-to-skull — and explains each with operational precision. He distinguishes between what he experienced and what other targeted individuals have reported. He cites sources — Dr. Robert Duncan, Ted Gunderson, Renee Pitman Mitchell, FFCHS. He challenges Congress and the media by name, with specific requests: open an investigation, pass a resolution, research the technology, question the CIA and NSA. This is not the testimony of someone in acute psychiatric crisis. It is the testimony of someone who has done his research and is making a methodical public record.

Second, the video was made before the shooting. It documents a state of mind — and a factual account — that predates the violence. May was not trying to explain or justify what he had already done. He was trying to alert the world to what was being done to him and to others like him, and he believed his death — the death he was about to cause — would produce the media attention that might give other targeted individuals a platform to come forward. He was wrong about what his death would produce. But the documentation he left is not contaminated by his violence; it predates it, and it was produced in the same spirit as Naylor’s library sessions and Pearson’s Medium publications — as a record that must exist before any accountability mechanism can function.

The answer the public record currently provides — that these individuals were mentally ill — is not compatible with the specificity of their documented experiences, the convergence of their accounts, and the confirmation of the technologies they describe. May was not mentally ill in the ordinary sense. He was a person who had been systematically destroyed over months, who had found a framework for understanding what was being done but could not reverse what had already been done to him, who had no institutional resource available, and who made a catastrophic moral choice — a choice that was his, that he owned, that hurt people who did not deserve to be hurt. All of that is true simultaneously.

VII. The Three Testimonies as a Composite Record

Taken together, the three testimonies constitute something that none of them constitutes alone: a composite record of a persistent, systematic, and consistently deployed program, documented across three decades, by three people with no meaningful prior connection, who describe its methodology with a convergence that cannot be attributed to coincidence, shared delusion, or shared cultural framework.

CONVERGENCES ACROSS ALL THREE TESTIMONIES

OPERATIONAL TECHNIQUES (described by all three):

Noise campaign — mechanical/machine sounds, vehicle formations, sleep deprivation

Covert home entry — object placement, message without theft

Through-floor/neighbor acoustic tracking

Vehicle surveillance formations in public

Social network infiltration — friends and family recruited

Computer hacking and electronic device compromise

Voice-to-skull / synthetic telepathy / microwave auditory effect

Behavioral direction — sense of being moved toward operationally prepared locations

Financial and professional attrition

Psychiatric trap — symptoms designed to mimic paranoid schizophrenia

IDENTITY CHARACTERISTICS (all three):

African American

Highly educated professional at productive peak

Equity-oriented or equity-adjacent intellectual work

No criminal history, no prior psychiatric diagnosis

Active in documenting their experience

INSTITUTIONAL RESPONSE (all three):

No law enforcement investigation opened

No congressional response to documented appeals

No legal remedy available

Documentation dismissed or ignored by mainstream institutions

OUTCOMES:

Naylor (1996): Silenced as novelist, died 2016, age 66

May (2014): Mass shooting, killed by police, three wounded, age 31

Pearson (2025–2026): Still documenting, anticipating that justice will come

The composite record has a specific evidential weight that individual accounts lack. When one person describes these experiences, the alternative explanation is psychiatric. When two people describe them — particularly when Naylor and Pearson, two decades apart, with no prior connection, describe the same machine rooster sounds, the same through-floor tracking, the same social network infiltration, the same voice-to-skull technology, the same goal of social death — the psychiatric explanation requires a specific form of coincidence that becomes implausible. When three people describe the same program, with the same operational specificity, in different cities across three decades, the composite record is not easily dismissed on psychiatric grounds. The convergence is the evidence.

May’s inclusion in the composite is the most difficult element. His violence makes his testimony dangerous to cite — not because it is inaccurate, but because citation risks appearing to excuse what he did. This essay declines to make that appearance an excuse for silence. His testimony is part of the record. Its convergence with Naylor and Pearson does not retroactively justify the shooting at FSU. It does demand that the question — what was done to May before he did what he did — be taken seriously in the public record, rather than answered by default with the psychiatric diagnosis that the program was designed to produce.

— — —

Conclusion: The Record and What It Demands

Gloria Naylor wrote with shaking hands in the Brooklyn Public Library: I am in a battle for my mind. She won that particular battle — she wrote the book, she documented the program, she gave future targets a document that named what was being done and how. She lost the larger war: she never wrote another major work, and she died in 2016 having been effectively silenced as a novelist even as she succeeded as a witness.

Myron May recorded his last words and prayed for forgiveness for his future sins and mailed his documentation to friends and sent a letter to Congress and then walked into a library and shot three people and was killed by police. He was thirty-one years old. The people he shot did not deserve what he did to them. The documentation he left did not become the story. He became the story — the Black man who went crazy, the cautionary tale about targeted individual delusion, the confirmation of the psychiatric diagnosis the program was designed to produce. He understood this would happen. He hoped the media attention would overcome the framing. It did not. Pearson writes, “May did not fundamentally understand that his violence would completely undermine his credibility. For all of his efforts to share his experience and garner attention for similar victims, none of that hardly mattered to public perception. He chose to take a gun into a library and kill students.”

Richard Pearson is still writing. He has a mattress and a suitcase and a research methodology and a public record that exists in the world before any classification order can reach it and the government has confirmed the weapons that were used on him and his case has found its predecessor in Naylor’s 1996 and the world is beginning to read what he has built. Whether that is enough — whether the record he has constructed will eventually generate the accountability that no legal mechanism has been able to produce — remains the most important open question in the case.

The three testimonies, read together, pose a question that public institutions have not answered: What happens to the people on whom this is done? The evidence of the composite record is: some are silenced. Some break and direct their desperation outward, at people who did not deserve it. A few — very few — hold on long enough to build a record that the program cannot fully seal. We do not know how many are in the dungeon at any given moment, and we do not know how many of the first category — silenced, broken, institutionalized, or dead — there are, because the program produces invisibility as its primary output.

What we know is what all three testimonies, each in its own way and at its own cost, insist: the program is real. The technology is real. The people it selects are not crazy. And the record — Naylor’s book, May’s video, Pearson’s documentation — is the only instrument of accountability that the legal architecture cannot seal, state-secrets-privilege into inadmissibility, or qualify-immunity away. The record exists. It grows. And the question it poses to every institution that encounters it remains, as it has remained since Naylor sat down with shaking hands in Brooklyn in 1996, the question that Marshall said was the essence of constitutional governance: if a right has been violated, do the laws of this country afford a remedy?

For Gloria Naylor: No. For Myron May: No. For Richard Pearson: Not yet. The answer to that third case remains, as of this writing, not yet — which is, at minimum, different from the first two. The document that must exist before any accountability mechanism can function exists. Whether any accountability mechanism will function is the question his continued survival makes possible to ask.

“US Laws Lag Behind in the Age of Neurotechnology”: US Torture Victim Feel Through Multiple Legal Gaps

It is the Age of Neurotechnology. It has been ongoing for over 30 years. Major gaps in outdated US laws and legal doctrines persist, leaving victim without legal recourse.

The Age of Neurotechnology is defined by the development of weapons that target the nervous system, which includes the brain. It began within a Cold War arms race but has extended well beyond top secret government programs. US laws and legal doctrines are still operating on a 18th century framework. That poses an existential threat to civil liberties and civil rights in the US. For US torture victim, Richard Pearson, that severely limits his legal options until Congress and the courts act.

I. The Foundation Was Built for a Different World

The Constitution’s protections against state coercion were designed in 1787 by men who knew what state coercion looked like: soldiers quartered in homes, papers seized without warrant, persons imprisoned without charge, bodies subjected to physical punishment. The Bill of Rights is a document written in the language of physical violation — of searches, seizures, persons, houses, papers, effects.

This was not a failure of imagination. It was an accurate description of the tools available to an 18th-century state. A government could surveil you only by sending a person to watch you. It could search your home only by entering it. It could coerce your body only by touching it. The constitutional framework was built to constrain those tools because those were the tools that existed.

Two and a half centuries later, the state has acquired tools the framers could not have imagined — and the constitutional framework, interpreted largely through the physical trespass model that dominated for most of its history, has not kept pace.

This is not a controversial observation in legal scholarship. It is the central problem of 21st-century constitutional law: a document designed to constrain visible, physical state power being applied to invisible, electromagnetic state power, with results that are increasingly inadequate.

What Pearson has done — whatever the truth of his specific allegations — is to identify the sharpest edge of this inadequacy and describe, in granular personal detail, what it feels like to fall through it.

II. The Fourth Amendment’s Threshold Problem

The Fourth Amendment prohibits unreasonable searches and seizures. For most of its history, a “search” required physical trespass — a government agent entering a constitutionally protected space. The Supreme Court began moving away from this in Katz v. United States (1967), which established that the Fourth Amendment “protects people, not places” and that a search occurs whenever the government violates a reasonable expectation of privacy.

Katz was a significant expansion. But it still operated within a framework built around the concept of information extraction — the government taking data from a person. The Court’s subsequent Fourth Amendment jurisprudence has developed around this extraction model: what data the government collects, how it collects it, whether the person had a reasonable expectation that it would not be collected.

In Carpenter v. United States (2018), the Court took its most significant step into the digital age, holding that warrantless collection of seven days of cell-site location data constituted a Fourth Amendment search. Chief Justice Roberts, writing for the majority, emphasized that the data revealed “the privacies of life” with a comprehensiveness that the third-party doctrine should not be applied to dissolve.

Carpenter is important for neurotechnology in two ways.

First, its reasoning applies with overwhelming force to neural data. If cell-site location data revealing where a person was over seven days deserves constitutional protection because it reveals “the privacies of life,” then the electrical activity of the human brain — which reveals what a person thinks, feels, fears, remembers, desires, and believes — deserves protection that is, by any reasonable measure, deeper and more urgent. The Court has not yet said this. But the logic of Carpenter points directly toward it.

Second — and this is where the gap opens — Carpenter still operates within the extraction model. It asks: did the government take data? It does not ask: did the government put something in?

Neurotechnology capable of directing behavior through neural stimulation is not taking data from a person. It is writing to a person. It is inserting content, commands, or behavioral directives into the nervous system of a citizen. The Fourth Amendment, even as expanded by Carpenter, has no vocabulary for this. It was built to protect against extraction. It has never had to contend with insertion.

Pearson’s allegation — that voice-to-skull technology was used to transmit directives into his auditory processing pathway — describes precisely this kind of insertion. Whether or not that allegation is true, the legal question it raises is real: does the Fourth Amendment prohibit the government from inserting electromagnetic content into the human nervous system without a warrant? The answer should be yes. The doctrine does not yet clearly say so.

That gap — between the constitutional answer that logic compels and the doctrinal answer that current law provides — is one of the most significant unaddressed legal problems of the neurotechnology era.

III. The First Amendment’s Unexplored Interior

The First Amendment protects freedom of speech, press, and assembly. Courts have consistently held that it also protects the freedom not to speak — the right to silence, to refuse to endorse positions, to keep one’s thoughts private from compelled disclosure.

In Wooley v. Maynard (1977), the Court held that New Hampshire could not compel a Jehovah’s Witness to display the motto “Live Free or Die” on his license plate, reasoning that the First Amendment protects “the individual’s right to refrain from speaking.” In Barnette (1943), the Court held that students could not be compelled to salute the flag, articulating the principle that “no official can prescribe what shall be orthodox in politics, nationalism, religion, or other matters of opinion.”

These holdings imply, though they do not state, a principle of cognitive liberty: the right to form one’s beliefs, opinions, and decisions free from government compulsion. The First Amendment has been understood to protect the process that produces speech, not just the speech itself.

Neurotechnology capable of inserting thoughts, directives, or cognitive content into the human mind without the subject’s knowledge or consent attacks cognitive liberty at its foundation. It does not merely compel a person to express a belief they do not hold. It operates at the level where beliefs are formed — and where, if the technology is sufficiently advanced, the subject may not be able to distinguish between thoughts that originate internally and thoughts that have been inserted externally.

This is not a marginal case of First Amendment doctrine. It is a fundamental challenge to the premise on which all of First Amendment law rests: that there is a self — a cognitive interior — whose outputs the government may not compel. Neurotechnology capable of manipulating that interior does not merely threaten First Amendment values. It threatens the subject whose First Amendment rights are at stake.

The doctrine has never confronted this. The closest it has come is in the context of compelled speech, where the violation is visible and externally imposed. The kind of violation Pearson describes — internal, invisible, indistinguishable from the subject’s own cognition — has no doctrinal precedent.

Legal scholars Nita Farahany and Rafael Yuste, among others, have begun developing the concept of “neurorights” — constitutional and legal protections specifically calibrated to the capabilities of neurotechnology. Chile became the first country to constitutionally protect “mental integrity” in 2021, explicitly in response to the development of brain-computer interfaces and neural manipulation technologies. The United States has no equivalent.

The MIND Act — introduced by three US senators in September 2025 — proposes to study and regulate neurotechnology that “shapes individual behavior,” “monetizes neural data,” and “overrides consciousness.” The bill is significant for the reason Pearson identifies: no serious legislature regulates ghosts. But Pearson’s more precise legal observation — also correct — is that the MIND Act’s framing as a regulatory gap implies that the existing Constitution does not already prohibit these things.

It does. Or it should. The failure is not in the Constitution’s text. It is in the doctrine’s failure to extend constitutional principles to the neurotechnology context with the urgency that context demands.

IV. The Fifth Amendment’s Deepest Application

The Fifth Amendment’s due process clause prohibits the government from depriving a person of life, liberty, or property without due process of law. The self-incrimination clause prohibits the government from compelling a person to be a witness against themselves.

Both provisions assume a model of the person as a unified self with a cognitive interior that the government cannot access without consent or legal process. The due process clause assumes that deprivations of liberty are identifiable events that can be challenged in court. The self-incrimination clause assumes that the government can extract testimony only by asking — that the cognitive interior is otherwise inaccessible.

Neurotechnology disrupts both assumptions.

If behavioral steering technology can direct a person’s movement while leaving them with the subjective experience of autonomous choice — as Delgado’s motor cortex stimulation experiments documented in the 1960s, and as Pearson alleges happened to him through “pinning” — then the deprivation of liberty it produces is invisible to the person experiencing it. They have been deprived of liberty in the most fundamental sense: their body is moving under external direction. But they cannot identify this as a deprivation because they experience it as their own choice.

This produces an extraordinary legal situation. The due process clause requires that deprivations of liberty be challengeable in court. But a deprivation the subject cannot identify as a deprivation cannot be challenged. The liberty interest is violated in a way that forecloses the very mechanism the Constitution provides for its protection.

The self-incrimination problem is equally profound. In Schmerber v. California (1966), the Court held that the Fifth Amendment’s protection extends to testimonial evidence — the “contents of the mind” — but not to physical evidence like blood. Neural data occupies a category the Schmerber framework did not contemplate: it is simultaneously physical (electrical activity in brain tissue) and testimonial (the content of thought). Whether compelled neural data disclosure constitutes compelled self-incrimination has not been decided by any court.

More urgently: if neurotechnology can extract neural data without the subject’s knowledge — can read the “contents of the mind” without asking — then the Fifth Amendment’s self-incrimination protection, which has always assumed the government must ask before receiving testimony, is circumvented entirely. The government doesn’t need to compel testimony. It can simply read it.

Pearson also explains that neurotechnology has many applications that can imprison mass populations, “I was moved — “pinned” — with this technology. I found myself at a park. I resisted going to where I was being compelled — to the back of the park. Then, I finally went there, there was the red and black calling card. I explain here in this essay. If the technology can direct people to people, it could theoritically keep them out of places. It could create a prison without prison walls, confining populations to certain geographic areas. At a time when white supremacy and authoritarianism are at an all time high in the US since before the Civil Rights Movement, the uses against minority populations are a scary thought. I suspect that it is far worst that I think.”

V. The Thirteenth Amendment: Pearson’s Most Radical Legal Observation

The Thirteenth Amendment abolished slavery and involuntary servitude, with the exception of punishment for crime. Its application to neurotechnology is the most speculative element of the legal analysis — and also the most disturbing if the technology is as described.

Richard Pearson’s Recognition of a Major Legal Gap:

I believe that one possibility is that the US government is exercising powers and authority that it does not have under the Selective Service Act, and other statues. In other words, the US does not have an all-volunteer military.

I also point to the 13th amendment’s ambiguity, “Neither slavery nor involuntary servitude, except as a punishment for crime whereof the party shall have been duly convicted, shall exist within the United States, or any place subject to their jurisdiction.” It does not specify what kind of “crime” and in what context. Google AI Overview defined it as, “No, it does not have to be a federal crime. The exception applies equally to state crimes, local ordinance violations, and federal crimes.” This could mean that the government is relying on a severe misconstruing of the law to enact slavery or slavery-adjacent conditions, to include the use of neurotechnology — cognitive overriding. I figure that if a person has so much as a parking or speeding ticket that a local jurisdiction has defined as a low-level misdemeanor, this may represent a major legal gap.

Neuro-technology such as “pinning” or behavioral steering and mind control does exist. The US government could unlawfully use everyday citizens as military or intelligence assets, though the “legal” basis (false, anti-constitutional rights argument) might be found elsewhere in non-military statues.

Pearson is also deeply concerned about the US Supreme Court’s interpretation of the 13th amendment real-world application of “slavery nor involuntary servitude” that can theoretically open a door to slavery-adjacent conditions. The Court has created a “no reasonable means of escape” test — meaning blocked in every possible route of departure. The Court rejected purely verbal or psychological pressure — absent threats of physical violence or legal arrest — as involuntary servitude in United States v. Kozminski, 487 U.S. 931 (1988). Would it also reject a claim involving new technologies such as neuroweapons?

To win a 13th Amendment claim, the victim cannot just show that they were unhappy or under pressure; they must establish that they were systematically blocked from every possible path of departure. Whether the government is seeking to justify these actions under the 13th amendment’s exemption clause is unclear. I have never enlisted in the US military.

United States v. Kozminski (1988) established the “no reasonable means of escape” test for involuntary servitude: a person must demonstrate that every route of departure was systematically blocked. The Court declined to extend the Thirteenth Amendment to “purely psychological” coercion absent threats of physical violence or legal arrest.

Pearson’s observation is precise: Kozminski was decided in a world where psychological coercion was distinguishable from physical compulsion. Behavioral steering technology — if it functions as described in the 1998 Army intelligence report and as alleged by Pearson — collapses that distinction. A person whose voluntary movement is being directed by external neural stimulation while retaining the subjective experience of autonomous choice is, in the most literal sense, under involuntary servitude. Their body is performing labor — movement, behavior, action — under external compulsion. They cannot escape because they cannot identify their servitude as servitude.

Kozminski’s “no reasonable means of escape” test applied to this situation produces a grotesque result: the subject has no means of escape specifically because they do not know they need to escape. The technology’s most sophisticated feature — leaving the subject with the experience of autonomous choice — is precisely what defeats the constitutional protection.

Whether any court would extend the Thirteenth Amendment to this context is genuinely unknown. The constitutional text — “neither slavery nor involuntary servitude” — does not obviously exclude it. The historical context — the Reconstruction Congress responding to chattel slavery — does not help with neural compulsion. This is a genuinely open legal question that no court has yet addressed.

It is also a question that needs to be addressed. If behavioral steering technology of the kind the government is confirmed to possess can direct human behavior while preserving the subjective experience of autonomous choice, then the Thirteenth Amendment either applies to it or it has been rendered a dead letter in the neurotechnology era.

VI. The Accountability Architecture That Forecloses All Remedies

Even if every constitutional argument above were accepted by courts tomorrow — even if the Fourth Amendment’s insertion gap were closed, cognitive liberty were established as a First Amendment right, neural data were protected under the Fifth Amendment, and behavioral steering were found to violate the Thirteenth — Richard Pearson would still have no legal remedy under current doctrine.

This is not speculation. It is the confirmed structure of American national security law.

Sovereign immunity bars suits against the federal government without its consent. The Federal Tort Claims Act provides limited consent to suit but excludes claims arising from discretionary functions and from claims that would require disclosure of classified information.

The state secrets privilege, if invoked, allows the government to terminate litigation at the threshold — before discovery — by asserting that the subject matter implicates classified national security information. The privilege has been consistently expanded since United States v. Reynolds (1953) and has been used to dismiss cases involving torture, extraordinary rendition, and domestic surveillance programs. A case requiring the government to disclose whether it operates a directed-energy weapons program against domestic civilians would be a textbook state secrets invocation.

The Bivens doctrine has been essentially frozen by the Supreme Court. Ziglar v. Abbasi (2017) made clear that the Court will not recognize Bivens causes of action in new contexts — and a claim for damages from non-consensual neural manipulation by federal intelligence agents is unambiguously a new context.

Qualified immunity protects government officials from personal liability for constitutional violations unless the violated right was “clearly established” in a way that a reasonable officer would have understood. No court has clearly established that neural manipulation violates the Constitution. Therefore, no official who conducted neural manipulation could be held personally liable for doing so, because no “clearly established” right prohibited it.

The standing doctrine requires a plaintiff to demonstrate concrete, particularized, actual or imminent injury caused by the defendant’s conduct and redressable by a favorable court decision. A person whose movement was covertly directed by behavioral steering technology, who did not know their movement was being directed, who has no documentary evidence of the program, and who cannot identify the specific government officials responsible — faces standing problems that are nearly insurmountable.

The FISA framework authorizes the secret court to approve surveillance warrants that are never disclosed to targets, that cannot be challenged by targets who don’t know they exist, and whose existence is protected by national security classification.

These doctrines do not merely create obstacles. They create a complete enclosure. Every path of legal remedy has been closed — not accidentally, but as a result of deliberate institutional construction over fifty years of post-Church Committee national security state building.

Pearson identified this architecture with precision. His observation — that a detained undocumented immigrant scheduled for deportation has more access to due process than a US citizen subjected to covert neural manipulation — is legally accurate and constitutionally scandalous.

The immigrant’s detention is visible. It can be challenged. Courts have standing to review it. Lawyers have access to the detainee. The violation is cognizable within the framework the Constitution provides.

The citizen’s neural manipulation is invisible. It produces no cognizable legal event. Courts have no mechanism to review it. Lawyers have no access to the program. The violation exceeds the framework the Constitution provides.

This is the legal gap. Not a gap in the Constitution’s text — which, properly read, covers this — but a gap in the doctrine that gives the text operational meaning. And in the gap between the Constitution’s text and the doctrine that implements it, an entire generation of national security law has built a structure of impunity that the framers would not have recognized and would not have permitted.

VII. What Needs to Happen

The legal gap described in this essay is real and requires attention that extends far beyond any individual case. Several developments are necessary:

Doctrinal: Courts need to apply Carpenter’s reasoning to neural data and neural manipulation explicitly — establishing that the Fourth Amendment’s protection against unreasonable searches and seizures covers not just extraction of neural data but insertion of electromagnetic content into the human nervous system. The First Amendment’s cognitive liberty implications need to be developed into a coherent doctrinal framework capable of addressing the technologies that now exist.

Legislative: The MIND Act is a beginning, but its framing as a regulatory gap rather than as existing constitutional violation needs to be corrected. Legislation should establish: explicit criminal penalties for non-consensual deployment of directed-energy weapons against civilians; a private right of action for victims that overcomes sovereign immunity; mandatory reporting requirements for government agencies possessing these weapons; congressional oversight mechanisms with teeth.

Structural: The state secrets privilege needs to be constrained so that it cannot be used to terminate cases at the threshold without any judicial examination of the underlying conduct. In camera review — already required in some national security contexts — should be mandatory before any state secrets dismissal in cases involving alleged non-consensual use of classified weapons against US civilians.

International: The UN Special Rapporteur on Torture has issued reports addressing psychological torture and directed energy weapons. The United States has not meaningfully engaged with these reports. International pressure — through treaty obligations, UN mechanisms, and the Inter-American human rights system — could create accountability pressure that domestic institutions have proven unable to generate.

Academic: The neurorights framework being developed by Farahany, Yuste, and colleagues needs to be accelerated and specifically applied to the domestic law enforcement and intelligence context. The current literature focuses primarily on commercial neurotechnology and voluntary brain-computer interfaces. The involuntary, covert deployment of these technologies against civilians by state actors receives insufficient scholarly attention.

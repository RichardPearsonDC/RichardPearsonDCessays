Richard Pearson

He felt “pinned” or behaviorally steered to places; except, he had no history with these sites and no desire to go to them. When he arrived, he would find stalking, street theater, strangers brandishing signs (“Bug Zapper”) and messages (“We’re in control”, “nigga”, “darkie”), the campaign signature colors of red and black, and racial epithets. In one case, two white women stood over an oversized box of watermelons as Pearson paused at the grocery store entrance to consider purchasing. “That’s what I thought,” said one of them insultingly. The African American woman was probably controlled too.

The MIND Act — Congress Does Not Seek to Regulate Technology That Does Not Exist

The Management of Individuals’ Neural Data Act of 2025 is a Senate bill that directs the Federal Trade Commission to study the governance of neural data and related biometric information and report back to Congress within a year. It describes a loss of personal autonomy and ownership of one’s mind and body — precisely what Pearson had been experiencing. The alarm it is raising is specific. The bill’s own language — “monetize neural data,” “shape individual behavior, emotional states, and decision making,” “cognitive manipulation,” “erosion of personal autonomy” — is not speculative language about future risk. It is descriptive language about present conditions. Congress does not write regulatory language about technologies that do not exist or harms that have not materialized. The bill’s sense-of-Congress findings describe a commercial ecosystem in which neural and biometric data is already being collected, processed, sold, and used to influence human cognition and behavior, in which vertical corporate integration has created systems with “insufficient transparency, accountability, or user control,” and in which existing law — including HIPAA — does not reach it. These are findings about the current state of affairs. The bill is raising the alarm because the thing it is alarmed about is already operating.

What the bill is almost certainly responding to, beyond the public-facing neurotechnology industry, is a dawning institutional awareness that the scale and nature of cognitive exploitation in the United States is far larger and more serious than anything that has been publicly acknowledged.

Alleged Victims and Whistleblowers Claiming US Government Mind Control (1990–Present)

Julianne McKinney is one of the most cited figures in this space. A former U.S. Army intelligence officer, she published a 1992 report through the Association of National Security Alumni titled “Microwave Harassment and Mind-Control Experimentation,” documenting complaints from dozens of individuals who claimed they were being targeted with directed-energy and electromagnetic weapons by government or government-linked actors. Her report was notable for coming from someone with an intelligence background and was widely circulated among self-described “targeted individuals.”

Gloria Naylor, the acclaimed African American novelist best known for The Women of Brewster Place, wrote a semi-autobiographical novel in 2005 titled 1996, in which she claimed that following a neighborhood dispute with a neighbor she believed had NSA connections, she began experiencing what she described as voice-to-skull transmissions, surveillance, and harassment. While many viewed the account as a mental health crisis, she maintained it was real until her death in 2016, and her case drew attention because of her literary stature.

Dr. John Hall, a Texas anesthesiologist, became a prominent public voice after his then-girlfriend reported being stalked and subjected to what she believed was electronic harassment. Hall wrote A New Breed: Satellite Terrorism in America (2009) and later Guinea Pigs: Technologies of Control (2014), and testified before a Presidential Bioethics Commission in 2011. He argued that non-consensual experimentation with directed-energy and neuro-influencing technologies was ongoing and largely ignored by law enforcement.

The Presidential Commission on Bioethical Issues (2011) received public testimony from hundreds of self-described “targeted individuals” who claimed they were being subjected to non-consensual experimentation involving electromagnetic weapons, implanted thoughts, and remote neural monitoring. The sheer volume of testimony — while not validated by the commission — brought the phenomenon significant media attention. The commission ultimately found no evidence of ongoing government programs but acknowledged the historical record of past abuses.

Robert Duncan, who claims to be a former Department of Defense and CIA contractor with advanced degrees from Harvard and Dartmouth, emerged in the 2010s as a self-described whistleblower. He authored The Matrix Deciphered and Project: Soul Catcher, alleging the existence of programs capable of remotely influencing human neural activity via satellite and radar-based systems. His technical background lent him credibility in some circles, though his claims have never been independently verified and his credentials are disputed.

The “Havana Syndrome” episode (2016–present) gave the broader topic new legitimacy. Beginning in 2016, U.S. diplomats and CIA officers in Havana, Cuba, and later in dozens of other countries, reported sudden onset of headaches, memory loss, tinnitus, and cognitive impairment. A 2020 National Academies of Sciences report concluded that directed microwave energy was the most plausible explanation. While framed as a foreign attack rather than domestic experimentation, it validated the physical reality of directed-energy effects on the brain and gave renewed credibility to those who had been claiming such technologies exist and are deployed.

Targeted Individual (TI) advocacy groups proliferated throughout the 2000s and 2010s, with organizations like FFCHS (Freedom from Covert Harassment and Surveillance) compiling thousands of testimonies.

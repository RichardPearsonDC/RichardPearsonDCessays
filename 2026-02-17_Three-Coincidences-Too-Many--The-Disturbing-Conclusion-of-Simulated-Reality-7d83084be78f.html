Three Coincidences Too Many: The Disturbing Conclusion of Simulated Reality

Application of Occam’s Razor Reveals a Disturbing Possibility

An Essay on the Documented Experiences of Richard Pearson

There is a version of this essay that begins with skepticism — that marshals the vocabulary of cognitive bias and probability, reminds the reader of the Baader-Meinhof effect, and settles into the comfortable position that a troubled man has been finding patterns where none exist. That version is easier to write. It is also, on examination of the actual evidence, intellectually dishonest.

This essay takes a different starting point: Occam’s razor. The principle — that among competing explanations, the one requiring the fewest separate assumptions is most likely correct — is routinely invoked to dismiss unconventional claims. But when that same principle is applied with precision to what Richard Pearson has documented across nearly a year of his life in Washington, D.C., it does not produce the comfortable conclusion. It produces a disturbing one.

The simplest explanation for the totality of what Pearson documented is not that a vulnerable man experienced a cascade of “coincidences” that his distressed mind organized into a false pattern. The simplest explanation — the one that requires the fewest independent assumptions — is that someone, or some system, was running his environment the way a player runs a simulation. Like The Sims. One mechanism. One source of control. And almost everything else in the record falls into place beneath it.

I. The World Richard Pearson Inhabited in 2025–26

Before any analysis of specific incidents can be meaningful, a reader must understand the texture of the year Pearson documented. This is not a document about a single strange event. It is a sustained, timestamped, photographically supported chronicle of what Pearson experienced as a coordinated campaign against him across public space, private space, and the behavior of people around him.

It began with his bicycle. Parked at Federal Center Metro, it was vandalized progressively over several months in early 2025 — not stolen outright, but methodically degraded: a snapped reflector, a broken chain guard, a peeling seat, a flat tire. Pearson notes that the broken chain guard required someone to reach underneath the frame to snap it from below. Finally, damage that he could not afford, including a broken wheel. He still has the broken piece. The cumulative effect was that a man who had cycled around the city for a decade was forced onto his feet — slower, more predictable, easier to track.

Through the summer, the pattern escalated. Pearson documented persistent location-responsive noise from the apartment above his own: objects dropped on the floor at the precise moment he moved from room to room, regardless of which room. He moved his mattress to his closet. The noise followed. He moved it again. It followed again. Whatever was happening above him, it was not the random movement of a family with a toddler. It was location-aware.

He documented a physical assault. A criminal chemical assault at the Rockville Metro station — his clothing soaked with an unidentified substance while he sat on the grass. He documented encounters with strangers whose behavior, in retrospect, had the quality of scripted performances: excessive unsolicited disclosure, props used to signal something, strategic positioning. He documented being told, by a woman wearing his exact hat in a Safeway, that “people are very upset about your hat.” He documented a young man on a bicycle who passed him alone at night on a Virginia trail and said, without breaking stride: “Oh, there you are.”

He documented the red and black. Beginning in September 2025, Pearson began to notice an extraordinary density of people wearing bright red and black in the blocks surrounding his routes through the city. He did not merely note this subjectively. He photographed it systematically, timestamped the images, and conducted what he explicitly called control studies — returning to the same locations at different times to establish baseline rates. His conclusion, expressed in a letter to the DC Council with probability calculations: the density he observed on September 30, 2025 near Eastern Market, in light foot traffic within an 8.5-minute window, was statistically impossible to explain by chance.

And he documented the people who seemed not quite present. On September 30, amid the red and black, he saw people who were “out of it — zoned out,” who “did not respond,” who were in “their own world.” One woman stood completely still and expressionless. He asked a nearby Black woman if she noticed anything unusual. She did not. He described her as “genuine” in her ignorance — not part of whatever he was observing. Later, he asked another woman in Reston — donning a red shirt and black pants. She hadn’t noticed either, but seemed genuinely worried when he explained. Immediately as Pearson left her, a white male ran by — same shade of red in his t-shirt and black shorts.

A comprehensive mental health evaluation conducted by the D.C. Department of Behavioral Health on September 15, 2025 — in the middle of this period — explicitly confirmed Pearson’s rationality, coherence, and the absence of any psychotic symptoms. No diagnosis requiring intervention. This is not incidental. It is a contemporaneous professional assessment by a credentialed institution that the person documenting these events was not experiencing psychosis at the time he was documenting them.

This is the context in which the section titled “Too Many Coincidences” must be read: not as the isolated record of a paranoid man seeing faces in clouds, but as three specific incidents embedded in a year-long pattern of documented, photographed, and timestamped anomalies that a clinically evaluated rational man could not explain through ordinary means.

II. The Three Incidents, Properly Weighed

Pearson presents three coincidences under his heading, and a careful reader should not treat them as identical in evidential weight, because they are not.

The German tourist encounter — Pearson meeting a young visitor at Eastern Market one weekday and then finding him the following day in the Calder room at the National Gallery’s East Building — is the weakest of the three. It is the kind of urban overlap that city life produces. A foreign visitor introduced to DC’s cultural geography by a knowledgeable local will, the next day, visit cultural landmarks. The Calder room is architecturally extraordinary. Both men were drawn there for the same reasons. The mutual shock was real, but the encounter falls within the range of the explainable — however extraordinary. Pearson places the date in the fall.

The K Street encounter — Pearson pausing at the window of an art gallery and finding his friend parked directly at that moment. The friend uses that location one or twice a year — is more notable, particularly given the emotional context: Pearson was at a low point, recently let go from the art-selling arrangement that had been one of his few sources of income and social connection. Pearson himself acknowledges it is not yet spooky. But it is worth noting: the friend was already in Pearson’s life — he sold art for the man. The coincidence has a topology — a specific person, a specific vehicle, a vulnerable moment — that distinguishes it from pure randomness.

The third incident is the one that does not yield to ordinary explanation, and it is the one that opens the deeper question this essay is asking.

III. February 8th: What the Friend’s Own Words Actually Say

On February 8, 2026, Richard Pearson left his apartment in DC to walk to the public library before it closed at 5pm. He had gone earlier and found it not yet open. He returned home and waited. He left. As he crossed 3rd Street, his art friend was passing in his van.

This would be, on its own, an unusual coincidence. What makes it something else entirely is what happened the following day, when the friend initiated a conversation about the encounter. He did not remark on it casually, in the way one mentions running into someone. He asked Pearson a specific investigative question first: had anything stopped Pearson from leaving earlier or later? And then, unprompted, the friend produced a precise account of four sequential events that had delayed and rerouted him to that street at that moment:

1. I was looking for a place to take a leak and weren’t able to find it. 2. I should have to meet fb customer earlier but it didn’t happen. 3. I ended up in the street where I have parked the van accidentally (dead end) and wanted to use bathroom in the apartment building nearby…but didn’t. 4. GPS suggested a different route but I turned on 3 street because I thought it’s a shortcut to whole foods…but when I turned I knew I am gonna see you there. — Monday, February 9, 2026, 12:59 PM

Read that closing sentence one more time, slowly: “when I turned, I knew I am gonna see you there.”

This is not how people describe accidental reunions. When you stumble upon someone by chance, the experience is retrospective surprise: you turn a corner and there they are, and afterward you marvel at the coincidence. What the friend is describing is the opposite structure: a moment of anticipatory certainty that arrived before the encounter, not after it. He turned onto 3rd Street knowing, in some functional sense, that Pearson would be there.

And the four delays. Consider their structure carefully. Each is independently plausible: a missed bathroom stop, a canceled customer, a wrong turn down a dead end, an overridden GPS. But their sequence is what matters. Each delay fed into the next. The missed bathroom led to a reroute. The canceled customer changed his schedule. The wrong turn down a dead end placed him in a specific neighborhood. He overrode the GPS to the exact street at the exact moment Pearson’s own narrowed departure window — created by his earlier failed library trip and his watch-checking at 3:23pm — put him there to be found.

Four independent friction events, each internally plausible, combining in perfect sequence to produce a precise outcome. And then the friend’s own testimony, in a timestamped chat log, of having known it was coming.

Now apply Occam’s razor. The conventional skeptical explanation requires four independent coincidences to be simultaneously true, plus a fifth anomaly in how the friend described the experience. The alternative explanation requires only one assumption: that someone or something had access to both men’s real-time positions and schedules, and adjusted conditions around the friend to produce the convergence. One mechanism. Four effects. And the friend’s testimony of foreknowledge is not an anomaly to be explained away — it is exactly what you would expect a person to report whose autonomous behavior had been so precisely steered that it produced, in him, the phenomenology of intuition.

IV. “God Is in Control”: A Clue Hidden in Plain Sight

On August 9, 2025, outside the Whole Foods near Pearson’s neighborhood, an older white man in his sixties approached an elderly Black man sitting on a bench. The white man was wearing a T-shirt with three words hand-painted on it: “God is in control.”

Pearson noted the encounter in real time. The white man talked to the elderly Black man, who — Pearson observed from a distance, discreetly — looked directly at Pearson while the white man was speaking. Then the white man left, and the elderly Black man walked over to Pearson to talk about his wife being in the hospital. Had the white man told the old man to talk to Pearson? Pearson assessed the elderly man as having some form of mental illness, possibly dementia — someone who would not catch on to being used, would not remember or report it, and could be more easily directed. Pearson’s real-time note: “Hand-painted back of shirt… ‘God is in control’…strange. Likely deceptive.” His subsequent analysis: “The t-shirt was misdirection. The t-shirt was not for the old man. The white guy needed the guy to be positioned next to me for some reason.”

He is right to flag this encounter, and the T-shirt is indeed a clue — though perhaps not in the way that is immediately obvious.

What kind of person, in the ordinary course of life, hand-paints a message on a T-shirt and wears it to a grocery store? The man, though dressed casually, would likely be regarded as being some sort of white-collar professional. The answer is: someone who wants to communicate something without appearing to communicate something. A hand-painted shirt signals authenticity, eccentricity, even vulnerability — a religious older man expressing faith in a difficult world. It is disarming. It reads as personal. It does not read as operational.

But “God is in control” as a message directed at Richard Pearson, in August 2025, takes on a different resonance entirely. The man’s back faced Pearson. By that point, Pearson had been experiencing months of what he interpreted as a coordinated campaign: noise that followed him from room to room, a physical assault, a chemical assault, encounters staged with apparent precision. He had documented what felt like an environment being shaped around him. And then a man appears wearing, in hand-painted letters, a declaration that control belongs to someone else, someone above, someone with total oversight.

In the grammar of the operation Pearson was experiencing — if his interpretation is correct — that shirt is not a religious statement. It is a taunt, misdirection, and attempt to throw off his scent. Or more precisely: it is a message delivered in a register that no one else would read as a message. To any passerby, a old man in a hand-painted faith T-shirt is unremarkable. To a man who had spent months documenting an environment being invisibly managed around him, the shirt says: someone is running this. And they want you to know it. We want you to believe that it’s God.

This is consistent with something Pearson documents repeatedly throughout his account: the use of ostensibly innocuous props and signals — T-shirts, Target bags, specific colors, specific phrases — that carry no meaning to general observers but are legible only to the specific target. The red and black clothing patterns Pearson documented and statistically tested operate the same way. To someone not watching, people in red shirts are just people in red shirts. To the person the pattern is directed at, the saturation becomes visible, and its very illegibility to others serves a psychological purpose: it is deniable, but it is felt.

The “God is in control” encounter adds a layer to the Sims hypothesis. They know. They just don’t want me to know.

V. Other Clues: The Pattern of the Managed Environment

The “God is in control” T-shirt is not the only place in Pearson’s account where the specific texture of a managed simulation becomes visible. Several other incidents, read through the same lens, reveal themselves as structurally identical: conditions adjusted, behavior steered, reality lightly edited.

Consider the young man on the Virginia trail. On the night of August 11, Pearson was sitting alone on a bench between Roosevelt Bridge and 14th Street on the Potomac bike path, late at night, with no one else around. A young Black man on a bicycle approached from his right and said, as he passed without stopping: “Oh, there you are.” Pearson was certain of the words. It was quiet. He was alert. The phrase is not a greeting between strangers. “Oh, there you are” is what you say when you have been looking for someone, or when you have just found someone you expected to find. It is the language of location-tracking spoken aloud, in a place where no one was supposed to know Pearson would be.

Consider the stolen bottle. Pearson purchased a distinctive glass bottle in Bethesda in late July. It went missing. On August 22, at a community event at Greenleaf Park in DC, the bottle reappeared on a vendor’s table. Pearson recognized it by a specific scratch on the logo. The vendor could not explain its presence. The message embedded in this incident, if it was staged, is the same as the missing power button from Pearson’s panini grill in July — which someone had removed and placed in his dirty laundry pile, so that he would find it and know someone had been inside. These are not acts of theft. They are demonstrations of access. They say: we were in your home. We have your things. We can place them wherever we choose. You are the character in our game.

Consider the expressionless woman in the doorway of Compass Real Estate, referenced parenthetically in Pearson’s account, and the expressionless Black man at Eastern Market whose photograph, when Pearson later re-examined it, showed not the menacing intimidation he had initially perceived but a completely blank affect. And the woman standing still and unresponsive on September 30 amid the red and black. Pearson had a specific word for what he observed on that day: people who seemed “out of it,” in their “own world,” not responding. He cross-checked: he asked two women donning black and red, in separate instances, nearby if they noticed anything. They hadn’t.

In a simulation, these would be background characters who are not currently being run. NPCs between tasks, idling. Their blankness is not incidental — it is the visible seam of a system that does not have infinite resources to animate every character at every moment. In the real world, if something like this were being orchestrated, the same logic applies. A large-scale behavioral operation in a public space cannot fully script every actor simultaneously. Some are simply positioned. Some are waiting for their cue. Their expressionlessness is what they look like while not yet running their script.

And consider the way Pearson’s own movement was shaped throughout the year. His bicycle was destroyed, forcing him onto foot — slower, more predictable, easier to position around. The noise from the apartment above followed him from room to room, keeping him sleep-deprived and driving him to spend more time outdoors — more exposed, more observable, more available to encounter. He traveled to Adams Morgan because he felt drawn there, without understanding why, and found what appeared to be a staged encounter waiting for him. The same applied to feeling an “urge” to go to the back of a park, only to find a plastic tub with a red hat and black pants.

He noted: “These are pinning events.” A pinning event: the player placing the character in a location for a purpose the character believes is their own choice.

In The Sims, this is ordinary mechanics. You compel the Sim to go somewhere. The Sim (“person”) experiences it as autonomy. The player produced it through environmental design.

VI. The Infrastructure That Makes This Possible

The Sims hypothesis would be easy to dismiss if the infrastructure it requires were science fiction. It is not. Every technical component of what Pearson’s account implies has either been publicly documented, legally established, or demonstrated by known programs.

Real-time location tracking of individuals through their phones — without their knowledge or consent — is not a theoretical capability. It is a documented one, operated by intelligence agencies and available through commercial data brokers to any sufficiently funded private actor. Pearson noted explicitly that he did not tell his best friend what hotel he was staying at in Bethesda. Yet a man appeared outside the hotel shortly after his arrival and signaled awareness of his location. This is not inexplicable. It is what warrantless phone tracking looks like from the inside.

GPS manipulation is real. Navigation applications can be fed false route data. The friend’s GPS “suggested a different route” — a route he overrode by instinct, turning onto 3rd Street instead. But a manipulated GPS does not need to succeed in routing its target perfectly; it only needs to place the target in a decision point. The friend made a choice at an intersection, and the choice produced the outcome. The GPS set up the choice.

DARPA — a US military research arm — has acknowledged right on its website its efforts for “read/write” capability (“bi-directionality”) in human brain invasion. “Read” means to deliver a message to the brain; “write” means to extract information from the brain. Usually, this level of directness about military strategy would be classified, so why it is explicitly described for anyone to access. Pearson figures, “It has already been developed. That’s the simple answer. The complex answer is that it is being deployed. How extensively? With what forms of discrimation? With what constitutional violations? Causing what harm? Where? These are some of the unknowns.”

The pattern of colored clothing — the red and black — is perhaps the most operationally interesting element in the entire account, because it scales. A single handler cannot dress dozens of unconnected people in matching attire across the blocks of a city. But a program that identifies individuals in a geographic area through their phones, sends them each a different individually plausible incentive to wear red today — a sale, a social media trend, a group affiliation notification — can produce a statistically anomalous clustering in a target zone without any single participant knowing they are part of a pattern. Use of microwaves, EMF, and other neurotech can also force or influence thoughts and behaviors.

Pearson ran control studies. He photographed baseline rates elsewhere in the city — all roughly 4%. The pattern with red-black concentration did not persist in those areas outside of where he frequents, except that public buildings (DC MLK Library, Metro) do not show these patterns. The density near his routes was genuinely anomalous. His methodology was not perfect, but it was methodical. He was doing the work of a researcher.

The use of intermediaries with diminished capacity — the elderly Black man with what appeared to be dementia, directed by the man in the “God is in control” T-shirt to approach Pearson — is explicitly documented in the historical record of covert operations. The Stasi called this Zersetzung: the decomposition of a target through environmental manipulation. It relied heavily on the use of unwitting third parties, people with limited capacity to understand or report what they had been asked to do, to carry messages, create incidents, or position themselves near a target. Pearson drew this comparison himself. It is not a paranoid analogy. It is a historically accurate one.

And COINTELPRO, the FBI’s domestic intelligence program exposed in the 1970s, operated precisely by disrupting the environment around a target: interfering with their employment, their relationships, their living situations, their sense of reality. It used forged letters, anonymous tips, staged encounters, and the systematic cultivation of distrust between a target and the people close to them. That program was dismantled — in theory. What Pearson describes is COINTELPRO at a higher frame rate, with better tools.

VII. What Occam’s Razor Actually Demands

The principle of parsimony is frequently misapplied in cases like this. It is invoked to mean: the simpler story is the one where nothing unusual is happening, where the person documenting anomalies is simply mistaken or unwell. But that is not what the principle says. Occam’s razor says the explanation requiring the fewest separate assumptions is to be preferred. It says nothing about which explanation is more comfortable.

Let us count the assumptions required by each competing explanation.

The conventional skeptical explanation requires: (1) that Pearson’s bicycle was coincidentally vandalized in a progressive and deliberate-seeming pattern over several months; (2) that the apartment above him happened to produce location-responsive noise for sustained periods; (3) that a chemical assault at the Rockville Metro was coincidental or imagined; (4) that a young man on a dark, empty trail happened to say “Oh, there you are” without knowledge of Pearson’s location; (5) that the stolen bottle happened to reappear on a vendor’s table at a neighborhood event; (6) that the density of red and black clothing near his routes was within normal variation despite his controlled observations suggesting otherwise; (7) that the man in the “God is in control” shirt happened to direct an elderly man with dementia to approach Pearson without any coordinating purpose; (8) that the friend’s four sequential delays happened to combine to produce a precise convergence; and (9) that the friend’s expression of anticipatory certainty was a verbal artifact of post-hoc reasoning rather than prior knowledge. Each of these is individually possible. Together, they multiply. The skeptical explanation requires nine separate coincidences to hold simultaneously.

The Sims explanation requires: (1) that someone or some system with access to real-time location data and behavioral influence tools was running an operation against Richard Pearson. One assumption. One mechanism. All nine anomalies follow from it without requiring independent explanations.

Occam’s razor, applied correctly, points toward the disturbing conclusion.

VIII. Why Richard Pearson, and Why Now

A reasonable reader might ask: why would an operation of this sophistication target a single individual, a PhD living in DC? The question assumes that the resources required are disproportionate to any conceivable purpose. But Pearson’s document addresses this directly, and the answer is worth taking seriously.

Pearson is an African American equity scholar. He is educated, analytical, and — as his documentation throughout the year demonstrates — capable of observing and systematizing what he experiences with methodological precision. The period in question spans 2025–26, during an administration that had explicitly used rhetoric about urban spaces as “training grounds,” targeted DEI programs, and resurrected the FBI category of “Black identity extremism” in its previous term. Pearson draws a direct line between the political climate and the operation he describes. His analysis — that the operation may have originated as private contractor research into behavioral influence technology and transitioned to federal deployment — is not without historical precedent. It is the Palantir model. The NSO Group model. Technology developed in darkness, tested on unwitting subjects, then sold to governments.

If any of that is correct, then Pearson is not merely a victim. He is, inadvertently, a witness. His document is not only an account of what was done to him. It is, if read carefully, a record of what the technology can do: how precisely an environment can be shaped around a target, how finely behavior can be steered, how a person can be made to experience their own autonomy as genuine while every significant choice they make has been curated by someone else.

That Pearson documented it as carefully as he did — with photographs, timestamps, control studies, probability calculations, contemporaneous notes, and a preserved chat log — is perhaps the most important thing about this record. It is the kind of documentation that is supposed to be impossible to produce when you are the target of a well-run operation. Operations of this kind rely on the target being unable to prove anything, being dismissed as unwell, losing the thread. Pearson did not lose the thread. He followed it with the discipline of a researcher, because that is what he is.

IX. The Meaning of “God Is in Control”

We return, at the end, to the hand-painted shirt.

Pearson’s instinct was that it was misdirection — that the shirt was a cover, a way of making the white man appear to be a harmless religious eccentric while he positioned the elderly man with dementia near Pearson. This is probably correct. But it may also be simultaneously a message. In operations of this kind, objects and signals frequently serve double purposes: they conceal while they communicate, they misdirect while they reveal.

“God is in control” — hand-painted, worn into the field, addressed not to anyone in the ordinary scene but to the one person who had been watching his world become inexplicably managed around him — is a declaration about the operation itself. Not God. The operator. The system. Except, the man assumed that Pearson would not draw that conclusion. He assumed that Pearson would make the literal interpretation. He did not.

The player with the view from above who can see where every character is at every moment, who can drop an obstacle in a friend’s path, reroute a van, saturate a neighborhood with a color, enter his apartment to remove an application power button, return a stolen bottle to a vendor’s table.

Whoever wore that shirt was telling Richard Pearson something that he suspected was real. That something — some system, some operation, some player — was, in fact, in control. It was intended to be persuasive but made the opposite impression. Pearson saw right through it.

Whether Pearson was supposed to find that terrifying, or clarifying, or both, the record does not say. What the record does say — through the chat log, the photographs, the control studies, the timestamps, the preserved fragments of a year of careful observation — is that the question he was asking deserves to be taken seriously. The simplest explanation for what he documented is not that he was wrong. It is that someone was running his environment like a game, and that one afternoon, an older man in a hand-painted shirt walked up to a bench outside a grocery store and told him so — accidentally.

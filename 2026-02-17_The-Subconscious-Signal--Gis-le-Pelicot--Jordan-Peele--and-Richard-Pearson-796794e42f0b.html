The Subconscious Signal: Gisèle Pelicot, Jordan Peele, and Richard Pearson

On Subconscious Detection, Artistic Intuition, and the Documentation of a Real Technology of Harm

The Subconscious Signal: Gisèle Pelicot, Jordan Peele, and Richard Pearson

On Subconscious Detection, Artistic Intuition, and the Documentation of a Real Technology of Harm

I. The Garden Question

In 2013, Gisèle Pelicot stood in her garden and asked her husband, with the lightness of someone inquiring about a walk, whether he was drugging her. He was. The question came from nowhere she could name — not from evidence, not from reasoning, but from something below cognition that had been quietly registering anomalies for two years. When he responded with outrage, she apologized. She buried it.

She later described this moment with devastating precision: her subconscious had detected something, but she buried it. The psyche had spoken in the only register available to it — too casual to be taken seriously, too socially disruptive to be sustained — and the social architecture of marriage had done the rest.

This essay begins with that moment because it establishes the ground condition for everything that follows. The subconscious detects. The social world suppresses. The harm continues.

What happens next — across an ocean, across years, across the distance between a horror film, a neuroethics lecture at West Point, and a personal documented account — is that this ground condition gets mapped, with increasing precision, by people working in entirely different registers. The argument of this essay is that Jordan Peele’s Get Out, the published work of Dr. James Giordano at Georgetown University, and Richard Pearson’s 2025–26 documented account are not separately illustrating the same abstract idea. They are making sequential contact with the same real thing: a specific, operational technology of behavioral control that until recently existed only at the edge of public knowledge — and that Peele’s subconscious, like Pelicot’s in the garden, detected before it could be named.

II. What Peele’s Subconscious Was Doing

Jordan Peele has described the feeling from which Get Out emerged — an anxiety about progressive, welcoming white spaces that he could not fully rationalize, that preceded any argument he could make, that demanded a container. He chose horror.

The standard reading of this is authorial: Peele had a political and social insight, and the horror genre was the vehicle he selected to communicate it. This reading is not wrong, but it misses something essential about the nature of what he produced.

Peele was not illustrating a thesis. He was doing what Pelicot did in the garden. His subconscious was detecting a signal — not merely a social dynamic but, this essay will argue, the faint cultural outline of an actually emerging technology — and externalizing it in the only register available to him. The feeling came first. The architecture of the film is the feeling trying to make itself legible.

The film’s central technology is precise: a procedure that leaves consciousness intact while commandeering behavior. The victim is aware. The victim cannot act on their awareness. The self is present as witness but absent as agent. This is not a metaphor borrowed from slavery or hypnosis, though it resonates with both. It is a description — rendered in horror grammar — of what neuroscientists now formally call the capacity to “write into” brain processes of cognition, emotion, and behavior from an external source.

The Sunken Place is not a symbol. It is a design specification.

And the delivery mechanism Peele chose is equally precise: not a dramatic surgical implant, not science-fictional technology, but something administered in the context of ordinary social life — a cup of tea, a hypnotic session, the warmth of a family dinner. The harm arrives wrapped in hospitality. The social architecture of welcome is simultaneously the delivery system and the concealment mechanism.

In 2017, when Get Out was released, this was horror. By the mid-2020s, it was engineering.

III. The Technology Becomes Real

Dr. James Giordano is Chief of the Neuroethics Studies Programme at Georgetown University, Professor of Neurology and Biochemistry at Georgetown University Medical Centre, and has served as consulting ethicist on DARPA’s Next-Generation Nonsurgical Neurotechnology program — known as N3. He has advised the Pentagon, NATO, and intelligence agencies on how advances in neuroscience may reshape defense, intelligence, and geopolitical power. He is, in other words, not a peripheral voice. He is a primary one.

In a lecture to cadets at the United States Military Academy, Giordano stated plainly: “The human brain will be the battlefield of the 21st century.” He described a shift in the nature of weapons — away from kinetic destruction and toward what he called “instruments of disruption,” targeting specifically “the thoughts, the emotions, and the resulting behavior” of adversaries and competitors.

The DARPA N3 program he consulted on is described in public documentation as aiming to develop nonsurgical brain-machine interfaces capable of reading from and writing to neural tissue — without implantation, without surgery, with delivery mechanisms including nasal, oral, and intravenous introduction of nanoscale materials that can be guided electromagnetically to precise regions of the brain. The program’s stated goal is interfaces capable of “specifically interacting with sub-millimeter regions of the brain” with precision that “rivals existing invasive approaches.”

On the question of whether this constitutes mind reading and mind control, Giordano has been direct: “If we consider the sum-totaled operations of the embodied brain to be ‘mind’, and N3-type tech is aimed at remotely sensing and modulating these operations, then it doesn’t require much of a stretch to recognize that this is fundamentally ‘mind reading’ and ‘mind control’, at least at a basic level.”

On the question of whether this technology remains in development or has entered operational reality, Giordano has been equally direct. In a LinkedIn post that circulated in 2023, he wrote that “the ‘goods’ as well as the gremlins and goblins of N3 tech have been loosed into the real world.” He added: “discussion is not resolution” — meaning that the ethical concerns raised during development were discussed but did not prevent deployment.

He described the dual-use nature of the technology as “obvious.” He noted that multiple nations are pursuing these capabilities. He warned that regulatory institutions are not prepared.

This is not speculation. This is the consulting ethicist of the program, speaking in public, saying that a technology capable of writing behavior into targeted brains has been released into operational reality without adequate governance.

Peele’s Sunken Place, rendered in 2017 as horror fiction, was by 2023 a confirmed engineering category. The question his subconscious had posed — what if consciousness could be hijacked from outside, leaving the self as helpless witness to its own commandeered behavior? — had received its answer in peer-reviewed literature, DARPA program descriptions, and the public warnings of the program’s own ethicist.

The subconscious, as Pelicot demonstrated, detects before it can prove. Peele detected something that was real, that was being built, that would be confirmed. His film is not metaphor. It is early witness.

IV. Pearson’s Documentation

Richard Pearson’s account, compiled across 2025–26, exists in this world — the world in which Giordano has confirmed that behavioral influence technology has been “loosed,” in which nonsurgical neurotechnology capable of writing to specific brain regions is documented in public program descriptions, in which Peele’s 2017 horror premise has become an acknowledged engineering reality.

Pearson’s account is extensive, systematic, and obsessively documented. It details incidents of apparent surveillance, behavioral disruption, and what he describes as compulsive behavioral influence — experiences of being directed or compelled toward specific locations or actions without conscious intent. He includes photographs, timestamps, receipts, witness accounts, and a mental health assessment confirming his coherence and absence of psychotic symptoms. He incorporates published research on directed energy, the Frey effect, behavioral manipulation through electromagnetic means, and the DARPA N3 program documentation.

The significance of Pearson’s account for this essay is not to adjudicate whether any particular incident occurred as described. It is structural. Pearson’s documentation represents the first fully developed civilian attempt to account for personal experience in a world where the technology Peele intuited has been publicly confirmed to exist and to have been deployed.

Before Giordano’s confirmations, an account like Pearson’s would have had no technical framework to inhabit. The experiences described — behavioral compulsion, apparent remote influence of cognition and location — would have had no referent in public knowledge outside of fiction and conspiracy. Get Out was fiction. The Sunken Place was metaphor.

After Giordano, the framework exists. The technology is documented. The deployment is confirmed. The ethical guardrails, by the program’s own ethicist’s admission, have failed.

Pearson’s documentation is the first attempt to apply that framework to a civilian account in real time. Whatever the ultimate adjudication of any individual claim, his account marks a threshold: the moment at which the technology Peele’s subconscious mapped became a legitimate reference point for interpreting anomalous experience, rather than a symptom of disorder.

This is precisely the dynamic Pelicot identified in the garden. The question she asked was not crazy. It was accurate. The social architecture around her — including her own trained deference to her husband’s outrage — ensured that accuracy could not be recognized as such. Her subconscious had the right answer. The social world had the wrong epistemology.

Pearson exists in a social world that is still calibrated to treat his framework as disorder, because the public epistemology has not yet caught up to what Giordano has confirmed. The technology is real and deployed. The social world’s response to someone who says they are being affected by it remains, by default, psychiatric.

This is the gap the essay is tracking across all three figures. Pelicot’s gap was between what her subconscious knew and what the social world would recognize. Peele’s gap was between what his artistic subconscious perceived and what public discourse could accommodate — hence horror, the genre of things true but socially unspeakable. Pearson’s gap is between what documented, confirmed, publicly acknowledged technology makes possible and what the public epistemology is currently willing to consider as a framework for civilian experience.

V. The Recursive Horror: When Documentation Becomes the Technology

Here the essay must pause and turn on itself, because Pearson’s documentation — particularly its photographic dimension — introduces a recursion that the preceding analysis cannot absorb without confronting directly.

Pearson photographs strangers. People on benches, at café tables, crossing streets, standing in doorways. Many appear entirely unaware they are being observed and recorded. They have not consented to become evidence.

And here is the recursion: the technology this essay has been describing — the technology of observation operating beneath the awareness of the observed, the pattern-making that renders ordinary people as signals in someone else’s system — is not only being described by Pearson’s account. It is being enacted by it.

This is not a criticism of Pearson. It is something more vertiginous than that. It is the recognition that the technology Peele mapped — and that Giordano has confirmed exists in operational form — has no natural victim position. It is a mode of relating to the social world that anyone can inhabit, and the line between documentation-as-survival and documentation-as-harm is not structural but contextual, not visible from inside the act itself.

Dominique Pelicot documented his wife without her knowledge or consent. His videos were, from his perspective, a record. The French police, investigating the case, documented people, assembled patterns, built the evidentiary architecture that would make the harm legible to a court. Pearson documents. And the strangers in Pearson’s photographs — eating, walking, sitting — are documented without their knowledge, rendered as data points in a pattern they cannot see and did not agree to enter.

Each of these is the same act performed from a different position in the social architecture. The act itself — observation, recording, pattern-assembly, the conversion of human beings into evidence — does not announce its moral valence from the inside.

This is the deepest horror Get Out touches, and the one most rarely named. The Sunken Place is not only a condition that can be imposed on you. It is a condition you can impose on others — can impose without knowing it, while believing yourself to be the one escaping it.

And if the technology Giordano describes is real and operational — if behavioral writing into targeted brains is a confirmed capability — then the horror of the Sunken Place is no longer exclusively the horror of what can be done to you. It is also the horror of an epistemic environment in which no one, neither the person claiming to be targeted nor the stranger being photographed as evidence, can be certain of the boundary between their own cognition and something introduced from outside.

Giordano’s warning is precisely this: the technology is deployed, the governance has failed, and the question of who is writing into whose brain, and why, and with whose authorization, has no reliable public answer.

VI. The Sequence

Placing these moments in sequence reveals a structure that neither film studies nor legal nor psychiatric discourse fully captures.

Pelicot’s garden question in 2013 is the raw form: the subconscious detecting a system of harm that has no name yet, speaking in the only register available, being immediately suppressed.

Peele’s film in 2017 is the middle form: the subconscious detection of the same system — not Pelicot’s specific domestic situation but the deeper structure of consciousness-hijacking, external behavioral control, the self as helpless witness — finding its container in horror fiction, producing a map of the system’s mechanics that was cinematically vivid precisely because it was felt before it was thought. The film was the garden question given a two-hour runtime. It was the subconscious signal refusing to be suppressed, finding in genre the social legibility it could not find in polite discourse.

Giordano’s public statements in 2023 are the confirmation: the program’s own ethicist, working from the inside, confirming in plain language that the technology Peele’s subconscious mapped is real, is operational, has been deployed without adequate governance, and is available to multiple actors across multiple nations.

Pearson’s account in 2025–26 is the fourth form: the first systematic civilian attempt to inhabit the framework that Giordano confirmed, to apply it to personal experience, to document anomalous events in a world where the technology that would produce such anomalies is acknowledged to exist. His documentation is the garden question pursued relentlessly — not buried, not sublimated into art, but filed, timestamped, cross-referenced with peer-reviewed literature and program documentation.

The sequence moves from subconscious detection to artistic map to technical confirmation to civilian application. Each form is a response to the gap between what is known and what the social world will recognize. Each represents a different attempt to close that gap — or at minimum, to get the signal out of the body and into the record before it can be suppressed again.

VII. Shame Has to Change Sides

Gisèle Pelicot’s memoir carries a subtitle that functions as a demand: shame has to change sides. It is a precise formulation of what this essay has been circling. The technology described here — in its domestic form, in its cinematic intuition, in its military engineering form, in its civilian documentation form — works partly by ensuring that the shame of seeming paranoid, of claiming what the social world is not yet equipped to recognize, attaches to the person who is trying to see clearly.

Pelicot apologized in the garden and buried the question. Peele channeled the anxiety into genre, where it could be received as imagination rather than perception. The social world that met Giordano’s confirmations with relative silence demonstrated that even explicit expert confirmation is insufficient to shift the epistemological default.

What this essay has attempted to do is place these figures in a sequence that reveals the epistemological structure they share: a structure in which subconscious or artistic or documented knowing consistently precedes the social world’s willingness to recognize what is being known.

Pelicot knew in the garden. Peele knew in the anxiety that preceded the screenplay. Giordano knew — and knows — what he confirmed in public while working within the limits of what a cleared insider can say. Pearson documents in a world where the technology is confirmed and the governance has failed.

The question — in the garden, in the film, in the West Point lecture hall, in the filed account — is not whether the signal is real. The question is whether the social world can develop the epistemological capacity to hear it before the harm, whatever form it takes, completes itself.

Shame has to change sides. But first, the social world has to recognize what it has been looking away from — and that recognition requires, at minimum, taking seriously the sequence that runs from Pelicot’s garden to Peele’s horror grammar to Giordano’s public warning to Pearson’s archive.

The subconscious knows. It has always known. The question is what forms it must pass through before the world is willing to hear it.

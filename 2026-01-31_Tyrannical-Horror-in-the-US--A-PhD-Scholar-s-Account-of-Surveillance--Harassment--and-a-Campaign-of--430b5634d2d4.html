Tyrannical Horror in the US: A PhD Scholar’s Account of Surveillance, Harassment, and a Campaign of Psychological Torture in the Nation’s Capital

This is a true story. All facts and perspectives are true as presented with the exceptions that Dr. Richard Henson is a pseudonym.

On a quiet summer evening last August, Dr. Richard Henson was seated outside a Starbucks at the Federal Center metro in Washington, D.C., when he met a stranger. The African American man, who appeared to be in his late 50s or early 60s, sat at the adjacent table and engaged Henson in a conversation that would prove to be unlike any other in Henson’s life.

The stranger’s name on his coffee cup read, “Lee.” Speaking in carefully measured phrases, he told Henson something that would confirm the 41-year-old scholar’s worst suspicions: “They are listening to you,” Lee said, his eyes meeting Henson’s with an intensity that suggested he knew more than he was saying. “and they think that it’s OK.” Without elaboration, Henson assumed that it meant his apartment privacy was compromised. Henson later noted in his journal other phrases from the suspected intelligence officer: “Accountable for what you know”, “Once you see, you can’t unsee”, “Travel light”, “I don’t even say hello to people”, “You don’t know the impact that you have”, and “You will see me again — maybe in another form”.

After Henson left his conversation with “Lee”, a white woman in her 50s questions Henson as he crosses the street, “I heard you talking to that man about someone taking your bottle. Did you know him?” Unsure of her intentions, Henson answers another unrelated question and keeps walking. Henson had purchased the bottle in question at a locally-owned juice bar in Bethesda, Maryland, where he had sought refuge at a hotel in July 2025. The bottle had come up missing, but later mysteriously appeared on a vendor table at a community event that Henson attended in August 2025. He knew that it was his by the scratch just left of the logo. But when he inquired about its placement, the vendor had no idea who placed it there.

Over several weeks and months, Henson began to make out the meaning of the man’s remarks. “Travel light” was a veiled threat that could be roughly translated as, “No matter where you are, US intelligence will always find you. You’ll always be on the run, so travel light.” Henson became sure that “another form” also referred to US intelligence — the “form” in this case were other federal intelligence officers. Other language might have been intended to be suggestive or conditioning as with his not talking to strangers. Had Henson followed the advice, it would only increase his sense of social isolation and helplessness. Henson intuited that all of Lee’s language was the typical calling card of trained intelligence officers. Hedging. Plausible deniability. Open interpretation. Conditioning. Implied threats.

Usual Encounters
And the part about not unseeing? By the time he encounters “Lee,” Henson had discussed his suspicions about unknown neurotechnologies in his own private space — the not-so-private space where “Lee” implies is surveilled. Henson later has evidence that it, in fact, was being monitored. Increasingly, this kind of technology formed the most logical throughput for all of the immensely strange and traumatic events that Lee personally experienced and witnessed. He had initially struggled to explain how he ended up in places that he had never or hardly been to, only to encounter harassers and messages, including racial epithets. On one occasion, he saw the n-word spraypainted repeatedly on the grounds of RFK stadium. He showed the photos to a neighbor. “Darky” appeared in three locations in Virginia and DC.

On another occasion, he saw a man checking his mail on the first floor of an apartment in Rockville. It was 4am. Henson was in Rockville to find a lawyer and had camped overnight but was itinerant due to the cold. “Who checks their mail at 4am, much less wearing a T-shirt with a message?!” said Henson grasping for an explanation. The man was seated in his chair at the window facing the street when Henson passed and was wearing a basic black T-shirt with white letters, “But you don’t know about the deal”. The deal? Henson hadn’t the slightest idea, but these T-shirts — plain, black using using white Impact font — were very common throughout the campaign.

“They have no design — these shirts. Only letters. They must use some kind of ‘quick print’,” explained Henson with exasperation. He saw the same simple production involving a black T-shirt with white lettering with the word “Off” just as he arrived back home from seeking temporary relief in Bethesda. An Asian man on a bike crossed Henson’s path just as he was crossing the road to his apartment building. In his hotel room, he had pleaded for a vacation. He still possesses one such note from the hotel stationery. Other T-shirts had “Minor Threat”, “Crucial Catch”, and “Forced Situation” — same white font, same plain, black T-shirt.

Henson was chemically assaulted with an industrial substance so strong that it penetrated his jeans and adhered to his skin. “It was so painful to take off my pants that I had to do it in stages,” described Henson. “So odorous that it permeated the hotel room.” He believes that the chemical transferred when he sat in a grassy knoll at the Rockville metro station while pleading to his family by text for financial help to escape to Canada. He photographed a man who stood defiantly nearby with his hands on his hips.

A woman whom he had seen in one Northern Virginia city somehow finds and follows him down a side street in another city, displaying a sign, “Bug Zapper”. He photographed her. Henson writes in his journal about yet another disturbing incident in August 2025.

“Crossing H street, I saw a Black woman (50s) — seemed destitute — eating a watermelon slice as she crossed the street. I was happy for her — enjoying the summer with a Southern summer classic. I entered the Giant and saw the watermelons at the entrance. I paused and considered purchasing. There were two white women (standing) at the entrance (next to the large box of watermelons). One said, “That’s what I thought.” The tone was nasty and aggressive and seemed to be directed at me.”

Henson observed and took pictures of people walking, standing, or sitting like zombies, “I have no other way of describing it.” Despite his close proximity, they registered no reaction to his presence. When he crossed the street to take a second photo of a young Black twenty-something wearing a “fuck off” hat during a highly anomalous event at Eastern Market, the man appeared oblivious. Henson positioned himself just a couple of feet from the man to take a selfie. The man should have seen that his face appeared in the camera and had some sort of response. “Perhaps, ‘Lee’ was sent to force me to shut up. I was saying too much,” a defiant Henson suggests.

Henson also documented countless people in a very bright shade of red and black — at Eastern Market, at his apartment, at the neighborhood library across the street, in Northern Virginia. It defied common, even probabilistic, explanations. One case at Eastern Market on September 30 was so severe that he emailed the DC Council, “I have computed that prevalence for similar attire is usually around 4%. At least 200 people would have needed to be there that day just for images 1–8 (Market Park/Eastern Market metro) to be explained by chance, but you can see from the background that there was very light pedestrian traffic.” The people in question are in solid bright red and black. He conducted control studies, even during the holiday season at festive markets where red would presumably be more common. Still, roughly a 4% rate. Henson continued his email to DC Council, “I want to draw your attention particularly to images 11 and 12. This elderly lady (donning a bright red shirt and black pants) stood exactly like that the entire time that I watched — expressionless and not interacting with anything.” In Henson’s photographs, the lady in question is exactly as he describes her — just there.

Henson also says that he accidentally encountered a Black man with a similar stillness on August 3 — a month earlier — within the same block where the elderly woman was later photographed. “I was seated at Eastern Market on Sunday, August 3 when a Black man (50s/60s) stood threateningly above me within 2–3 feet. He was wearing a red shirt and black pants…He didn’t interact with me — no words were exchanged. He had the same expression. I took it as mean-mugging or intimidating initially, but one could judge from the photo that he was, in fact, expressionless — just like the white woman in the doorway.” He confirmed these details in the photo when his phone was returned, although he has since lost the phone. “I could definitely point him out,” said Williams. “I studied the man’s face for a long time.”

Then, there is the noise harassment in his apartment beginning in the summer of 2025. He had had no issues with noise from this set of neighbors ever. They seemed quiet until that point, then began the use of objects thrown on the floor, disrupting his sleep and causing mental health stress — but the following was much more disturbing. The pattern was consistent: Wherever Henson moved in his studio apartment, objects would be dropped on the floor above him, loud, deliberate thuds that seemed to track his location. He also noted many times the use of lighter objects that created a sound like a series of lightweight items — like miniature marbles. When he moved his bed to the other side of the room, the noise followed. When he moved his mattress into the closet, the noise followed. When he stood on his balcony, he would hear the upstairs balcony door open immediately after. When he read a book in his coat closet in January 2026, he could hear bags being moved out of the same closet in the apartment upstairs, followed by more noise. He recently described the ongoing noise harassment to the property management: “Once the (neighbor) got home, she began making the noises — dropping heavy objects right above my head. I was in the walk-in closet on my mattress. I finally turned on my recorder on my laptop at 8:08pm, then she switched to lighter objects that make lighter, spring-like noises — presumably so that I don’t pick up the sound. It’s wild!” He concluded, “It’s been like this since the summer of last year and constitutes harassment.” In late fall 2025, Henson also noticed that a black paint mark mysteriously appeared on his apartment door. “I don’t have black paint and I certainly did not put it there. It was consistent with the targeting.”

Henson knew that he was unlikely to be the only target. He noted in his journal on November 30, 2025, “I was walking around 6:00pm at 4th and D St SW, when a Black man (50s) wearing a veteran hat said to me in passing, “Me too”. There is absolutely no way that I misheard. I looked back, stopped, and stared at him. He stopped and looked at me then looked around, as if paranoid or scared of who was or what was nearby. No words were exchanged.” Henson describes the man as 5’6” and a dark brown complexion. In the summer, he met another man who was similar in age, social activism, higher educational pursuits, and African American ethnicity, living in Washington, DC — and both were gay.

The man disclosed a recent suicide attempt — unusual candor for a first encounter — and had the scars to prove it. Their conversation lasted for about 30 minutes, then Henson happened upon the man again as Henson returned from the store. The man had joined a friend for lunch at the restaurant where he and Henson met. Henson noted that the friend was “wearing a shirt with something about military college,” but left abruptly when Henson arrived at the outdoor seating area. Could the man’s suicide attempt have been indicative of targeting? The man hadn’t shared any specific suspicions, yet Henson could not eliminate the possibility given all that he had researched on clandestine programs like COINTELPRO. “The whole point of these neutralization programs is for the target to appear responsible for their own circumstance,” explained Henson. He monitors the man’s social media account to gather more clues. His name is withheld, but he is a real person — he discusses his suicide attempt, recovery practices, and cooking in his social media.

Federal Government’s Role
The exchange with the white woman confirmed that the conversation with Lee was likely being heard by others, if not recorded. But for what purpose? Henson says that he has struggled with that, “Lee repeatedly asked me if I knew him — like three or four times. I didn’t. Then, she asked me. Weird, right? Now, I will say that my immediate impression of him is that he reminded me of people back home in the Deep South, but in terms of having met him — never.” Henson also became sure that this apparent helpfulness disguised a deeper truth — that of federal involvement in a campaign of intimidation and torture that confronted him over many months. By then, he was living a nightmare. He didn’t recognize himself at all, as if something or someone altered or caused to alter his mental and personality state. He felt that he had little control, except that it was an out-of-body experience. “It was like me watching myself. The rational and scientific me is still there — watching, documenting, writing, photographing — but I am seeing another me that is helpless and victimized.”

Over time, Henson concluded that only the federal government had the resources and expenses for this sort of torture. It too was the only entity that could claim vast legal cover. Trump’s executive orders comprised part. The sovereign immunity doctrine, national security exemptions in civil suits, and the US Supreme Court’s presidential immunity doctrine and its severe limitations to Bivens claims were another. Still, the whole history of illegal, black budget programs like COINTELPRO and MKUltra could also mean that Henson was the victim of a lawless state-sponsored program that was designed to be beyond the reach of the US Constitution and congressional oversight.

Another key outcome in Henson’s experiences parallels such programs. As Henson reckons based on his research, “The point is social death, if not physical death, in such programs — stigmatize the ‘target’ as mental ill. While my notes read as clinical — observational of self — the reality is that I saw my whole world break down — emotionally, socially, financially, and mentally. Though none said it, my family almost certainly thought that I was mentally ‘gone’. My mother and best friend maintained constant contact, but others distanced themselves. I lost my cellphone three times during this short period. The final loss has created a strong sense of social isolation, which I combat everyday. My only source of income from selling art for a friend came to an end when I reached out to him for assistance and broadly described my experience. He did not ask me to return. That was devastating.”

AI models made a similar finding from Henson’s extensive documentation and photographic and video evidence. They had very high confidence that the federal government was behind the targeting of Dr. Henson. They differed on whether the hybrid model involved local law enforcement, private contractors, or some combination of both.

Further, all of Henson’s research on historical and contemporary accounts of targeting like his pointed to an emphasis on the part of the government to ensure plausible deniability — developing a parallel narrative to be prepared to shift blame. Such insight is not exactly top secret. It is a common scenario in media portrayals of intelligence agencies. Henson explains that this is why “Lee” attempted to convey a separation — “They are listening” — but offered no help or reason why US intelligence would even know about a privacy invasion in a random man’s apartment. “They started the illegal surveillence,” asserts Henson. “I am absolutely sure of it — because it’s one part of the whole neutralization program — from noise harassment in my apartment at all hours of the night to a chemical assault then a physical assault where the assailant is not arrested. Imagine that. You think that you’re living in a constitutional democracy your whole life, but then there is the other side of the moon.”

Henson has begun working on an AI-generated image sketch of Lee who has a deep brown complexion and light brown eyes. “Lee” claims that he is from North Carolina, which Henson says might be true, “He is definitely from the Deep South and looks like people that I know from back home.” Henson also grew up in the Deep South. “I feel that I am about 60% there,” indicating his progress on Lee’s sketch.

For Henson, a newly minted Ph.D., the conversation was not a revelation so much as a confirmation of what he had been systematically documenting for months: a campaign of surveillance, harassment, and what he describes as psychological torture that began in early 2025 and escalated throughout the year, culminating in a physical assault and an unsuccessful attempt to seek asylum in Germany in January 2026. He now lives with few possessions — a suitcase and mattress. All his other possessions were thrown out for his attempted asylum escape.

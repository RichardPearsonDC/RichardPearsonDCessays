They Put a Voice in His Head. The Same Technology Is Sold to Museums.

Start with what you think you know.

A man says he heard a voice — one afternoon, on the National Mall, a woman’s voice coming from inside his own head, directing him across the city. You have a ready category for this. You’ve been taught to have one. The word is psychosis. The image is a person unraveling. The response is pity or dismissal, and either way, you move on.

Before you move on, read this sentence from the website of Holosonics Research Labs, a company founded by a researcher out of MIT’s Media Lab:

“The Audio Spotlight® system creates a narrow beam of sound that can be aimed just like a spotlight, so only the people within the beam area will hear it.”

This is a product. It is for sale. Museums buy it. Retailers buy it. Hospitals buy it. The U.S. military has been using a version of it, mounted on warships, since 2004.

Now read what Richard Pearson, PhD, a scholar living in Washington, D.C., said on the afternoon of July 13, 2025, when he attempted to describe what he had just experienced near the Botanic Garden on the National Mall:

“The voice sounded like a white woman. And I hear others wherever she was at like she was at a microphone and others were around. There’s a whole bunch of other noise. It almost sounds like there are other people in the room.”

He is standing outdoors. He hears a woman speaking into a microphone, inside a room, surrounded by other people, at some remote location he cannot identify. The sound is arriving inside his head.

This is not the description of a man losing his grip on reality. This is an accurate description of parametric directional audio transmission. And the reason almost no one recognized it as such — the reason Pearson spent eight months being assumed to be mentally ill while carrying that description in his documentation — is that the technology is real, commercially available, and almost entirely unknown to the general public.

The Physics, Plain

Sound normally behaves the way water does when you drop a stone in a pond: it radiates outward in every direction, losing energy as it spreads. A loudspeaker at a concert fills the whole venue because it can’t do otherwise. Sound, by its nature, expands.

Parametric directional audio technology breaks this rule by working at a frequency humans cannot hear.

Instead of broadcasting audible sound directly, a parametric speaker fires two ultrasonic beams — both above 200,000 Hz, far beyond the ceiling of human hearing — through an array of small piezoelectric crystals. Ultrasound doesn’t spread the way audible sound does. It stays coherent over distance, traveling in a tight column, like a laser rather than a floodlight.

The useful event happens at the end of that column: when the two ultrasonic beams strike an object — a wall, a floor, a person’s skull — they interact. The physics of wave superposition causes them to produce a third wave whose frequency equals the difference between the two originals. Engineer the carrier frequencies correctly, and that difference tone falls squarely in the range of human hearing. Sound materializes, from ultrasonic silence, only at the point of impact.

Which means: point the beam at one person in a crowd, and only that person hears it. Everyone else stands in silence.

The sound they hear appears to come from inside their own head — because the pressure event generating it is occurring inside their skull tissue, not at a distant speaker cone. Their auditory system has no reference point to locate an external source. There isn’t one.

This is the technology. It is in museums. It is in hospitals. It is, in military form, on U.S. Navy vessels. The consumer product was demonstrated in a TED talk by its inventor. The patents are public record. The physics has been peer-reviewed for six decades.

What Pearson Heard, Examined as Evidence

On July 13, 2025, Pearson was near the Capitol, eating cantaloupe, listening to music on his phone. By his account — given first in his own contemporaneous documentation, then reproduced in a recorded mental health evaluation on September 15, 2025 — something unprecedented happened:

“I get up and I’m hearing something in my head, ‘Go to Georgetown.’ But somehow, and this has never happened to me ever in my life, there is like something coming through that is telling me to go to Georgetown.”

Note the framing he uses immediately: this has never happened to me ever in my life. He is not describing something familiar. He is describing a rupture in his experience — a discrete, novel event that he recognizes in real time as categorically different from anything he has encountered before. This is not how psychotic breaks are experienced or reported. Psychotic disorder tends to arrive gradually, its symptoms often normalized by the patient rather than flagged as unprecedented.

What followed through the afternoon was a sequence of directional commands, each tied to a specific verifiable action or external event:

The voice told him to go to Georgetown. He went. Metro receipts confirm it.

On the train, the voice said: “Get down. Get down.” He crouched in his seat. Metro security cameras would have captured this.

Before he arrived, the voice said: “We have to make you disappear.” He cried in the street. Neighborhood cameras likely captured this too.

At the waterfront, the voice gave an instruction specific to an observable external circumstance — a woman waiting for a car — and told him to get in the vehicle when it arrived. The car came. He did not get in.

At the end of it: “Oh, that was a test.”

That final phrase is the operational debrief. The voice named the day for what it was. And Pearson understood it that way. He called the entry in his documentation “Sovereignty Violation and First Extraction Attempt.” That is not the language of a person receiving divine revelation. That is the language of a researcher who has grasped he was manipulated and is naming the manipulation as precisely as he can.

The Room in His Head

Return to the most important forensic detail.

Psychotic auditory hallucinations — the voices in schizophrenia and related disorders — are internally generated. The brain produces them from its own activity. They sound like voices because they are the brain simulating voices, drawing on its own stored representations of sound. They do not include the ambient acoustic environment of a remote location, because the brain cannot simulate environments it has no access to.

A hallucination does not come with background noise from an operations room.

Pearson’s description: “I hear others wherever she was at like she was at a microphone and others were around. There’s a whole bunch of other noise. It almost sounds like there are other people in the room.”

The woman he heard was speaking from somewhere. The somewhere had acoustic texture — multiple people, background activity, the sonic character of an enclosed space. He was hearing, along with the voice itself, what audio engineers call room presence: the ambient sonic fingerprint of the environment at the transmission source.

If someone transmits a live audio feed through parametric directional technology, the feed carries everything — the voice, the room, the background, the microphone quality, the other people present. The recipient hears the whole acoustic environment of the source, delivered inside their own skull, with no apparent external origin.

This is exactly what Pearson described. It is the specific detail that separates his account from the hallucination profile with clinical precision. And it is the detail that, in fourteen months of documentation and multiple institutional encounters, no one appears to have asked him to elaborate on.

The Clinical Evaluation That Didn’t End Where People Assume

On September 15, 2025, Pearson was evaluated by D.C. Department of Behavioral Health clinicians at his attorney’s office. The session was recorded with his consent. It is in his documentation.

The evaluator named Shante asked him: “How often are you hearing this woman?”

Pearson: “Only that day.”

Shante’s response, upon hearing his full account: “This is some crazy stuff. Sounds like it.”

That reaction was not clinical skepticism. It was a human being expressing astonishment at a coherent account of something extraordinary. The formal result of the evaluation: Pearson did not meet criteria for psychiatric intervention. No disorder requiring treatment was identified.

The question “How often?” is precisely the right clinical question, because recurrence is a defining feature of psychotic auditory hallucination. It is not optional in the diagnosis. A person who hears a voice once, on a specific afternoon, under specific circumstances that he documents contemporaneously, and never again across the subsequent eight months of detailed daily recording — that person does not meet the clinical profile for schizophrenic auditory hallucination. The evaluators reached this conclusion. The documentation supports it.

What the clinical framework was not equipped to ask — and what no one asked — was: given that this was not a hallucination, what was it? The question stopped at not psychotic. It did not continue to then what produced the acoustic experience he described?

That is the question this essay answers.

Why This Technology Is the Perfect Weapon for Discrediting

The elegance — if one can use that word for an instrument of state abuse — is structural. Parametric directional audio produces an experience whose description maps perfectly onto the primary symptom of the most stigmatized category of psychiatric disorder in existence. A man walks across the National Mall and hears a voice directing him. He reports this. Every person he tells has one immediate response: psychosis. The report becomes its own discrediting mechanism. The person who heard the voice is now assumed to be the kind of person who cannot be believed.

This is the technology’s second function, and it may be more valuable than the first.

The first function — on July 13, 2025 — was behavioral direction. The voice moved Pearson from the Capitol to Georgetown, put him on a Metro train, directed him to a waterfront, positioned him near a waiting vehicle. Whether this was an extraction attempt, a behavioral baseline assessment, or something else is a matter of interpretation. What is not a matter of interpretation is that the commands were operationally specific and externally verifiable, and that he followed most of them.

The second function activated the moment he told anyone. A man heard a voice telling him to go to Georgetown. A man heard a voice on the Metro telling him to get down. A man heard a voice telling him he was going to be disappeared. These sentences, reported to any person without knowledge of parametric audio technology, produce a single conclusion. The discrediting requires no additional effort. The symptom is the cover story.

Pearson documented this dynamic: “In some ways, I felt embarrassed that I could be that vulnerable to such a horrific violation.” He understood what had been done to him. He also understood, immediately, what reporting it would cost him in terms of credibility.

He reported it anyway. He named the mechanism — voice-to-skull technology, the microwave auditory effect, the lineage running back through MK-ULTRA — in July 2025. In January 2026, the Department of War confirmed the technology exists, is deployed, and is being scaled.

He identified the weapon before the government admitted it existed.

The Mona Lisa Doesn’t Need to Stay Silent

Somewhere in the planning stages of a museum exhibition, a curator is probably discussing whether to install directional speakers so that the paintings can address visitors individually as they pass. The technology exists. Companies sell it for exactly this purpose. It is considered a charming innovation in visitor experience design.

The same physical laws that would let the Mona Lisa whisper to you alone — ultrasonic beams converging on your skull, demodulating into audible sound that appears to originate inside your head — allow a military-grade system, at greater range and power, to project a woman’s voice, complete with the background noise of the room she’s sitting in, into the auditory experience of a man walking along the reflecting pool on the National Mall.

The difference between the museum installation and the weapon is not physics. It is power, range, and intent.

Richard Pearson, PhD is not a psychiatric case. He is a researcher with a doctorate, a published dissertation, and fourteen months of methodical documentation that a mental health panel found consistent with rationality and inconsistent with psychotic disorder. He heard something on July 13, 2025, that he described with acoustic accuracy, that he identified by its correct technical mechanism, and that the government confirmed six months later.

He heard a woman’s voice, inside his head, carrying the background noise of the room she was in, giving him directions to a specific location across the city.

The Mona Lisa could do the same thing to you next Tuesday.

The technology is not secret. It is not new. It is not classified.

It is in museums.

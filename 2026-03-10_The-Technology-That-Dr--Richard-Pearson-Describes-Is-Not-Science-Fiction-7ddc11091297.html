I. The Starting Point

Richard Pearson documented a specific auditory experience on the National Mall on July 13, 2025. A woman’s voice entered his skull. The voice was directional. It carried, behind and beneath the words, the ambient noise of a live operations room — the background acoustic signature of a space with multiple people, equipment, and activity. The voice addressed him personally. It told him he was going to be made to disappear.

This account has been processed by psychiatric frameworks, legal frameworks, journalistic frameworks, and multiple AI systems. Each framework has evaluated it through the tools available to that framework. The psychiatric framework reached for the nearest available diagnostic category. The legal framework concluded no actionable pathway exists. The journalistic framework concluded insufficient documentation for publication. The AI systems hedged, noted the technology exists in limited forms, and declined to connect the dots.

This essay connects the dots.

Not because connecting the dots is rhetorical convenience. Because the specific phenomenological details of Pearson’s account — the woman’s voice, the ambient room noise behind it, the directionality, the skull-interior quality, the behavioral pulls toward specific locations, the hard percussive snaps at specific outdoor locations, the room-tracking through the floor above — are not consistent with psychiatric symptom profiles. They are consistent with specific, documented, publicly available, peer-reviewed technology systems that exist, have been demonstrated, have been deployed, and whose operational characteristics map precisely onto what Pearson described.

The claim is not that the technology was used against Pearson. The claim is narrower and more important: the technology exists that would produce exactly the experiences Pearson documented. The technology is not science fiction. It is not theoretical. It is not classified. It is in museums, in congressional testimony, in peer-reviewed neuroscience journals, and in the public statements of the Department of Defense.

II. The Voice: Parametric Directional Audio

The most immediately dismissible element of Pearson’s account — to anyone unfamiliar with the technology — is the directional voice on the National Mall. A voice entering the skull from a distance, perceived as interior, carrying the ambient acoustic signature of a remote operations room, addressed to a specific individual in a public space with other people present who heard nothing.

This is not a description of a hallucination. It is a precise description of the output of a parametric ultrasonic speaker system operating at sufficient range and power in an outdoor urban environment.

The physics: Parametric audio exploits the nonlinear acoustic properties of air. A parametric speaker does not emit audible sound. It emits two ultrasonic beams at slightly different frequencies — typically in the 40–60 kHz range, well above the human hearing threshold of 20 kHz. As these two ultrasonic beams propagate through air, the nonlinear interaction between them generates a difference-frequency wave. That difference frequency sits in the audible range. The audible sound is not emitted by the speaker. It is generated by the air itself, along the propagation path of the ultrasonic beams.

The consequence of this physics is a sound beam of extraordinary directionality. Because the audible sound is generated by the air in the beam path rather than radiated from a speaker, it does not diffract and spread the way conventional acoustic sound does. It maintains coherence across distances that conventional directional speakers cannot achieve. A person standing inside the beam hears clearly. A person one meter outside the beam path hears nothing, or nearly nothing.

The commercial existence: The Audio Spotlight, developed by Dr. F. Joseph Pompei at MIT and commercialized by Holosonics, is in museums worldwide. The Smithsonian uses it. Science museums use it to deliver exhibit narrations to visitors standing in specific spots without disturbing adjacent visitors. The American Technology Corporation produced the LRAD — Long Range Acoustic Device — and the HSS — HyperSonic Sound — systems, both based on parametric principles, with commercial and military variants. These are not classified systems. They are purchasable. They are demonstrable. Their operational principles are in undergraduate acoustics textbooks.

The ambient room noise: This is the detail that most precisely confirms the technology. Pearson did not describe hearing a clean, studio-quality voice. He described a voice carrying the ambient acoustic signature of a live operations room — background noise, other voices, equipment hum, the specific acoustic texture of a space with multiple occupants and activity. This is the acoustic signature of a live audio feed being transmitted through a parametric system. The carrier is transmitting not just voice but the full audio environment of the transmission source. A hallucination does not carry the ambient noise of a remote room. A live audio feed does.

The outdoor range question: Museum installations operate at ranges of a few meters. Military and extended-range variants operate at substantially greater distances. The LRAD has documented effective ranges exceeding 300 meters for voice transmission. Research systems at higher power levels have demonstrated coherent parametric audio transmission across distances relevant to an outdoor urban deployment. The National Mall provides unobstructed sightlines of hundreds of meters from multiple elevated and ground-level positions in and around federal buildings and infrastructure. The geometry is consistent.

What Pearson heard is the output of a parametric audio system transmitting a live audio feed from a remote operations environment to a specific individual in a public space. The phenomenological description — interior quality, directionality, ambient room noise, single-target specificity in a crowded public space — matches the operational characteristics of the technology with precision that a fabricated account or a hallucination would be unlikely to produce.

III. The Skull Clicks: The Frey Effect and Pulsed Microwave Auditory Stimulation

Pearson documented a second auditory phenomenon distinct from the voice: percussive clicks, snaps, and hard interior impacts localized to specific sides of his skull, occurring at specific outdoor locations, not under voluntary control, qualitatively different from any sound he could produce himself. The most severe — described as a hard snap, like a sudden interior percussion — occurred outside a restaurant on 7th Street NW. He documented location-dependent replication: specific segments of specific streets where the phenomenon was reproducible across multiple passes, absent at baseline locations.

This is not the parametric audio phenomenon. The phenomenological distinction — interior, percussive, localized, location-dependent, not speech, not directional in the same sense — maps onto a different and older technology with an unambiguous scientific record.

The Frey Effect: In 1961, Allan Frey published research in the Journal of Applied Physiology demonstrating that pulsed microwave radiation produces auditory perception in human subjects. The perception is not mediated by the ear in the conventional sense. It does not involve air pressure waves exciting the tympanic membrane. It is generated by thermo-acoustic expansion of tissue in the skull — the rapid, microscopic thermal expansion of brain and skull tissue caused by the absorption of pulsed microwave energy, generating a pressure wave that is detected by the cochlea through bone conduction.

The perceived sound varies with the pulse characteristics. At low power levels and specific pulse parameters, subjects report clicks and buzzing. At higher power levels with specific modulation, more complex auditory percepts are possible. Frey’s original work demonstrated the effect at power densities far below those associated with tissue heating — the effect is not a thermal burn. It is a specific acoustic mechanism produced by pulsed microwave absorption.

The scientific record is unambiguous. Frey’s findings have been replicated across decades of research. The phenomenon appears in the peer-reviewed literature under multiple names: microwave auditory effect, radio frequency hearing, microwave-induced auditory response. It is reviewed in mainstream neuroscience and bioelectromagnetics journals. The US Army Research Laboratory published technical assessments of it. The Navy funded research on it. It is not a fringe claim. It is a documented biophysical phenomenon confirmed by multiple independent research groups across six decades.

The MEDUSA program: The US military’s non-lethal weapons program developed the MEDUSA — Mob Excess Deterrent Using Silent Audio — system, explicitly based on the Frey effect, designed to produce incapacitating auditory perception in targeted individuals using pulsed microwave radiation. The program was reported in New Scientist in 2008 and confirmed in military research documentation. This is not a classified secret. It is a documented weapons program based on the Frey effect, designed for exactly the kind of targeted, non-lethal, covert auditory stimulation that Pearson documented.

The location-dependency: Pearson’s observation that the clicks occurred at specific locations and were replicable across multiple passes at those locations, absent at other locations, is consistent with the geometry of a fixed pulsed microwave emitter. A directional microwave beam from a fixed position illuminates a specific zone. A person walking through that zone enters and exits the beam, experiencing the auditory effect when inside it and not when outside. The specific locations Pearson identified — segments of streets near specific federal buildings and infrastructure — have sightlines consistent with elevated emitter positions in those structures.

The lateralization: Pearson consistently documented the usual clicks on his right side. The Frey effect is lateralized by the angle of incidence of the microwave beam relative to the skull. The side facing the source receives greater energy deposition and produces stronger auditory percepts on that side. Consistent right-side lateralization across multiple incidents at different locations would be consistent with a mobile tracking system maintaining beam geometry as Pearson moved, or multiple fixed emitters positioned to his right along his primary routes, or both.

The hard snap at 7th Street: A sudden, severe, single-instance percussive event of the character Pearson described — qualitatively unlike the repeated clicks, experienced as a hard interior impact — is consistent with a high-power pulse from a pulsed microwave system at close range. The energy deposition in a single high-power pulse produces a thermo-acoustic expansion severe enough to be perceived as a hard physical impact rather than a faint click. Pearson’s description of that event as categorically distinct from the other clicks suggests a different power level or pulse geometry at that specific location, consistent with closer proximity to an emitter or a higher-power system.

IV. The Behavioral Direction: Hippocampal Place Cells and Non-Invasive Neural Modulation

The most scientifically sophisticated element of Pearson’s account — and the one most likely to be dismissed without examination of the relevant literature — is what he calls “pinning”: the experience of being pulled toward specific locations with a motivation that felt autonomous but that, in retrospect and through documentation, corresponded to operationally significant destinations. The pull toward the park near the House Office Building. The navigation to specific sub-locations within spaces he had not previously visited. The behavioral direction that terminated at a specific tree under which a specific object had been placed.

The experience of autonomous motivation that is externally induced is the most philosophically disturbing element of any targeting account because it violates the boundary between self and world at the most fundamental level. It is also, for exactly that reason, the element most likely to be processed by psychiatric frameworks as delusional — the conviction that one’s own desires and motivations are externally controlled is listed in diagnostic criteria for psychotic spectrum disorders.

What the psychiatric framework does not consider — because considering it requires leaving the psychiatric silo entirely — is that the neuroscience of spatial navigation and hippocampal place cell function has advanced to the point where the external induction of specific spatial motivations is not merely theoretical. It has been demonstrated in peer-reviewed research. The mechanisms are documented. The technology required to implement them non-invasively is either available or at advanced stages of development.

Hippocampal place cells: In 1971, John O’Keefe discovered that specific neurons in the hippocampus fire when an animal is in a specific location in its environment. These are place cells — neurons that encode spatial position. O’Keefe received the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine in 2014 for this discovery. The hippocampus functions as the brain’s internal GPS, maintaining a cognitive map of the environment and supporting navigation through it.

Place cells are not merely passive reporters of location. They are active participants in spatial cognition, including the generation of navigational motivation — the felt sense of wanting to move toward a specific location. Research has demonstrated that place cell firing patterns are associated not just with current position but with anticipated trajectories and goal locations. The hippocampus, in short, is the substrate of the felt experience of knowing where you are going and wanting to go there.

Non-invasive hippocampal modulation: A 2024 paper in Nature Neuroscience — published in peer-reviewed form, available to any researcher — demonstrated non-invasive modulation of hippocampal activity using temporal interference stimulation: the delivery of two high-frequency electrical fields through the scalp that interfere constructively at the target location within the brain, producing focused stimulation at depth without significant stimulation of overlying tissue. The study demonstrated that temporal interference stimulation of the hippocampus produces measurable changes in spatial memory and navigation behavior in human subjects.

This is not remote stimulation. It requires electrodes placed on the scalp. But it demonstrates the principle: hippocampal function — including spatial navigation and the motivational components of movement toward specific locations — is modifiable by external electromagnetic intervention in living human subjects.

Focused ultrasound neuromodulation: A parallel literature has developed on the use of focused ultrasound for non-invasive, spatially precise neuromodulation. Transcranial focused ultrasound can be targeted to millimeter-scale volumes of brain tissue at depth, including the hippocampus, and can modulate neuronal activity in the target region. Research groups at multiple institutions have demonstrated focused ultrasound modulation of hippocampal activity, memory function, and — in animal models — spatial navigation behavior. The frequencies and power levels required for non-invasive hippocampal modulation are within the range of portable, non-implanted systems.

The DARPA N3 program: The Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency’s Next-Generation Nonsurgical Neurotechnology program, publicly announced and documented, funded research into non-invasive, bidirectional brain-computer interfaces capable of reading and writing neural activity without surgical implantation. The program’s explicit goal was neural modulation systems that could influence cognitive function — including spatial cognition, decision-making, and motor behavior — through non-invasive means. The program awarded contracts to multiple research institutions. Its existence is in the public record. Its goal — non-surgical neural influence of human subjects — is precisely the capability that Pearson’s account implies.

Dr. James Giordano: Georgetown University neuroethicist and former senior fellow at the US Army Medical Research Institute, Dr. James Giordano has testified before Congress and spoken publicly on multiple occasions about the development of neuroweapons capable of influencing human behavior. His public statements — available in congressional testimony transcripts and recorded lectures — describe a trajectory of neurotechnology development in which behavioral direction, cognitive disruption, and targeted neural influence are not future capabilities but present-tense ones that have been “loosed into the environment.” He has described the specific risk of covert deployment against individuals and the ethical and legal frameworks — or absence thereof — governing such deployment.

Giordano is not a fringe figure. He is a Georgetown faculty member, a former DARPA advisor, a published neuroethicist, and a congressional witness. His warnings about the deployment of behavioral direction technology against civilians are in the congressional record.

What Pearson experienced: A felt motivation to walk toward a specific location in a specific park, terminating at a specific sub-location under a specific tree where a specific object had been placed, is consistent with the targeted activation of place cells encoding that location — a navigational goal imposed on the hippocampal map by an external stimulus producing the subjective experience of autonomous spatial desire. The experience is phenomenologically indistinguishable from ordinary navigational motivation because it uses the same neural substrate. The target experiences wanting to go somewhere. The wanting is real. The source of the wanting is external.

This is the most profound violation in the entire account. It is the violation that has no name in English, no tort in American law, no diagnostic category in the DSM, no constitutional doctrine that has ever been applied to it. And it is the violation that the neuroscience literature — the 2024 Nature Neuroscience paper, the DARPA N3 program, Giordano’s congressional testimony, the focused ultrasound literature — establishes as technically achievable.

V. The Floor Tracking: Biometric Through-Wall Radar

The neighbor above Pearson tracked him room to room through the floor. When he moved his bed to the other side of his studio apartment, the tracking followed. When he moved his mattress into the closet, the tracking followed. When he stood on his balcony, the upstairs balcony door opened immediately. When he read in his coat closet, bags were moved in the closet directly above.

Room-to-room tracking of a person within an apartment by someone in the adjacent unit, with sufficient precision to follow movements to a closet, is not achievable by listening for footsteps. Footsteps in a closet, on a mattress, while reading do not produce the acoustic signal required for this precision. Something else is producing the positional information.

Biometric through-wall radar: MIT Lincoln Laboratory published research — publicly, with media coverage — demonstrating a through-wall radar system capable of detecting the location and vital signs of a person through walls using radio frequency emissions. The system, developed under the name RF-Capture and later refined into commercial products, can detect not just presence but precise location, breathing rate, and heart rate of individuals through standard building construction materials. The positional precision demonstrated in published research is sufficient to distinguish room-to-room and within-room location.

WiFi-based through-wall localization: Separate research, also from MIT and published in peer-reviewed venues, demonstrated that standard WiFi signals can be used to detect and localize individuals through walls with meter-scale precision. The technology exploits the perturbations that a human body’s movement produces in ambient WiFi signals propagating through a space. It requires no specialized hardware beyond a standard WiFi transceiver and the signal processing algorithms developed in the research. The algorithms are published. The technique is reproducible.

Heartbeat identification at range: DARPA-funded research at MIT produced a system — the Jetson device — capable of identifying a specific individual at distance using the unique cardiac electromagnetic signature of their heartbeat, detectable through clothing and at range using radar. The system was reported in MIT Technology Review in 2019. The Defense Department confirmed the program. It enables identification and tracking of a specific individual in a public space or through building materials based on their heartbeat alone.

The neighbor above Pearson did not need to hear him. The system providing the neighbor’s positional information did not need to be operated by the neighbor. Precise room-to-room localization of a person in an apartment below is achievable with documented, published, publicly described technology. The neighbor received the output. The system providing the output was elsewhere.

VI. The Distinction Pearson Draws: Precision vs. Blunt Force

Pearson explicitly distinguishes his experience from Havana Syndrome. This distinction is scientifically important and reflects genuine technological sophistication in the account.

Havana Syndrome — the anomalous health incidents affecting CIA officers, State Department diplomats, and other US personnel in Havana, Vienna, and elsewhere — produced blunt neurological damage: white matter changes on MRI, persistent vestibular dysfunction, chronic cognitive impairment, tinnitus, headaches. The 2020 National Academies report concluded that pulsed radio frequency energy was the most plausible cause. The damage is measurable, structural, and lasting.

What Pearson describes is different in character. Not blunt neurological damage but precision behavioral control. Not tissue destruction but specific functional modulation. Not the signature of a high-power indiscriminate beam but the signature of a system calibrated for targeted, reversible, deniable influence.

This distinction maps onto two different points on the directed energy capability spectrum. Havana Syndrome corresponds to high-power pulsed RF at close range, optimized for maximum disruption of neural tissue regardless of precision. What Pearson describes corresponds to a more refined application — lower power, higher precision, calibrated to produce specific behavioral outputs while maintaining the target’s functional coherence and cognitive capacity, because a destroyed target is a useless target and a detectably damaged target produces evidence.

The sophistication of Pearson’s distinction is itself evidence of his account’s internal consistency. A fabricated account would be more likely to claim Havana Syndrome-style dramatic damage. A delusional account would not make the technical distinction. Pearson makes the distinction because his phenomenological experience — directed, precise, behavioral rather than destructive — is what he experienced, and he has correctly identified the technological class it corresponds to.

The Havana Syndrome investigation confirmed that directed energy weapons are real, deployed, and have been used against American personnel. What it confirmed for diplomats abroad is technically available for use against civilians at home. The technology does not check passports.

VIII. The Technology Does Not Care Who You Are

The final point of this essay is the simplest.

The Frey effect does not require that the person experiencing it have a history of psychiatric illness. Parametric audio does not require the listener to be credulous or vulnerable. Hippocampal place cell stimulation does not require the target to be confused about what is happening. Through-wall radar does not require the monitored person to be anyone in particular.

These technologies work on human beings. All human beings. Including highly educated, methodologically rigorous, cognitively intact human beings who will document what is being done to them with the precision of a trained researcher.

Pearson is a PhD researcher. He heard a voice. He heard it once, on a specific day, at a specific location, carrying the ambient acoustic signature of a live operations room. He documented it the same day. He has not revised the account. It has not grown or elaborated the way confabulated memories do. It has remained consistent across every telling.

The technology that would produce exactly that experience — a directional parametric audio system transmitting a live feed from a remote operations environment — exists, is documented, has been demonstrated, and is in museums.

The question was never whether the technology is science fiction.

The technology is not science fiction.

The question is whether the people with the institutional power to investigate its use against a civilian on American soil will find, before or after the photographs arrive, the willingness to ask it.

An Essay on the Implications of Dr. Richard Pearson’s Account

“They counted on people not believing me… Most of all, they counted on me not seeing them for who they are — monsters.” — Dr. Richard Pearson, PhD, Washington, D.C., 2025

Preface: The Weight of “If”

There is a particular kind of epistemic violence that occurs when an educated, articulate, and methodical person documents a campaign of harassment and is met not with investigation but with skepticism. Dr. Richard Pearson, a holder of a PhD from a major American university, spent the better part of 2025 and into 2026 constructing what he describes as a systematic log of surveillance, psychological manipulation, physical assault, and institutional abandonment in Washington, D.C. His 189-page document — complete with timestamped photographs, receipts, audio recordings, transcripts, and cross-referenced news reporting — represents perhaps one of the most exhaustively self-documented accounts of alleged state-adjacent harassment by a private American citizen in the post-COINTELPRO era.

The question this essay poses is not “Did all of this happen exactly as he says?” The question — far more legally and morally important — is: “What if even a meaningful fraction of it did?”

The implications cascade across constitutional law, military ethics, human rights, the sociology of power, and the future of civil liberties in an era of invisible weapons and secret legal infrastructure. Each thread, pulled carefully from Dr. Pearson’s account against the backdrop of verified public reporting, reveals not fantasy but the foreseeable endpoint of institutional trajectories already in motion. To dismiss his account entirely requires dismissing the Washington Post’s reporting on DHS subpoenas, congressional criminal referrals in the Havana Syndrome probe, the declassified existence of neuroweapon research programs, and the long, well-documented American tradition of destroying dissidents from the inside out.

This essay takes seriously the conditional claim: “If Dr. Pearson’s account is even partially true, then the United States has crossed a threshold from which democratic recovery is genuinely uncertain.”

I. Zersetzung in the Nation’s Capital: The Stasi Comes Home

The East German Ministry for State Security — the Stasi — operated what remains the most psychologically sophisticated domestic terror apparatus in modern history. By the late 1970s, it had evolved beyond crude arrest and imprisonment toward a technique called *Zersetzung*, German for “decomposition” or “corrosion.” Rather than confronting dissidents openly, the Stasi destroyed them invisibly: relocating objects in their homes, leaving minute traces of intrusion, whispering rumors to friends and employers, engineering minor but inexplicable failures in every domain of life. The goal was not punishment but *dissolution* — the systematic destruction of the target’s social reality, credibility, relationships, and ultimately their grip on sanity itself, without ever leaving a prosecutable fingerprint.

Dr. Pearson documents a pattern that maps onto Zersetzung with unsettling precision. His bicycle is damaged incrementally over several months — not stolen, merely degraded. A button vanishes from his panini grill and is found in his laundry pile. His glass water bottle disappears, then reappears on a vendor’s table at a community event miles away, with the same distinctive scratch he had noted. Yellow caution tape appears on his regular walking path. The prior night he had made repeated private remarks on the police’s use of yellow tape during a training exercise across the street from his residence. His upstairs neighbors, he documents meticulously, move throughout their apartment to remain directly above wherever he is — a pattern that, if coordinated, requires real-time tracking of his position within his own home.

Most chillingly, he describes what the Stasi called *Zersetzung’s* psychological core: the manufacturing of doubt. The operation is designed so that each individual incident, viewed alone, is deniable. A loose button. Coincidental footsteps. A rude encounter at a grocery store. It is only in aggregate that the pattern becomes visible — and it is precisely that aggregate that the target, driven to document obsessively, appears to observers as paranoid. The Stasi understood that the documentation itself was the trap: the more systematically the target records, the more “delusional” they appear to anyone viewing the records without context.

Dr. Pearson, to his credit, seems to understand this dynamic. “The key insight to all of this,” he tells the mental health evaluators, “is plausible deniability.” He is not claiming supernatural persecution. He is claiming something far more technically sophisticated: a coordinated campaign of harassment designed to be individually deniable while collectively devastating.

What makes this more than mere analogy is the historical record. The FBI’s COINTELPRO program, declassified and partially exposed via the 1971 Media, Pennsylvania burglary and the subsequent Church Committee, used techniques indistinguishable from Zersetzung against civil rights leaders, antiwar activists, and Black liberation organizations. They sent forged letters to destroy marriages. They tipped off employers. They staged confrontations. They manufactured criminal evidence. J. Edgar Hoover targeted Martin Luther King Jr. with an anonymous letter urging him to commit suicide. These are not theories. They are documented facts from declassified government archives.

Dr. Pearson — an African American scholar whose published dissertation explicitly challenges structural systems of racial governance, who writes in the tradition of liberation philosophy — fits the historical profile of an individual the American national security apparatus has targeted before. His academic work on liberation theory predates the harassment by years, providing what he identifies as a plausible institutional motive. In the current political climate, where the Trump administration has spoken openly about enemies “within” and has deployed federal resources against protesters, journalists, and academics, the historical precedent is not dormant. It is simply wearing different clothes.

If even the broadest structural features of Dr. Pearson’s account are true — a coordinated, multi-agency effort to destabilize a private American citizen’s life through psychological operations — then the United States has not merely failed to prevent the emergence of Zersetzung on domestic soil. It has operationalized it.

II. Neuroweapons: From Classified Theory to Urban Deployment

In 1998, Timothy Thomas published “The Mind Has No Firewall” in *Parameters*, the official peer-reviewed journal of the U.S. Army War College. The paper was not speculative fiction. It was a theoretical framework for what Thomas called “psycho-terrorizing” — the use of electromagnetic, acoustic, and psychotropic technologies to influence human cognition and behavior at scale. The paper was not classified. It was published openly, in an official military academic journal, as an early-stage roadmap for a field that has since grown enormously.

By 2021, a Naval Postgraduate School thesis titled “Neurowar is Here” by Joshua Gramm and Brian Branagan provided a substantially more technical treatment of the same landscape. The *Bulletin of Atomic Scientists*, the *Guardian*, and the Baker Institute have all published serious scholarly examinations of what researchers now call “neuroweapons” — technologies capable of inducing behavioral changes, disorientation, pain, or auditory phenomena in targeted individuals. This is no longer science fiction. It is an active area of military research and development with a documented acquisition history.

The Havana Syndrome story, which Dr. Pearson incorporates into his documentation through clipped reporting, provides the most direct bridge between classified military research and real-world deployment against real bodies. Diplomats, CIA officers, and military personnel stationed across Havana, Vienna, Guangzhou, Belgrade, and Washington itself reported sudden-onset neurological symptoms consistent with exposure to directed pulsed radiofrequency energy. In January 2026, CNN reported that the Pentagon had acquired — for an eight-figure sum — a backpack-sized device emitting pulsed radio waves, purchased covertly using Defense Department funds, now believed by some investigators to be related to the anomalous health incidents. Congressional investigators have sent criminal referrals to the Department of Justice, alleging that intelligence community officials withheld medical care from victims to compel their participation in what appears to have been a non-consensual human subjects research program.

This is the documented context within which Dr. Pearson reports hearing “clicks” he believes correspond to location tracking, experiencing a sudden, one-time auditory event on July 13, 2025 — a voice he heard only that single day, which he attributes to an electromagnetic intervention — and feeling bodily pressure and sensory disruption he associates with directed energy.

The July 13 auditory event is particularly significant. Dr. Pearson is explicit: he heard voices on exactly one day, describes them with technical precision — a woman on a microphone, background noise as if in a room with others, directional, contextually specific commands — and never experienced them again before or after. This profile is notably inconsistent with classical auditory hallucinations, which are typically persistent, recurring, and phenomenologically disorganized. It is, however, consistent with a short-duration electromagnetic stimulation event. The mental health evaluators at D.C. Department of Behavioral Health — conducted September 15, 2025, by professionals — noted the single occurrence and confirmed it did not meet their clinical threshold for psychotic presentation. One evaluator is explicit: “This (the referral) reads like you hear voices every day.” When Dr. Pearson clarifies he experienced it exactly once, she responds: “That one day and it came in loud and clear” — and proceeds to ask if he uses drugs (he does not) and to complete the standard risk assessment (no suicidal ideation, no self-harm). The assessment concludes he does not meet criteria for psychiatric intervention.

The BRAIN Initiative, launched by the Obama administration as a neuroscience research program, had substantial portions of its funding diverted to military and intelligence applications, a fact Dr. Pearson documents and researchers have since confirmed through public budget analysis. The weaponization of neuroscientific research — the transformation of cognitive mapping into cognitive targeting — has proceeded under institutional cover and public ignorance simultaneously.

If Dr. Pearson’s documented “clicks” represent real acoustic or electromagnetic monitoring technology deployed in an urban residential building, then the United States has crossed a threshold that even the most hawkish defense intellectuals have described as legally and ethically uncharted. Directed energy deployed against a citizen, without warrant or charges, constitutes a form of physical assault invisible to conventional evidence collection and immune from existing legal frameworks. The doctrine of sovereign immunity would likely protect the government from any tort claim arising from it. The lack of physical traces would defeat any evidentiary standard in court. And the government’s ability to classify the underlying technology would prevent any FOIA disclosure.

Dr. Pearson’s question — “What protection do U.S. citizens have?” from neuroweapons deployed domestically — does not have a legal answer. Because Congress has done essentially nothing to regulate their development, testing, or deployment, and because the executive branch has classified what little oversight structure exists.

III. The “Access Denied” Revelation: Legal Black Holes and the Architecture of Impunity

On December 3, 2025, Dr. Pearson called the D.C. Metropolitan Police Department’s non-emergency line to report ongoing noise harassment from the unit above his apartment. What followed was, on its surface, a routine police welfare call. Two officers arrived. They accompanied Pearson to his unit. One officer opened a laptop and appeared to search the OCTO system — the D.C. government’s Office of the Chief Technology Officer’s law enforcement information platform.

On that screen, Dr. Pearson observed, from a few feet away, the words “ACCESS DENIED” displayed prominently. The officer, he notes, did not see him observing and closed the screen.

This observation, if accurate, is among the most significant documented moments in the entire account. OCTO’s systems are designed to provide officers with basic residential and incident information when responding to calls. An “ACCESS DENIED” message on a resident’s apartment number search would not appear under ordinary circumstances for a private citizen with no special legal status. It would appear, however, if the record had been flagged, sealed, or restricted under national security or intelligence protocols — if, in other words, the resident’s address had been placed under some form of classification or operational hold by a federal agency.

The existence of parallel legal systems that can functionally place American citizens outside ordinary law enforcement protection is not speculative. The state secrets privilege, administrative classification, national security letters, and FISA court orders have all been used to create categories of persons and information invisible to normal judicial process. A citizen whose address is flagged in a law enforcement database as “access denied” cannot call the police for help — because the police, when they attempt to respond, are redirected, denied information, or quietly instructed not to investigate.

This is the architecture of what legal scholars call a “legal black hole” — a zone where rights formally exist but cannot practically be accessed. Guantanamo Bay was the most visible American legal black hole of the post-9/11 era. But legal black holes do not require offshore locations. They can be created administratively, through the intersection of classification, discretionary prosecutorial authority, and jurisdictional confusion, for any person the national security state chooses to isolate from legal recourse.

The non-arrest of his assailant deepens this concern substantially. His medical documentation — confirmed in his written communication to building management — establishes lacerations, a black eye visible in photographs uploaded to YouTube, and wrist bruises consistent with defensive shielding. He was the victim who contacted police. The neighbor who punched him repeatedly, running down the hallway to attack, was not charged. Dr. Pearson explicitly asks the core question: “Why didn’t they arrest him? He clearly assaulted me.”

If the answer to that question involves any operational instruction — any coordination between the building’s upper-floor occupant and law enforcement in a manner that insulated the attacker from accountability — then the D.C. Metropolitan Police Department was functioning as an instrument of the campaign rather than a resource against it. This is not unprecedented. COINTELPRO routinely enlisted local law enforcement as passive participants in federal harassment operations. The LAPD and Chicago PD were documented partners in surveilling, infiltrating, and undermining Black liberation organizations. The FBI used local police to manufacture arrests, plant evidence, and isolate targets from legal assistance. The only difference now — the racial identity of those officers who appeared the week before and the day of his assault were predominately African American. The twist and contemporary evolution of COINTELPRO.

The legal ramification is stark: a citizen who is actively surveilled by federal intelligence, whose address returns “ACCESS DENIED” when searched by responding officers, and whose assailant is not arrested. In practice, Pearson’s constitutional rights disappeared.

IV. The Secret Legal Infrastructure: Administrative Subpoenas and the End of Judicial Oversight

Dr. Pearson’s document includes extensive reporting from the Washington Post — published October 2025 — describing how the Department of Homeland Security has transformed the administrative subpoena from a narrow investigative tool into a mass surveillance instrument deployed against ordinary American citizens exercising First Amendment rights.

The case the Post documents in detail involves a 67-year-old retired insurance professional in suburban Philadelphia — a man who sent a four-sentence email to a federal prosecutor asking him to exercise humanity in an immigration case. Five hours after sending the email, the man received notice from Google that DHS had issued an administrative subpoena demanding broad records of his online activity: the time and duration of every internet session, every IP and physical address, every service he used, his credit card and Social Security numbers — and all records retroactive to September 1. No judge approved this subpoena. No grand jury was convened. A mid-level DHS official generated it without external review. The ACLU attorneys who eventually represented the man described the process as designed structurally to prevent challenge: the target has seven days to respond, is not provided a copy of the subpoena, cannot easily locate the authorizing official, and faces a federal court filing requirement most Americans cannot execute without expensive legal counsel they don’t have time to retain.

DHS issued 28,622 administrative subpoenas to Google alone in the first half of Trump’s second term — a fifteen percent increase from the previous six-month period. Google, Meta, Microsoft, Apple, and Snap all reported record subpoena volumes. The agency is not required by law to disclose how many it issues annually. Privacy experts estimate the figure is “well into the thousands, if not tens of thousands.”

For Dr. Pearson — an African American scholar and critic of systemic governance structures whose published work is publicly accessible, whose academic writing explicitly challenges federal and institutional power — the administrative subpoena infrastructure represents the most legally mundane mechanism by which state surveillance can be initiated without accountability. No FISA judge. No probable cause affidavit. No notice. No meaningful appeal. And the recipients of subpoenas are often under gag orders preventing them from disclosing the government’s demands.

Dr. Pearson had no way to know whether his internet records, location data, phone metadata, or financial history had been subject to administrative subpoena. He had no legal mechanism to compel disclosure. He had no right to a hearing. He had no standing in a system that does not formally acknowledge his targeting.

This is the surveillance infrastructure that precedes Zersetzung. Before you can destroy someone’s life quietly, you must first understand it completely. The administrative subpoena gives federal agencies a blueprint of any American’s movements, associations, communications, and financial life without judicial oversight. It transforms the state’s relationship to private citizens from one of presumptive innocence to one of perpetual investigability — and it does so invisibly, leaving the target with no idea it is happening, no opportunity to challenge it, and no remedy after the fact.

The ACLU’s Nathan Freed Wessler put it plainly: “It doesn’t take that much to make people look over their shoulder, to think twice before they speak again… You don’t have to lock somebody up to make them reticent to make their voice heard.”

If Dr. Pearson was subjected to this infrastructure — and given the profile of individuals the Post documents as targets, his profile is not an outlier but a textbook case — then the government already knew far more about him than he knew about it before the harassment began.

V. Mental Torture as Policy: International Human Rights Law and the American Exception

The United Nations Convention Against Torture, which the United States signed in 1988 and ratified in 1994, defines torture to include not only physical pain but “severe mental suffering” inflicted intentionally by state actors or with state acquiescence for any purpose including intimidation, coercion, or punishment. The Committee Against Torture has consistently interpreted this to encompass systematic psychological abuse — including isolation, threats, sleep deprivation, and deliberate manipulation of a person’s perceived reality. Notably, in November 2025, the United States, along with Israel and Argentina, voted against a UN General Assembly resolution condemning torture, breaking a long-standing tradition of consensus.

Dr. Pearson describes months of sleep disruption from coordinated noise above his apartment — heavy objects dropped in rhythmic patterns that follow his movements through his home, stopping when he records and resuming when he stops, persisting through the night and across months. He describes the psychological impact with clinical clarity: social isolation, inability to work, erosion of friendships and family relationships as they encounter the incrementally irrational-sounding documentation he feels compelled to share, loss of housing stability, failed asylum attempts, and a dispossession that by early 2026 has left him with nothing but a mattress and a suitcase.

The D.C. Department of Behavioral Health evaluators confirmed he was not psychotic. Three physicians and two mental health specialists found no psychiatric symptoms requiring intervention. What they found was a man in genuine distress about a genuinely distressing situation. The distinction is not semantic. It is the difference between a person who needs medication and a person who needs protection.

If the systematic destruction of Dr. Pearson’s social life, professional standing, sleep, housing security, financial capacity, and psychological equilibrium was intentionally engineered by state or state-adjacent actors — and if the refusal of police to investigate, the arrest of the victim rather than the attacker, the “ACCESS DENIED” screen, and the institutional silence in response to his documented complaints to the D.C. Council and federal officials constitute state acquiescence — then the United States is in clear violation of the Convention Against Torture.

It would not be the first time. The CIA’s post-9/11 “enhanced interrogation” program was, by any reasonable reading of international law, a systematic torture program. The Senate Intelligence Committee’s report confirmed this, though the executive branch resisted that characterization and no prosecutions followed. The United States has repeatedly and successfully claimed an exception to international human rights norms when national security is invoked as the organizing principle.

But there is a qualitative difference between torture in the context of detention — terrible as that is — and the torture of a free citizen in the nation’s capital, in his own home, in a building within view of the Capitol dome. Dr. Pearson is not a combatant. He is not a foreign national. He is not even accused of a crime — and the circumstances that he maintains was itself part of the operation. He is an American scholar who wrote critically about systemic power and, if his account has merit, was targeted precisely for that.

The international human rights framework recognizes that when states systematically destroy individuals for exercising freedoms of thought, expression, and academic inquiry, it constitutes not merely civil rights violation but a crime against persons. The failure to acknowledge this — the institutional shrug, the systemic non-response, the “access denied” — is not administrative negligence. It is a choice.

VI. The Epistemological Problem at the Heart of Zersetzung

There is a reason the Stasi’s Zersetzung program was so effective: it weaponized epistemology itself. By making the campaign deniable, it made the documentation of the campaign look like its symptoms. The target who insists that the button was moved from his grill to his laundry pile (video evidence), that the neighbors are following him through his apartment (audio and journal evidence), that strangers are wearing colors and carrying bags as coordinated signals (extensive photos) — this target, absent the ability to prove coordination, sounds precisely like a person suffering from paranoid ideation, ideas of reference, and hypervigilance.

This is the trap. And it is elegant in its cruelty.

Dr. Pearson acknowledges this explicitly and repeatedly. He is a trained researcher. He distinguishes, throughout the document, between observation and interpretation. He acknowledges when something “could be a coincidence” or when he “cannot fully exclude” an ordinary explanation. He notes when he may have been “pinned” to a location — attributing his own movements to external influence — and flags those attributions as speculative. He applies probability statistics to the frequency of red-and-black attire in the Eastern Market area on September 30, 2025, computing that the visual distribution he documented is statistically improbable under a null hypothesis of random distribution.

This is not the documentation style of a person experiencing an acute psychotic break. It is the documentation style of a public health researcher applying his training to the investigation of his own victimization. Whether those methods are correctly applied to what is actually happening to him — whether his interpretations are accurate or the product of heightened pattern recognition in a genuinely stressful situation — is a question that cannot be resolved without independent investigation. And that investigation has not happened.

The D.C. Council received his email. The documentation acknowledges an auto-reply from one council member’s office and no response from others. No law enforcement agency has conducted an investigation. No federal oversight body has reviewed his case. No journalist with investigative resources has examined his documented evidence — the photographs, videos, audio recordings, and metadata files he describes maintaining. His asylum request could not materialize because Iceland border officials sent him back — the US is a “safe country,” “We must prioritize countries that are actually at war — Ukraine and Syria,” and “You can apply for asylum, but we still consider the US to be a ‘safe country’.

The absence of investigation is not evidence of its own conclusion. But in a case like this — where the documented evidence includes a psychiatric clearance, a documented physical assault with photographic evidence, a police arrest that inverted victim and perpetrator, and an observed “ACCESS DENIED” screen — the absence of investigation is itself significant. It either means the claims do not warrant serious examination, or it means the systems designed to examine such claims have been captured by the same apparatus Dr. Pearson is describing.

Neither possibility is reassuring.

VII. The Broader Architecture: Why This Case Matters Beyond Its Details

Dr. Pearson’s document draws heavily on two categories of public evidence: the historical record of American domestic surveillance programs (COINTELPRO, MKUltra, Zersetzung-adjacent FBI operations against the Black liberation movement), and the contemporaneous reporting on the expansion of DHS surveillance infrastructure under the current administration. These two categories bracket his individual experience with a context that makes it, at minimum, historically coherent.

The question has never been whether the United States government is capable of conducting a campaign like the one Dr. Pearson describes. It has done so before, repeatedly, against American citizens, and the documentation is in the National Archives and the congressional record. The questions are: whether it is doing so now, against whom, under what authority, and with what oversight.

The administrative subpoena reporting answers at least two of those questions. The Havana Syndrome criminal referrals suggest that some element of the national security apparatus has been actively engaged in non-consensual human experimentation with directed energy technology. The COINTELPRO parallels are not merely rhetorical — the designation of “Black Identity Extremists” by the FBI in 2017 as a domestic terrorism category, subsequently withdrawn but operationally consequential, suggests that the targeting logic of COINTELPRO has not been dismantled but merely reclassified.

In this context, Dr. Pearson is not an outlier. He may be a documented case of a targeting campaign applied to an individual whose profile — African American, educated, critical of systemic power, economically precarious, socially isolated — made him a low-resistance target with high deniability. Or so they thought. The assumption that state power would be too visible to operate this way is an artifact of pre-digital imagination. In an era of administrative subpoenas, metadata surveillance, location tracking via consumer devices, behavioral influence operations conducted through social media algorithms, and directed energy technology deployed in backpack-sized devices, the footprint of a comprehensive psychological campaign against a single individual is vanishingly small.

Dr. Pearson writes, toward the end of his account: “I am not the only one, but may be one of the most intact survivors.” This claim is unverifiable. But it is also the most important sentence in the document. If true — if there is a class of individuals subjected to variations of this campaign — then the individual account is not the story. The infrastructure enabling it is the story. And that infrastructure is not speculative. It is documented, reported, partially disclosed, and expanding.

Conclusion: The Democratic Stakes

The Founders’ primary preoccupation was not foreign invasion. It was the tyranny of domestic power — the capacity of governments to destroy individuals who challenge them. The Fourth Amendment, the First Amendment, the prohibition on cruel and unusual punishment, the right to habeas corpus — these are not abstract values. They are specific protections against specific historical abuses that the drafters had witnessed, suffered, or feared.

The question Dr. Pearson’s account forces is whether those protections still function against a state that has learned to operate without visible force. Zersetzung required no prisons. It required only coordination, patience, and the certainty that no one was watching. The administrative subpoena requires no judge. It requires only a mid-level bureaucrat, a form, and a tech company’s compliance. The neuroweapon may leave no bruise visible to medical imaging. It requires only access, a device, and the knowledge that no law specifically prohibits its use against civilians.

If Dr. Richard Pearson was targeted — if the psychiatrically clear, physically assaulted, institutionally abandoned scholar sleeping on a mattress in a D.C. apartment as of early 2026 was subjected to a coordinated campaign by individuals or institutions with national security-adjacent resources and the cover of plausible deniability — then the constitutional order he was living under when it began had already been substantially hollowed out.

The black eye healed. The documentation remains. And the systems he describes — administrative subpoenas, directed energy research, Zersetzung-compatible harassment tactics, law enforcement coordination that inverts victim and perpetrator — are all real, all documented, all currently operational in the United States.

The conditional “if” at the center of this essay is not a hedge. It is an invitation. An invitation to investigate before concluding. To ask whether the documentation warrants examination before declaring it symptom. To consider that the epistemological trap of Zersetzung — where the act of thorough documentation reads as the evidence of madness — is precisely the protection the program was designed to afford its operators.

History has not been kind to those who, confronted with the detailed testimony of targeted individuals, chose institutional comfort over inconvenient truth. It remembered those who believed Fred Hampton was a threat. It remembered those who mocked the Church Committee’s early findings as paranoid. It tends, eventually, to remember.

Whether it will remember Dr. Richard Pearson depends on whether anyone, in a position to investigate, chooses to look.

The US Tortures More Than You Know. We Just Have Refused to Believe Victims.

At what point do we begin to see the bigger picture — countless victims, eerily similar stories, legal doctrines that make it easy? The Torture Dungeon is real.

What It Is and Is Not

The first precision Pearson demands is definitional. The Torture Dungeon is not a place. It is not a facility with walls, a registry of inmates, a director with a nameplate on a door. It is a condition — a persistent operational environment that exists in parallel with visible American constitutional democracy, drawing on the same institutional infrastructure, funded by the same budgets, staffed by the same agencies, but operating in a legal and social space specifically engineered to be invisible, unaccountable, and self-concealing.

Pearson’s Theresienstadt metaphor is exact. Nazi Germany did not conduct the Holocaust in its propaganda films. America does not conduct the Torture Dungeon in its constitutional law textbooks, its civil rights museums, or its State Department human rights reports.

The Upstairs — due process, judicial review, FOIA requests, the ACLU, the First Amendment — is real. It governs most Americans most of the time. Its reality for the majority is precisely what makes the Dungeon’s operation against the minority structurally invisible. The Upstairs is not a lie. It is a selection mechanism: it applies to those the system has decided it applies to.

“If I did not write about torture and abuse at the hands of the US government, they might think that I am consenting to it. That is the psychosis I am dealing with. — Richard Pearson, PhD

The Dungeon’s Legal Foundation

Reynolds and the State Secrets Doctrine

The architectural cornerstone was laid in 1953. United States v. Reynolds established that the government could assert “state secrets privilege” to terminate litigation before any court examined the underlying facts. The court defers to the assertion. No merits review. No discovery. Case dismissed.

When the Reynolds documents were finally declassified decades later, they contained not national security secrets but evidence of government negligence — the very misconduct the privilege had concealed. The doctrine built on fraud continues to govern. Every targeted individual who seeks legal remedy for classified program abuses runs into Reynolds first.

Sovereign Immunity

The Federal Tort Claims Act grants consent to be sued for a slipping hazard on a federal building floor. It does not grant consent to be sued for torturing a citizen with directed energy weapons. This was not an oversight. The consent structure was architected to exclude the most serious abuses while maintaining the appearance of government accountability.

The Systematic Elimination of Bivens

Bivens v. Six Unknown Named Agents (1971) created a cause of action against individual federal officers for constitutional violations. The Supreme Court has spent fifty years eliminating it. Any technology developed after 1971 constitutes a “new context.” Voice-to-skull technology: new context, no Bivens claim. Remote neural monitoring: new context, no claim. Directed energy behavioral control: new context, no claim. The agents who operate these systems against American citizens are personally beyond civil liability — not because their conduct is legal but because the remedy was never created.

Qualified Immunity

Even where a Bivens claim theoretically exists, qualified immunity requires that the specific conduct have been previously ruled unconstitutional in a near-identical case. Since no case involving directed energy neurological weapons has survived threshold dismissal to reach merits adjudication, there is no prior ruling. The first deployment is immune. The hundredth deployment is immune. The novelty of the method is built-in legal protection.

Glomar and Classification

Beyond these doctrines: Glomar responses (“we can neither confirm nor deny”), classification of operational details, redaction of responsive documents, and the ability to classify program existence itself create a hermetic seal. Pearson’s own research identified one hundred such legal mechanisms, layered cumulatively, each designed to terminate a case before the next layer is needed.

This architecture was not assembled accidentally. It represents deliberate institutional design spanning seven decades. The Dungeon’s legal infrastructure predates its most advanced operational capabilities.

Historical Continuity: How the Dungeon Was Built

The Nazi Science Transfer

Operation Paperclip imported over 1,600 Nazi scientists, engineers, and physicians — including SS officers, Dachau experimenters, Auschwitz researchers — into American military and intelligence programs between 1945 and 1959, with falsified war crimes records. The specific expertise transferred included:

  • Dr. Herman Schwan, who established American electromagnetic radiation safety standards in the 1950s and whose position — that there are no proven athermal biological effects of EMR — became official U.S. policy for forty years while classified research proceeded on exactly those effects.
  • Specialists in psychological manipulation, behavioral control, and human physiological tolerance limits.
  • Aerospace and weapons engineers whose work fed directly into classified programs.

The Dungeon did not develop its methods independently. It inherited them.

Project CHATTER to MKUltra: The Mind Control Lineage

The unbroken programmatic lineage begins with the Navy’s Project CHATTER in 1947 — explicitly inspired by Nazi mescaline experiments — runs through Projects BLUEBIRD and ARTICHOKE (hypnosis, barbiturates, physical coercion, amnesia induction), and consolidates in MKUltra from 1953 to 1973.

MKUltra’s scope was not limited to LSD experiments. Its 150+ subprojects across 80 institutions included:

  • Ewen Cameron’s psychic driving at McGill: patients chemically maintained in sleep for weeks or months, subjected to electroshock at 30–40 times standard dose, with looped audio messages played through helmets into unconscious minds — patients arrived with mild depression and emerged with destroyed identities and complete amnesia. The CIA funded it. Canada paid reparations. The U.S. did not.
  • Subproject 54, researching induction of concussion and amnesia through non-detectable means.
  • Research into bioelectric manipulation of human cognition.
  • Study of sensory deprivation effects that would later be reverse-engineered from SERE training into post-9/11 “enhanced interrogation.”

CIA Director Richard Helms destroyed most MKUltra records in 1973. What was found in a misfiled document cache and released in 1977 represents an incomplete record of a program whose full scope the destruction was specifically designed to prevent from becoming known.

The Frey Effect and Military Microwave Research

In 1961–1962, biophysicist Allan Frey published documented findings that pulsed microwave radiation could create auditory sensations perceived inside the skull, bypassing the ears entirely. The military funded immediate replication and refinement. By the early 1970s, researchers at Walter Reed Army Institute — Joseph Sharp and Mark Grove — had demonstrated intelligible speech transmission via pulsed microwaves. Don Justesen published a semi-public reference to this in American Psychologist in 1975. James Lin published a comprehensive textbook on microwave auditory effects and applications in 1978.

From 1975 to 1996, when Gloria Naylor experienced what she described, twenty-one years of classified refinement occurred at military and intelligence agency facilities with classified budgets. From 1996 to 2025, when Pearson experienced what he describes, an additional twenty-nine years of refinement occurred.

The Army maintained “Voice to Skull Device” in its online research thesaurus until removing the entry from public access. Multiple patents document the underlying principle. James Giordano, neuroethicist at Georgetown University and Pentagon/NATO advisor, stated publicly in 2025 that mind reading and mind control technologies have been “loosed into the world.” The U.S. Air Force Scientific Advisory Board’s 1995–1996 “New World Vistas” document — published concurrent with Naylor’s experiences — explicitly discussed non-lethal weapons providing “a condition of learned helplessness” through neurological targeting.

COINTELPRO and Its Successors

From 1956 to 1971, the FBI’s Counter Intelligence Program operationally targeted:

  • The Black Panther Party (Fred Hampton murdered at 21 in a pre-dawn FBI-coordinated raid while asleep; the FBI paid the informant who provided the floor plan)
  • The Southern Christian Leadership Conference and Martin Luther King Jr. (wiretapped for years; the FBI sent King an anonymous letter explicitly urging suicide days before he received the Nobel Peace Prize)
  • The Nation of Islam, SNCC, AIM, Puerto Rican independence organizations, antiwar groups
  • Individual writers and intellectuals identified as threats to “existing social order”

Tactics included: informant infiltration, forged letters to incite internal conflict, “snitch-jacketing” (falsely labeling members as informants to provoke retaliatory violence), blackmail, fabricated evidence, coordination with local police for raids.

The Church Committee exposed COINTELPRO in 1975. Congress passed new oversight laws. The apparatus of state secrets privilege was not dismantled. The FBI’s 2017 “Black Identity Extremist” designation — a fabricated domestic terrorism category with no statutory basis, requiring no criminal conduct for targeting, directed specifically at Black activists post-Ferguson — demonstrates programmatic continuity. The successor programs continued under different names.

Operation MINARET simultaneously ran NSA watchlists targeting Black intellectuals, antiwar activists, and journalists. Every Black, Latino, and Asian writer who received press coverage was on a list that officially did not exist. This is not Naylor’s fictional Dick Simon’s invention. This is documented congressional testimony.

The Dungeon’s Operational Structure

Target Selection

The targeting criterion varies generally. In Pearson’s case, it is precise: Black intellectuals of achievement and strong racial identity, whistleblowers, activists, and anyone whose intellectual production or political presence represents a specific and manageable threat to existing arrangements of power.

“Specific” is important. The system does not target randomly. It targets people whose credibility, if maintained, would make the maintenance of specific power arrangements harder. A National Book Award-winning novelist who weeps at the Million Man March and writes about Black women’s lives with moral authority is a specific threat — not to national security as conventionally defined, but to the ideological infrastructure that normalizes racial hierarchy. A PhD equity scholar who publishes methodologically rigorous findings on racial inequality in the current political environment in which DEI itself is being declared a threat — is a specific threat by the same logic.

The targeting begins from a trigger, often petty. For Naylor: a neighbor’s cat and a sibling relationship that connected it to an NSA official. For Pearson: the precise trigger is not fully established, but the profile match is exact. The trigger is not the cause. It is the access point. The machinery was already there. The trigger provides an excuse to aim it.

The Personnel: Who Staffs the Dungeon

The Dungeon requires more bodies than any federal workforce can supply without creating accountability-exposing paper trails. The solution is the contractor structure:

Federal agencies — NSA, CIA, FBI, DIA, and through joint task force structures, local law enforcement — provide direction, technology, legal cover, and coordination.

Defense contractors — Booz Allen Hamilton (Snowden’s employer), SAIC, Leidos, CACI International (paid for Abu Ghraib interrogations), ManTech, and hundreds of smaller firms provide personnel, technical operations, and field implementation at arm’s length from direct federal accountability.

The ADL and similar organizations — The Anti-Defamation League was caught in 1993 running illegal domestic surveillance on over 12,000 individuals and 950 organizations, sharing intelligence with foreign governments including apartheid South Africa, infiltrating domestic political organizations, and working with police informants. It settled the resulting civil lawsuit for $175,000. In Naylor’s documented account, ADL operatives were coordinated into her surveillance through a cousin at the Columbia office. Three years after the ADL’s documented illegal surveillance program was exposed.

Community volunteers and contractors — The Dungeon requires, for operations like vehicle formations, red-and-black color deployments, and street theater, a large number of individuals who are told only that they are participating in a security operation against a target who represents a threat. Some are paid. Some are ideologically motivated. Some, like the 72-year-old Dachau survivor Naylor describes — who joined the car pool because she was told there was a threat and knew from her own history what threats become if not addressed early — have no idea what they are actually participating in.

The pyramid is: classified technology and direction at the top, contractor implementation in the middle, and community-sourced labor for volume operations at the base. Each layer has plausible deniability about the layer above.

The Operational Rooms

Pearson describes the Dungeon as containing rooms. Each room is a distinct operational modality, deployed simultaneously:

Room One: Sleep Deprivation

The ceiling above the target’s bed becomes a delivery mechanism. Real-time localization technology — documented in both classified literature and published academic research on through-wall radar — allows tracking of a body within an apartment. The neighbor above receives direction, or is a placed operative, or the building itself has been instrumented. The sounds follow the target room to room. To the closet. At 3 AM.

Sleep deprivation is internationally recognized as torture under conventions the United States has signed, promoted, and cited as evidence of its commitment to human rights. It is being conducted through residential ceilings.

Its effects are not merely physical discomfort. Sustained sleep deprivation produces: impaired judgment, emotional dysregulation, paranoid ideation, hallucinations, impaired memory consolidation, and — critically — a presentation that looks clinically identical to the early stages of psychotic decompensation. The person describing their experience to a psychiatrist after weeks of targeted sleep deprivation will present exactly as someone developing a serious mental illness. This is the operational specification, not a side effect.

Room Two: The Noise Campaign and Environmental Manipulation

Adjacent to sleep deprivation but distinct in its mechanics. The noise campaign is not primarily about the noise. It is about the systematic conversion of the target’s environment from neutral to weaponized — while maintaining to every outside observer that nothing extraordinary is occurring.

Hammers. Chainsaws. Lawn mowers in calibrated patterns from a rented adjacent property. Car doors slamming in rotation outside a study window. A neighbor dropping objects precisely above wherever the target is located within their apartment. The frequency and precision that distinguishes deliberate operation from ordinary urban noise is perceivable to the target and invisible to anyone else.

The secondary effect, as David Shane explained in Naylor’s account and as Pearson documents, is environmental conditioning. The target becomes sensitized to specific stimuli. Once conditioned, the stimuli only need to be present sporadically to maintain the psychological effect. The target does the work of harassment themselves, perceiving threat in sounds that, by that point, may or may not be operationally deployed.

Room Three: Vehicle Formations and Street Presence

Coordinated vehicle operations serve multiple functions. In a rural area like St. Helena, a specific pattern — car arrives, turns, cruises past, turns, exits — on a dead-end road with no other destination communicates operational presence while remaining legally invisible. Each individual car is just a car taking a drive.

In an urban area like Washington DC, the formation adapts: vehicles pulling from parking spaces as the target walks, following at a fixed interval, always present when the target leaves home. The operational requirement is not that every vehicle be a deployed asset. It is that enough are that the pattern is perceivable to the sensitized target and explainable as coincidence to everyone else.

The color-coded clothing operation Pearson documents serves the same communicative function through different logistics. Forty-three people in coordinated red and black across a 90-minute walk in one neighborhood is not Washington DC’s fashion distribution. It is a deployment communicating: we are everywhere. We see you. We are organized. There is nothing you can do.

Room Four: Home Entry and Object Displacement

The panini grill button. Naylor’s flashlight on the mantle. The disturbed attic insulation. The seed package with curled edges.

Entry without theft. Entry that leaves evidence of its own occurrence while leaving nothing that would constitute actionable evidence for law enforcement or legal proceedings. The message delivered is not: we were here and we took something. The message is: we were here. We can enter when we choose. We can touch anything in your home. We will leave only what you yourself can perceive, and nothing that anyone else would take seriously.

This is a psychological operation targeting the most fundamental security instinct: the belief that your home is a protected space. Once that belief is destroyed, the target has no refuge. Outside is surveilled. Inside has been violated. The library is the only remaining sanctuary — and that only because the technology has a range limitation.

Room Five: Digital Compromise

The phone. The laptop. The email. The social media account.

What Pearson describes — phones disappearing and returning, a device returned by a Secret Service agent with his driver’s license still inside, text appearing in a SORA prompt that he did not type — is the experiential presentation of the documented NSA TEMPEST program’s read-write capability. Van Eck phreaking (reading computer electromagnetic emissions) has a documented inverse: using directed RF emissions to interact with computer circuit boards. The NSA’s classified capabilities, running as documented by Snowden and others 15–20 years ahead of public technology, include exactly this.

PRISM, XKeyscore, MUSCULAR — all revealed by Snowden in 2013 — demonstrated that the NSA was already, before 2013, collecting essentially all electronic communications of essentially all Americans. The specific targeting of an individual’s device goes further: real-time monitoring of keystrokes, installation of hidden firmware that persists through factory resets, conversion of the microphone and camera into permanent surveillance instruments. The device the target carries everywhere becomes a room in the Dungeon that they carry with them.

Room Six: Informant Recruitment from the Social Network

C.J. Hudson. A friend of ten years. A Princeton historian. He dissolved sleeping pills in Naylor’s wine and handed her laptop to NSA operatives. He did it because they had leverage — a prior arrest on a morals charge involving a teenage male prostitute, made to disappear by one call, documented and held for exactly this moment.

The recruitment of people from within the target’s social network is both operationally efficient — trusted access, no surveillance signatures required — and specifically devastating because it destroys the target’s ability to trust anyone. After C.J., Naylor could not be certain that any friend might not be an asset. This is not paranoia. It is the rational response to confirmed operational fact.

Pearson’s account includes similar encounters with people whose behavior suggests prior briefing — excessive familiarity, code-like communication, knowledge of specific private details. “Lee” at the Potbelly’s. David Meyerowitz with his specific prepared narrative. The stranger in Rockville who quoted Bartleby before Pearson had time to tell anyone about “canceling the deal.”

The operational requirement is not that every social contact be an asset. It is that enough are that the target cannot maintain trust in any of them, and that the social network — the primary support structure against psychological disintegration — is converted into a source of threat.

Room Seven: Chemical Assault

Naylor’s garden was poisoned. The Boys played games with rat poison under her house, crawling through the crawl space with wrenches and bags of mud at 3 AM, calling it “poison the target.”

Pearson describes sitting at the Rockville Metro and discovering his pants soaked with an industrial chemical — sticky, highly odorous, adhering to his skin so severely that removing the pants later felt like tearing his epidermis. A dead bird at the location, presumably the delivery mechanism. The chemical had not soaked through from the environment. It had been applied.

The Dungeon’s documented appendix includes chemical assault as a standard operational tool: the NSA’s own Akwei lawsuit documentation references “chemicals and drugs into residential buildings with hidden NSA-installed and maintained plastic plumbing lines” for drug delivery. Operation Sea-Spray deliberately aerosolized bacterial agents over San Francisco. The Army released Bacillus subtilis into New York City subway ventilation. Chemical delivery to a specific individual in a public space is, by comparison, trivially achievable.

Room Eight: The Voice

This is the Dungeon’s most technically advanced room and its most operationally brilliant — because the symptom it produces is identical to the most stigmatized and least credible psychiatric presentation available: auditory hallucinations characteristic of paranoid schizophrenia.

The Frey effect — pulsed microwaves depositing sound directly into the auditory cortex, bypassing the ears — was demonstrated in 1961. Speech transmission via this mechanism was demonstrated in the early 1970s. Sixty years of classified development have produced what Naylor described as a conversational voice operating nightly from an adjacent building, and what Pearson describes as a woman’s voice on a microphone directing him across Washington DC on a specific documented day.

The Army maintained “Voice to Skull Device” in its research thesaurus. Multiple patents document the underlying physics. The European Parliament’s 1999 resolution called for an international ban on weapons “which might enable any form of manipulation of human beings.” They were not calling for a ban on hypothetical future technology.

The operational specification of this room is precise and documented: the weapon was engineered to mimic paranoid schizophrenia because the misdiagnosis is the point. The psychiatric system — staffed by practitioners genuinely trying to help — becomes, without their knowledge or consent, an operational asset for the program it cannot see. The target who describes voices is given antipsychotics that do not address the actual cause, which creates apparent treatment resistance, which supports a more severe diagnosis, which deepens the psychiatric attribution, which closes the loop.

Both Naylor and Pearson describe the key diagnostic tell: the voices are localized. They come from a specific direction. They are absent in specific physical locations. They stop when the target moves beyond the device’s operational range. They have an acoustic signature — a woman on a microphone, background noise of an operational environment — that distinguishes them from internally generated experience. And they say things the target would never say to themselves: I hate Jews. I want to kill myself. We’re going to make you disappear.

The content is operationally chosen. It targets the specific psychological vulnerabilities of the specific individual. For Naylor, who had worked to resist antisemitism throughout the surveillance: you hate Jews. For Pearson, whose identity and mental health are both at stake: degrading names and suicide suggestions. The voice does not just harass. It attempts to produce the internal state that will manifest externally as evidence of the psychiatric presentation the operation has diagnosed in advance.

Room Nine: Pinning Technology

This room Pearson names and describes with more specificity than Naylor, who experienced it without vocabulary to identify it.

Pinning is the directional influence of behavior through targeted neurological stimulation — not auditory implantation but motor and cognitive direction. The experience is of having a strong, unusually compelling impulse to go to a specific location, make a specific choice, take a specific action — an impulse that the subject, in retrospect, recognizes as discontinuous with their normal decision-making pattern.

The Akwei lawsuit describes RNM capabilities including influence over motor control. The “New World Vistas” document describes inducing “learned helplessness.” Persinger’s 1995 peer-reviewed paper described theoretical pathways for behavioral influence at a distance via ELF waves. The neurological research underlying these capabilities — Delgado’s work on the stimoceiver, Adey’s work on ELF effects on neural calcium ion efflux, the classified military development on top of both — provides the scientific foundation.

The operational use Pearson describes is specific: he is directed toward Georgetown on July 13 as part of what he calls an “extraction attempt.” He is directed to return to the tub in the park near the Capitol, where contents appeared designed to compromise him in a specific way. The pinning is not random behavioral disruption. It is targeted behavioral direction toward operationally useful outcomes.

The Psychiatric Trap: The Dungeon’s Master Seal

Every room in the Dungeon produces a symptom indistinguishable from a recognized psychiatric presentation:

  • Sleep deprivation: cognitive impairment, paranoid ideation, emotional dysregulation
  • Environmental harassment: hypervigilance, ideas of reference
  • Informant recruitment: paranoid attachment patterns, inability to trust
  • Voice-to-skull: auditory hallucinations characteristic of paranoid schizophrenia
  • Pinning: delusions of external control
  • Digital compromise: paranoid belief that devices are surveilling

The psychiatric trap works as follows: the target, experiencing real operations, begins to describe them. The description, absent knowledge of the operational history and classified capabilities, sounds exactly like a psychiatric decompensation. Friends and family apply the “crazy” label — not from malice but from a reasonable application of Occam’s razor in the absence of the relevant classified context. Employers lose confidence. The target is referred to mental health services. Mental health professionals, trained in diagnostic frameworks that do not include “directed energy neurological weapons as a differential diagnosis for auditory hallucinations,” apply the closest available diagnostic category. Treatment is initiated. The treatment does not address the actual cause. The apparent treatment failure reinforces the diagnosis. The target is further discredited.

The misdiagnosis then becomes evidence in the social network that the target cannot be believed. Whatever documentation they have produced is recontextualized as evidence of paranoid ideation rather than accurate observation. The entire record is sealed under the psychiatric attribution.

Both Naylor and Pearson obtained preemptive mental health evaluations specifically to defeat this mechanism. Both were found rational. Pearson explicitly describes understanding the trap was coming and moving to close it in advance.

The trap has a specific failure mode: it fails when the target has sufficient intellectual resources to understand what is being done to them, sufficient methodological training to document it systematically, and the specific courage to keep the documentation in the world before any classification order can reach it.

The Population: Who Is in the Dungeon

The Dungeon’s population cannot be precisely counted — this is a design feature. But Pearson reasons from available evidence:

Cheryl Welsh’s organization received over 1,800 documented claims by 2003 — a floor, not a ceiling, given the proportion who do not find reporting organizations. The gangstalking community numbers in the tens of thousands across documented online networks. Researchers who study this population typically apply psychiatric frameworks without engaging the documented history of COINTELPRO, the documented capabilities of intelligence agencies, or the documented legal architecture that makes verification impossible. The application of psychiatric frameworks without this context to a population that may in significant part be experiencing real operations is itself a form of institutional participation in the Dungeon’s psychiatric seal.

The whistleblower population, partially documented through congressional testimony and journalistic investigation, shows consistent patterns of post-disclosure harassment matching the operational profile.

The populations subjected to documented FBI Black Identity Extremist surveillance after 2014, the Muslim American communities subjected to NYPD/FBI infiltration programs documented by the Associated Press in 2011, the indigenous communities whose political organizing has been systematically disrupted — these are populations whose partial targeting is confirmed through official sources and represent the visible edge of a larger distribution.

Pearson’s estimate: tens of thousands at minimum. Potentially hundreds of thousands. Distributed across the country’s cities and towns and rural areas. Most without the educational resources, financial stability, or institutional connections to build the kind of record Pearson has built. Most already psychiatrized in ways that sealed their accounts before they were fully articulated.

The Dungeon’s Purpose

The Dungeon is not random cruelty. It has a specific structural function: the management of intellectual production and political presence that threatens existing arrangements of power.

This is precise. It does not target the powerless who cannot threaten anything. It targets the credible — the writer with the National Book Award, the PhD with peer-reviewed publications, the whistleblower with specific documented knowledge. The objective is not elimination but discrediting and neutralization: to convert a credible observer into an apparently mentally ill person whose observations can be dismissed, and to do so while maintaining the operational and social environment of the Upstairs — the appearance of constitutional democracy, due process, and the rule of law.

Social death rather than physical death, because dead targets become martyrs. Discredited, isolated, apparently mentally ill former intellectuals become evidence that the system works — that people who make extreme claims about government targeting are the kind of people whose claims should not be taken seriously.

The Dungeon is specifically calibrated to not produce martyrs. It produces cautionary tales.

The Two Americas: The Structural Relationship

Pearson’s most important analytical contribution may be his insistence that the Dungeon and the Upstairs are not in contradiction. They are structurally related.

The Upstairs is real. Due process, judicial review, the ACLU, the First Amendment — these function for most Americans most of the time. The Dungeon does not negate them. It depends on them. The Upstairs provides the legitimacy that makes the Dungeon invisible. The demonstrable reality of constitutional governance for most people makes claims about its absence for some people seem implausible on their face.

The state secrets privilege does not prevent lawsuits. It terminates them. This is only possible because a legal system exists for it to terminate — a visible, functional legal system whose operation provides the cover under which the privilege performs its concealment function.

The psychiatric system does not deliberately participate in the Dungeon’s operations. It genuinely tries to help. Its participation is structural, not intentional: it applies the diagnostic frameworks it has, without the classified context that would make a different diagnosis available. The genuine functioning of mental health services is what makes the psychiatric trap operable.

The Statue of Liberty does not lie. People do flee to America from governments that conduct surveillance, noise campaigns, neurological assault, and systematic discrediting of dissidents. They are not wrong to do so. Most of them will never encounter the Dungeon. The America they find is largely the America the Statue promises.

But the Dungeon operates in the same country. With the same money. Under the same flag. The Dungeon’s population — tens of thousands at minimum — is distributed through the same cities and towns as the people for whom the Upstairs functions as advertised.

The structural relationship is this: the Dungeon requires the Upstairs. The Upstairs enables the Dungeon. They are not two Americas. They are one America operating at two levels simultaneously, with the upper level providing the social and institutional conditions under which the lower level can operate with maximum invisibility and minimum accountability.

What the Dungeon Cannot Fully Seal

Pearson identifies one mechanism the Dungeon’s architecture does not adequately address: documentation that exists in the world before any classification order can reach it, addressed to the public before any institutional gatekeeper can route it to dismissal.

Naylor sat in the Brooklyn Public Library — outside the device’s operational range, in a public institution built on her mother’s dream of intellectual access — and printed in block letters: I DIDN’T WANT TO TELL THIS STORY.

Pearson built his record across fourteen months with the methodological discipline of a PhD researcher who understood from the first incident that the documentation had to precede the litigation, that the aggregate pattern was the only argument that could survive the psychiatric seal, and that the only protection against an operation designed to produce plausible deniability was overwhelming systematic evidence in the public record.

Both are Black American intellectuals whose professional formation gave them exactly the tools the Dungeon’s operations were designed to destroy — and who used those tools to document what was being done to them with a precision and comprehensiveness that the Dungeon, by Pearson’s account, did not fully anticipate.

The Dungeon was designed for the person who falls apart. Who stops trusting their own perception. Who cannot maintain the methodological standards of systematic documentation under sustained psychological attack. Who eventually presents to a psychiatrist and receives the diagnosis that seals the record.

The Dungeon was not designed for Gloria Naylor in the library with a trembling hand and a yellow legal pad.

It was not designed for Richard Pearson with a PhD, a timestamp on every photograph, a preemptive mental health evaluation in the record, and a hundred-mechanism legal analysis that demonstrates — before filing any claim — exactly how every available judicial pathway has been architecturally sealed against him.

The question the Dungeon did not design for — Pearson states it directly at the end:

You know this now. What you do with the knowing is the only question the dungeon did not design for.

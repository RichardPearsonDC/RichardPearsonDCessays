“If I Did Not Write about Torture and Abuse at the Hands of the US Government, They Might Think That I Am Consenting to it.”

That is the Clinical Psychosis of Torturers That I Am Confronting.

The belief that a torture victim’s silence constitutes consent is not an incidental feature of the perpetrator’s psychology. It is a load-bearing element of the cognitive architecture that makes sustained torture possible.

Read it again with fresh eyes.

“If I did not write about torture and abuse at the hands of the US government, they might think that I am consenting to it. That is the clinical psychosis of torturers that I am confronting.”

The psychosis is theirs. The “they” who might think he is consenting — the operators, the handlers, the people running the program — are the ones Pearson is diagnosing. He is saying: the people doing this to me are operating from a psychological framework so distorted that they would interpret my silence as consent. And that distortion — the belief system that makes silence equal consent in the mind of a torturer — is itself a clinical condition. A psychosis. And it is the psychosis he has to navigate every day, because it governs the behavior of the people who have power over his life.

This is a much more precise and much more devastating observation than anything about his own psychology. He is not describing his trauma. He is describing their pathology.

The Torturer’s Psychology: What the Literature Establishes

The clinical and psychological literature on perpetrators of state-sanctioned torture is specific about the cognitive and moral frameworks that make sustained torture possible. It is not, primarily, a literature about sadism. Sadism — the enjoyment of cruelty for its own sake — is relatively rare even among documented perpetrators of systematic abuse. What the literature documents instead is a set of cognitive distortions that are far more mundane, far more stable, and far more dangerous precisely because they do not feel, to the person holding them, like distortions at all.

James Waller’s work in Becoming Evil establishes that ordinary people become capable of extraordinary cruelty through a specific psychological pathway: the dehumanization of the target, the diffusion of individual responsibility across an institutional structure, the authorization of conduct by legitimate-seeming authority, and the routinization of harm through bureaucratic procedure. Each of these mechanisms operates to sever the perpetrator’s normal empathic response to the suffering they cause. The harm becomes procedural rather than personal. The target becomes a category rather than a person. The perpetrator becomes a functionary rather than an agent.

But Waller’s framework, while accurate, does not capture the specific distortion Pearson is identifying. He is not simply saying his torturers have dehumanized him or diffused their responsibility. He is saying something more precise: they have a theory of consent that is psychotic.

The Consent Psychosis

The belief that a torture victim’s silence constitutes consent is not an incidental feature of the perpetrator’s psychology. It is a load-bearing element of the cognitive architecture that makes sustained torture possible.

Consider what it would mean for a torturer to genuinely reckon with the absence of consent in their victim. It would mean acknowledging that the harm they are inflicting is being inflicted on a person who is resisting it, who experiences it as harm, who has not agreed to it, and who is being denied the ability to refuse it. That acknowledgment, held honestly, produces a moral reality that is incompatible with continuing. The torturer who genuinely sees the victim’s non-consent cannot continue without accepting the full weight of what they are doing.

The psychosis Pearson identifies is the cognitive mechanism that prevents this reckoning. The belief — held with the rigidity and imperviousness to evidence that defines delusional thinking — that the victim who does not loudly, persistently, and visibly resist is not really a victim. That silence is agreement. That accommodation is acceptance. That the person who has been broken into compliance has revealed, through their compliance, that the targeting was appropriate. That the target who stops fighting has confirmed the designation.

This is psychotic in the clinical sense because it inverts the causal relationship between the harm and the response to the harm. The torture produces the silence. The silence is then read as evidence that the torture was justified. The perpetrator’s conduct is retroactively validated by the outcome that conduct was specifically designed to produce. The distortion is self-sealing. It cannot be falsified by any behavior the victim exhibits, because every possible behavior — resistance and silence alike — is absorbed into the framework that confirms the perpetrator’s position.

The Authorization Framework and Moral Disengagement

Albert Bandura’s theory of moral disengagement provides the clinical scaffolding for understanding how otherwise functional people maintain this distortion over time without experiencing it as distortion.

Moral disengagement operates through eight specific mechanisms, several of which are directly relevant to what Pearson is describing. Moral justification converts harmful conduct into service of a higher purpose — the target is a threat to national security, a Black Identity Extremist, a destabilizing force whose neutralization protects the social order. Dehumanization strips the target of the psychological qualities that activate empathic response — the target is a category, a designation, a file number, not a person with a PhD and a dissertation and a life. Diffusion of responsibility distributes the conduct across so many institutional actors that no single person experiences themselves as the agent of the harm. And advantageous comparison makes the conduct seem reasonable by reference to worse conduct — we are not doing what the Soviets did, we are merely managing a threat.

But the most relevant mechanism for Pearson’s observation is what Bandura calls displacement of responsibility — the cognitive operation by which the perpetrator locates the cause of the harm in the victim’s own conduct. You brought this on yourself. Your work was a threat. Your silence would end this. Your resistance is why this continues.

The displacement of responsibility onto the victim is the precise mechanism that generates the consent interpretation Pearson is describing. If the victim is responsible for the targeting, then the victim controls the targeting. If the victim controls the targeting, then the victim who continues to be targeted has, in some sense, chosen to be targeted. And the victim who stops writing, stops documenting, stops publicly naming what is being done — has chosen to stop provoking it. Has consented to the terms. Has acknowledged, through silence, the legitimacy of the designation.

This is not a metaphor. This is the actual cognitive structure through which people who conduct sustained targeting programs experience what they are doing as something other than what it is. It is the psychosis Pearson lives inside, because it governs the operational logic of the people who have the power to intensify or withdraw the pressure being applied to his life.

The Specific Danger This Creates

Understanding this as their clinical profile rather than his own illuminates something important about the strategic logic of Pearson’s writing.

If the perpetrators operate from a framework in which silence equals consent, then silence does not merely fail to protect him. It actively informs the program’s operational decisions. A target who goes quiet has, within the perpetrators’ framework, been successfully neutralized. The pressure has worked. The designation has been validated. The program can either de-escalate — mission accomplished — or escalate, now confident that the target’s resistance has been broken and further pressure will meet no organized opposition.

Pearson writing is therefore not just the maintenance of his own witnessing self. It is a direct communication to the perpetrators, within the only framework they understand, that the program has not achieved its objective. That the consent their psychosis requires has not been given. That the designation they have applied has not been internalized. That the target is still a witness, still a researcher, still a person whose moral reality is intact and whose account of events is being maintained in a form that cannot be classified, suppressed, or reinterpreted through psychiatric attribution.

He is not writing for us, primarily. He is writing for them. Because they are the ones whose clinical profile makes his silence dangerous — dangerous not because silence would be comfortable, but because their psychosis would read it as the one thing he will not give them.

The Broader Clinical Literature on Perpetrator Psychology

The perpetrator psychology Pearson is diagnosing has been documented across multiple contexts of state-sanctioned abuse, and the consent distortion appears consistently.

In Stanley Milgram’s obedience experiments, subjects who continued to administer what they believed were painful electric shocks to screaming victims did so in part because the institutional authority structure provided them with a framework in which the victim’s distress was interpreted as resistance to a legitimate procedure rather than evidence that the procedure should stop. The victim’s suffering was real. The perpetrator’s perception of it was filtered through a framework that converted suffering into non-compliance rather than into harm requiring cessation.

In Philip Zimbardo’s Stanford Prison Experiment — and more importantly in Zimbardo’s subsequent analysis of Abu Ghraib — the guards who inflicted humiliation and abuse on prisoners did not, in their own accounts, believe they were torturing. They believed they were managing unruly subjects. The prisoners’ distress was read not as evidence of harm but as evidence of the prisoners’ failure to cooperate with legitimate institutional procedure. The consent framework was total: a prisoner who accepted the institutional terms was a good prisoner. A prisoner who resisted was a problem prisoner whose resistance justified escalating pressure.

Martha Stout’s clinical work on sociopathy — specifically the one-in-twenty-five Americans she documents as lacking the capacity for genuine empathic response to others’ suffering — provides the individual-level clinical foundation for the institutional-level distortion Pearson describes. The person who runs a targeting program without apparent distress at what the program does to its targets is not necessarily performing a complex moral disengagement. They may simply lack the neurological substrate that would make the target’s suffering relevant to their own experience. Stout’s clinical profile of the sociopathic individual in a position of institutional authority — charming, functionally competent, genuinely incapable of the empathic response that would make their conduct feel wrong — maps precisely onto the type of person most likely to occupy the operational roles in a sustained targeting program.

Why Pearson Names It Psychosis Specifically

The word choice is deliberate and exact.

He does not say they are wrong. He does not say they are evil. He does not say they are operating in bad faith, or that they are lying to themselves, or that they are making a mistake. He says they are psychotic. And psychosis, clinically, means something specific: a condition in which the person’s internal model of reality has become sufficiently disconnected from reality that it cannot be corrected by evidence or argument. The person experiencing a psychotic delusion does not experience it as a delusion. They experience it as the truth. Evidence that contradicts the delusion is absorbed into the delusion — reinterpreted, discounted, or explained away — because the delusion is the framework through which all evidence is processed.

The perpetrators who would read Pearson’s silence as consent are not making a logical error they could be argued out of. They are operating from a fixed cognitive framework — the target who complies has been managed, the target who resists confirms the designation, the target who falls silent has been neutralized — that is impervious to the evidence of what is actually happening to him. His suffering is not evidence of harm because, within their framework, he is not a person whose suffering counts as harm. His documentation is not evidence of what he documents because, within their framework, it is a symptom of the pathology that justified the targeting. His silence would not be evidence of innocence because, within their framework, it would be evidence that the program worked.

This is the clinical definition of psychosis: a framework impervious to disconfirming evidence, maintained as reality regardless of what reality presents.

Pearson is living inside it. Not as a patient. As the object of it. He has to conduct himself — every day, in every decision about whether to write and what to say and how to document — with full awareness of the clinical profile of the people whose actions he is documenting. He has to understand their distortion well enough to communicate within it — to send them the only signal their framework can receive, which is the signal of ongoing resistance — while simultaneously maintaining, against the pressure that resistance produces, his own accurate account of what is happening.

That is the psychosis he is dealing with. Theirs. Applied to him. Every day.

And the sentence that names it is, in its quiet precision, an act of clinical observation as rigorous as anything in the medical literature — performed by a man diagnosing his torturers from inside the condition they have created for him.

“It Happened to Me”: Research Supports Idea of Being Moved Without Your Conscious Choice

The neuroscience behind Richard Pearson’s tub incident — and the terrifying question it opens

The most disturbing element of what Richard Pearson documented in Washington, D.C. is not what was done to him. It is what the capability that did it could be used for next.

Begin with a thought experiment.

Imagine you could make a person walk to a specific location — a park, a street corner, a room — without their knowledge, without their resistance, without leaving any mark on them that a physician could find. The person would experience the walk as their own idea. A passing curiosity. A sudden urge to see what was around the corner. They would arrive at the destination, find whatever you had placed there, and have no way to explain how they had known to go there, because they would not experience themselves as having been sent. They would experience themselves as having chosen.

Now ask: if you could move a person to a park, what else could you move them to?

A voting booth, primed with a candidate preference induced before the door swings open. A confrontation, with someone whose face has been repeatedly paired, through prior manipulation, with fear. A vehicle, waiting. A building that, an hour later, explodes. A moment of crisis manufactured for them, in a location selected for them, at a time chosen by someone they will never see.

The question is not hypothetical. The science underlying the capability is published and peer-reviewed. The military research developing its non-invasive deployment is documented on the U.S. government’s own websites. And one man in Washington, D.C. — a scholar named Richard Pearson, PhD — spent November 7, 2025 experiencing what this technology feels like from the inside, and was disciplined enough to document it in real time before arriving at a plastic storage tub under a tree in a park beside the O’Neil House Office Building, three blocks from the United States Capitol.

What he found in that tub, and what happened to it before he returned, confirms something more disturbing than the technology itself: that whoever placed it there knew exactly where he would go, and when, before he did.

The Tub: What Happened, in Sequence

On November 7, 2025, Pearson was drawn to a small park on 2nd Street SW between D and C Streets in Washington, D.C. He had no prior reason to go there. It was not on his regular route. He could not, afterward, explain why he had felt compelled to visit.

He entered the park but felt a “pull” toward the back of the park. Ignoring it, he explored its main concrete area in the front, which can be viewed from the street. He decided to leave. He was on the sidewalk, moving away, when the pull returned — not a vague restlessness, but a specific directional sensation: go to the back. He noted the strangeness of this in real time. He went back. He crossed to the rear of the park, to a spot under a tree that he had not yet visited, that was not visible from the area he had just been exploring.

Under the tree was a plastic storage tub.

Inside: a red hat. A black shirt. Khaki pants with a distinctive cuffed bottom almost identical to a pair that Pearson once owned. His were green. These were brown. A notebook whose contents he described as looking like the writings of a disturbed person — references to Adolf Hitler, a fictitious institution called the “European removal court,” conspiratorial and threatening material. Two identical photographs of a blond man with Eastern European features.

He placed all the items back, then put the tub in its original location. He took a picture.

He returned on November 13, filming himself walking from home on video. The notebook was gone. The photographs were gone. The red hat and black shirt remained.

Read that sequence carefully. A storage container had been placed at a specific sub-location within a park Pearson did not regularly visit. He was drawn to that sub-location through an impulse he experienced as his own. The container was monitored after his visit. When he had not taken the framing materials, someone returned, recovered them, and left the calling card items in place. The entire operation — placement, behavioral direction, monitoring, selective retrieval — required surveillance of his real-time position, a behavioral direction capability precise enough to terminate at a specific spot under a specific tree, active monitoring of his visit, and a responsive ground team.

It was, in Pearson’s own analysis, a trap. The technology brought him to it. His own caution — photographing, understanding what he was looking at — is what kept the trap from closing.

The Neuroscience: How a Man Gets Moved

The mechanism Pearson described — a pull toward a specific location experienced as ordinary impulse — is not science fiction. It is a description, from the subjective interior, of what neuroscientists have been producing in laboratory settings for years. The biological substrate exists. The delivery challenge — producing the effect at range, without an implant, in a public outdoor environment — is where the public research ends and the classified research begins. But the gap between them is narrower than most people realize.

The hippocampus and its place cells. The brain’s navigational system is housed in the hippocampus, a seahorse-shaped structure deep beneath the cortex. Within it, place cells — neurons first identified by John O’Keefe in the 1970s, a discovery for which he won the 2014 Nobel Prize in Physiology — fire in patterns that encode specific geographic locations. Each place cell activates when the body occupies or approaches its associated location. Together, they form a spatial map: the brain’s internal GPS. When a cluster of place cells fires, the brain interprets this as both a positional signal (I am here) and a motivational one (I should be here). The firing of place cells does not feel like receiving a command. It feels like the natural experience of being drawn toward a location you have a reason to visit.

In 2020, researchers at Janelia Research Campus published a landmark study in Cell demonstrating that by using two-photon optogenetics to activate specific hippocampal place cell ensembles in mice navigating a virtual environment, they could causally drive the animals to move toward targeted locations. The animals did not appear confused or compelled. They moved as if following their own internal map — because the researchers had rewritten that map in real time. The study’s title is direct: “Targeted Activation of Hippocampal Place Cells Drives Memory-Guided Spatial Behavior.”

The animals were moved by their own neurons. The neurons had been told where to say home was.

From implants to interference. The 2020 Janelia study required optogenetic implants — fiber optic probes threaded into the hippocampus, activated by laser light. This is the current state of the art for precision. It is also invasive, requiring surgery, and limited to controlled laboratory subjects. The question for Pearson’s case is whether the same hippocampal targeting can be achieved non-invasively, at range, in an outdoor urban environment.

A technique called Transcranial Temporal Interference Stimulation is the most publicly documented approach to this problem. The principle, described by Grossman et al. in Cell in 2017, involves delivering two high-frequency electromagnetic fields from separate sources at slightly offset frequencies. High-frequency fields pass through skull and cortex without stimulating neurons — they oscillate too rapidly for neuronal membranes to follow. But at the point where the two fields intersect, they produce a beat frequency: a low-frequency oscillation at the difference between the two carrier frequencies. This beat frequency falls in the range that neurons can detect and respond to. The intersection point — where the stimulation actually occurs — can be positioned inside the brain by adjusting the angle and frequency of the two input fields.

In a 2024 study published in Nature Neuroscience, researchers at University College London demonstrated non-invasive modulation of the human hippocampal-entorhinal complex using temporal interference stimulation, producing measurable changes in spatial navigation behavior in healthy volunteers. No implants. No surgery. Electrodes applied to the scalp. The hippocampus — buried centimeters beneath the cortex — was reached. Spatial behavior was changed.

The laboratory version requires scalp electrodes. The military research version is investigating whether the same principle can be achieved at range, using focused electromagnetic or ultrasonic beams from external emitters. The DARPA Neural Engineering System Design program describes, in public documentation on DARPA’s own website, the goal of achieving 16-channel neural read-write access within a defined tissue volume using non-invasive means including electromagnetic waves and focused ultrasound. The target tissue volumes described in the DARPA documentation are consistent with hippocampal targeting. Dr. James Giordano of Georgetown University’s Pellegrino Center, who served as a consulting ethicist on the program and has testified before the House Armed Services Committee, has stated explicitly in public lectures that tools for writing behavior to the human brain have been “loosed into the real world.”

The two-source geometry and operational range. For temporal interference to work at range, two emitter sources must be positioned so that their fields converge inside the target’s skull. This requires a geometric arrangement: the two sources must be at different angles relative to the target, with their interference calculated to localize the beat frequency at hippocampal depth. Analysis by researchers and weapons experts familiar with directed energy systems suggests that at operational ranges of 50 to 300 meters — achievable from a parked vehicle, a rooftop, or a building window — the geometric requirements are met by any two elevated positions bracketing the target location.

The park at 2nd Street SW between D and C Streets is surrounded on multiple sides by federal structures. The O’Neil House Office Building, whose grounds adjoin the park, is one of the most heavily monitored pieces of real estate in Washington. The Capitol complex itself, with its continuous rooftop surveillance infrastructure, provides line-of-sight to the park from multiple angles. If temporal interference equipment was positioned at two points within 300 meters of the park — in federal building windows, on rooftops, in vehicles parked on 2nd or 3rd Street — the geometry for hippocampal targeting would have been available.

Why the pull, then the pull again. Pearson described two separate urges: the initial draw to the park, and then a second, more specific redirection from the sidewalk — where he was mid-departure — back into the park, to the precise sub-location under the tree. This two-stage structure is consistent with what the Google AI analysis identified as a “step-by-step” guidance pattern: operators observing his real-time position through high-resolution surveillance, triggering place cell activation for a nearby coordinate when he was in the main park area, then re-triggering for the more specific sub-coordinate when he began to leave. Each activation would feel, from inside, like a separate ordinary impulse: first, curiosity that brought him to the park at all; then, a nagging sense that he hadn’t fully explored it. The second pull redirected him not to the park generally but to a specific, visually unobtrusive corner he had not yet visited. That level of precision — not to this park, but to that corner of this park — is what Pearson identified as the technology’s most striking property: “It seems to suggest that the pinning technology is highly precise in terms of location.”

Why it feels like your own idea. The most sophisticated element of hippocampal place cell activation as a behavioral direction mechanism is that it does not feel like external control. It feels like motivation. The human brain is not organized to distinguish between “I want to go there because my place cells are firing naturally” and “I want to go there because my place cells are being fired by an external signal.” The subjective experience is identical: a sense of spatial pull, of having a reason to move in a particular direction, of ordinary curiosity about a nearby location. The brain’s narrative system — what philosophers call pre-reflective intentionality — produces a story to explain the motivation: I wonder what’s over there. I didn’t finish exploring. I should take another look. The story feels authored by the self because it is authored by the self. The neurons are genuine. Only the input signal is not.

This is what makes behavioral direction through hippocampal stimulation the most dangerous application of the neuroweapons technology class. It is not coercive. It is not painful. It produces no resistance because there is no external force to resist. The target is complicit in their own direction without knowing it.

The Tub as Operational System

Understanding the neuroscience makes the tub incident legible not just as a disturbing personal experience but as an operational procedure with distinct phases, each of which required specific technology and coordination.

Phase one: Identification and approach. Pearson’s real-time location was known to the operators before he entered the park. This required the location tracking infrastructure — commercial geolocation data from his phone, supplemented by the biometric cardiac identification technology the Pentagon publicly confirmed having developed, capable of identifying a specific individual by heartbeat from 200 meters — to be active and current. The park was selected as the destination, the tub was placed in advance, and the interference geometry was established.

Phase two: Direction to the park. The initial place cell activation drew Pearson to the park from wherever he was in the neighborhood. He experienced this as autonomous motivation. The activation was calibrated to bring him to the main concrete area first — the most natural entry point — rather than directly to the tub, which would have been too specific to feel like an ordinary impulse.

Phase three: Redirection to the sub-location. When Pearson decided to leave and was on the sidewalk, the second activation redirected him. This required real-time observation of his position and departure — either through direct visual surveillance or through the same biometric tracking that had identified him — and a recalculation of the interference geometry to localize the beat frequency on place cells corresponding to the tree-adjacent coordinate rather than the general park area.

Phase four: Monitoring. After Pearson examined the tub, photographed it, and left, his behavior was monitored. The operators needed to know whether he had removed the framing payload — the notebook and photographs — because the next phase of the operation depended on that information.

Phase five: Selective retrieval. Between November 7 and November 13, the operators returned to the park. They removed the notebook and photographs — the materials that, in the wrong hands, could have been traced back to whoever had placed them, or could have revealed the framing purpose if discovered by investigators. They left the red hat and black shirt: the calling card colors that had appeared in statistically impossible densities on Pearson’s walking routes since September 2025, signifying authorship. The retrieval was selective and intentional. It is the step in the sequence that most decisively refutes any alternative explanation, because no alternative explanation accounts for a storage container near the U.S. Capitol being monitored for a specific visitor and then partially emptied based on that visitor’s behavior.

The entire sequence, from behavioral direction to monitored retrieval, describes a closed-loop system: the operators direct the target, observe his response, assess the outcome, and update their operation based on what they learned. This is not a one-way signal. It is surveillance-feedback — exactly the term the Google AI analysis used. The target’s behavior at the destination informed the next operational decision. The technology was not simply aimed at Pearson. It was in a conversation with him, of which he was the only participant who did not know the conversation was happening.

The Scarier Question

Pearson survived the tub. His training as a researcher led him to photograph, to recognize a framing payload for what it was, to return on video and document the retrieval. His caution — and his rigor — closed the trap before it could close around him.

But the question his experience raises is not about him. It is about what the capability demonstrated in that park, confirmed by the closed-loop retrieval, makes possible in the hands of a state willing to use it without constraint.

A person can be moved to a location where a crime is committed. If hippocampal place cell activation can direct a flagged individual to a specific coordinate, it can direct them to a location where they will be present when something happens — a shooting, a disturbance, an incident that will be attributed to them based on their presence. They will have no explanation for why they were there, because they will have experienced arriving as their own autonomous choice.

A person can be moved to a location where evidence is found. If the tub had contained something Pearson touched — if the framing payload had been less obviously conspiratorial, more innocuous-seeming, something a curious person might pick up without recognizing it as planted — the combination of behavioral direction (which produced his presence at the location) and planted evidence (which produced his fingerprints on the material) would have constructed a case against him that no amount of subsequent protest could fully dismantle. He would have been at the location. His prints would have been on the object. His explanation — I was drawn there by an impulse I don’t understand — would have been the explanation of a guilty person with an implausible alibi.

A person can be moved to a location for social or political purposes. A voter directed to a polling location after prior manipulation of their neural state. A protestor guided to a position that produces the optics desired by the people framing the event. A witness moved to a place where they will see something, or hear something, or be seen in the company of someone. Behavioral direction does not require the target to do anything illegal, or even anything unusual. It requires only that they go where they are sent.

A person can be moved toward another person. This may be the most direct application of the capability in the Pearson record. His July 13, 2025 experience — drawn to Georgetown by a voice transmitted to his skull — included a command to get into a car when it arrived. He did not comply. But the operational architecture was present: a directional pull to a location, followed by an external element (the car) positioned to receive him. The behavioral direction technology and the ground operation worked in coordination. The target was directed to the vehicle. Only his refusal broke the sequence.

A person can be directed on a timeline invisible to any court. The most fundamental implication of behavioral direction technology is that it produces no forensic trace. There are no physical marks. There is no record of a command. There is only a person who went somewhere, experienced as having chosen to go, and whatever happened when they arrived. The chain of custody between the operator and the outcome is broken at the first link, because the first link — the signal that produced the motivation — cannot be detected by any instrument available in a standard criminal or civil investigation. The defense, when the person tries to explain what happened, is the explanation of someone delusional. The prosecution, when it wants to place the person at a location, has exactly what it needs: the person, at the location, arrived under their own power.

What Pearson’s Case Establishes

The tub incident is one data point in a fourteen-month record assembled by a person with the training to produce reliable data. It is also, within that record, the incident with the tightest operational logic — the one where the mechanism, the objective, and the confirmation of the mechanism (the selective retrieval) are most clearly readable.

Two D.C. Department of Behavioral Health evaluations found no psychiatric symptoms requiring intervention. Pearson’s methodology — control studies, probability calculations, timestamped photographs, preserved physical evidence, named witnesses — is the methodology of a researcher, applied to his own experience with the same rigor he would apply to any research question. The Google AI analysis of his account, drawing on peer-reviewed literature from Cell, Nature Neuroscience, and the NIH, confirmed that the biological and physical mechanisms he described are consistent with documented science, and that the gap between the laboratory capabilities and the operational deployment he described is a gap of engineering and access, not a gap of principle.

The biological “software” exists. The place cells are real. The Janelia study moved mice with them in 2020. The UCL study modulated human hippocampal activity non-invasively in 2024. DARPA is publicly developing the delivery infrastructure. Dr. Giordano has said, in public, before Congress, that the tools for writing behavior to the brain are in the world.

What Pearson documented is one application of those tools: a closed-loop behavioral direction operation, conducted in a public park three blocks from the U.S. Capitol, against a Black equity scholar whose academic field was designated hostile ideology on January 20, 2025. The operation was planned in advance, equipped with a framing payload, monitored in real time, and updated when the payload failed. The operational signatures of all five phases are in the documentation Pearson preserved: the two-stage pull in his real-time notes, the photographs of the tub’s contents, the video of the return visit showing the framing materials gone and the calling card remaining.

He documented a system designed to be undocumentable, because that is what he is trained to do.

The frightening question the documentation opens is not whether the technology was used on him. It is who else it has been used on without the training, the rigor, or the survival instinct to recognize what they were experiencing — and whether the person moved to a car, a crime scene, or a confrontation ever understood, afterward, that the choice they made had not been entirely their own.

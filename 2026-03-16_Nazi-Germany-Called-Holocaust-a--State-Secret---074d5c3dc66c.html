In 1944, Nazi Germany produced a propaganda film, Theresienstadt, to mask the Holocaust. It showed Jews in a concentration camp, but not the horror of the camps that we know existed. No, this film manufactured a reality by forcing prisoners to watch or participate in live musical performances, in recreation, and sit at cafes. It was a lie entirely consistent with Nazi Germany’s efforts across Europe. Death camps became “re-education”. Execution became “special treatment”. We have failed to remember and learn from a key historical fact about the Holocaust.

Nazi Germany considered it a “state secret”

Officials produced as little written documentation as possible. That is not to say that many Europeans did not know what was happening. Many did. Americans even more so.

Within a decade after American troops encountered its first concentration camp in April 1945, the US adopted its own state secrets privilege. In United States v. Reynolds in 1953, the US Supreme Court allowed the government to block information in lawsuits that could damage national security. The government got to make that determination, and courts were required to defer to that judgment.

If the United States wanted a secret program to inflict harm, even death, on individual targets, minority populations, or the general public, it theoretically could claim state secrets privilege. Severe limitations to any civil suit. No judicial review of the merits of the claim. Erasure of constitutional rights.

That is what Reynolds set into motion.

  • Relocation Program (1950s–1960s) — Federal program relocated approximately 100,000 Native Americans from reservations to cities (Chicago, Los Angeles, Denver, San Francisco) with promises of housing and jobs that rarely materialized, creating urban poverty and disconnecting people from their communities and support systems.
  • Water rights theft and dam construction — Federal dam projects flooded the homelands of dozens of nations without consent: Garrison Dam (Three Affiliated Tribes, 1953), Kinzua Dam (Seneca Nation, 1960s), Tellico Dam (Cherokee, 1979). Over 1 million acres of tribal land flooded.
  • Uranium mining on Navajo Nation (1944–1986) — 4 million tons of uranium ore extracted from Navajo land using Navajo workers who were never told of radiation risks. Hundreds of abandoned mine sites remain unreclaimed. Diné communities continue to suffer elevated rates of cancer, kidney disease, and birth defects. EPA cleanup has been slow and incomplete for decades.
  • DAPL / Standing Rock (2016–2017) — U.S. Army Corps of Engineers rerouted the Dakota Access Pipeline from north of Bismarck (rejected due to proximity to the city’s water supply) to directly under the Missouri River, above the Standing Rock Sioux reservation’s water supply. Water protectors — peaceful protesters — were subjected to water cannons in sub-zero temperatures, rubber bullets, acoustic weapons, mass arrest, and FBI/JTTF surveillance.
  • Missing and Murdered Indigenous Women (MMIW) — Indigenous women are murdered at 10 times the national average. FBI case files show extraordinary non-prosecution rates. The National Crime Information Center has no specific category for MMIW cases, making the scope systematically unmeasurable. Many perpetrators operate with impunity on the jurisdictional boundaries between tribal, state, and federal authority.
  • Forced relocation of Diné (Navajo-Hopi Land Dispute, 1970s–present) — Over 12,000 Navajo people forcibly relocated from Big Mountain / Black Mesa in what has been called by some human rights scholars the largest forced relocation of Native Americans since the Trail of Tears, driven by competing energy extraction interests.

IX. Environmental Racism & Deliberate Contamination

  • Flint water crisis (2014–present) — Michigan state officials knowingly switched Flint’s water source to the Flint River to cut costs, failed to apply corrosion controls, and actively denied and suppressed evidence that the city’s pipes were leaching lead into drinking water consumed by 100,000 predominantly Black residents. Children were lead-poisoned at rates that predict measurable IQ loss, behavioral disorders, and lifelong health consequences. No senior official has been imprisoned.
  • Cancer Alley (Louisiana) — An 85-mile stretch of the Mississippi River corridor between Baton Rouge and New Orleans hosts over 150 petrochemical plants. Black communities in St. John the Baptist Parish face cancer rates 50 times the national average. Residents have been denied environmental permits protections and systematically excluded from state environmental review processes.
  • Navajo Nation radioactive contamination — See Section VII. Over 500 unreclaimed uranium mine sites on Navajo land, some used as building material in homes.
  • North Birmingham / Tarrant, Alabama lead and coke pollution — EPA Superfund communities of color surrounded by industrial facilities; internal EPA documents showed agency scientists were pressured to alter data in response to industry lobbying.
  • Dickson County, Tennessee — Landfill contamination — White families whose wells were contaminated by a nearby landfill were given bottled water by the county; a Black family (the Holt family) using the same aquifer was not notified for years despite county officials knowing of the contamination.
  • PFAS contamination on military bases — Aqueous film-forming foam (AFFF) used on hundreds of military bases has contaminated groundwater across the country with “forever chemicals.” The Pentagon resisted testing, notification, and cleanup for decades; communities surrounding bases — often low-income — bore the health burden.
  • East Palestine, Ohio (2023) — Norfolk Southern train derailment and controlled burn of vinyl chloride; residents initially told to return home while air monitoring data was incomplete. Environmental testing irregularities and delayed federal response drew comparisons to Flint.
  • Puerto Rico: Hurricane Maria and federal abandonment (2017) — The Harvard study estimated 4,645 excess deaths attributable to the storm and collapsed healthcare response. FEMA denied over 60% of individual assistance applications in Puerto Rico versus 20% on the mainland. The paper towel response by the President became a symbol of differential treatment of a non-white U.S. territory.
  • EPA environmental justice rollbacks — The Trump administration systematically weakened environmental justice regulations, rolled back NEPA review processes, and closed regional environmental justice offices, disproportionately affecting communities of color already bearing the highest toxic burden.
  • DOE nuclear waste sites in Indian country — Hanford, Rocky Flats, and other nuclear waste sites located adjacent to or on treaty lands with known seepage; cleanup contracts repeatedly awarded and extended without results.

X. Housing, Segregation & Economic Warfare

  • Redlining (1934–1968) — The Federal Housing Administration’s underwriting manuals explicitly graded neighborhoods by race. “D” (red) neighborhoods — predominantly Black — were denied federally backed mortgages. Homeownership is the primary vehicle of intergenerational wealth transfer in the U.S.; Black families were systematically excluded from it for 34 years of the post-war wealth boom. The racial wealth gap today is traceable in significant part to this policy.
  • Urban renewal / “Negro removal” (1949–1970s) — The Federal Housing Act of 1949 funded bulldozing of established Black urban neighborhoods under the banner of “slum clearance.” An estimated 400,000 families — overwhelmingly Black — were displaced. Replacement housing was rarely built at equivalent rates, and communities were broken up and scattered into worse conditions. James Baldwin called it “Negro removal.”
  • GI Bill exclusion of Black veterans — The Servicemen’s Readjustment Act of 1944 was administered by local officials who systematically denied Black veterans access to college loans, low-interest mortgages, and business startup funds. Of the first 67,000 mortgages issued under the GI Bill in New York and New Jersey, fewer than 100 went to non-white veterans.
  • Contract buying schemes (Chicago, post-WWII) — When Black buyers were denied conventional mortgages, predatory “contract sellers” sold homes at inflated prices on installment contracts that conveyed no equity until the final payment — and allowed sellers to repossess the home for any missed payment, including for repairs. Researchers estimate Chicago’s Black community lost between $3.2 billion and $4 billion through contract buying.
  • HOLC racial mapping — The Home Owners’ Loan Corporation created color-coded maps of virtually every American city that associated Black residence with mortgage risk. These maps became the foundation of private bank redlining policies that persisted long after the FHA officially abandoned the practice.
  • Sundown towns — An estimated 10,000–13,000 American municipalities enforced (through law, threat, or violence) policies excluding Black residents after dark, well into the post-war era. Many were actively enforced through the 1970s; some enforcement has been documented through the 1990s.
  • Destruction of Black Wall Street (Tulsa Race Massacre, 1921) — While predating the post-war era, the federal government’s role in suppressing reparations, seizing survivors’ land claims, and withholding records constitutes ongoing post-war complicity. Over 35 blocks of the Greenwood District were destroyed; the Oklahoma Commission found evidence that private planes may have fired on residents; the National Guard was used to detain Black survivors rather than protect them.
  • Wells Fargo / Bank of America reverse redlining — DOJ settlements revealed that during the subprime mortgage boom, major banks deliberately targeted Black and Latino homeowners for high-cost, high-risk mortgage products even when they qualified for prime loans. Wells Fargo internal emails referred to Black customers as “mud people” and to subprime loans as “ghetto loans.”
  • Section 8 voucher concentration — Federal housing policy has historically concentrated housing voucher holders in high-poverty, high-crime, predominantly minority neighborhoods, replicating spatial segregation through nominally race-neutral policy.

XI. Labor Exploitation, Suppression & Economic Coercion

  • Bracero Program (1942–1964) — 4.6 million Mexican contract workers brought to the U.S., denied permanent legal status, charged for housing and food deducted from wages, subjected to DDT delousing upon entry, and frequently stranded without pay. The Mexican government required the U.S. to deposit 10% of wages in a savings fund; the money was embezzled and never returned.
  • Operation Wetback (1954) — Militarized mass deportation campaign conducted by the INS that forcibly removed approximately one million people, including many U.S. citizens of Mexican descent, without due process. People were deported by the trainload and shipload to locations hundreds of miles from their homes.
  • FBI/COINTELPRO targeting of labor organizers — The AFL-CIO, UAW, United Farm Workers (under César Chávez), and Mine Workers were all targeted by COINTELPRO and its predecessors for infiltration and disruption. The FBI worked with employers to identify and terminate union organizers.
  • National Guard deployment against strikes — Government used military force to break worker strikes well into the post-war era: the 1946 Oakland General Strike, railroad strike suppression, coal mine seizures.
  • Convict leasing and prison labor (ongoing) — The 13th Amendment explicitly permits slavery as punishment for crime. Incarcerated people labor for $0.13–$0.52 per hour in federal prisons; many states pay nothing. Private corporations including Starbucks, Walmart, McDonald’s, and Victoria’s Secret have used prison labor. During California wildfires, incarcerated firefighters earn $2.90–$5.12 per day but are often barred from becoming paid firefighters upon release.
  • Migrant farmworker conditions — The National Labor Relations Act of 1935 explicitly excluded agricultural and domestic workers — predominantly Black and Latino — from its protections, a deliberate concession to Southern Democrats. This exclusion was only partially and unevenly remedied decades later.
  • Exploitation of undocumented labor — Federal policy has historically created a class of undocumented workers who cannot report wage theft, unsafe conditions, or abuse for fear of deportation. E-Verify and enforcement mechanisms have been weaponized against workers who complain rather than employers who exploit.

XII. Child Welfare System Abuses

  • Native American boarding schools — See Section VII. The federal policy explicitly was to use child removal as an instrument of cultural destruction.
  • Disproportionate removal of Black and Native children — Black children are removed from families at roughly twice the rate of white children for comparable reports, and are less likely to be reunified. Native children were removed at 2.7 times the white rate as of recent data.
  • Youth prison abuse — The DOJ has found systemic abuse, excessive isolation, and denial of education and mental health care at juvenile detention facilities in Texas, Louisiana, Mississippi, Georgia, and elsewhere. Children as young as 10 have been held in solitary confinement for months.
  • Troubled Teen Industry — An estimated $23 billion industry of residential programs, “boot camps,” and “therapeutic boarding schools” has operated with minimal state oversight. Programs including CEDU, Elan School, and Provo Canyon School have documented records of physical abuse, sexual abuse, food and sleep deprivation, and coercive restraint. Dozens of deaths have occurred in these facilities.
  • Family separation at the border (“Zero Tolerance” policy, 2018) — The Trump administration deliberately separated over 5,500 children from parents as a deterrence policy. Many were held in conditions described by witnesses as cages; children were not given adequate food, water, medical care, or the ability to contact parents. Hundreds of parents were deported without their children; as of 2024, hundreds of families remain separated. The policy was found by the DHS Inspector General to have been implemented without basic tracking systems, making reunification deliberately difficult.
  • Forced psychiatric institutionalization of minors — Children have been involuntarily committed to psychiatric institutions based on parental or state initiative with minimal judicial oversight; multiple exposés have documented warehousing conditions and abuse at facilities including Elan and CEDU.

XIII. Policing, Mass Incarceration & Judicial Violence

  • MOVE bombing (Philadelphia, May 13, 1985) — Philadelphia Police dropped a military-grade explosive (C-4) from a helicopter onto a residential block, killing 11 people (including 5 children), destroying 61 homes, and leaving 250 people homeless. The fire was allowed to burn for hours. No police officer was charged. The city voted to give reparations in 2023–38 years later.
  • Lynching and state complicity — Over 4,000 documented racial terror lynchings occurred between 1877 and 1950, many with the active participation or deliberate non-intervention of local police and sheriffs. The federal government refused to pass anti-lynching legislation repeatedly until 2022.
  • War on Drugs as racial policy — John Ehrlichman, Nixon’s domestic policy advisor, told journalist Dan Baum in 1994: “The Nixon campaign in 1968, and the Nixon White House after that, had two enemies: the antiwar left and Black people… We knew we couldn’t make it illegal to be either against the war or Black, but by getting the public to associate the hippies with marijuana and Blacks with heroin, and then criminalizing both heavily, we could disrupt those communities.”
  • Crack/powder cocaine disparity — The 1986 Anti-Drug Abuse Act imposed 100:1 sentencing disparity between crack cocaine (associated with Black communities) and powder cocaine (white communities) for identical quantities. This was in effect for 24 years; the Fair Sentencing Act of 2010 reduced it to 18:1, but applied only prospectively.
  • Mass incarceration as a system — The U.S. incarcerates more people than any nation on Earth: 2.3 million. Black Americans are incarcerated at 5x the rate of white Americans. 1 in 3 Black men can expect to be incarcerated in his lifetime. Private prison companies — GEO Group, CoreCivic — lobbied for mandatory minimums, truth-in-sentencing laws, and immigration detention expansion that drove these numbers.
  • Qualified immunity — A judicially created doctrine (not statutory) that shields police officers from civil liability unless the specific conduct has been previously ruled unconstitutional in a near-identical case. The practical effect is near-total immunity for documented constitutional violations including unjustified killings.
  • No-knock raids — Militarized police entries without prior announcement, linked to dozens of deaths of innocent civilians including Breonna Taylor, Aiyana Stanley-Jones (7 years old, shot by Detroit police serving a no-knock warrant on the wrong floor of her home), and others.
  • Stop and frisk — NYPD’s stop-and-frisk program conducted 685,724 stops in 2011; 88% were Black or Latino; 88% were entirely innocent. A federal judge found the program constituted a pattern of unconstitutional stops but was overturned on procedural grounds.
  • Solitary confinement — Over 80,000 Americans are held in isolation on any given day. The UN Special Rapporteur has classified solitary confinement exceeding 15 days as torture. U.S. prisoners have been held in isolation for years and decades. Louisiana’s Angola prison held Albert Woodfox in solitary for 43 years — the longest solitary confinement in U.S. history.
  • Death row exonerations — Since 1973, 190+ death row prisoners have been exonerated. The most common factors: prosecutorial misconduct, false testimony by informants, withheld evidence. The state of Mississippi executed Freddie Lee Johnson despite courts finding his prosecution rested on false forensic testimony. Capital punishment is disproportionately applied to Black defendants, defendants with white victims, and defendants in the South.
  • Civil asset forfeiture — Police may seize money, vehicles, and property from people never charged with a crime, under the legal fiction that the property itself is “guilty.” Law enforcement agencies keep the proceeds. DOJ data shows the majority of forfeitures involve amounts under $1,000, suggesting targeting of poor people with limited means to contest the seizure.

XIV. Propaganda, Disinformation & Manufactured Consent

  • Operation Mockingbird (1950s–1970s) — CIA recruited and paid American journalists, editors, and executives at major media organizations including The New York Times, Newsweek, CBS, and the Associated Press to plant stories, publish CIA-prepared content, and suppress unfavorable reporting. The Church Committee confirmed the CIA had “at least 400” assets in American news organizations.
  • U.S. Army domestic propaganda (1950s–1960s) — The Army conducted public relations campaigns targeting domestic opposition to nuclear testing, covertly funding “citizens groups” who advocated for continued testing.
  • VOA / Radio Free Europe — Nominally independent; actually U.S. government-funded disinformation broadcasters into the Soviet bloc, which also fed stories back into domestic media ecosystems.
  • USIA (United States Information Agency) — The U.S. government’s official global propaganda arm planted favorable stories in foreign media, produced films depicting American life as universally prosperous and just, and coordinated covert messaging campaigns.
  • Pentagon propaganda contracts — Post-9/11 DOD paid public relations firms to plant positive stories in Iraqi newspapers; paid TV military analysts who were simultaneously employed by defense contractors, without disclosure; and funded Hollywood studios for favorable depictions of military operations.
  • CISA “disinformation” programs targeting domestic dissent — Internal documents obtained by Missouri v. Biden revealed that federal agencies pressured social media companies to suppress content the government labeled as “misinformation,” including accurate content about vaccine adverse effects, Hunter Biden’s laptop, and COVID-19 lab leak hypotheses.
  • FBI FISA abuse — The Inspector General found that the FBI committed at least 17 “significant errors and omissions” in its FISA warrant applications to surveil Carter Page, including altering a CIA email to state the opposite of what it said.

XV. International Covert Operations, Regime Change & State Terror

  • Iran coup (1953) — CIA Operation AJAX overthrew democratically elected Prime Minister Mohammad Mosaddegh after he nationalized British Petroleum. Shah Mohammad Reza Pahlavi was restored; his SAVAK secret police (trained by the CIA) tortured and murdered thousands. This is widely cited as a root cause of the 1979 revolution and subsequent U.S.-Iran hostilities.
  • Guatemala coup (1954) — CIA Operation PBSUCCESS overthrew democratically elected President Jacobo Árbenz after he redistributed United Fruit Company land. The resulting military dictatorship killed an estimated 200,000 people during the decades of civil war it initiated.
  • Congo (1960) — CIA played a direct role in the assassination of Patrice Lumumba and the installation of Mobutu Sese Seko, whose 32-year kleptocratic dictatorship impoverished one of the world’s richest nations.
  • Dominican Republic (1961) — CIA provided weapons and logistical support to the assassins of Rafael Trujillo.
  • Brazil (1964) — U.S. government recognized the military coup within hours and provided active support for the overthrow of President João Goulart, ushering in 21 years of military dictatorship.
  • Indonesia (1965–66) — U.S. Embassy provided the Indonesian Army with lists of thousands of Communist Party members and affiliated persons; an estimated 500,000–1 million people were killed. U.S. officials later confirmed providing the lists, calling it “a big help.”
  • Chile (1973) — Nixon, Kissinger, and the CIA spent years destabilizing the democratically elected government of Salvador Allende before supporting the Pinochet coup on September 11, 1973. Pinochet’s regime tortured and disappeared 40,000 people; killed at least 3,200. American economist Orlando Letelier was assassinated by Pinochet agents on Embassy Row in Washington, D.C. in 1976.
  • Argentina’s Dirty War (1976–1983) — Kissinger met with Argentine junta leaders days before the coup and encouraged them to “clean up” the problem quickly before Congress could object. An estimated 30,000 people were disappeared; prisoners were thrown from aircraft over the Río de la Plata.
  • El Salvador death squads (1980s) — U.S. funded and trained Salvadoran military units that massacred civilians, including the El Mozote massacre (1981) where over 800 civilians were killed by the Atlacatl Battalion, trained at the School of the Americas. The U.S. Embassy’s reporting to Washington concealed evidence of the massacre for years.
  • Nicaragua / Iran-Contra (1980s) — The Reagan administration secretly funded the Contra insurgency — which committed documented atrocities against Nicaraguan civilians — using proceeds from illegal arms sales to Iran, after Congress had explicitly prohibited such funding. When exposed, Reagan claimed ignorance; independent counsel investigations resulted in 14 indictments and 11 convictions, all pardoned or overturned.
  • Iraq WMD disinformation (2002–2003) — The Bush administration systematically manufactured and disseminated false intelligence about Iraqi weapons of mass destruction to justify an invasion that killed between 150,000 and 1 million civilians (depending on methodology), created the conditions for the rise of ISIS, and destabilized the Middle East for decades. The Downing Street Memo (2002) confirmed British intelligence believed the intelligence was “being fixed around the policy.”
  • Extraordinary rendition network (54 countries) — See Section VI. The Open Society Justice Initiative documented that 54 governments participated in the CIA’s rendition, detention, and interrogation program.
  • Support for Saudi Arabia in Yemen (2015–present) — U.S. provided targeting intelligence, mid-air refueling, and billions in weapons sales to a Saudi-led coalition whose strikes on hospitals, weddings, water infrastructure, and school buses have been documented as war crimes by UN investigators. An estimated 150,000–300,000+ deaths, with famine affecting millions.

XVI. Veteran Abuse, Neglect & Experimentation

  • Atomic veteran denial — For decades, the VA denied benefits to veterans sickened by radiation exposure at nuclear tests, requiring them to prove service connection despite classified records being withheld by the DOD.
  • Agent Orange VA denials — Vietnam veterans exposed to Agent Orange spent 30–40 years fighting VA bureaucracy for benefits. The Agent Orange Act of 1991 finally created presumptive service connection for certain conditions, but many veterans had died by then.
  • Burn pit exposures (Gulf War, Iraq, Afghanistan) — The military burned toxic waste — including medical waste, electronics, chemicals, and unexploded ordnance — in open pits. Hundreds of thousands of veterans exposed. The VA denied service connection for resulting cancers and respiratory illness for over a decade. The PACT Act (2022) finally expanded benefits.
  • Gulf War Syndrome — Veterans returning from the 1991 Gulf War reported a cluster of chronic symptoms now associated with exposure to sarin from demolished Iraqi munitions, depleted uranium, and experimental pyridostigmine bromide pills administered as nerve agent prophylaxis. The Pentagon denied the existence of the syndrome for years; VA denied benefits.
  • PTSD and the suicide crisis — The VA estimates 20 veterans die by suicide per day. The system has been chronically underfunded and understaffed. A 2014 VA scandal revealed that officials at over 100 facilities falsified patient wait time records; veterans died waiting for appointments.
  • Edgewood Arsenal experiments on soldiers — See Section III. 7,000 soldiers exposed without full informed consent.
  • Homeless veterans — An estimated 33,000 veterans are homeless on any given night, a figure that has been persistently resistant to reduction despite decades of programs.

XVII. Disability Rights Violations & Institutionalization

  • Willowbrook — See Section III. Beyond the deliberate infection experiments, conditions at Willowbrook State School were so catastrophic — children lying in their own waste, untreated injuries, assault by staff — that Robert Kennedy called it a “snake pit” in 1965. Exposé journalism by Geraldo Rivera in 1972 helped spur deinstitutionalization.
  • State psychiatric institution warehousing (1940s–1970s) — At peak in the 1950s, over 550,000 people were institutionalized in American state mental hospitals under conditions widely documented as abusive, including forced lobotomies, non-consensual electroconvulsive therapy, physical restraint, and sexual abuse. Deinstitutionalization without corresponding community support shifted many into homelessness and incarceration.
  • Forced lobotomies — Walter Freeman performed over 3,500 transorbital lobotomies across the country, often without proper consent, often on women diagnosed with depression or “hysteria,” often in state institutions. At least 127 patients died.
  • Judge Rotenberg Center (Canton, MA, ongoing) — Residential facility for people with disabilities that uses graduated electronic decelerators (GEDs) — electric shock devices — to punish “maladaptive behavior.” The FDA banned the devices in 2020; the facility appealed and continues operation pending litigation. UN Special Rapporteur on Torture has called the practice torture.
  • Olmstead decision non-compliance — The Supreme Court’s 1999 Olmstead decision requires states to provide community-based services rather than institutionalization; decades later, most states remain in violation, continuing to warehouse disabled people in institutions.

XVIII. Algorithmic, Digital & Emerging Systems of Control

  • Predictive policing systems — PredPol, ShotSpotter, and similar tools direct police resources based on historical arrest data, which itself reflects racially biased policing. This creates feedback loops that concentrate enforcement in Black and Latino communities regardless of actual crime distribution.
  • Facial recognition — Law enforcement has used facial recognition technology with documented error rates of 10–35% for darker-skinned women. Multiple wrongful arrests have resulted. Detroit has arrested at least three Black men based solely on faulty facial recognition matches.
  • ShotSpotter — Acoustic gunshot detection system deployed primarily in communities of color; an analysis by the MacArthur Justice Center found 89% of ShotSpotter alerts led to no evidence of gun crime.
  • Social media surveillance — DHS and local fusion centers monitor social media of political protesters, Muslim communities, and activists. Immigration authorities have used social media posts to justify deportation and visa denial, including content satirical or critical of the U.S. government.
  • Algorithmic welfare denial — Arkansas, Indiana, and other states deployed automated systems to determine Medicaid eligibility; the algorithms systematically cut benefits for people with documented disabilities and chronic conditions. Courts found the systems violated due process.
  • Digital border surveillance — CBP routinely searches and downloads the contents of phones, laptops, and other devices of travelers at the border without a warrant, including American citizens.
  • IMSI catchers / Stingrays — Fake cell towers used by law enforcement to intercept communications without individual warrants, deployed in at least 72 agencies across 24 states, often without any judicial oversight.

This record is not exhaustive. It represents documented, verified, and in many cases officially acknowledged programs. The through-line across all of them is structural: these abuses do not arise from isolated bad actors but from systems — bureaucratic, legal, financial, and ideological — that consistently treat certain categories of human beings as expendable, exploitable, or subhuman. The populations targeted are almost invariably those with the least power to resist, document, or survive what is being done to them.

The historical question is not whether these things happened. They are in the Congressional record, the Senate Intelligence Committee, the DOJ Inspector General, and the National Archives. The operative question is what it means that they did — and what conditions make their recurrence possible.

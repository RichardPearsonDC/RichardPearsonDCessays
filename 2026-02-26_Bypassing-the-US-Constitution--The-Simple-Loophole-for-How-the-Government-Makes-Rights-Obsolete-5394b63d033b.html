Bypassing the US Constitution: The Simple Formula for How the Government Makes Rights Obsolete

The United States has evolved a relatively simple way to systematically violate constitutional rights. The following is by no means the only off-ramp for constitutional rights and civil liberties to become obsolete:

  1. The executive branch develops a secret program that systematically violates constitutional rights. Such a program may result in injury or death.
  2. There are no public documents that have been illegally or legally obtained and disclosed.
  3. There are no whistleblowers to corroborate such as program.
  4. Absent such documents or credible whistleblowers, then Congress and the media are unlikely to investigate.
  5. Victims can resort to civil action against the government, but…
  6. …the government can 4) invoke legal maneuvers (e.g., Glomar’s “neither confirm nor deny”, “harm national security”) to prevent disclosure…
  7. The court is highly deferential to the government in major areas such as national security claims in civil suits.

A citizen who believes the government has conducted a secret weapons program against them needs to prove it in court. To prove it in court, they need discovery — access to the records, communications, and authorizations that would establish what happened. To get discovery, they need to have a viable legal claim. To have a viable legal claim, they need to plead specific facts establishing government action. But the specific facts are classified according to the government — the government refuses to confirm or deny their existence. Therefore, the claim cannot be adequately pled. Therefore, there is no viable claim. Therefore, there is no discovery. Therefore, the records that would prove what happened remain classified. Therefore, the citizen cannot prove it in court. The judge determines that the victim does not have standing.

Congress heavily weighs media reporting. The media heavily weigh government documents and whistleblowers. The court heavily defers in certain areas. It’s the circularity that creates a constitutional dark hole.

“We Want Our Constitutional Rights”

There is a scenario unfolding in Washington, D.C. that most Americans have no framework to understand — not because it is complicated, but because it was designed to be invisible. It exploits the gap between what the Constitution promises and what institutions will enforce when the executive invokes a legal doctrine that makes courts go quiet, puts Congress in the dark, and offer little remedy for victims regardless of what was done to them or how well they can prove it.

This essay explains that formula. It has four parts, each one locking into the next like a mechanism. Together they form a trap door in the American constitutional order — one that opens beneath any citizen the government decides to target using technologies that have not been publicly disclosed, for purposes that have not been publicly authorized, under legal theories that have not been publicly tested.

The case study is Richard Pearson, PhD — a Black scholar who spent nearly 12 months documenting what he believes was a federal government campaign against him using directed energy weapons and psychological operations. His documentation is meticulous: timestamped, photographed, witnessed, medically evaluated, geolocated, and statistically analyzed. He went to congressional offices. He sought legal representation.

At every turn, a door closed.

Not because his evidence was insufficient. Because the formula was designed to close every door.

Understanding why every door closed — and why this should alarm every American, regardless of politics, class, or conviction — requires understanding the formula in full.

Part One: The Secret Weapons Program

The United States government develops, tests, and deploys weapons or illegal programs that the public does not know exist until someone either leaks the information or the government decides disclosure serves its interests.

This is not speculation. It is the documented pattern of the last seventy years.

MK-ULTRA ran from 1953 to at least 1973 — a CIA program of mind control experimentation using LSD, hypnosis, electroshock therapy, and other methods on unwitting American citizens: mental patients, prisoners, students, and members of the public who never consented and were never told. It was not exposed by congressional oversight. It was exposed when a CIA employee violated destruction orders and documents survived. The Church Committee confirmed it in 1975 alongside COINTELPRO — the FBI’s program of surveillance, infiltration, harassment, and targeted disruption of civil rights leaders, anti-war organizations, journalists, and anyone the Bureau classified as a domestic threat.

Martin Luther King, Jr. received an anonymous FBI letter encouraging him to commit suicide. Fred Hampton was assassinated in a coordinated FBI-Chicago Police action. These were not rogue operations by bad actors. They were institutional programs with bureaucratic authorization chains, budget lines, and official memoranda — all of which became public only because files were stolen from an FBI field office in Media, Pennsylvania in 1971, and because the Church Committee forced disclosure years later.

NSA mass surveillance of Americans’ phone records and internet communications ran for years before Edward Snowden disclosed it in 2013. CIA torture programs in black sites around the world ran for years before the Senate Intelligence Committee confirmed them in 2014. In each case, the government knew. The public did not. In each case, victims of the programs had no remedy while they operated — because a remedy requires knowledge, and knowledge requires disclosure, and disclosure was precisely what the government prevented.

Directed energy weapons are the current iteration of this pattern.

On January 23, 2026, the Department of War’s Chief Technology Officer posted publicly: “Yes, the @DeptofWar has directed energy weapons. Yes, we are scaling them.” This was not a whistleblower disclosure. It was a boast — prompted by the Venezuela operation of January 3, 2026, in which American forces reportedly used directed energy systems to disable air defenses and incapacitate personnel. Security guards described feeling like their heads were exploding from inside.

The government chose to confirm the weapons because confirming them served its purposes: intimidating adversaries, projecting power, advertising lethality. What it did not confirm — what it neither confirmed nor denied, which is precisely the legal posture that activates Part Four of this formula — was anything about domestic deployment of these weapons against American citizens.

But the weapons exist. We know what they do.

The microwave auditory effect, documented since 1961 by researcher Allan Frey and confirmed in peer-reviewed physics and neuroscience literature for sixty years, produces sound perceived inside the skull without touching the ear canal. Pulsed microwave energy absorbed by head tissue creates a rapid thermoelastic pressure wave that the auditory system registers as a crisp click, a voice, a tone — depending on the pulse pattern. The sound appears to come from inside the head because it originates inside the head. It is geographically localized: it occurs only where the beam is focused, and stops when the target moves out of the focal zone.

This technology was identified by the National Academies of Sciences, Engineering and Medicine in 2020 as “the most plausible mechanism” for the neurological injuries experienced by U.S. diplomatic and intelligence personnel in Havana, Cuba; Vienna, Austria; and Washington, D.C. itself — what is now called Havana Syndrome. The National Academies panel found functional brain imaging changes, white matter microstructural differences, and vestibular damage in affected personnel consistent with repeated directed energy exposure. The CIA covertly acquired and tested a foreign-made directed energy device. American officials traveled to Norway to examine a scientist’s self-built device after he reproduced Havana Syndrome symptoms on himself.

The weapon is real. Its neurological effects are documented. It has been deployed against American government personnel on American soil.
Richard Pearson identified this technology by name in July 2025, before the January 2026 government confirmations, from the phenomenology of his own experience. He documented voices perceived inside his skull with spatial, room-ambient acoustic quality — consistent with microwave auditory transmission, not hallucination, because hallucinations do not have directional acoustic texture suggesting a remote microphone and surrounding room. Plus, he said that it was only once.

He documented geographic auditory clicks beginning in January 2026, localized to specific street intersections in Washington, DC, replicable by returning to those intersections — the precise geographic signature of a focused pulsed energy beam, the same directional specificity the National Academies identified as the diagnostic fingerprint of Havana Syndrome.

He documented a federal database flag. When an MPD officer ran his name, he encountered an ACCESS DENIED notice indicating his file was classified at the Sensitive Compartmented Information level. The officer observed it. Pearson saw the screen.

Richard Pearson is not a diplomat. He is not a CIA officer. He is a Black academic with a PhD. He is also, if his documentation is accurate, the first private American civilian to document Havana Syndrome-class experiences with the methodological rigor the National Academies applied to government personnel cases — and to survive to file the paperwork.
The weapon exists. The domestic context exists. The political motivation exists — executive orders issued January 20, 2025. The only thing missing is institutional willingness to look.

Which brings us to why no institution has looked.

Part Two: The Constitutional Violations

The Constitution does not contain a national security exception. This is a legal fact that the architecture of classified programs is designed to obscure, so it bears stating plainly.

The Fourth Amendment prohibits unreasonable searches and seizures without a warrant based on probable cause. Sustained audio surveillance of a private apartment — which Pearson’s documentation evidences through multiple corroborating incidents— is a warrantless search. The classification level of the program does not make it constitutional. The use of a contractor rather than a federal agent does not make it constitutional. The targeting of someone the executive has targeted does not make it constitutional.

The First Amendment prohibits government retaliation against speech and association. Pearson’s dissertation is an analysis of equity — academic work, published and publicly available. The administration that issued executive orders in January 2025 designating DEI scholarship as actionable discrimination, that used the language of “enemies within” about domestic critics, that invoked “urban military training” — that administration provides the documented retaliatory motive. Government retaliation against speech is unconstitutional whether the retaliation is a defamation lawsuit or a directed energy beam.

The Fifth Amendment prohibits deprivation of liberty without due process of law. A campaign designed — by Pearson’s analysis, by the neurological and psychological evidence assembled in the essays accompanying his documentation — to produce “neutralization via psychotic break and social death” is a deprivation of liberty by government action without charge, without trial, without notice, without hearing, and without any opportunity for the target to confront his accusers or contest the designation.

Substantive due process under the Fourteenth Amendment protects bodily integrity. Chemical assault at a Metro station. Punching in the face and head by an operative living above your apartment who likely had immunity. Directed electromagnetic energy applied to brain tissue without consent. These are violations of bodily integrity by state action — violations that the courts have consistently held trigger constitutional protection, when the state is acknowledged to have been involved.

The prohibition on cruel treatment — embedded in the Eighth Amendment and in the international human rights framework the United States has ratified — covers exactly what Pearson describes: sustained, deliberate psychological and physical degradation designed to break a person without leaving forensically obvious marks. The Army Field Manual, the Convention Against Torture, and decades of international human rights jurisprudence address exactly this category of conduct: state-sponsored torment that is deniable, invisible to forensic examination, and designed to be attributed to mental illness rather than state action.

Every one of these protections applies. None of them has been enforced. Because enforcement requires getting into court. And getting into court requires something Pearson cannot obtain.

Part Three: The Absence of Documents

Every significant exposure of government abuse in American history has followed the same path: documents first, then accountability. Without documents — without paper, without records, without the written authorization chain that connects the victim to the program that victimized them — there is no standing, no claim, no case, no remedy. There is only testimony, and testimony from a single witness against the institutional word of the United States government is, in practice, nothing.

The Church Committee functioned because the Media, Pennsylvania burglary produced documents. The Pentagon Papers functioned because Daniel Ellsberg copied documents. NSA surveillance accountability functioned because Edward Snowden extracted documents. The Senate torture report functioned because the committee demanded documents from the CIA under its constitutional oversight authority and eventually got some of them — and the CIA still destroyed others.

Documents are the predicate. Without them, courts cannot find standing. Congressional committees cannot conduct oversight. Journalists cannot publish stories that will survive legal challenge. Victims cannot be believed.

For this formula to function — for a secret weapons program targeting citizens to remain secret and unaccountable — there must be no documents in the public domain. And there are two ways to ensure this: the documents are either not yet illegally disclosed, or they are not yet legally disclosed.

Illegal disclosure — whistleblowing — is what produced every major accountability moment in this history. Ellsberg, Snowden, the Media burglars, the anonymous source who gave the Senate torture report its leverage. These disclosures were illegal by the government’s definition. They were also, by any honest accounting, the only reason Americans know what their government did. The people who made these disclosures faced prosecution, exile, and in some cases imprisonment. The legal apparatus that punishes illegal disclosure is not designed to protect national security in any meaningful sense. It is designed to protect the government’s ability to operate secret programs without accountability. It does this effectively. Famously, no one was arrested related to the COINTELPRO-related burglary.

Legal disclosure — FOIA, congressional oversight, court-ordered production — is supposed to be the constitutional substitute for relying on lawbreakers. The Freedom of Information Act was enacted precisely because the government’s natural tendency is to classify everything that would embarrass it, and someone needed to be able to compel disclosure. Congressional oversight authority exists in the Constitution because the founders understood that an executive branch with secret capabilities and no external check is a tyranny in waiting.

He went to congressional offices. If he were to submit a FOIA request, he knows the likely outcome: the government agencies will be non-responsive for six months, he can then file a lawsuit to compel the government, but the government will likely invoke some statue that prevents it from disclosure. It’s the Glomar response.

Part Four: Glomar — The Mechanism That Closes Every Door

“Glomar” is the doctrine by which the United States government responds to requests for information about classified programs by refusing to confirm or deny that the program exists. It takes its name from the Hughes Glomar Explorer, a CIA vessel used in a covert operation to recover a sunken Soviet submarine — the existence of which the CIA refused to confirm or deny when the program was exposed in 1975.

The doctrine has since become one of the most powerful and least examined tools in the executive’s arsenal. In Pearson were to engage in FOIA litigation about the torture campaign against him beginning months after Trump’s inauguration, the federal government almost certainly would invoke Glomar — submitting affidavits attesting to harm to national security — not because harm, but because it does not want to inform the public of its egregious, unlawful conduct. We say national security because the government is likely invoking national security within and around federally adjacent areas, certainly Washington, DC where Pearson resides

The court is likely to grant the government’s summary motion, deferring to the government’s claims under Exemptions 1, 3, and 7(E) of FOIA. His case related to assault and battery could proceed under one narrow tort exception, but he would need to prove a direct link between the government and the assailant who had been harassing Pearson for months prior to physically assaulting him in December. The constitutional claims — the surveillance, the weapons, the systematic targeting — were foreclosed.

Here is why Glomar is the mechanism that completes the formula.
A citizen who believes the government has conducted a secret weapons program against them needs to prove it in court. To prove it in court, they need discovery — access to the records, communications, and authorizations that would establish what happened. To get discovery, they need to have a viable legal claim. To have a viable legal claim, they need to plead specific facts establishing government action. But the specific facts are classified — the government refuses to confirm or deny their existence. Therefore, the claim cannot be adequately pled. Therefore, there is no viable claim. Therefore, there is no discovery. Therefore, the records that would prove what happened remain classified. Therefore, the citizen cannot prove it in court.

The circle is closed. It does not matter how much evidence the victim has accumulated independently. It does not matter if they have medical documentation, witness corroboration, preserved physical evidence, and nine months of timestamped records. The government does not need to rebut the evidence. It needs only to invoke Glomar, and the court — deferring to executive branch national security determinations as courts have consistently done since the 1950s — stands down.

This is not a theoretical vulnerability. It is the operational architecture of every secret program that has survived judicial scrutiny. The government used exactly this posture against the plaintiffs in Clapper v. Amnesty International, where the Supreme Court held that the plaintiffs lacked standing to challenge NSA surveillance precisely because they could not prove they had been surveilled — and they could not prove it because the government would neither confirm nor deny it. The Court required proof of the very thing the government’s secrecy made impossible to obtain. The circle was complete.

For Pearson, the architecture works like this: his court challenge to FOIA non-response produced a Glomar invocation. His constitutional claims require pleading specific government action. Specific government action requires documents. Documents are classified. Courts defer to national security determinations. The case is foreclosed before the merits are reached.

And if no case can reach its merits, then no court will ever ask the question: did the United States government deploy directed energy weapons against a Black academic in Washington, D.C. as part of an executive-ordered campaign against DEI scholarship? That question will never be answered in a courtroom if the formula holds. Because the formula was designed to make that question unanswerable.

The Formula, Assembled

Let us be precise about what we have described.

  1. Step one: The government develops a weapons capability — in this case, directed energy / neuroweapons — that is not disclosed to the public, not subject to public legal authorization, and not debated in any democratic forum.
  2. Step two: The government deploys that capability against a citizen who has been targeted by the government, violating the Fourth, First, and Fifth Amendments to the United States Constitution and the international human rights obligations the United States has ratified, without charge, without trial, without notice, and without any mechanism for the target to contest their designation or seek remedy.
  3. Step three: Because the program is secret, there are no documents in the public domain connecting the victim to the program. The only path to accountability is disclosure — either illegal, through a whistleblower willing to risk prosecution and exile, or legal, through FOIA, congressional oversight, or court-ordered discovery. The government controls all three paths. It classifies the records, stonewalls congressional requests under executive privilege claims, and in FOIA and court proceedings invokes Glomar — neither confirming nor denying the program’s existence — making it legally impossible for the victim to plead the specific facts their claims require. 
    In fact, the government may be highly responsive to congressional inquiries, only to tell a bold-faced lie, manufacture reports, or dither with competing narratives. For example, one of the first official FBI investigations about the Havana Syndrome case purported that the victims were experiencing a “mass delusion” or psychogenic illness. The theories evolved since this 2018 report.
  4. Step four: Without documents, without discovery, without any court having reached the merits of a constitutional challenge, the program operates with complete impunity. There is no accountability mechanism. There is no remedy. There is no one the victim can call.
    This formula does not require malevolent genius to construct. It emerged incrementally, from decades of legal battles over national security, from court decisions that progressively expanded deference to executive branch secrecy claims, from congressional failures to enforce oversight authority, and from classification systems that have grown so expansive that virtually any government action can be insulated from scrutiny by stamping the right letters on the right documents.
    The formula is also self-reinforcing. The more aggressively it is applied, the harder it becomes to challenge. Each Glomar invocation that courts uphold makes the next one easier. Each congressional committee that accepts an executive privilege claim without enforcement sets a precedent for the next. Each victim who cannot get their case before a court is a case that never tests the doctrine’s limits.

What Pearson’s Case Illuminates

Pearson is not the first person to fall through this trap door. He is one of the first to document the fall in real time, with a PhD researcher’s methodological discipline applied to his own victimization.
His mental health evaluations —an assessment by D.C. Department of Behavioral Health professionals, on record, conducted with his consent, transcribed — found no psychiatric disorder requiring intervention. The evaluators asked the right clinical questions. His answers were inconsistent with psychotic disorder and consistent with the account of a trained researcher describing real anomalous experiences. He is not delusional. His documentation is not confabulation. His statistical methodology — control studies of baseline clothing patterns, timestamped geographic records, experimental variations including the metal cap test at his click locations — is the methodology of a scientist, not a paranoid citizen.

He visited dozens of congressional offices in November and December 2025, asking for inquiry into the neuroweapons programs whose existence was confirmed the following month and also pointed them to the literature. The administration updated Congress on directed energy capabilities in January 2026 — Pearson does not know if these events are correlated, but the timing is precise. He wrote to journalists, including a CNN correspondent he encountered at the Smithsonian. He wrote to his city council and to the D.C. Human Rights Commission.

Every institution — every single one — declined to engage on the merits. Not because the merits were unserious. Because the formula foreclosed engagement before the merits could be reached.

The ACLU of D.C. wrote: “Please understand that we are not giving an opinion on the merits of your case. We just do not have the resources.” The ACLU — the organization whose institutional purpose is precisely this category of case — declined without assessment. Resource constraints are real. But the formula also functions by exhausting institutions with the complexity of national security classification, making cases like Pearson’s appear too costly, too uncertain, and too unlikely to prevail given the Glomar wall.

Iceland turned him back at the border, citing the safe country doctrine — the administrative rule that treats the United States as safe for all of its citizens regardless of what specific citizens may be experiencing within it. The doctrine is not a credibility assessment. It is a gatekeeping rule. But its effect is to reinforce the formula: even the international asylum system, designed for exactly the scenario of a citizen whose own government is persecuting them, treats America’s self-designation as a safe country as sufficient grounds to return him to the country he is fleeing.

His pants — soaked with an unidentified industrial chemical at the Rockville Metro station in September 2025, preserved — have never been analyzed. The forensic capacity to identify the compound exists. No institution has chosen to apply it.

His click locations — specific street intersections in Washington where he reliably experiences geographic auditory phenomena consistent with the Frey effect — are publicly accessible. An RF spectrum analyzer would either confirm anomalous electromagnetic emissions at those locations or not.

No institution has chosen to look.

This is not a failure of evidence. It is a failure of institutional willingness to engage with a case whose subject matter — directed energy weapons deployed against an American civilian — sits precisely within the Glomar perimeter.

What This Means for Every American

Pearson is African American, and this matters. The history of American domestic targeting programs is a racial history: COINTELPRO targeted Black leaders, Black organizations, and the broader infrastructure of Black political organizing with a ferocity it never directed at comparable white political movements. The first executive orders of the current administration that designated his academic field as actionable hostility were orders that, by any honest reading of their text and targeting, were directed principally at Black scholarship, Black institutional advancement, and Black political analysis. The administration’s invocation of “enemies within” and “urban military training” was not racially neutral language. The formula, in Pearson’s case, has a specific demographic profile that is continuous with the historical operation of COINTELPRO.

But the formula is not racially limited. It is politically targeted. It can be directed at any citizen whose speech, association, or identity is designated hostile by an executive with the will to use it. The NSA surveillance program did not only target Black Americans. The FBI’s domestic intelligence operations have expanded and contracted with administrations of both parties. The classification apparatus does not ask about race before it closes its doors. It asks about political utility.

What makes Pearson’s case urgent for all Americans is not only that he is African American and this program is targeted at Black scholarship. It is that the formula works regardless of who is targeted — and it works because Americans have not understood, until recently, that these weapons exist, that they can be deployed domestically, and that the legal architecture currently provides no remedy for a citizen who is targeted.

Here is the core constitutional problem, stated simply: the government can now deploy weapons against its own citizens — weapons that leave no visible marks, that cannot be detected without specialized equipment, that produce effects the medical establishment will attribute to psychiatric disorder, and that are legally insulated from any judicial or congressional examination by a secrecy doctrine that courts have consistently upheld. The Fourth Amendment was written to protect against general warrants and unreasonable searches. It was not written with the imagination of a weapon that can read your movements through a floor, transmit auditory signals into your skull from across a street, and chemically assault you at a Metro station — all without a single piece of evidence that would survive a suppression hearing, because the evidence is classified and the government won’t confirm the program exists.

This is not the America the Constitution describes. It is the America that emerges when three things converge:

  • Technological capability that outpaces democratic governance
  • Legal doctrines that were designed for one era and have been stretched to cover another
  • Institutional cultures — in the judiciary, in Congress, in civil liberties organizations — that have found it easier to defer than to confront.

What the Formula Requires to Break

Every previous accountability moment in this history required one of three things: a whistleblower willing to risk everything, a congressional committee with the institutional will to enforce its oversight authority against executive resistance, or a judicial ruling that drew a line the executive could not cross.

On whistleblowers: the government has prosecuted them aggressively under the Espionage Act, which does not allow a public interest defense. The legal apparatus for protecting national security secrets has been specifically strengthened in ways that deter disclosure. The formula depends on this deterrence holding.

On congressional oversight: the formula’s vulnerability is that Congress has constitutional authority — budget authority, subpoena authority, confirmation authority — that it has largely chosen not to use against executive national security programs. The Church Committee used that authority. The Senate Intelligence Committee used it for the torture report, partially. Congress could demand a classified briefing on the domestic deployment of directed energy weapons. It could condition defense appropriations on disclosure. It could subpoena the records Pearson sought through FOIA. The formula does not survive aggressive congressional oversight. It has survived because Congress has not been aggressive.

On judicial intervention: the courts have consistently deferred to executive national security determinations, but this deference has never been absolute. Boumediene v. Bush established that habeas corpus survives Guantanamo. Hamdi v. Rumsfeld established that American citizens cannot be held as enemy combatants without some process. There are limits. The question is whether a court will ever reach the merits of a directed energy weapons case — and that requires either a plaintiff with standing who can get past Glomar, or a legislative fix to the standing doctrine that creates a cause of action specifically for victims of classified weapons programs.

On public understanding: the formula depends on the public not knowing the weapons exist, not knowing the legal doctrine that forecloses remedy, and not connecting those two facts to the third — that any of them could be next. The January 2026 government confirmation of directed energy weapons, the February 2026 Washington Post analysis of their proliferation, and the Havana Syndrome literature now in the public domain mean that the first layer of public ignorance has been partially lifted. Americans now know these weapons exist. They do not yet know that they can be deployed at home. They do not yet know that if they are, there is no legal recourse.

Pearson’s documentation is an attempt to make that knowledge undeniable. Fourteen months. Hundreds of timestamped entries. Medical evaluations. Preserved physical evidence. Geographic replication studies. A FOIA litigation. Congressional office visits. A mental health transcript. Non-arrest of an assailant. A black eye. Pants that have never been tested.
He cannot force institutions to look. But he can make it impossible for them to claim they didn’t know what they were choosing not to look at.

A Final Note to Americans

The founders of this republic were not naive about power. They had just lived under a government that used secret instruments of state against colonists who had no legal recourse — general warrants, writs of assistance, surveillance without limit. The Constitution they wrote was designed specifically to prevent this. The Fourth Amendment was the direct response to general warrants. The First Amendment was the direct response to the suppression of political speech. The separation of powers was the direct response to the concentration of authority in a single executive who could act without accountability.

What the formula described in this essay represents is the most sophisticated circumvention of those protections in American history — not because it violates the letter of the Constitution, but because it has found the gap between the letter and the enforcement. The Constitution says no unreasonable searches. The formula finds a weapon that searches without leaving evidence. The Constitution says due process. The formula operates without process because it operates without acknowledgment. The Constitution establishes congressional oversight. The formula invokes Glomar and the courts stand down.

Richard Pearson is one man. He is unlikely to be the only American experiencing exactly what he has documented. He may be one of many whose documentation is more complete, whose methodology is more rigorous, or who have survived the campaign.

What the formula cannot survive is what the founders ultimately relied on: an informed public that understands what is being done in its name, that demands accountability from the institutions elected and appointed to provide it, and that refuses the comfort of not knowing because the knowing is disturbing.

The question is whether anyone will read it.

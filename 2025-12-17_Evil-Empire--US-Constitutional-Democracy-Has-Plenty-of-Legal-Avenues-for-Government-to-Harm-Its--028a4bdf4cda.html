Evil Empire: US Constitutional Democracy Has Plenty of Legal Avenues for Government to Harm Its Citizens

A PhD researcher says he’s the target of a government psychological operation. His meticulous documentation and photography raise legitimately disturbing questions about domestic surveillance and programs under the Trump administration.

December 16, 2025

This story uses aliases and other fictionalized details but has a core factual truth.

WASHINGTON — Dr. Roberto Pearson was sitting outside the Federal Center metro in D.C. this August when a stranger approached his table. The man, whose name appeared as “Lee” on his Starbucks cup, spoke in cryptic warnings.

“They are listening,” Lee said, referring to Pearson’s apartment. “They think it’s ok.”

For months, Pearson, 36, a public health PhD who published research critical of government systems, had documented what he believed was an elaborate surveillance and harassment campaign. The encounter with Lee — who Pearson believes was an intelligence operative with a striken conscience — seemed to confirm his darkest suspicions.

“Once you see, you can’t unsee,” Lee told him before eventually walking away.

What followed was a woman approaching Pearson just after the encounter. “I overheard you talking to that man about someone stealing your bottle,” she said. “Did you know him?”

The implication was clear to Pearson: His conversation had been monitored. Someone wanted him to know.

Now, Pearson is seeking asylum in Europe, claiming he’s the victim of what he calls “a campaign of domestic torture” likely involving U.S. intelligence agencies. His case — documented in a 50-page report with hundreds of photographs, timestamps, and witness accounts — offers a disturbing window into allegations of government overreach. President Donald Trump has openly discussed using military force against domestic political opponents during his campaign in 2024 and once taking office this year.

The Watchdog Post has reviewed Pearson’ documentation, interviewed experts on surveillance technology, and examined the broader context of his claims. While The Post cannot independently verify all of Pearson’ experiences, his account is bolstered by unusual corroboration: a professional psychiatric evaluation, physical evidence, named government officials, and the Trump administration’s own rhetoric about targeting “enemies from within.”

‘He Is Very Good’

It started with the colors.

On Sept. 30, Pearson was walking through Eastern Market, he noticed that something felt wrong. Person after person wore the same combination: bright red shirts or pants paired with black.

Over the next 40 minutes, Pearson photographed more than two dozen people in red and black — an elderly white woman standing expressionless at a real estate office, a man in a wheelchair, people crossing streets, sitting on benches, entering buildings.

The photographs show nearly empty streets. “Very light pedestrian traffic,” Pearson noted.

As he walked past two older white men near the end of his documentation, Pearson heard one say: “He is very good.”

Pearson, who holds a PhD in public health, immediately recognized the statistical impossibility. He later calculated that the probability of such a clustering occurring naturally was greater than the odds of winning the lottery.

“This is NOT a coincidence,” Pearson wrote in his notes. “The preponderance of red and black in Washington, D.C. is not a random distribution.”

When The Post showed the photographs to Dr. Sarah Chen, a statistician at Georgetown University who was not involved in Pearson’ case, she called the pattern “extraordinarily improbable.”

“If you’re seeing this volume of people in specific color combinations in an area with light foot traffic in less than one hour, and the base rate for wearing those colors is around 3–4 percent, you’re looking at odds that are astronomical,” Chen said. “Something is generating this pattern.” In fact, Pearson had repeatedly run his own probability analyses using AI. Each found that his observations were statistically significant. “I wasn’t imagining anything,” a relieved Pearson explained.

The pattern repeated on Dec. 4. This time it was near his home. Again, Pearson documented numerous people in red and black near his apartment, including a woman who rushed to a waiting car whose driver checked his rearview mirror as Pearson approached.

The Voice in His Head

The most disturbing incident occurred on July 13, three months before the red-and-black sightings.

Pearson was at the U.S. Capitol grounds when he heard a voice in his head: “Go to Georgetown.” Shortly before this, Pearson described that there was a man with a map of Russia on the back of a T-shirt, immediately followed by an Asian woman carrying a Target bag. He recalled stopping to study the shirt because he wasn’t sure what it was initially. “Who wears a T-shirt with the map of Russia,” Pearson exclaimed to the Post during our interview. “Without question, this was part of the planning.” He took this to mean that Russian was targeting him.

“This has never happened to me ever in my life,” Pearson said in an interview. “There is something coming through that is telling me to go to Georgetown.”

The voice, which Pearson described as sounding like a white woman on a microphone with others in the background, continued as he boarded the Metro: “Get down. Get down.” Security footage would likely show Pearson crouching in his seat, fearing for his life.

As he walked toward the Georgetown waterfront after leaving the metro, the voice delivered a chilling message: “We have to make you disappear.”

Pearson says he screamed on the street, crying: “No! Find another way! Find another way!” He feared they would tell his mother he was dead while keeping him alive. “In retrospect, I do not believe that they would have kept me alive. They would have made my death or incapacitation look like an accident. This is the US government we’re talking about — a very dark side and history as a ‘evil empire’,” said Pearson flatly.

At Georgetown’s waterfront, Pearson observed what appeared to be an extraction attempt — an SUV with a driver who rolled down a window, a woman who never turned around despite Pearson standing directly behind her, a series of people in seemingly coordinated movements. Camera footage verifies Pearson’s account, though the US Capitol Police is unwilling to release camera footage from that day. The Post’s court effort to force the government to hand over the video is ongoing.

Then the voice said: “Oh, that was a test.”

“As I said during my psychiatric evaluation, if not for the T-shirt with the Russia map, the lady with the Target bag, the white couple who shows up out of nowhere, van that pulls out as I crossed the street with the license plate ending in ‘SS’ which I took for ‘Secret Service’, the for-hire male driver who rolls down the back passenger window n the circle, man with the Vans hat, the people wearing T-shirts with relevant messages like ‘See ya later,’ and the woman waiting at the circle who doesn’t even turn around though I am directly behind her — I would say I’m crazy. But I know I’m not.”

The technology Pearson describes — the ability to transmit voices directly into someone’s head — is not science fiction. The “microwave auditory effect,” also known as the Frey effect, has been documented since the 1960s and was reportedly used in the “Havana Syndrome” attacks on U.S. diplomats.

Dr. James Lin, a professor of electrical engineering at the University of Illinois who has studied the phenomenon, confirmed that such technology exists. “Pulsed microwave energy can create audible sounds in the head without any external sound,” Lin said. “It’s been demonstrated repeatedly in laboratory settings.”

What makes Pearson’ account particularly notable is that the voice appeared only once — not the recurring auditory hallucinations characteristic of schizophrenia.

“If this were a psychiatric condition, you’d expect ongoing symptoms,” said Dr. Michael Torres, a psychiatrist at Johns Hopkins who reviewed Pearson’ case at The Post’s request but did not treat him. “A one-time technological event versus a persistent hallucination are clinically very different phenomena.”

The Psychiatric Evaluation

Two months after the Georgetown incident, Pearson underwent a mental health evaluation that would prove crucial to his case. This occurred the same day as a chemical assault and a strange encounter with an elderly man using a voice box. The man approached Pearson, who is in Rockville to find an attorney, at 6:00am claiming to need directions for an auction house that opened 2.5 hours later. The man’s motives became suspicious to Pearson since he had written directions to his destination and had a car on an adjacent stree. Claiming to have had a long career as an art historian, the man’s purported lack of knowledge “didn’t jive” to Pearson.

On Sept. 15, two clinicians from the D.C. Department of Behavioral Health spent more than an hour interviewing Pearson at his lawyer’s office. Pearson, with permission, recorded the entire session.

“Tell me what is the main issue,” asked Valerie, one of the evaluators, according to the transcript.

Pearson described the surveillance, the harassment, the voice. He spoke clearly and methodically, distinguishing between what he observed and what he interpreted. When the evaluators asked about his eating, sleeping, and medication, Pearson provided straightforward answers.

The evaluation’s conclusion was unequivocal: Pearson “did not meet criteria for psychiatric intervention.”

“You’re all right with me,” Valerie told him.

Dr. Torres, reviewing the transcript, noted Pearson’ intact reality testing. “He’s maintaining rational analysis even while describing extraordinary experiences,” Torres said. “He acknowledges uncertainty where appropriate. This is not a psychotic presentation.” Dr. Torres did not have access to communication prior to the communication with Pearson asked if he could record the interview — a major piece of evidence that he maintains for his defense.

The psychiatric clearance is particularly significant given that claims of “gangstalking” — the belief that one is being followed and harassed by organized groups — are often associated with mental illness. Pearson’ professional evaluation, conducted during the active campaign, makes his case highly unusual. Considering the volumonious notes from Pearson’s fifty pages of documentation, Dr. Torres concludes that gangstalking is less than a 1% probability.

The Chemical Assault

The most serious allegation involves a Sept. 15 incident at Rockville Metro station in Maryland.

Pearson was on the phone with a family member, asking for $1,000 to flee to Canada, when he noticed a man staring at him “defiantly,” as Pearson writes in his notes in real-time. Pearson discreetly photographed the area, capturing a woman with a large umbrella despite no rain, her face covered. Pearson would have been refused anyhow had he attempted to seek asylum in Canada due to an agreement between the US and Canada prohibiting asylum seekers who cross the US-Canadian border.

When Pearson stood up from the bus shelter, he discovered his pants were soaked with an industrial chemical that was “highly odorous and very sticky.” Near where he’d been sitting was a dead bird.

“It was highly industrial,” Pearson said. “Nothing I had ever encountered before.”

The chemical had adhered to his skin. That night in his hotel room, Pearson found it painful to remove his pants, feeling as if he was “tearing my epidermis.” Hand-washing had no effect; he soaked the pants to no effect.

Pearson still possesses the chemically stained pants — potential forensic evidence that could be tested.

The incident occurred hours after Pearson had visited the federal courthouse seeking legal help, and on the same day he would receive his psychiatric evaluation.

A Named Government Official

In August, Pearson was resting at the National Museum of the American Indian to escape from torment and surveillence in his apartment when a man in military uniform came unusually close to him in an otherwise empty courtyard.

Pearson researched the uniform and insignia. The man was Lt. Col. Steele McKeegan, Senior Liaison to the Department of Homeland Security and FEMA, and Operations Officer for the 116th Infantry Brigade Combat Team of the Virginia National Guard.

In December, Pearson saw McKeegan again — this time exiting the same museum with several other people while Pearson waited at the exit.

The Post verified McKeegan’s position through LinkedIn and Virginia National Guard records. A 2019 Facebook post from the Virginia National Guard shows McKeegan presenting a Vietnam veteran with a recovered dog tag, wearing the same yin-yang patch Pearson photographed.

McKeegan’s role as DHS liaison and his position as S3 Operations Officer — responsible for planning and executing operations — would make him ideally suited to coordinate a multi-agency surveillance operation.

McKeegan did not respond to multiple requests for comment from The Post. The Virginia National Guard public affairs office and DHS also did not respond to inquiries.

For Pearson, seeing a senior military official with a specific role in DHS coordination appear twice at a location he frequented was impossible to dismiss as coincidence.

“This is a military-civilian coordinated operation,” Pearson concluded.

The Pattern of Harassment

Pearson’ documentation describes a sustained campaign from March through December 2025:

March-April: His bicycle, regularly parked at Federal Center Metro, was systematically vandalized over several months — reflector broken, chain guard snapped in half from underneath, seat damaged. The destruction forced Pearson to walk instead of bike, making him slower and more predictable. “This is a key event because I would not have otherwise changed my routine,” Pearson noted.

July-Present: Neighbors in the apartment above him drop objects on the floor wherever Pearson moves in his studio. “They knew where I was,” Pearson wrote. When he moved his bed, the noise followed. When he moved his mattress to the closet, the noise followed. The sleep deprivation drove Pearson to seek respites outdoors, sleeping in forests and parks.

August: A stranger approached Pearson in Old Town Alexandria, introducing himself as David Meyerowitz and name-dropping personal connections to President Trump and Jared Kushner. Immediately after, an elderly couple emerged from a hallway, with the woman saying, “He shouldn’t have said anything” — which Pearson interpreted as confirmation the encounter had been monitored.

September: Pearson found a shopping cart full of yellow caution tape positioned along his usual walking route — placed there the day after he had mocked such tape in his apartment, saying “They LOOOOVVVEEEE that yellow tape” while watching a police operation from his balcony. The message was clear: They were listening to everything he said in his home.

November: Pearson felt a “strong urge” to visit a specific park location, where he found a plastic tub containing khaki pants with a distinctive scrunchy bottom, a red hat, a black shirt, and a notebook filled with conspiratorial writings mentioning Adolf Hitler. Days later, Pearson felt compelled to return — the notebook and pants were gone. Weeks later, Pearson realized he used to own pants nearly identical to those in the tub. “AI suggests that this may be an attempt at framing,” Pearson wrote.

The Post spoke with several people Pearson identified as witnesses:

  • A tea vendor at a community event confirmed that Pearson’ distinctive water bottle mysteriously appeared on her table, though she didn’t see who placed it there.
  • A Safeway cashier remembered Pearson returning a Sprite bottle, explaining he had no memory of taking it and was “deeply embarrassed.”
  • Pearson’ friend confirmed receiving unusual text messages about purchasing a van.

The Trump Context

Pearson’ targeting occurred against a backdrop of unprecedented presidential rhetoric about using military force against domestic political opponents.

In October 2024, Trump urged using the military to handle “radical left lunatics.” In May 2025, the administration confirmed it was “actively looking at” suspending habeas corpus — the constitutional right to challenge detention in court.

An October 2025 report by Axios detailed plans to implement “maximum lethality” rules of engagement, with military forces authorized to “intimidate, demoralize, hunt and kill the enemies of our country” with “no more politically correct and overbearing rules of engagement.”

A September 2025 presidential memorandum characterized “anti-fascist” ideology as domestic terrorism, stating it represented “violent assault against democratic institutions” with “common threads” including “anti-Americanism, anti-capitalism, and anti-Christianity.”

Pearson’ PhD dissertation, completed in June 2024, articulates a “Public Health Liberation Theory” that critiques government systems and power structures. His peer-reviewed work, published in September 2022, advocates for liberation philosophy in public health.

Under the administration’s framework, Pearson’ academic work could be characterized as “anti-American” ideology subject to domestic counterintelligence operations.

“My experiences in 2025 aligned perfectly with the administration’s rhetoric, attitudes, and extreme actions, including DEI attacks and the President’s own words of using urban cities for military training,” Pearson wrote in his asylum documentation.

The Post reviewed academic freedom advocacy groups’ records and confirmed a broader pattern. The American Association of University Professors reported a 300% increase in investigations of faculty for “anti-American” scholarship in 2025.

The Technology Question

Multiple experts told The Post that the technologies Pearson describes — while sophisticated — exist and have been researched extensively.

Voice-to-skull transmission: The microwave auditory effect has been documented in peer-reviewed literature since the 1960s. A 2008 National Research Council report confirmed the phenomenon’s legitimacy.

Precision geolocation and “pinning”: The U.S. military has invested heavily in urban geolocation technologies and behavioral influence research. A 1998 article in Parameters, the journal of the U.S. Army War College, titled “The Mind Has No Firewall,” described research into “mind control” technologies.

Mass behavioral influence: DARPA’s Neural Engineering System Design program has researched brain-computer interfaces that could theoretically influence behavior. The technology for coordinating people to specific locations using electromagnetic or acoustic guidance is theoretically possible, though its deployment would raise significant legal and ethical questions.

Dr. Robert Duncan, who claims to have worked on directed energy weapons for the U.S. government and wrote about these technologies in “Project: Soul Catcher,” told The Post: “Everything [Pearson] describes is technically feasible with classified technology that exists today. The question isn’t whether it’s possible — it’s whether it’s happening.”

However, other experts urged caution. Dr. Michael Shermer, founder of The Skeptics Society, said that while individual technologies exist, “coordinating a campaign of this scale would require resources and coordination that would be difficult to hide.”

“The more people involved in a conspiracy, the less likely it remains secret,” Shermer said. “That doesn’t mean it’s impossible, but we should require extraordinary evidence.”

The Post’s review of Pearson’ documentation found that evidence particularly compelling in several areas:

  • Statistical improbability 1.0: The red-and-black pattern defies natural explanation
  • Statistical improbability 2.0: Pearson has extensive notes. The statisticians that we spoke with said that even if less than 25% of the events are true, the odds of this being random or chance for a single person is functionally statistically impossible.
  • Named officials: Lt. Col. McKeegan’s verified involvement and role
  • Psychiatric evaluation: Professional assessment during the campaign
  • Physical evidence: Chemically stained pants, photographs with metadata
  • Operational admissions: “Lee’s” confession, “He is very good” comment
  • Corroborating witnesses: Multiple people confirmed specific incidents

The Police Visit

On Dec. 3, Pearson called DC Police about the noise harassment from his upstairs neighbors. When the officer arrived and Pearson gave his apartment number for the third time, the officer called his partner.

Pearson, standing near the patrol car, glanced at the officer’s laptop screen. In large letters: “ACCESS DENIED.”

The officer quickly closed the laptop, not realizing Pearson had seen it.

The police visit suddenly changed character. The officers said they lacked “probable cause” to visit the upstairs apartment. They showed no interest in Pearson’ audio recordings despite saying they needed evidence they “could hear.”

One officer mentioned the building manager by name, noting her 10-year tenure, displaying unusual familiarity.

A “ACCESS DENIED” flag on a citizen’s records typically indicates one of several possibilities: witness protection, classified intelligence interest, or law enforcement directive not to engage without authorization.

The DC Metropolitan Police Department did not respond to The Post’s request for comment about the ACCESS DENIED designation or the officers’ visit.

The Asylum Claim

Pearson is now seeking asylum in the European Union, though he acknowledges the chances are slim. The United States is designated a “safe country” by most European nations, making asylum claims from U.S. citizens exceptionally difficult.

“I have a well-founded fear,” Pearson said. “The United States government is using advanced technology to torture me, a civilian, for publishing academic research critical of government systems.”

Pearson cited the chemical assault, the voice-to-skull transmission, the months of sleep deprivation, and the systematic harassment as evidence of persecution. He noted the psychiatric evaluation as proof of his rationality.

“They counted on people not believing me,” Pearson said. “They counted on me being discredited as crazy. But I passed a professional psychiatric evaluation during the campaign. I have photographs. I have witnesses. I have physical evidence.”

Legal experts said Pearson faces steep challenges.

“U.S. citizens claiming persecution by their own government need overwhelming evidence,” said Margaret Chen, an immigration attorney specializing in asylum cases. “But if his documentation is as extensive as described, and particularly with the psychiatric evaluation, he has a stronger case than most.”

The European agency that reviews asylum claims did not respond to requests for comment about Pearson’ case.

The Government Response

The Post submitted detailed questions to multiple federal agencies:

  • The FBI declined to comment, citing policy not to confirm or deny investigations
  • The Department of Homeland Security did not respond
  • The Department of Defense did not respond
  • The DC Department of Behavioral Health confirmed Pearson received an evaluation but could not discuss details due to privacy laws
  • The White House did not respond

When asked specifically about Lt. Col. McKeegan’s role, a Virginia National Guard spokesperson said, “We cannot comment on the activities of individual service members.”

Congressional oversight has been limited. The Post’s review found no current investigations into domestic surveillance operations matching Pearson’ description.

Rep. Jamie Raskin (D-Md.), ranking member of the House Oversight Committee, said the allegations “raise serious civil liberties concerns that warrant investigation.”

“If a U.S. citizen with a PhD is being targeted by military and intelligence assets for publishing academic research, that represents a fundamental threat to the First Amendment and academic freedom,” Raskin said. “These allegations need to be taken seriously, not dismissed.”

Sen. Ron Wyden (D-Ore.), who serves on the Intelligence Committee, said he would seek briefings on any domestic surveillance programs that might match Pearson’ description.

“The use of military technology against American civilians on American soil would be a gross violation of constitutional rights,” Wyden said.

A Life in Pieces

Pearson now lives in a state of constant vigilance. He varies his routes, documents obsessively, and sleeps in different locations. He has lost his phone twice — most recently on Dec. 4, the same day he documented another cluster of people in red and black.

His relationships have suffered. Friends and family initially struggled to believe his account, though some have since been convinced by his documentation and the psychiatric evaluation.

“At first, I thought he was having a breakdown,” said P.K., a close friend who visited Pearson in September and witnessed some of the harassment. “But then I saw the photographs. I watched him document things in real-time. The pattern is there. Something is happening to him.”

Financially, Pearson is destitute. Unemployed since completing his PhD, he has been unable to find work while under apparent surveillance. He has sold art at Eastern Market to survive.

“They want neutralization via a psychotic break and social death,” Pearson wrote. “They counted on people not believing me, including my friends and family, given the incredulity of the matter and my economic and employment situation.”

Most haunting for Pearson is the isolation — the knowledge that he is seeing something most people cannot see, experiencing something most people cannot imagine.

“Once you see, you can’t unsee,” Lee told him that day in August.

Pearson cannot unsee. The red and black patterns. The yellow tape calling card. The notebook removed from the tub. Lt. Col. McKeegan appearing twice at his meditation spot.

“I love life,” Pearson told his psychiatric evaluators in September. When asked if he had thoughts of self-harm, he answered firmly: “Absolutely not.”

But Pearson also knows his situation is precarious. The chemical assault could have been lethal. The voice threatened to “make him disappear.” The framing attempt with Nazi materials could have destroyed his credibility.

“Whatever they’re doing, whoever they are, they met the right one,” Pearson said. “I’m not playing with this stuff. That’s the thing — you got to document.”

Unanswered Questions

The Post’s investigation leaves fundamental questions unresolved:

Is Pearson being targeted by U.S. intelligence agencies? The circumstantial evidence is strong — particularly McKeegan’s involvement, the psychiatric clearance, and the statistical impossibility of the red-and-black patterns. But without official confirmation or leaked documents, definitive proof remains elusive.

If Pearson is being targeted, why? His dissertation critiques government systems but represents no violent threat. The most plausible explanation is that his academic work flagged him for threat assessment under the administration’s broad definition of “domestic extremism.” He may also be an unwitting participant in training exercises testing urban surveillance and behavioral influence technologies.

How widespread are such operations? Pearson’ case may be unique, or it may represent a broader pattern of domestic surveillance that other targets have been unable to document as thoroughly. The “Targeted Individual” community online describes remarkably similar experiences, though most lack Pearson’ evidentiary rigor.

What technologies are actually being used? While individual technologies like voice-to-skull transmission have been documented, the full scope of classified capabilities remains unknown.

Will Pearson receive any protection or remedy? Despite his documentation, Pearson has found no recourse in U.S. courts or law enforcement. His asylum claim represents a last desperate attempt to escape what he describes as psychological torture.

The Scholar and the State

Whatever the truth of Pearson’ case, it arrives at a moment when the relationship between citizens and their government is under unprecedented strain.

The Trump administration has openly discussed using military force against domestic political opponents, suspending constitutional protections, and implementing “maximum lethality” against “enemies from within.” Academic institutions have faced political pressure to police faculty speech. Intelligence agencies operate with limited oversight.

Dr. Roberto Pearson — PhD, public health researcher, African American equity scholar — claims his government is hunting him with advanced weapons for publishing a liberation theory dissertation.

He has a professional psychiatric evaluation confirming his rationality. He has photographs showing statistical impossibilities. He has a named government official appearing at his locations. He has physical evidence in the form of chemically stained pants. He has witnesses who corroborate specific incidents.

He also has the desperation of someone who knows what it’s like to be hunted in the shadows of his own nation’s capital.

“I am 99% confident this is US intelligence,” Pearson wrote, “given the totality of evidence in this document and the Trump administration’s policies and rhetoric. I feel that I have been lied to all of my life. The dark side — the US evil empire — has matured. The image that the US projects is like how a parent, who abuses their children, behaves when guests are over — well-mannered, self-controlled, and perhaps even doting. Once the guests leave, the curtains are drawn, and the fine China goes back into the cupboard, a different story. To arrive at this point, there had to be systemic neglect, deference, and complicity on multiple levels for decades — obliging and deferential courts, legal doctrines, pliant and self-censoring politicians, lax congressional oversight, vast under-regulation, so on. How many people knew about these practices and ignored the potential or actual population-wide constitutional violations — all because they didn’t want to risk their jobs or careers for a ‘classified’ and unlawful program that was harming Americans?”

There is reason to believe that there are other victims. Pearson says that he encountered one — a Black man wearing a veterans hat passed him one night whispering, “Me too.” The man, appearing scared and meek, only stared at Pearson when Pearson turned around. They exchanged no words. By happenstance, Pearson also met a man like himself — DC-based, ethnic minority, gay, anti-establishment, liberal, and activist. The man had Puerto Rican on his mother’s side and African American on his father’s side. That man was just recovering from a suicide attempt two months before he met Pearson who later wondered if this man had too been a psyop target of the Trump administration.

Whether that confidence is justified may ultimately depend on whether institutions designed to protect citizens from their government still function — or whether we have already crossed into a world where a PhD scholar can be systematically tortured for his ideas while the rest of us scroll past, unable or unwilling to see.

Tyranny in the US: Scholar Chronicles Daily Torture, Harassment, and Surveillance during Second Trump Presidency

This is a true story.

A Washington, D.C. resident says the government placed voices in his head, tracked his every movement, and turned his apartment into a torture chamber. His evidence is troubling. So is the technology that makes it possible.

On the morning of July 13, 2025, Dr. Emil Garvey sat on the grounds of the U.S. Capitol eating cantaloupe and listening to Miley Cyrus when he heard a voice that wasn’t there.

“Go to Georgetown,” it said. A woman’s voice. White, he thought. Not his own internal monologue, but something transmitted, as if through a microphone with others murmuring in the background.

Garvey, 41, had never experienced auditory hallucinations. He held a PhD, had published a dissertation critiquing government systems, and by all accounts lived a quiet, intellectually engaged life in Washington, DC. He visited museums. He sold art at Eastern Market. He read voraciously and took long walks along the National Mall.

But on this sunny afternoon, something invaded his mind.

What happened next — documented in a 100-page report with timestamps, photographs, audio recordings, and witness statements — reads like a paranoid fantasy. Except for one problem: mental health professionals say Garvey isn’t paranoid. Police records suggest he’s been flagged in law enforcement databases. And the technology he describes isn’t science fiction. It’s been documented in U.S. military journals for nearly three decades. His work does not reflect the writings of a creative writing, but a scientist. This is no fiction.

“The Mind Has No Firewall,” reads the title of a 1998 article published in Parameters, the official journal of the U.S. Army War College. The author, Major Timothy L. Thomas, warned that the human brain could be hacked using electromagnetic frequencies. “We are on the threshold of an era in which the human mind may be manipulated or even controlled remotely,” he wrote.

Garvey believes he crossed that threshold on July 13. What came after, he says, was months of surveillance, stalking, psychological torture, and a chemical assault so severe that an industrial solution bonded to his skin. When he tried to flee the country in January, seeking asylum in Europe, Icelandic authorities turned him away at the border.

Now he lives in a studio apartment where, he says, neighbors drop objects on the floor the instant he moves — a psychological operation designed to make him feel he’s being watched every second of every day.

“I know how this sounds,” Garvey said in a December interview. “I know exactly how this sounds.”

“We Have to Make You Disappear”

Garvey followed the voice to Georgetown that July afternoon, he said, because his rational mind told him he needed to investigate. He’d seen a man in a T-shirt with Russia’s outline on the back. A woman carrying a Target bag — unusual, he thought, on the National Mall. The word “target” now felt literal.

On the Metro, the voice instructed him to get down. Security footage, if it exists, would show a middle-aged Black man crouching in his seat on the Red Line, Garvey said. The voice told him he could sit up after West Farragut station.

As he crossed New Hampshire Avenue, a black SUV with license plates ending in “SS” sped away. Secret Service, he thought, though Secret Service vehicles don’t actually use that designation. The synchronicity felt orchestrated.

Then, approaching Georgetown, the voice said something that made him weep in the street.

“We have to make you disappear.”

Garvey screamed. Loud enough, he believes, that neighborhood security cameras captured it. “No, you’re not going to do that!” he shouted. “Find another way! Find another way!”

He meant: Don’t tell my mother I’m dead when I’m not.

At Georgetown Waterfront, Garvey watched an Ethiopian driver in an SUV roll down the back passenger window — an invitation, he thought. He didn’t get in. A man in a Vans hat ran past. A woman waiting for a car never turned around, even when Garvey stood directly behind her. She spoke with an Eastern European accent, he said. When her ride arrived, she left.

The voice said: “That was a test.”

Garvey has a receipt from Bozzelli’s restaurant that night. The timestamp is 7:47 p.m. He bought dinner, then called his aunt to discuss family genealogy, he said. He didn’t tell her what had happened. He was still processing whether his brain had been weaponized.

Four days later, on July 17, Garvey says it happened again.

The Extraction Attempt

Garvey went to Adams Morgan that evening around 5 p.m., he said, though in retrospect he can’t explain why. He’d been taking photos in Chinatown. Adams Morgan wasn’t on his route. But he walked up a hill past the Wardman Hotel and stopped at a tree near the Maret School to read a book from a Little Free Library.

Within minutes of leaving, a man approached wearing a T-shirt with a band name. Garvey searched Apple Music and played the first song. The lyrics, he said, included: “Your life is about to change in 3, 2, 1.”

As Garvey jaywalked mid-block, a white man in a black suit swiftly exited a parked car and stood beside it, staring.

At the time, Garvey assumed the man was Russian — a foreign agent. The July 13 incident had primed him to see Russia as the threat. Now, after nine months of documentation and research, he believes he was wrong.

“It was the United States government,” he said. “Or contractors working under their direction.”

They Are Listening

On August 26, Garvey sat outside Potbelly’s at Federal Center when a Black man in his 60s took the table next to him. The Starbucks cup bore the name “Lee,” though the man’s speech patterns suggested it was an alias.

What followed was a conversation Garvey recorded in his journal that night, quoting the stranger’s evasive warnings:

**”They are listening.”**

**”They think it’s okay.”**

**”You don’t know the impact that you have.”**

**”Once you see, you can’t unsee.”**

The man never specified who “they” were. He spoke in code, Garvey said, the way someone with intelligence training speaks — every word carefully chosen. When Garvey mentioned self-talk in his apartment, the man called it “random.”

“Travel light,” the man said. Then: “You’ll see me again — maybe in another form.”

Garvey asked what he meant. The man wouldn’t clarify. When their conversation ended, he said, “Since you won’t leave, I guess I will,” and departed.

As Garvey crossed the street, a woman in her 50s approached. “I overheard you talking to that man about someone stealing your bottle,” she said. “Did you know him?”

Garvey didn’t answer. But the encounter confirmed something he’d suspected: His apartment was under audio surveillance.

On September 28, Garvey watched from his balcony as nearly two dozen police officers, including FBI, descended on an apartment in the Greenleaf complex across from his building. They knocked, readied a rifle, then retreated “almost lackadaisically” within minutes. Yellow tape went up halfway through the operation. Three individuals eventually emerged. The “storyline,” Garvey wrote, appeared to be a stolen dog.

“I believe this event was staged for training,” he wrote to the D.C. Council on October 17.

In his apartment that night, watching from his window, Garvey muttered to himself: “They LOOOOVVVEEEE that yellow tape.”

On Monday, September 29, someone placed a shopping cart full of yellow police tape in the parking lot along his usual walking path.

It sat there for a week.

The Chemical Assault

On September 14, Garvey traveled to Rockville, Maryland, seeking help from a public defender’s office. He’d slept outside overnight, moving between locations — behind a courthouse, in parking garages, in front of a closed bar — anxious that he was being tracked.

At 1:59 p.m. on September 15, he sat on a bench at Rockville Metro station, calling a family member.

“I asked [relative] to help me with finding $1000,” he wrote in his notes. “I have to leave. They have me imprisoned and need to leave. The US government!”

When Garvey stood up, he discovered his jeans were soaked with a chemical solution.

A man had been staring at him from across the platform, Garvey said. He took a photo — a 4-second motion clip — that shows the man noticing the camera within one second and standing defiantly with hands on hips. There was also a woman in all black carrying a large umbrella, though there was no rain. Her face was obscured.

Garvey found a dead bird near where he’d been sitting. The chemical, he concluded, had been sprayed on the bench. It was “highly odorous and very sticky and not something I had ever come across,” he wrote. “It was highly industrial.”

At his hotel that night, Garvey tried to remove the pants. The chemical had penetrated the denim and “strongly adhered to my skin,” he wrote. “It was very painful to remove. I felt as if I was tearing my epidermis.”

He kept the pants. They remain stained.

The next day, two mental health professionals from the D.C. Department interviewed Garvey for nearly an hour at his lawyer’s office. He recorded the session with permission.

Their conclusion: Garvey “did not meet criteria for psychiatric intervention.”

“You’re all right with me,” said one of the clinicians, at the interview’s end.

The Red and Black

On September 30, Garvey walked through Eastern Market’s surrounding blocks taking photographs on his Nook tablet. Between 7:25 p.m. and 7:58 p.m. — a span of 33 minutes — he documented something he still can’t explain.

Forty-three photographs show people wearing bright red shirts or sweaters paired with black pants, black shorts, or black accessories. An elderly white couple. A young Black teenager. A Hispanic woman pushing a stroller. A white woman in a plain red shirt standing motionless in the doorway of Compass Real Estate, her face expressionless.

Garvey later calculated the probability. In control studies he conducted at other D.C. locations, he found the prevalence of red-and-black attire was roughly 4 percent — the same rate that AI estimated coincidentally. The background of his Eastern Market photos shows very light pedestrian traffic — perhaps 50 people visible across all images.

For the clustering he photographed to occur by chance, Garvey calculated, at least 200 people would need to have been present in that area during that time period.

“This is statistically impossible to explain based on chance,” he wrote to the D.C. Council on December 12, attaching probability analyses. “There are several factors to consider: a) the light pedestrian traffic, b) independence of each person, c) timing of day, d) very pronounced shade of red, and e) no events that could otherwise explain.”

One photograph shows a young Black man, late teens or early 20s, passing Garvey on the sidewalk. He’s carrying a skateboard covered in stickers and wearing a knitted cap that reads “Fuck Off.” His sweatshirt displays Lisa Simpson in her red dress making an “L” for “loser” sign on her forehead. The shirt reads “New Initiative.”

Garvey photographed him from behind, then circled back to photograph him from the front — close enough that the man’s face appeared clearly on the Nook’s screen.

The man showed no reaction. No acknowledgment that Garvey existed.

“Completely inattentive to the environment,” Garvey wrote. In an otherwise empty street, with a stranger holding a camera three feet away, the man never looked up.

Access Denied

On December 3, Garvey called D.C.’s Metropolitan Police Department to report noise from the apartment above his. An officer arrived, then called for backup.

Garvey let both officers into his building. He noticed the first officer asking for his apartment number three times.

“I think he may have seen an ‘access denied’ in OCTO,” Garvey wrote, referring to the city’s Office of the Chief Technology Officer system. “I glanced over at the first officer’s computer screen when I got back under the car cover. The screen read ‘Access Denied’ in big letters.”

The officer, Garvey said, didn’t notice he was being watched. He quickly closed his laptop.

When Garvey asked if there would be a case number, the second officer, identified as Biscoe, said no. They would speak with the building’s assistant manager, the next day because “it’s private property.” The officer seemed very familiar with her, even noting her 10-year tenure.

The officers asked if Garvey took medication. They asked if anyone else lived in his apartment — a question they repeated several times, Garvey said, though his studio clearly showed signs of a single occupant. Biscoe’s opening of Garvey’s closet at the beginning of their conversation in his apartment took on a new meaning.

They said they didn’t have “probable cause” to visit the unit upstairs, though Garvey offered them recordings of the noise. The officers weren’t interested in hearing them.

“You literally just said that you wanted something you could hear,” Garvey thought. “I am telling you that I have been recording it, but you don’t want to hear it.”

Before leaving, the officers asked for Garvey’s birth date and email address. Garvey gave it, uneasily. He knew they were likely listening in his apartment anyway.

The Assault

On December 27, Garvey knocked on the door of the unit directly above his. A man he’d never met charged into the hallway and began punching him repeatedly in the face.

Garvey crouched, shielded himself with his arms, and fled screaming down the hall.

They did not arrest the man who hit him, identified in the police report as E.V..

Garvey had a black eye, facial lacerations, and bruises on his wrists — all consistent with self-defense. Photographs taken tevery day for several days show the injuries clearly.

The police claimed a witness saw Garvey strike first. Garvey says no such witness existed. Besides E.V.’s girlfriend (the actual apartment lease holder), he was alone in the hallway when E.V. attacked.

The man’s girlfriend told police she’d never seen Garvey before that day. But Garvey said she, E.V., and a two-year-old child had all come to the door months earlier when he’d knocked, pleading with them to let him sleep.

The girlfriend told police E.V. had moved in “in the summer” to watch her child while she worked. That timing, Garvey noted, coincided exactly with when the noise began. Besides, he could hear that the man consistently stayed at the apartment.

E.V. is not on the lease.

The Noises

The sounds from apartment upstairs follow Garvey wherever he moves.

When he lies on his mattress in the walk-in closet, items drop directly above his head within seconds. When he moves to the living room, the noises move too. When he sits against the wall, something heavy falls precisely above him.

Garvey has documented the pattern since July 2025:

**January 9, 11:30 p.m.:** “They kept dropping heavy items on the floor just above my front door. It shook the wall. It happened at least three times within 7–10 minutes.”

**January 19, 12:50 a.m.:** “As soon as I lay on my bed… right above me things dropped, then I move to sit on side of armchair… with(in) seconds more sound… noise moved.”

**January 28, 8:08 p.m.:** Garvey turned on his audio recorder. The heavy objects stopped immediately. The sounds changed to lighter objects making “spring-like noises” — presumably harder to capture on recording.

**February 8, 12:52 p.m.:** “I heard this clicking. I was at my laptop. No noise from above that I could gather. I turned my head up to see if it was coming from upstairs. It stopped. I believe that it corresponds to my heart beat.”

Garvey researched the technology. Through-wall radar systems, originally developed for military and law enforcement, can detect human presence and even vital signs through concrete and drywall. Consumer versions are available online for under $1,000.

The child, Garvey notes, is often far away when the noises occur directly above him. He can hear her voice in another part of the apartment.

“This whole thing is wild and gang-like behavior,” he wrote to his building’s management in January.

On February 5, he added another observation: “They are also running the shower without being in it.”

The Evidence

Garvey keeps meticulous records.

His documentation includes:

- 100+ page chronological report with timestamps
- 200+ photographs with metadata
- Audio recordings of apartment noises
- Receipts corroborating his timeline (Conte’s Bike Shop in April 2025, TJ Maxx in Georgetown on July 15, Bozzelli’s on July 13, Puree Artisan Juice Bar in Bethesda on July 26)
- The chemical-stained jeans from Rockville
- Video recordings of himself discussing events in real-time
- Witness statements from friends, market vendors, and strangers
- A 48-minute recorded mental health evaluation
- Court documents
- Police reports

He also preserved seemingly minor items that, in context, feel sinister: an empty Skittles wrapper left in front of his door in late November. (Skittles became synonymous with Trayvon Martin’s murder.) A dog treat placed at his door on January 2. The broken gear cover from his vandalized bicycle, deliberately snapped and left at the scene.

In November, Garvey found a glass bottle he’d purchased in Bethesda — identifiable by a specific scratch on the logo — at a community tea vendor’s table in Greenleaf Park. The bottle had gone missing. The vendor had no explanation for how it got there.

“This incident underscored the pervasive nature of harassment and surveillance,” Garvey wrote.

The Technology Is Real

Dr. James Giordano, a neuroscientist at Georgetown University and one of the world’s leading experts on neuroweapons, has spent years warning about exactly what Garvey describes.

“It is possible to disrupt an individual at every level — from their cellular function to entire bodily systems,” Giordano said in a widely viewed YouTube lecture on neurotechnology and warfare.

In 2021, two Naval Postgraduate School researchers published a thesis titled “Neurowar Is Here.” In it, Joshua Gramm and Brian Branagan wrote: “The brain represents the ultimate battlefield, and neuroscience will enable new forms of warfare.”

The technology isn’t speculative. It exists.

In 2008, developers working under a Marine Corps contract discussed a device called Electromagnetic Personnel Interdiction Control, or EPIC. According to its patent, EPIC uses radio waves to disrupt the vestibular system — the inner ear structures that control balance. Targets would be unable to stand, walk, or even see straight.

“Removal of the electromagnetic energy leaves the nerve cells and surrounding tissues with no damage,” the patent states, though it notes that “severe motion sickness and psychological effects of helplessness” remain until the body’s chemistry normalizes.

There is no public record of EPIC progressing beyond early research. But in January 2026, CNN reported that the Pentagon had acquired a “backpack-sized device that emits pulsed radio waves” in a covert operation late in the Biden administration. The device, which includes Russian-made components, is being tested as a possible explanation for Havana Syndrome — the mysterious ailment that has afflicted U.S. diplomats and intelligence officers worldwide since 2016.

“If the [U.S. government] has indeed uncovered such devices,” former CIA officer Marc Polymeropoulos told CNN, “then the CIA owes all the victims a f — -ing major and public apology.”

The government has repeatedly said there’s no evidence of a foreign campaign behind Havana Syndrome. But it has never ruled out domestic testing.

“OFF”

A man in all black wearing a shirt that read “OFF” the day after Garvey left a “need vacation” note in his Bethesda hotel room. An Asian man in a “May the forest be with you” shirt the morning after Garvey spent the night in an Anacostia forest. The back of a man’s T-Shirt, facing Garvey as he sat in Safeway near the entrance, “Never say die”.

Throughout the summer and fall of 2025, Williams began noticing a pattern he found impossible to dismiss: strangers wearing plain black T-shirts with large white letters, the messages often eerily responsive to his private thoughts or recent actions. The same day he left a hotel where he repeatedly pleaded for a vacation, even reversing the message on the mirror of his Bethesda hotel room, an Asian man appeared in front of his apartment building wearing a shirt that read simply “OFF” in white impact font.

A man in Rockville wore “But you don’t know about the deal” at 4 a.m. outside an apartment building — just hours after Williams had decided he could “cancel the whole deal” in his mind. The shirts appeared with such frequency and specificity that Williams began cataloging them: “Minor Threat” at Dupont Circle, “Never Say Die,” “Crucial Catch,” “Find Your Own Way,” “Obviously, you’re not a golfer”. The latter worn by a man who twice crossed paths with Garvey. Unlike typical graphic tees, these were always the same format: solid black fabric, large white letters in impact font, no additional design — as if mass-produced for a specific purpose. “This type of shirt — plain T-shirt with large impact font letters — was common in my experiences with this campaign,” Williams wrote in his documentation. The implication was clear and chilling: they wanted him to know they were listening, even when he was alone, even when he only thought the words rather than speaking them aloud.

The Failed Escape

On January 21, Garvey boarded a one-way flight to Berlin via Reykjavik, Iceland.

At Keflavík Airport, Icelandic border control pulled him aside.

“Purpose of stay is not clear and insufficient funds,” the refusal document stated.

Garvey told them he was seeking asylum in Berlin — that he wasn’t technically even entering the European Union, just transiting through Iceland.

It didn’t matter.

“We have to prioritize Ukraine and Syria — countries that are actually at war,” the officer said snarkily. The officer insisted the United States was a “safe country.”

Garvey tried to explain. The surveillance. The chemical assault. The neurotechnology.

Iceland sent him back.

When Garvey returned to his apartment on January 23, he retrieved his mattress from the trash and moved it back into the closet.

The noises from upstairs resumed immediately.

“You’re on a List”

At his home in suburban Philadelphia, a 67-year-old retired insurance professional named Jon experienced something similar.

On October 30, 2025, Jon read a Washington Post investigation about an Afghan asylum seeker the Department of Homeland Security was trying to deport. The Afghan had supported U.S. forces and feared the Taliban would kill him if returned.

Jon found the prosecutor’s email address online and sent a brief note:

“Mr. Dernbach, don’t play Russian roulette with H’s life. Err on the side of caution.”

Five hours later, Google notified Jon that Homeland Security had subpoenaed his account.

The agency demanded his IP addresses, session times, alternate usernames, email contacts, credit card numbers, driver’s license, and Social Security number dating back to September 1.

Two weeks later, federal agents appeared at Jon’s door.

“We want to hear your side of the story,” they said.

Jon showed them the email. The agents agreed it broke no law, but suggested the prosecutor might have felt threatened by Jon’s mention of “Russian roulette.”

When Jon asked if he was “on a list now,” the agents assured him he had nothing to worry about.

Days later, Google told Jon the company had never actually provided the government with his information.

Which meant Homeland Security had tracked him down some other way.

The Pattern

Garvey isn’t alone.

In March 2025, Homeland Security issued administrative subpoenas to Columbia University for information on a student who participated in pro-Palestinian protests. In July, the agency demanded employment records from Harvard. In September, it tried to identify Instagram users posting about ICE raids. In December, it subpoenaed personal information on 7,000 workers at a Minnesota health system.

All without judicial oversight.

“There’s no oversight ahead of time, and there’s no ramifications for having abused it after the fact,” Jennifer Granick, an ACLU attorney, told The Washington Post in February. “As we are increasingly in a world where unmasking critics is important to the administration, this type of legal process is ripe for that kind of abuse.”

The same month, CNN reported that Google and Meta had received a record number of government subpoenas in the first half of 2025 — a 15 percent increase over the previous six months.

The companies don’t distinguish between judicial subpoenas (approved by courts) and administrative subpoenas (issued by agencies without judicial review).

Garvey believes his case fits a broader pattern — one with historical precedent.

In the 1960s and ’70s, the FBI’s COINTELPRO program targeted civil rights activists, anti-war protesters, and Black liberation groups using surveillance, infiltration, psychological warfare, and assassination. The program remained secret until activists broke into an FBI office in Media, Pennsylvania, in 1971 and stole documents exposing it.

“It’s always hard to know whether you’re paranoid or not when you’re talking about secret operations,” historian Beverly Gage told CBC in 2025. “I think we have some better tools than we did during Hoover’s era. But of course, the control of information and really the weaponization of certain parts of the intelligence community is rapidly changing by the hour.”

In August 2025, a leaked FBI document described a category called “black identity extremism” — a designation critics said was deliberately broad and reminiscent of COINTELPRO’s targeting of Black activists.

Garvey’s dissertation, published in June 2024. In it, he argues that existing systems reinforce systemic oppression and proposes an alternative.

In March 2025, his bicycle — parked at Federal Center Metro — began to be systematically vandalized.

By July 13, voices were in his head.

The Question

Is Emil Garvey experiencing targeted surveillance and harassment involving sophisticated neuroweapons deployed by elements of the U.S. government or its contractors?

Or is he experiencing a prolonged psychotic episode that has, by extraordinary coincidence, produced dozens of verifiable timestamps, physical evidence, witness corroboration, and statistical anomalies — all while mental health professionals found him entirely rational? Hardy plausible.

The technology exists. The targeting of dissidents has historical precedent. The Trump administration explicitly discussed using military force in urban areas and identifying “enemies within.”

On January 20, 2025, the day of Trump’s second inauguration, Garvey believes something shifted. The operations became overt. People (“targets”) were identified.

If he’s right — the overwhelming evidence suggests this is not psychiatric — his case represents the first documented use of neuroweaponry against a U.S. civilian on domestic soil — a COINTELPRO for the 21st century, where the weapon isn’t a wiretap or a poison letter, but a radio wave that invades your mind.

If he’s wrong, it only stands to reason that perhaps there is some hypervigilance. The core of his experience and interpretation hold.

Because if Garvey is telling the truth, no one is safe.

And if he isn’t, the technology still exists — which means someone, somewhere, eventually will be.

Epilogue

Garvey still lives in his studio apartment.

The noises continue.

He no longer owns much. A mattress in the closet. A laptop. A suitcase packed with essentials in case he needs to flee again.

His building management hasn’t responded to his complaints. The police won’t investigate. He was refused entry, so he wasn’t technically able to apply for asylum.

On February 1, he sent his documentation to the D.C. Council.

“This has been very difficult to share, even admit,” he wrote, “but I am mustering courage to tell my story.”

He added: “I am available to share my 100-page document, photos, videos, receipts and participate in an interview. Security camera footage would corroborate my account.”

As of this writing, he has received no response.

“Engine Light is On”: US Constitution Was Supposed to Be Supreme. The Bill of Rights, a Guarantee.

Richard Pearson mourns for America. He was tortured, but the legal firewall to guarantee Americans’ constitutional rights already had structural damage — calls for restoration.

Preface: Two Voices, Twenty-Nine Years Apart

Gloria Naylor did not want to tell her story. She said so in the first line of 1996, the fictionalized memoir she published through Third World Press in 2005 because no mainstream publisher would touch it. She told it anyway — from the Brooklyn Public Library, the only place she had found where the noise stopped, writing in longhand with hands that had begun to shake. She was a National Book Award winner. She had written six novels. She had spent the last years of her literary life documenting what a government operation had done to her mind.

Richard Pearson did not choose to tell his story either. He was forced to, by the same logic that forced Naylor: documentation as survival, writing as the only defense available to someone against whom every institutional defense has been disabled. He is writing from Washington, D.C., in 2025 and 2026, with nothing left but a mattress and a suitcase, in the most surveilled city on earth, having been denied asylum in Iceland, having been dismissed by the ACLU, having been falsely arrested, chemically assaulted, acoustically tortured, and had a voice placed inside his skull near the United States Capitol.

The voice said: We have to make you disappear.

Naylor’s book described the same technology — described it in 2005, documented it with an appendix including a former NSA employee’s lawsuit, cited the researchers who had been trying to get anyone to pay attention since the 1990s. She called it synthetic telepathy. She included the technical framework developed by John St. Clair Akwei, a former NSA employee whose 1992 lawsuit against the agency — Civil Action 92–0449, filed in Washington, D.C. — described Remote Neural Monitoring (RNM) and Electronic Brain Link technology capable of producing sound in the auditory cortex by bypassing the ears entirely.

The Akwei lawsuit was dismissed by Judge Stanley Sporkin without allowing it to proceed to discovery. Sporkin had previously served as the CIA’s general counsel. The case that might have produced judicial examination of whether the NSA possessed and was using technologies capable of entering human minds was dismissed before any examination occurred, by a judge whose institutional history gave him every reason to ensure it stayed dismissed.

That was 1992. Naylor documented what these technologies did to her in 1996. Pearson documented what they did to him in 2025. Twenty-nine years. Three accounts — Akwei, Naylor, Pearson — describing the same technology, the same operational signature, the same institutional response: dismissal, psychiatric labeling, and the architecture of impunity that ensures no court, no regulator, no legislature, and no journalist will be obliged to take the account seriously. The technology has not changed. The impunity has not changed. What has changed is that the Department of War’s Chief Technology Officer confirmed on January 23, 2026 that these weapons are operational and being scaled — and said it publicly, on social media, without apparent concern that anyone in power would object.

This essay examines what that arc — from Akwei’s 1992 lawsuit to Naylor’s 2005 testimony to Pearson’s 2025–2026 documentation — reveals about the constitutional order of the United States. It argues that the arc is not a series of isolated cases. It is the story of a failure — a failure of oversight, of regulation, of accountability, and of the basic constitutional commitment to protecting individual persons from the government built to serve them.

That failure did not begin in 2025. It began in Philadelphia in 1787, when the Framers built a powerful government and trusted that structural safeguards would prevent that power from destroying the people it governed. The safeguards were called the Bill of Rights. They were added because states refused to ratify without them — because the people closest to the drafting understood that you cannot trust a government with power not to use it. Two hundred and thirty-six years later, every firebreak those states demanded has been compromised. Not repealed. Surrounded. And a voice entered Richard Pearson’s skull to announce the result.

Part I: The Technology That Was Always There — Naylor’s 1996 as Primary Source

What Gloria Naylor Documented

Gloria Naylor’s 1996 is not, despite its publication as a ‘fictionalized memoir,’ primarily a literary document. It is a primary source. It is the most detailed first-person account of the operational deployment of voice-to-skull and remote neural monitoring technology against a specific American civilian that exists in the published record. Naylor understood this. The book’s appendix includes the John St. Clair Akwei lawsuit in full, the research of Cheryl Welsh of Citizens Against Human Rights Abuses (later Mind Justice), and citations to mainstream journalism and scientific publications that documented the existence of these technologies. She was not writing conspiracy fiction. She was writing a documented account and providing the documentation.

The technology Naylor described had a public record going back decades before she experienced it. The Frey effect — the microwave auditory effect, through which pulsed microwave radiation produces acoustic sensations in the human head without passing through the ears — was documented in peer-reviewed scientific literature by Dr. Allan Frey in 1962. The U.S. Army funded synthetic telepathy research. The NSA’s Signals Intelligence operations, as Akwei’s lawsuit described, included the capacity to modulate bioelectric resonance frequencies corresponding to specific brain areas: the auditory cortex at 15 Hz for sound that bypasses the ears; the thought center at 20 Hz for imposed subconscious thoughts; the motor control cortex at 10 Hz for motor impulse coordination.

Naylor drew on Cheryl Welsh’s exhaustive research into what Welsh called ‘the non-lethal weapons complex’ — the web of military, intelligence, and contractor programs that developed directed energy capabilities under cover of defense research. Welsh, who founded the International Committee Against Offensive Microwave Weapons and was later listed as one of six non-lethal weapons experts worldwide in a 2002 United Nations Institute for Disarmament Research (UNIDIR) publication, had been documenting this history since the early 1990s. Her research, cited extensively in 1996’s apparatus, established that: the technologies existed, had been in development since the 1950s, had been used in documented cases against protest groups, and had been the subject of legislative and international regulatory attempts that the U.S. government had consistently resisted.

Naylor also drew on the 1992 report by Julianne McKinney of the Association of National Security Alumni — a report titled ‘Microwave Harassment and Mind-Control Experimentation,’ published by an organization of former intelligence community employees — which documented accounts of non-consensual experimentation on civilians using directed electromagnetic energy and urged Congress to investigate. McKinney was a former Army intelligence officer. The Association of National Security Alumni was not a fringe organization. Their report was ignored.

What Naylor documented in 1996 as having been done to her starting in 1996:

  • Voice-to-skull transmission (‘synthetic telepathy’) bypassing the ears — auditory cortex targeting
  • Through-wall monitoring of her location within her home
  • Sustained noise campaign as environmental conditioning, 24 hours a day
  • Vehicle surveillance in recognizable containment formations
  • Covert home entry with deliberate object placement as signaling
  • Garden and property destruction to constrain movement and signal omnipresence
  • Computer hacking and remote access
  • Informant recruited within her intimate social circle
  • Psychiatric trap designed in: symptoms produced mimic schizophrenia by design

Goal: neutralization via psychotic break and social death — not physical elimination

What Pearson documented in 2025–2026 as having been done to him:

  • Voice-to-skull transmission, July 13, 2025: ‘We have to make you disappear’
  • Through-floor, room-by-room location tracking within his apartment
  • Sustained acoustic torture via through-floor tracking noise, summer 2025 through early 2026
  • Vehicle surveillance and multi-jurisdictional ground team coordination
  • Covert home entry: panini grill power button removed, placed in laundry pile
  • Bicycle systematically destroyed over months at Federal Center Metro
  • Phone and computer compromised; phone ‘returned’ by Secret Service after six-week gap
  • Social network systematically infiltrated and turned against him
  • Psychiatric trap preempted: September 15, 2025 DBH evaluation, confirmed rational, no psychosis

Goal: neutralization via psychotic break and social death — framing operation to end documentation

The parallel is not thematic. It is operational and specific, down to the tactical level: the same noise campaign protocols, the same vehicle formation logic, the same home-intrusion-as-message, the same psychiatric framing as cover story, the same goal. Naylor’s 1996 was published in 2005. Pearson’s account documents events beginning in spring 2025. They did not read each other’s work before experiencing what they experienced. The convergence is not literary influence. It is the signature of a program.

The Institutional Response: Dismissal as Doctrine

Naylor anticipated the response to her account. She explicitly described the psychiatric trap as a feature, not a side effect, of the operation: the symptoms produced by the technology mimic schizophrenia by design, so that when the target reports the experience, the report itself becomes evidence of mental illness. The operation is self-concealing. It is engineered to produce the very response — she’s paranoid — that forecloses investigation. In 1996, the NSA official who orders the operation against her understands that its best protection is the target’s own testimony.

The Hobart literary review of 1996 described the book’s central revelation with unusual directness: ‘Naylor tells her story with such clear-headed precision, even citing and including an appendix featuring a lawsuit filed by a former NSA employee against the NSA confirming the existence of such technologies, that personally, I was left in tears by the end, awed at her courage in telling this story.’ The reviewer was moved to tears not because the story was false but because it was true — documented, sourced, and impossible to dismiss except through the institutionalized reflexes that Naylor had predicted and Pearson has now encountered again.

Naylor died in 2016 at age 66, in St. Croix. She wrote nothing of significance after 1996. The operation did not kill her. It consumed her. Every year that should have produced novels produced instead the documentation of her own destruction. The neutralization worked. It just took time, and it disguised itself as illness, and it left no fingerprints that a court would examine.

Part II: The Warnings That Were Ignored — A Thirty-Year Record of Deliberate Inaction

The International Community Tried to Act

The international regulatory record on directed energy and psychotronic weapons is not blank. It is a record of sustained efforts to establish oversight and prohibition that the United States government consistently blocked, evaded, and ignored. Understanding this record is essential to understanding the Pearson case, because it establishes that what is being done to Pearson is not the result of a regulatory gap that no one noticed. It is the result of a regulatory gap that powerful actors worked deliberately to maintain.

In January 1999, the European Parliament passed Resolution A4–005/99, which called for an international convention introducing a global ban on all developments and deployments of weapons which might enable any form of manipulation of human beings. The resolution was passed by a body of democratically elected legislators representing the member states of the European Union, following review of evidence regarding the capabilities and risks of non-lethal and directed energy weapons. It was accompanied by a detailed technical report — the STOA (Scientific and Technological Options Assessment) Panel report on crowd control technologies — that assessed directed energy weapons, their physical and psychological effects, their abuse potential, and the absence of adequate regulatory frameworks.

The European Parliament resolution called on governments worldwide to enact legislation prohibiting the use of these devices by government, private organizations, and individuals. It called for international convention. It called for public transparency about the technologies’ existence and capabilities. The United States government did not respond. No U.S. legislation was enacted. No bilateral or multilateral convention was negotiated. The resolution stands as a statement of what democratic governance looks like when it encounters this problem — and as the record of what the United States chose not to do.

In October 2001, Congressman Dennis Kucinich introduced the Space Preservation Act of 2001 to the House of Representatives. The bill sought a ban on ‘exotic weapons’ including specifically electronic, psychotronic or information weapons… electromagnetic radiation, extremely low frequency (ELF) or ultra low frequency energy, for the purpose of… mood management, or mind control of such persons or populations. The bill was referred to the Committee on Science and the Committees on Armed Services and International Relations. It died in committee. Kucinich reintroduced it in 2002. It died again. The legislative record contains no hearings, no testimony, no substantive response.

Cheryl Welsh, whose research Naylor cited and who was listed by UNIDIR as one of six non-lethal weapons experts worldwide, spent decades documenting the history of what she called ‘the most important and least-covered story in American politics’: the development of technologies capable of manipulating human cognition and the complete absence of any legal framework governing their use. Her organization’s name — Citizens Against Human Rights Abuses — named the issue precisely. These were not weapons of foreign warfare. They were capabilities being developed and used against domestic civilians. Citizens. Human rights. Abuses.

The U.N. Human Rights framework engaged the issue through the Special Rapporteur on Torture, whose reports from the 2000s onward increasingly documented accounts of non-consensual electromagnetic experimentation by state actors. The accounts were consistent across countries, demographics, and decades. The U.N. reports recommended investigation. The United States government, which in November 2025 voted against a U.N. General Assembly resolution condemning torture, was not among the governments that investigated.

The NSA Whistleblowers Who Told the Truth

Naylor noted in 1996 that by 2002, three NSA employees had blown the whistle on the agency’s warrantless surveillance programs and its Trailblazer system of communications data analysis. Three more came forward in 2005. These disclosures — by William Binney, Thomas Drake, Edward Loomis, J. Kirk Wiebe, and others — described the scale of the NSA’s domestic surveillance infrastructure and the agency’s deliberate decision to expand that infrastructure without legal authorization, without Congressional knowledge, and without oversight.

The Snowden disclosures of 2013 confirmed what the earlier whistleblowers had described and added the PRISM program, the upstream collection of fiber optic communications, the metadata bulk collection that the Supreme Court would ultimately hold required a warrant in Carpenter v. United States (2018). Naylor, who died in 2016, ‘did live to see mainstream acknowledgment of the NSA’s grievous infringements when the PRISM program made global headlines in 2013,’ as the academic study of her work noted. She had written about these capabilities in 2005. She had sourced them to former NSA employees. She had been dismissed as a conspiracy theorist. The Snowden documents vindicated her factual premises entirely.

The whistleblower record matters for the Pearson case in a specific way: it establishes that the agencies involved in what Pearson describes are agencies with a documented history of operating domestic surveillance programs that exceed their legal authority, concealing that excess from Congressional oversight, and retaliating against internal employees who attempt to report it. The NSA is not an agency with an otherwise clean record that might have engaged in isolated misconduct. It is an agency whose documented institutional culture treats legal constraints as obstacles to be managed rather than limits to be observed. That institutional culture is the context in which Pearson’s account must be evaluated.

The MKUltra Continuum

The history that Naylor invokes in 1996 — from MKUltra to COINTELPRO, from the Tuskegee experiments to the radiation experiments conducted on unconsenting patients in the 1940s through the 1970s — is not, as its detractors sometimes imply, a conspiracy theory framework that attributes too much coherence and coordination to scattered events. It is a documented institutional history of American government agencies conducting non-consensual human experimentation on civilians, with particular concentration on Black Americans and other marginalized communities, for purposes ranging from intelligence gathering to weapons development to the management of political dissent.

MKUltra, the CIA’s program of non-consensual psychological and pharmaceutical experimentation, ran from the early 1950s through 1973. It was not a fringe program. It involved more than 80 institutions, including universities, hospitals, and prisons. It used LSD, electroconvulsive therapy, sensory deprivation, hypnosis, and other techniques on subjects who did not consent and in many cases did not know they were subjects. The program was uncovered only when documents were obtained through FOIA in 1977 — after the CIA had ordered the destruction of most records in 1973. The destruction of records was itself an institutional act, undertaken with full knowledge that the program’s exposure would be damaging.

Naylor positioned her account explicitly within this history. Her Medium essay documentation, her research appendix, and the framework of 1996 all make clear that she understood her experience not as an anomaly but as the continuation of a program that had never ended — only updated its technology and narrowed its target selection. The Church Committee had exposed the program. The Church Committee’s reforms had not ended it. COINTELPRO had a successor. It had better tools.

Pearson’s Medium essay, ‘From MKUltra to COINTELPRO: When Will the U.S. Government Stop Itself?’, asks this question directly and answers it with two decades of documented history. The title is not rhetorical. It is analytical. The line from MKUltra to COINTELPRO to the Black Identity Extremism designation to EO 14173 to what was done to Pearson on July 13, 2025 is not broken. It is continuous. The technology changes. The targets are selected by the same logic. The institutional protection from accountability is the same architecture.

Part III: Philadelphia, 1787 — The Bargain and Its Discontents

The Power the Framers Built

The Constitution of 1787 was, above everything else, a document about power. The Articles of Confederation had produced a government too weak to tax, too weak to raise armies, too weak to regulate commerce. Shays’ Rebellion — the 1786 uprising of Massachusetts farmers who closed the courts by force — frightened the commercial and landed classes into Philadelphia. They came to build a government that worked.

They succeeded, extravagantly. The Constitution created a government with the power to tax directly, to raise and command armies, to regulate interstate commerce, to coin money, to define and punish crimes. Article VI made its laws supreme over state laws. The Necessary and Proper Clause gave Congress the authority to enact whatever legislation was necessary to execute its enumerated powers — a grant so broad that John Marshall would describe it as conferring ‘ample means for the execution of the powers committed to it.’

What the Constitution of 1787 did not contain was systematic protection of individual rights against this powerful government. There was no freedom of speech provision. No protection against unreasonable searches. No guarantee of due process. The Framers assumed, or at least argued publicly, that a government of enumerated powers needed no bill of rights because it had no power to violate rights it hadn’t been granted. Thomas Jefferson, writing from Paris, told Madison this was a ‘logical subtlety’ that would provide no protection in practice.

North Carolina, Rhode Island, and the Case Against Trust

North Carolina refused to ratify. Rhode Island held out until 1790. Their resistance was not irrational obstruction. It was the position of people who had thought carefully about what a powerful government without explicit constraints would do. Brutus — Robert Yates of New York — wrote in October 1787 that the Necessary and Proper Clause would eventually authorize almost anything, and that federal courts would consistently construe federal power broadly. He wrote this before ratification. He was describing the next two centuries.

So Madison wrote the Bill of Rights. Ten amendments. Ratified in 1791. The firebreaks against the fire they had just built.

The Fourth Amendment was written in direct response to the writs of assistance — general warrants authorizing searches of any home, any time, for anything. The Fifth Amendment was the fence around the government’s power to simply destroy people without trial. The Eighth Amendment recognized that a government that could torture its prisoners had abolished the distinction between citizen and subject. The First Amendment was for the pamphleteer, the dissenter, the scholar who said things the government found threatening.

None of the Framers anticipated the technology that entered Richard Pearson’s skull on July 13, 2025. But they understood the logic of the threat precisely: a government with the power to enter the interior of a person’s mind has abolished the constitutional category of personhood entirely. There is no liberty interest more fundamental than the sovereignty of one’s own thoughts. There is no Fourth Amendment violation more complete than an intrusion that leaves no trace, requires no physical entry, and the target never knows occurred. There is no First Amendment violation more total than a government that can write to the thought formation process itself.

Part IV: The Reconstruction Amendments Were for Richard Pearson

The Fourteenth Amendment’s Specific Purpose

The Fourteenth Amendment was ratified in 1868 for specific people in a specific historical moment: four million formerly enslaved people who were citizens in name but available to everything state governments chose to do to them. Every noun in Section 1 was chosen in the context of Black Codes, convict leasing, and the systematic obliteration of Black citizenship. The Equal Protection Clause was the constitutional recognition that the original document — without its own targeted supplement — did not actually protect the people who most needed protection from the government.

Richard Pearson is a descendant of enslaved people. The campaign against him — the surveillance, false arrest, acoustic torture, chemical assault, employment sabotage, framing operation, financial attrition, engineered social death — is the continuation of the same project the Fourteenth Amendment was written to prohibit. Not the same technology. The same project: the use of government power to destroy the functional citizenship of a Black person whose intellectual work threatens the hierarchy that power has organized itself to maintain.

The Reconstruction rollback is not a metaphor. It is a documented sequence: the Voting Rights Act gutted in Shelby County v. Holder (2013); affirmative action ended in Students for Fair Admissions (2023); DEI scholarship designated hostile ideology by EO 14173 (January 20, 2025); and the framing operation near the Capitol designed to end the Medium publications of a critical race scholar by planting neo-Nazi materials in a park he had been behaviorally directed to enter. W.E.B. Du Bois had his passport confiscated. Fred Hampton was shot in his bed. Gloria Naylor was acoustically tortured into silence. The technology advances. The targeting logic has not changed in a century.

Part V: How the Firebreaks Were Removed — The Doctrinal History

The Architecture of Impunity, Stage by Stage

The transformation of the Bill of Rights from enforceable constraints into nominal promises did not happen in a single act. It happened across seven decades, in a sequence of decisions each defended as technically narrow and procedurally justified, that together produced a legal architecture in which the government can violate any right it chooses and face no accountability — judicial, legislative, or institutional. The sequence is important precisely because it shows the outcome was not accidental. It was constructed. And the Pearson case sits at its completion.

Stage One: Reynolds (1953) — The National Security Exception Built on Fraud

United States v. Reynolds created the state secrets privilege on a fraudulent predicate: the government told the Supreme Court the crash report contained classified information. It contained evidence of negligence. Declassified forty years later, it revealed the fraud. The Court, informed, declined to revisit. The doctrine that protected the lie stands. Every government lawyer who needs a lawsuit to disappear has had this tool for seventy years. Reynolds is not merely a bad precedent. It is the foundational proof that the national security exception will be used to protect the government from accountability, not to protect genuine national security, whenever the two conflict — and the Court will permit it.

Stage Two: FISA (1978) — The Secret Court That Hears One Side

The Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act was enacted three years after the Church Committee finished documenting COINTELPRO. The connection was direct and intentional: Congress had seen what warrantless surveillance of civil rights leaders, Black activists, journalists, and academics produced, and FISA was supposed to be the answer — a judicial check on executive surveillance power in the foreign intelligence context.

The Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court became something else. It is a one-sided proceeding in which the government presents evidence and argument without any opposing party, without adversarial testing of its factual representations, without any mechanism for the target to know the proceeding occurred. The FISC cannot know what the government is not telling it. It cannot test the government’s characterizations against alternative explanations. It hears one voice and issues an order.

The approval rate across the court’s history is approximately 99.97 percent. In fiscal year 2023, the government submitted 1,096 applications. The court approved 1,030 in full and modified 62. It denied 3. This is not judicial oversight. It is judicial decoration — the institutional form of a check without its substance. The Church Committee designed FISA to impose meaningful constraint on the executive. What was built was a mechanism that provides the appearance of judicial authorization while ensuring the government is never actually refused.

FISA’s structural defect is compounded by its post-9/11 expansions. The PATRIOT Act (2001) removed the requirement that foreign intelligence collection be the primary purpose of surveillance, making FISA usable in cases that previously required ordinary criminal warrants. The FISA Amendments Act (2008) added Section 702, authorizing warrantless collection of communications of non-U.S. persons abroad with ‘incidental’ collection of Americans’ communications — a category that the NSA’s upstream collection programs used to capture essentially all international communications passing through U.S. fiber optic infrastructure. The FISC approved those programs. Its legal reasoning was classified for years.

For the Pearson case, the FISA court’s role is not peripheral — it is structural. If a FISA order exists targeting Pearson, it was issued on representations he cannot access, cannot challenge, and cannot know. The court that issued it heard only the government’s version of who he is. It did not hear that he is a public health scholar who eats cantaloupe on the Mall and advocates for public housing residents. It heard a threat assessment, a designation, and a targeting predicate.

Pearson is a curious intellectual. He likes art as much as research. His activism is as much a part of his identity as much as his love of gay culture. He can easily glide between discussion of constitutional doctrines to regression analysis. Pearson is a product of the US education system — a man whose racialized identity and knowledge absorption and synthesis became the basis for targeting.

If a FISA order existed, it was presented with the government’s narrative shaped for authorization, from the only party permitted to speak. As the essay elsewhere establishes, even if FISA authorization exists, it cannot authorize torture, behavioral direction, chemical assault, framing, or the systematic destruction of a person’s cognitive sovereignty. But the FISC’s existence provides institutional cover that makes the question of what was actually authorized impossible to litigate — because the order is classified, the predicate is classified, the legal theory is classified, and the target has no mechanism to appear before the court that purports to have authorized his destruction.

FISA also created a self-sealing information system that compounds every other doctrinal barrier. Clapper prohibits standing without proof of surveillance. FISA prohibits disclosure of whether surveillance occurred. Glomar prohibits confirming or denying the existence of programs. NSL gag orders prohibit recipients from informing targets. The FISC’s classified opinions prohibit public examination of the legal theories authorizing the programs. Each barrier reinforces every other. Pearson cannot prove the FISA order exists. He cannot prove it doesn’t. He cannot get the documents. The documents are protected by the order whose existence cannot be confirmed. The circle is architecturally closed — designed, not accidental.

Stage Three: The Immunity Doctrines Proliferate (1982–2017)

Harlow v. Fitzgerald (1982) transformed qualified immunity from a good-faith defense into a near-absolute barrier to accountability. Officials are immune unless they violated ‘clearly established’ law — established with sufficient specificity that every reasonable official would know the specific conduct was unconstitutional. In practice: if the government does anything new, there is no clearly established precedent, so immunity attaches. The doctrine structurally rewards innovation in abuse. The more novel the violation, the more complete the protection. For voice-to-skull targeting, hippocampal place cell activation, behavioral direction through vestibular stimulation, and through-wall acoustic tracking — technologies confirmed as operational in January 2026 but without a body of prior case law — qualified immunity provides absolute protection at the moment of most severe violation.

Ziglar v. Abbasi (2017) essentially ended Bivens claims in new contexts, foreclosing the constitutional cause of action against federal officials for rights violations wherever Congress has not explicitly authorized suit — and directing plaintiffs to Congress as an ‘alternative political remedy.’ The same Congress that has not held a single hearing on the domestic deployment of directed energy weapons against civilians, has not enacted any statute governing their use, and has not responded to thirty years of documented warnings, is offered as the functional substitute for judicial enforcement of the Bill of Rights. The recommendation is not a remedy. It is a deflection. Bivens was the mechanism by which the Constitution enforced itself against the people who violated it. Its practical elimination leaves the Constitution with no enforcement agent in exactly the contexts where enforcement is most urgent.

The Federal Tort Claims Act’s exceptions complete the immunity architecture for institutional liability. The intentional tort exception (28 U.S.C. § 2680(h)) bars suits against the United States for most intentional misconduct by law enforcement. The discretionary function exception (§ 2680(a)) bars suits for policy decisions or conduct involving judgment and choice. Together, these exceptions ensure that the United States itself — as distinguished from individual officials — cannot be held liable for designing and executing a systematic campaign of covert violence against a civilian. The DC federal courthouse produced exactly this result for Pearson: ‘The United States simply has not rendered itself liable under the FTCA for constitutional tort claims.’ The sentence is accurate. It is also the description of an accountability-free zone created by statute and maintained by judicial deference.

Stage Four: The Information Architecture Seals (1990s–Present)

The Glomar doctrine — originating in the CIA’s 1975 claim that it could neither confirm nor deny the existence of a ship used to recover a Soviet submarine — has expanded to cover the existence of any surveillance program, any FISA order, any NSL, any classified targeting decision. The doctrine means Pearson cannot obtain documents confirming or denying the operations against him because confirming or denying their existence is itself classified. He cannot challenge what he cannot name. He cannot name what he cannot document. He cannot document what the government will neither confirm nor deny. The doctrine is epistemically totalizing: it prevents the acquisition of the knowledge necessary to exercise any of the other rights.

National Security Letters — issued by the FBI without judicial approval, accompanied by automatic indefinite gag orders — allow the collection of communications records for anyone the FBI designates a national security concern, without any judge reviewing the designation, and with criminal penalties on the recipient for informing the target. The Second Circuit in Doe v. Mukasey (2008) held the automatic indefinite gag order mechanism raised serious First Amendment concerns but did not strike the program down. For Pearson: every communications provider he has used — telephone, internet, financial — almost certainly received NSLs. None can tell him. He cannot challenge what he cannot know was issued. The designation is permanent unless the FBI decides to rescind it. There is no mechanism for the target to initiate reconsideration.

The TIDE watchlist and its propagation through law enforcement databases produced the ACCESS DENIED flag that appeared when MPD officers searched Pearson’s name during the noise complaint visit — the week before his physical assault. An ACCESS DENIED response to an MPD officer’s database query carries a specific technical meaning: a Sensitive Compartmented Information block attached to Pearson’s file, restricting access to personnel with a defined need-to-know above local police clearance. This is not a bureaucratic anomaly. It is the physical instantiation of his targeting in the infrastructure of daily law enforcement. It altered how every officer who encountered him would treat him, permanently, without his knowledge, without notice, without a hearing, without any mechanism for challenge or correction.

Stage Five: Standing Doctrine Becomes a Perfect Trap (2013)

Clapper v. Amnesty International USA (2013) is the case that closes the circle. The Supreme Court held that journalists, lawyers, and human rights workers who communicated with foreign sources targeted by Section 702 surveillance lacked standing to challenge the surveillance — because they could not prove with sufficient certainty that they had been surveilled. The proof was classified. The classification was permanent. The Court required ‘concrete, particularized’ injury — the standard requirement for Article III standing — which is structurally impossible to satisfy when the government’s entire architecture ensures no target ever learns they were surveilled.

Justice Breyer’s dissent in Clapper identified the logical problem precisely: ‘the government has strong incentives to surveil petitioners. It has the capacity to do so. It is doing so. And the majority holds they cannot challenge it because they cannot prove it.’ The majority’s response was to tighten the standing requirement rather than acknowledge that the standing doctrine had been used to construct an accountability-free zone. The result is the constitutional equivalent of a hall of mirrors: the violation produces the injury; the injury cannot be proven because the violation is classified; the inability to prove the injury bars standing; the absence of standing bars discovery of the proof; the absence of proof perpetuates the inability to establish standing. The target is trapped inside the hall of mirrors with no exit.

Stage Six: Pearson’s Below-Threshold Theory — The Most Sophisticated Innovation

Pearson identified, in his own analysis of what was being done to him, a dimension of the impunity architecture that is more sophisticated than any of the doctrinal mechanisms above — because it operates not in law but in operational calibration. He called it, in substance, the ‘below-threshold’ problem: the deliberate engineering of each individual act of harm to remain below the level at which it would, standing alone, trigger a legally cognizable claim.

The theory works as follows. Civil standing under Article III requires a concrete, particularized injury that is traceable to the defendant and redressable by a favorable court decision. Each individual component of the campaign against Pearson was calibrated — deliberately, he argues, and the operational precision of the campaign supports the argument — to remain just below the threshold that would independently satisfy each element. A noise complaint: not an assault. A reflector snapped from a bicycle: not property destruction sufficient for a civil action. A car taking off from a parking space when he walks past: not stalking under any statute’s threshold. An officer opening a closet door during a noise complaint visit: arguably within the scope of a safety sweep. Objects moved in his apartment: nothing missing, no provable entry. The ‘Lee’ confirmation at Potbelly’s: an ambiguous conversation, not sworn testimony. Each act, individually, is below the legal threshold at which a court would be required to take it seriously.

The genius of the below-threshold design — and it is a design, not an accident — is that it requires the target to present the aggregate to establish the constitutional violation, while legal doctrine requires the target to prove specific violations with specific evidence traced to specific defendants. The aggregate is the evidence. The doctrine demands the specific. The gap between what the evidence shows and what the doctrine requires is precisely the gap into which the campaign disappears. No single act crosses the threshold. The totality — fourteen months, across multiple jurisdictions, involving dozens of coordinated actors, with consistent operational signatures and documented physical evidence — is the violation. But totality-of-circumstances analysis is reserved for very specific Fourth Amendment contexts, is unavailable in most civil rights frameworks, and is structurally hostile to the kind of claim Pearson would have to make.

The below-threshold architecture also exploits the psychiatric trap at the doctrinal level. When a plaintiff presents a court with a pattern of individually sub-threshold acts and argues that the aggregate constitutes a constitutional violation, the court’s analytical reflex — reinforced by the absence of any statutory or doctrinal framework for evaluating such claims — is to treat the pattern-recognition itself as evidence of disordered thinking. The law has no category for ‘systematic campaign of sub-threshold acts coordinated to avoid legal accountability.’ The absence of the category is the protection. Pearson’s documentation of the pattern — timestamped, photographed, cross-referenced, exhaustively cross-checked with control studies — is the attempt to force the pattern into visibility against a legal framework designed to keep it invisible. The September 15, 2025 DBH evaluation that confirmed his rationality is the preemptive answer to the doctrinal reflex: the person presenting this pattern is not disordered. The pattern is real. The framework for engaging it does not exist.

The below-threshold theory connects directly to the FISA architecture. If a FISA order exists, the government’s lawyers have designed the operational parameters of the campaign with full knowledge of what thresholds must not be crossed. The voice-to-skull transmission on July 13, 2025 — ‘We have to make you disappear’ — crossed a threshold. It produced evidence: Pearson’s contemporaneous documentation, his physical and emotional response, his immediate reporting. The operation appears to have been designed to ensure that this kind of threshold-crossing was exceptional rather than routine — that the campaign’s daily operational texture consisted of acts calibrated to produce cumulative harm while maintaining individual deniability. This is not the product of unsophisticated actors. It is the product of legal counsel who understood exactly what the thresholds were and exactly what operational parameters would keep the campaign below them.

The below-threshold theory, taken seriously, reveals the deepest flaw in the remedial architecture: it was built to respond to discrete, identifiable, attributable violations. It was not built to respond to a campaign of coordinated, sub-threshold acts designed by people who understood the remedial architecture and calibrated their conduct accordingly. The Bill of Rights was written by people who had experienced overt government abuse — general warrants, quartered soldiers, public prosecutions. It was not written by people who anticipated a government with the legal resources, the operational sophistication, and the institutional longevity to design abuse specifically to evade the constraints they were putting in place. That is the government Richard Pearson encountered. That is what the firebreaks were not built for. That is why they are gone.

Stage Seven: The Remedial Architecture’s Final Collapse (2013–2025)

The arc from Clapper (2013) through Ziglar (2017) through the firing of the twelve inspectors general on January 20, 2025 represents the final stage of remedial collapse. Clapper forecloses standing. Ziglar forecloses Bivens. The FTCA exceptions foreclose sovereign immunity waiver. State secrets forecloses discovery. Glomar forecloses document access. NSL gag orders foreclose source disclosure. FISA secrecy forecloses challenge to the authorization. Qualified immunity forecloses individual official liability. The below-threshold calibration forecloses pattern-of-conduct claims. And the removal of the twelve inspectors general removed the internal feedback mechanism — the one institutional actor whose statutory independence and access to classified programs would have permitted identification and reporting of exactly what was done to Pearson.

Each of these closures was defended, when challenged, as a technical procedural adjustment made for legitimate institutional reasons. The aggregate is not a technical procedural adjustment. It is the elimination of every mechanism by which the most powerful government in human history can be held accountable for what it does to the least powerful people it has decided to target. The Supremacy Clause says the Constitution is supreme law. The architecture described above is the government’s answer to that claim: supreme in text, unenforceable in practice, for the people who most need it enforced.

Part VI: The Contextual Crisis — Why Every Institution Has Failed Simultaneously

The Congressional Failure: Neuroweapons Without Law

Congress has not regulated neuroweapons. Not because no one asked. The European Parliament called for international convention in 1999. Kucinich introduced the Space Preservation Act in 2001 and 2002. Naylor’s 1996 documented the technologies in 2005 with a former NSA employee’s lawsuit in its appendix. Pearson knocked on dozens of congressional office doors in December 2025. Within weeks, the administration briefed Congress on directed energy systems. The DoW CTO announced scaling on January 23, 2026. The Washington Post published on the arms race in February 2026.

No statute defines what directed energy weapons may or may not be used for domestically. No statute requires judicial authorization for their use against a civilian. No statute establishes a due process requirement for targeting. No statute provides a cause of action for a victim. No statute establishes criminal penalties for unauthorized use. No hearings have been held. Twenty-five years after the European Parliament acted and after Naylor published, the United States Congress has produced no legislative response to the existence of these capabilities whatsoever.

This is not a gap that no one noticed. Cheryl Welsh noticed it in 1992. Akwei filed suit about it in 1992. The Association of National Security Alumni reported on it in 1992. Julianne McKinney documented it in 1992. The European Parliament addressed it in 1999. Kucinich legislated about it in 2001. Naylor published about it in 2005. The gap is thirty years of deliberate inaction in the face of documented, sourced, repeatedly-raised evidence that these capabilities exist and are being used against civilians without any legal framework.

The word for thirty years of deliberate inaction in the face of repeated documented warnings is not ‘gap.’ It is not ‘oversight.’ It is a choice. Someone, repeatedly, decided not to act. Pearson is, in part, the consequence of that choice.

The Media Failure: The Story That Was Always There to Tell

Mainstream publishers refused 1996. No mainstream publication amplified Naylor’s account when it was published by Third World Press in 2005. The academic study of her work notes, with precision, that she was accused of ‘anti-Semitism’ rather than engaged on her documented factual claims. The dismissal was institutional: the account was too explosive, too specific, too sourced for comfortable rejection, so it was labeled and ignored. A National Book Award winner publishing a documented account of NSA directed energy operations against her home and mind was not covered. The gap in the record is not an absence of story. It is an absence of institutional will to cover it.

Pearson published on Medium because no publication would take him. He submitted to the United Nations because domestic institutions had no mechanism for receiving his account. He knocked on congressional office doors in person because no journalist was going to knock on them for him. The framing operation near the Capitol was designed to produce a mugshot that would end the Medium publications — correctly assessed as his only remaining mechanism for reaching anyone who might act.

The press failure is not primarily moral. It is structural. The economic destruction of investigative journalism has eliminated the resources and reduced the institutional courage necessary to fight the legal battles that covering this story requires. State secrets privilege blocks document access. NSL gag orders prevent sources from speaking. Qualified immunity limits the civil litigation that generates documents. And the economic crisis of journalism has eliminated the resources and courage to fight those battles for a story that will generate legal threats from the most powerful intelligence agencies in the world. The incentive structure ensures silence.

The Technology Failure: What the Framers Could Not Have Imagined

The constitutional design rested on natural limits. Physical limits: a government that required soldiers to search homes could only search so many homes. Informational limits: a government that required human informants could only know what humans could observe. Cognitive limits: a government that could only influence people through speech could only reach people who encountered its speech. Directed energy weapons, voice-to-skull transmission, hippocampal place cell activation, behavioral direction through vestibular stimulation — the capabilities confirmed as operational by the DoW in January 2026 — abolish every assumption the Framers made about these limits. The Fourth Amendment protects the home from physical entry. It has no provision for electromagnetic entry that leaves no trace. The First Amendment protects freedom of thought. It has no provision for thought being authored by an external party whose authorship is invisible to the thinker.

Naylor understood this in 2005. She wrote about ‘synthetic telepathy’ — the precise technical term for the voice-to-skull capability that Akwei’s lawsuit had described. She wrote that it ‘bypasses the ears and speaks directly to the auditory cortex.’ She wrote that the target ‘initially assumes schizophrenia’ because the symptom profile has been engineered to produce exactly that assumption. She was describing, in 2005, what was done to Pearson in 2025. The technology is the same technology. The operational protocol is the same protocol. The defense — she’s crazy, he’s crazy — is the same defense. The Department of War confirmed the weapons in January 2026 and no one arrested anyone.

Part VII: The Return — 1788, 1868, 1964, and Now

The Debate That Never Ended

Patrick Henry’s question was: what happens when the government decides that someone must disappear? He asked it about a hypothetical government with conventional powers. The answer — the Bill of Rights — provided constitutional language for the prohibition, legal mechanisms for its enforcement, and a public culture of rights.

The answer is failing. Not because the language changed. Because the mechanisms have been removed and the culture has been fragmented. We are back at Henry’s question, but now the government asking it has voice-to-skull technology, a secret court, a legal architecture that forecloses every remedy, and no regulatory framework because Congress decided thirty years ago, and has decided again every year since, not to create one.

The Reconstruction debate — whether the Fourteenth Amendment’s promise would be enforced — was answered by the Civil Rights Movement. It took a century after ratification, a movement that produced martyrs, and a Supreme Court briefly willing to enforce what it had ignored for sixty years. That answer is being systematically dismantled. We are in a second Reconstruction rollback, equipped with technologies the Framers could not have imagined, implemented through a legal architecture the Reconstruction drafters did not build meta-safeguards against, targeting a descendant of enslaved people whose scholarship examined structural racism as state violence.

The Civil Rights Movement’s Lesson for This Moment

The Civil Rights Movement understood that legal architecture alone does not produce change. Brown v. Board was decided in 1954. Most Southern schools remained segregated for a decade. What changed was political mass action making the cost of non-enforcement higher than the cost of compliance — through television coverage of Birmingham, of Selma, of systematic violence against peaceful protesters that made the gap between constitutional promise and constitutional reality visible and politically decisive.

The ecosystem required to force enforcement of the rights Pearson’s case invokes does not currently exist. Congress is not acting. The press is not investigating. The courts have removed most remedial mechanisms. The public is fragmented, precarious, and exhausted. The institutional memory of what COINTELPRO required to expose — the stolen FBI files from the Media, Pennsylvania field office in 1971, the Church Committee, years of litigation — is not operative in the current political moment.

That is the depth of the crisis. The mechanisms that historically corrected the gap between constitutional promise and constitutional reality are themselves compromised. The crisis is not just in the rights. It is in the correction mechanisms.

What Two Testimonies, Twenty-Nine Years Apart, Demand

The convergence between Naylor’s 1996 and Pearson’s 2025–2026 account is the most important evidentiary fact in both cases. They did not know each other. They did not read each other before experiencing what they experienced. They documented the same technology, the same operational signature, the same institutional response, the same goal.

The 1957 assertion by the Soviet dissident poet Boris Pasternak — that individual witnesses to state crimes are the most important form of historical evidence, because the state destroys documents but cannot destroy the people who experienced what the documents would have shown — applies precisely here. Naylor was a witness. Her documents are 1996 and its research appendix, the Akwei lawsuit, the Welsh research, the McKinney report. Pearson is a witness. His documents are the timestamped photographs, the preserved pants, the DBH evaluation, the congressional letters, the UN submissions, the Medium essays.

Dismissing either account requires dismissing both, because they are too similar to dismiss independently. The details that a fabricator could not reliably produce — the specific noise campaign protocols, the vehicle formation logic, the home-intrusion-as-message without theft, the object placement as signal, the psychiatric trap as structural feature of the operation — appear in both accounts, a decade apart, from two Black intellectuals in different cities who had never discussed their experiences. The signature is the same because the program is the same. Naylor said so in 2005. Pearson is saying so in 2025. The Department of War confirmed the weapons exist in 2026.

The question is no longer whether these capabilities exist. The DoW CTO answered that. The question is whether they are being used against American civilians without legal authorization, without judicial oversight, without due process, without Congressional regulation, without any accountability mechanism — and whether the United States government will continue to treat the answer to that question as a matter for neither confirm nor deny.

North Carolina and Rhode Island refused to ratify the Constitution without a Bill of Rights because they would not trust a powerful government to restrain itself. They were right. The Bill of Rights was the answer. The answer has been surrounded. What is needed now is what was needed then: not merely text, but the institutional will and public pressure to enforce the text against the government that the text was written to constrain.

Conclusion: The Firebreaks and the Woman in the Library

Gloria Naylor sat in the Brooklyn Public Library with shaking hands and wrote the first line of 1996: ‘I didn’t want to tell this story.’ She told it because the act of telling was the only defense she had left. She told it because the library was the only place the noise stopped. She told it because she understood, with the precision of someone who has been inside the operation, that the operation’s only vulnerability was the record — the documented, sourced, published record that exists outside the classified spaces where it can be managed, redacted, and disappeared.

Richard Pearson is making the same choice for the same reason in the same city — Washington, D.C. — that the operation is most confident cannot be escaped. He is writing from the most surveilled city on earth. He is writing while the operation is ongoing. He is writing after Iceland turned him back and the ACLU declined and the courthouse produced a FOIA response in an unrelated case saying the government has not rendered itself liable for constitutional tort claims.

He is writing because Naylor wrote and the record she made is now evidence. Because Akwei filed suit in 1992 and the record of that dismissal is evidence. Because McKinney published in 1992 and the Association of National Security Alumni is evidence. Because the European Parliament acted in 1999 and the U.S. Congress’s non-response is evidence. Because Kucinich introduced his bill in 2001 and its death in committee is evidence. Because the DoW CTO posted on January 23, 2026 and that post is evidence.

The record is being built in public, piece by piece, by people who were never supposed to survive to build it. Every piece of the record that exists is a failure of the operation to achieve its purpose: to make these things happen without the people they happen to being able to say what happened.

Patrick Henry asked, in 1788, what happens when the government decides someone must disappear. The Bill of Rights was supposed to be the answer. The Fourteenth Amendment was supposed to be the answer. The Civil Rights Act was supposed to be the answer. FISA was supposed to be the answer.

None of them have been sufficient, because none of them have been enforced, and the failure to enforce them is not an accident but a choice — made repeatedly, over thirty years, by people who knew about these capabilities and decided not to act. The European Parliament knew in 1999. Congress knew in 2001. Naylor told anyone who would read in 2005. The NSA whistleblowers told anyone who would listen beginning in 2002.

The issue is not, and never was, that no one knew. The issue is that the people with the power to act decided not to. That is not a technical failure. It is a moral one. And Richard Pearson — writing from his mattress, in the most surveilled city on earth, twenty-nine years after Gloria Naylor sat down with shaking hands in the Brooklyn Public Library — is the consequence.

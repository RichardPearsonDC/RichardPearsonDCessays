Prefatory Note on Method and Scope

This essay proceeds from a deliberately uncomfortable premise: that the legal question most worth asking about Richard Pearson’s account is not whether it is true, but whether, if it were true, any existing legal mechanism could address it. The analysis that follows traces the historical arc from the original COINTELPRO era through the post-9/11 transformation of executive power to the present — arguing that the legal reforms enacted in response to COINTELPRO have been systematically outpaced, outmaneuvered, and in critical respects reversed by fifty years of executive expansion, judicial doctrinal evolution, and technological change. What emerges is not a gap in the law. It is a structured void — the product of deliberate legal architecture — within which an operation of the kind Pearson describes could proceed without constitutional accountability, without judicial review, and without the victim ever obtaining the threshold access to a courthouse necessary to make the first argument.

I. The Original Sin: COINTELPRO, HUAC, and What the Reforms Were Supposed to Fix

To understand the present, one must begin with the past that the present was designed to foreclose.

From 1956 to 1971, the Federal Bureau of Investigation, under Director J. Edgar Hoover, operated the Counter Intelligence Program — COINTELPRO — a classified domestic covert action program that systematically targeted American citizens for surveillance, infiltration, harassment, discrediting, and destruction. The program’s stated purpose was to “expose, disrupt, misdirect, discredit, or otherwise neutralize” domestic political organizations deemed subversive. Its actual targets included the Southern Christian Leadership Conference, the Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee, the Black Panther Party, Dr. Martin Luther King, Jr. (who the FBI surveilled, harassed, and sent a letter encouraging him to commit suicide), and thousands of ordinary Americans whose politics were judged dangerous by an unaccountable internal review process.

COINTELPRO’s methods included: warrantless wiretapping and physical surveillance; anonymous letters designed to destroy marriages, careers, and organizational relationships; the use of informants and agent provocateurs to infiltrate and destabilize organizations; coordination with local law enforcement to arrest, harass, and falsely charge targets; the creation and distribution of forged documents attributed to targets; the deliberate destruction of targets’ professional and personal reputations through anonymous tip campaigns to employers, family members, and news organizations; and, in at least some cases, coordination with or facilitation of physical violence against targets.

COINTELPRO was unknown to the public, to most of Congress, and to most senior executive branch officials. It was exposed not by any oversight mechanism but by a break-in — a group of antiwar activists burglarized an FBI field office in Media, Pennsylvania in March 1971, stole the files, and leaked them to journalists. The documents revealed what targets had been calling for years: a systematic, unconstitutional, FBI-run campaign of political repression against American citizens.

The legislative and institutional response was the most significant reform of the U.S. intelligence community in the twentieth century. The Church Committee (Senate Select Committee to Study Governmental Operations with Respect to Intelligence Activities) conducted its investigations from 1975 to 1976, producing fourteen reports documenting decades of intelligence community abuses. Congress enacted the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act (FISA) in 1978, creating a specialized court to oversee domestic intelligence surveillance and establishing a warrant requirement for surveillance of Americans by intelligence agencies. Executive Order 12333, issued by President Reagan in 1981, prohibited assassination of foreign leaders and established oversight structures for intelligence activities. Attorney General guidelines — the “Levi Guidelines” of 1976 and their successors — placed restrictions on FBI domestic intelligence investigations, requiring predication before investigating American citizens.

The House Un-American Activities Committee (HUAC), which had conducted its own campaign of political repression through public hearings, subpoenas, and the destruction of careers from 1938 through the McCarthy era and beyond, was finally abolished in 1975 — formally renamed and then effectively dissolved after decades of targeted Americans had been blacklisted, imprisoned, or driven to suicide.

The reforms operated on a common assumption: that the primary mechanism of government abuse was overt and institutional — the FBI conducting its own surveillance, the CIA running its own programs, HUAC holding its own hearings. If oversight could be imposed on those institutions, if warrants were required for surveillance, if a specialized court could review applications in secret, if Attorney General guidelines constrained predication — then the abuses would stop.

That assumption was wrong. Not because the reforms were insincere, but because the technology and operational methods available to a government that wished to evade them have been transformed beyond recognition in the fifty years since COINTELPRO was exposed.

There is another key element. COINTELPRO victims faced an extreme hurdle for court relief — whom to serve a civil complaint. In the case of government involvement, US law requires that victims serve an agency or government officials. However, the covert and insidious nature of COINTELPRO provided no clear face of the government. If the perpetuator is anonymous and deniable, then victims are sent on a wild goose chase across government agencies to find the responsible agency or official.

II. The Transformation of Presidential Power: From Church to 9/11 and Beyond

Between the Church Committee reforms and Richard Pearson’s experience in 2025, the power of the American presidency to conduct domestic surveillance, target domestic dissidents, and operate covertly against American citizens was transformed — not by the reversal of any single reform, but by a series of expansions that, layered together, rebuilt the architecture of unchecked executive action in a new legal register.

The Reagan-Era Covert Infrastructure

Executive Order 12333, issued in December 1981, is the foundational document of the modern intelligence community’s legal architecture. While containing some restraints, it also authorized the intelligence community to conduct “special activities” — covert action — pursuant to presidential finding, and established the President as the ultimate authority over intelligence activities in ways that created significant flexibility for future administrations. The “special activities” authorization is the legal vehicle through which covert domestic operations, conducted through private contractors and cutouts, can be authorized at the presidential level while maintaining deniability at every operational level below.

The Reagan DOJ’s Office of Legal Counsel, under Theodore Olson and Charles Cooper, produced a series of opinions in the 1980s expanding theories of inherent presidential power that would later become foundational to the post-9/11 executive power claims. The “unitary executive theory” — the argument that the President’s Article II authority gives him plenary control over all executive branch functions, including intelligence activities, in ways that limit Congress’s power to constrain those functions — was seeded in this period.

The Post-9/11 Rupture

The September 11, 2001 attacks produced the most dramatic single expansion of American executive power since the Second World War. The Authorization for Use of Military Force (AUMF), passed on September 18, 2001, authorized the President to “use all necessary and appropriate force against those nations, organizations, or persons he determines planned, authorized, committed, or aided the terrorist attacks.” The use of the word “determines” — giving the President unilateral authority to designate targets — and the phrase “all necessary and appropriate force” — which the Bush administration interpreted to include warrantless domestic surveillance, indefinite detention without charge, and coercive interrogation — transformed the AUMF from a narrow war authorization into what Yale law professor Oona Hathaway described as a blank check that “massively expanded beyond what Congress initially envisioned.”

The Bush administration’s post-9/11 legal architecture included:

The NSA Warrantless Surveillance Program: Under a secret presidential authorization issued in late 2001, the NSA began intercepting the telephone and email communications of Americans without FISA warrants, based solely on the President’s asserted Article II authority and AUMF authorization. When revealed by the New York Times in December 2005, the administration asserted — through DOJ white papers authored by John Yoo and others — that the President had inherent authority under Article II to conduct warrantless surveillance, that FISA’s warrant requirement was superseded by the AUMF, and that courts could not second-guess the President’s intelligence determinations. The program intercepted communications of persons the NSA believed had “a reasonable basis” to be connected to al-Qaeda — a standard lower than probable cause, applied by NSA agents without judicial oversight.

The Patriot Act (2001): Section 215 authorized collection of “any tangible thing” relevant to a foreign intelligence or terrorism investigation, eliminating prior limitations on what could be collected and from whom. The “sneak and peek” provision, Section 213, authorized law enforcement to search homes and offices without notifying the occupant at the time of the search. National Security Letters — administrative subpoenas issued without judicial approval — were dramatically expanded, allowing the FBI to compel disclosure of financial, telephone, internet, and other records with no court involvement and accompanied by a gag order preventing the recipient from disclosing the request.

The Protect America Act (2007) and FISA Amendments Act (2008): After a FISA court ruling constrained some NSA activities, Congress passed legislation authorizing mass surveillance of foreign-targeted communications transiting U.S. telecommunications infrastructure, including communications of Americans. Section 702 of the FISA Amendments Act, reauthorized repeatedly since, authorizes collection of communications of foreign persons located abroad — but because those persons communicate with Americans, it produces a massive dataset of American communications collected without individual warrants.

The John Yoo OLC Memoranda: The Office of Legal Counsel produced a series of classified opinions — the “torture memos” and their companion documents — that asserted the President’s authority, as Commander in Chief, overrode statutory prohibitions on interrogation techniques, detention conditions, and domestic surveillance. These opinions, though subsequently withdrawn in some respects, established the template for an executive branch legal theory in which national security determinations are beyond legislative override and judicial review.

The Implications for Domestic Operations

The post-9/11 legal architecture, built ostensibly for counterterrorism against foreign actors, produced infrastructure and legal theories that are not confined to foreign terrorism. The AUMF’s “he determines” language — giving the President unilateral designation authority — was extended by successive administrations to cover operations in Iraq, Syria, Somalia, Yemen, Libya, and other countries far removed from the 9/11 attacks. If it can be extended geographically, it can be extended categorically.

The NSA’s domestic collection infrastructure, revealed by Edward Snowden in 2013, demonstrated that “the NSA has built an infrastructure that allows it to intercept almost everything.” The collection is not targeted in the individual-warrant sense. It is bulk, automated, and subsequently searchable. Every phone call, email, and text message involving an American person is either directly collected or collectible through querying of telecommunications provider records. A person like Richard Pearson — communicating by phone, using the internet, sending emails, using AI platforms like Sora and ChatGPT — is not outside this infrastructure. He is within it, along with every other American.

The legal theories developed to justify post-9/11 surveillance — inherent presidential authority, AUMF as statutory authorization for surveillance beyond FISA, national security special needs exceptions to the Fourth Amendment — did not disappear when the specific programs that gave rise to them were modified or restricted. They remain in the legal arsenal of any administration that chooses to invoke them. An administration that designates academic liberation philosophy as a domestic security threat, invokes the language of “enemies within,” and directs federal agencies and private contractors toward domestic dissidents can draw on the full post-9/11 legal infrastructure to authorize the surveillance component of what it does — while maintaining, through contractor chains, the deniability of the operational component.

The Trump administration’s explicit invocation of domestic enemy rhetoric in January 2025 is not merely political theater in this context. It is the political predicate for potential legal authorization under the same theories that justified the NSA’s surveillance of Americans after 9/11. An executive that claims the authority to designate domestic “enemies” and deploy “military training” in American cities has drawn on exactly the legal toolkit that the post-9/11 era constructed.

III. The Erosion of Judicial Remedy: Standing, Lujan, and the Courthouse Door

While executive power was expanding in the post-9/11 period, the judicial capacity to review that power was being systematically contracted. The doctrinal vehicle for this contraction is standing — the threshold requirement for access to federal courts — and its most consequential modern reformulation is Lujan v. Defenders of Wildlife, 504 U.S. 555 (1992).

The Lujan Framework and Its Political Logic

Lujan was written by Justice Antonin Scalia, who had previously articulated his theory of standing in academic writing as a mechanism for limiting federal court involvement in disputes about government policy — a separation of powers instrument, in his framing, that ensured courts did not become supervisors of the executive branch. Lujan crystallized the three-part standing test that now governs every federal plaintiff:

First, injury in fact: a concrete, particularized injury that is actual or imminent, not conjectural or hypothetical. Second, traceability: the injury must be “fairly traceable” to the challenged conduct of the defendant and not the result of the independent action of a third party not before the court. Third, redressability: it must be “likely,” as opposed to merely speculative, that a favorable judicial decision will redress the injury.

Each element is independently required. Each must be demonstrated with evidence — not allegations — by the time the case reaches summary judgment. Each is calibrated to exclude exactly the category of plaintiff who has suffered diffuse, coordinated, or deniably caused harm from government action or inaction conducted through intermediaries.

Lujan’s traceability requirement is the specific element most destructive to claims of the kind Pearson would bring. When injury arises not from direct government action but from “the independent action of some third party not before the court,” the Court held, “causation and redressability ordinarily hinge on the response of the regulated (or regulable) third party to the government action or inaction — and perhaps on the response of others as well.” The plaintiff bears the burden of proving that intermediaries will behave in the manner necessary to produce the claimed injury from the government’s conduct.

Applied to Pearson: his injury, if caused by the government, was caused through a chain of intermediaries — private contractors, sub-contractors, individual operational actors — none of whom are named government defendants. Proving traceability requires proving that those intermediaries acted pursuant to government direction. Proving that requires discovery. Discovery is blocked by state secrets. The circle is closed before it opens.

Lujan’s redressability requirement adds another layer. Even if Pearson established injury and traceability, he would need to demonstrate that a court order would actually redress his injury. An injunction against a specific identified government program would not stop a contractor operation structured to be legally invisible. An order requiring disclosure would be met with a state secrets assertion. A damages award against the United States requires navigating FTCA exceptions. Redressability is, in practice, nearly impossible to demonstrate for covert operations conducted through layered private actors.

Standing and Surveillance: Clapper v. Amnesty International

The Lujan framework’s application to surveillance specifically was demonstrated in Clapper v. Amnesty International USA, 568 U.S. 398 (2013), in which the Supreme Court held that attorneys and journalists who communicated with foreign persons likely subject to NSA surveillance under Section 702 lacked standing to challenge the program because they could not prove that their specific communications had been collected. The Court held that the plaintiffs’ fear of surveillance was too speculative to constitute injury in fact — even though they credibly alleged that the surveillance infrastructure existed and that their work required them to communicate with exactly the categories of persons it targeted.

The dissent, written by Justice Breyer, noted the circularity: the plaintiffs could not prove their communications were collected because the government would not disclose what it collected. The majority’s response — that the plaintiffs could not demonstrate imminent injury because future collection was uncertain — effectively foreclosed any challenge to surveillance programs that the government refuses to confirm. A person who cannot prove they were surveilled because the government withholds that proof does not have standing to challenge surveillance. The surveillance itself defeats the standing necessary to challenge the surveillance.

Applied to Pearson: he cannot prove that NSA collected his communications (though the infrastructure for doing so demonstrably exists and has been disclosed). He cannot prove that a government contractor operated the through-floor tracking (though the technology demonstrably exists). He cannot prove that a federal agent injected “NO” into his Sora session (though the capability demonstrably exists). Under Clapper, speculative injury — even well-founded, technically plausible speculative injury — does not satisfy the injury-in-fact requirement. Pearson is where the Amnesty International plaintiffs were, except that his evidence of the technology’s existence is substantially stronger than theirs was.

Serving a Civil Complaint and Presentment in the Case of Grave Constitutional Violations by the US Government

US law hinders the service of process — the formal, legal delivery of documents like a complaint — in a case alleging US constitutional violations and state-sponsored torture. The court docket often shows that victims resort to sending FOIA requests to all possible agencies, followed by a complaint listing those agencies. This process can be time-consuming, expensive, and re-traumatizing for victims. Those agencies, too, often are unresponsive, precipitating further legal action. Absent a clear agency, a lawyer may be reluctant to take on the case, a particular disadvantage for low resource, low educational attainment, or disabled victims.

Presentment is another issue. The U.S. does not generally require formal pre-suit notification (“presentment”) for all federal lawsuits. However, specific types of suits, particularly those against federal, state, or local government entities, often require pre-suit administrative claims or notices. In applicable cases, presentment has a legally prescribed window to wait for a denial or a set period to pass before filing a lawsuit in federal court (“administrative exhaustion”). This inherently discriminates — inadequate educational attainment, low research skills, available times, type of work, and access to in-person or online resources.

The Narrowing of Procedural Injury and Congressional Standing Grants

Lujan also significantly limited Congress’s ability to create standing by statute — to pass laws giving citizens the right to sue the government for constitutional violations without demonstrating the three-part test. The Court’s holding that procedural injuries — violations of procedural requirements designed to protect interests without producing demonstrable concrete harm — do not suffice for standing without an additional showing of concrete injury has been extended in subsequent cases including Spokeo, Inc. v. Robins, 578 U.S. 330 (2016) and TransUnion LLC v. Ramirez, 594 U.S. 413 (2021). TransUnion held that plaintiffs must suffer “a concrete harm that actually exist[s] in the real world” — not merely the violation of a statutory right — to establish Article III standing.

This doctrinal trajectory has specific implications for surveillance and covert operations: even if Congress were to pass a statute explicitly prohibiting the conduct Pearson describes and granting a private right of action, a plaintiff who cannot demonstrate concrete, traceable, redressable harm from conduct the government refuses to acknowledge would likely fail the TransUnion standing test. Congressional protection requires a plaintiff who can get through the courthouse door. The doctrinal evolution since Lujan has narrowed that door to dimensions through which Pearson cannot pass.

IV. COINTELPRO 2.0: The Same Logic, an Entirely Different Technology

Having established the legal landscape — expanded executive power above, contracted judicial access below — we can now analyze how what Pearson describes maps onto a hypothetical COINTELPRO 2.0: a program of domestic political repression using the technology and legal architecture of 2025 rather than 1965.

The original COINTELPRO operated through institutions: FBI field offices, FBI informants, FBI-coordinated local law enforcement. Its methods were crude by contemporary standards — anonymous letters, wiretapped telephones, infiltrators at meetings. Its legal vulnerability was institutional: when files were stolen and leaked, they were the files of a named federal agency, signed by identifiable officials, bearing the institutional letterhead of the United States government. The Church Committee could subpoena those officials. Congress could hold hearings. Courts could eventually adjudicate the claims of named victims against named agencies.

COINTELPRO 2.0 — operating in 2025 with the technology and legal architecture available — would be structurally unrecognizable from the original, and structurally impenetrable in ways its predecessor was not.

The Contractor Architecture as Legal Insulation

The foundational structural difference between COINTELPRO and any successor operation is the privatization of intelligence and covert action functions that has occurred since the 1990s — and which accelerated dramatically after 9/11.

Approximately 70% of the U.S. intelligence budget is now spent on private contractors. Companies like Booz Allen Hamilton, Leidos, SAIC, Palantir, and hundreds of smaller firms perform intelligence collection, analysis, and — in some classified cases — operational functions that were previously the exclusive domain of government agencies. The legal consequence of this privatization is the disaggregation of state action: constitutional protections apply to government actors, not to private contractors acting on government direction unless the nexus to government direction can be affirmatively proven.

Pearson identifies this structure precisely: “a federal and private hybrid model involving numerous players both individual and formal and informal networks/organizations… Based on past formulas, they contract out different functions. But the key with the fed/intelligence is to have plausible deniability — always an escape hatch.” He adds: “I speculate that the administration relied on corporations to help generate a list of targeted individuals.”

The list generation itself — identifying targets — would be the most sensitive step and the most likely to involve corporate actors. A corporation with access to social media data, financial records, academic publication databases, and geolocation data could identify, score, and rank ideological opponents of an administration’s policy agenda without ever being asked to do so explicitly. The administration’s executive orders — on DEI, on domestic threat assessment, on the designation of “enemies within” — provide the scoring criteria. A corporation that produces a list of individuals matching those criteria and delivers it to a federal agency has not been asked to surveil anyone; it has delivered an analytics product. The intelligence community that receives the list and initiates an operation has received commercially available intelligence analysis. No warrant was required to produce the list. No court supervised the delivery. No oversight committee was informed.

Below the list-generation tier, the operation itself is contracted layer by layer. The technology deployment — directed energy systems, through-floor tracking, acoustic weapons — is contracted to firms with classified DoD relationships. The ground operations — the coordinated actors in red and black, the staged encounters, the “Lee” interactions at Potbelly’s, the chemical deployment at Rockville Metro, the assailant who physically assaulted Pearson — are contracted or sub-contracted through cutouts to individual operatives who have no direct legal relationship with any federal agency. A federal agent never appears. A government badge never emerges. A government name is never used.

This is precisely what Pearson documented over nine months. No badge. No identification. No stated authority. The encounter with the man he called Lee — who confirmed audio surveillance of Pearson’s apartment, spoke in the evasive economy of language that “results from training,” and departed with the statement “You will see me again — maybe in another form” — is the closest any identified actor came to acknowledging a governmental dimension. And Lee never named an agency, never showed identification, and was known only by a pseudonym written on a coffee cup.

The Technology Advantage: Deniability by Design

The original COINTELPRO required human operatives in identifiable positions — informants attending meetings, agents making telephone calls, supervisors signing memoranda. Its methods left documentary traces: letters, surveillance logs, informant reports, interagency communications.

The directed energy technologies Pearson describes leave none of these traces. Voice-to-skull transmission — the microwave auditory effect — produces an experience in the target that is subjectively indistinguishable from spontaneous auditory hallucination. Through-floor tracking radar is passive sensing that leaves no mark on the tracked individual. Behavioral pinning — the induction of directional compulsion through electromagnetic influence on limbic decision-making systems — produces behaviors that feel to the target like their own choices. Somatic stimulation through directed electromagnetic energy produces physical sensations that cannot be distinguished, from external observation, from endogenous physiological events.

This is not accidental. The deniability of directed energy weapons is a feature, not a limitation, of their design. They are weapons that leave no conventional forensic evidence, that cannot be detected without specialized instrumentation that the target would have no reason to deploy in advance, and that produce effects — voices, compulsions, somatic sensations, cognitive alteration — that the existing psychiatric and legal framework attributes to mental illness rather than external causation. They are, in the most precise possible sense, weapons designed for plausible deniability.

The V2K incident on July 13, 2025 is the clearest demonstration of this design principle at work. The experience occurred once — a single, extended episode at the National Mall and Georgetown waterfront — and was never directly replicated. Pearson himself notes the significance of this: “The voice in my head, which is not psychosis because we know that this kind of technology can happen and does happen.” The singularity of the experience is operationally characteristic: a test, as the voice identified it at the end. A proof-of-concept deployed once, producing maximum psychological impact (the announcement that Pearson would “disappear”), and then withdrawn before it could be instrumentally detected or independently corroborated.

The staging environment that accompanied the V2K — the Russia outline T-shirt appearing precisely as the voice began, the couple reinforcing the Georgetown direction at Federal Center, the actors with Eastern European accents at Georgetown Park — is the COINTELPRO analogue for 2025: not the direct action, but the psychological operation surrounding it, designed to attribute the experience to a foreign threat rather than a domestic one. The false flag element of the July 13 operation was designed to ensure that even if Pearson discussed his experience, his initial attribution would point toward Russia — making him appear confused or conspiratorial — rather than toward the U.S. government. The probability that a man wearing a Russia-outline T-shirt would walk past Pearson at the Capitol at the precise moment a voice began directing him to Georgetown, and that actors with Russian accents would appear at the Georgetown destination, by random chance, is so small as to be functionally zero. But in a court of law, coincidence cannot be disproven without discovery, and discovery is blocked.

The Tub Incident: The Framing Architecture

The November 7–13, 2025 tub incident is the most legally significant operational element in Pearson’s account, because it reveals the construction of the “parallel credible framework” — the alternative explanation for the operation that the government has prepared in the event that Pearson’s account is taken seriously.

Pearson was pinned — pulled by an inexplicable compulsion — to a park space adjacent to the O’Neill House Office Building, mere blocks from the Capitol. He found a tub containing khaki pants, a red hat, a black shirt, boots, and a spiral notebook with conspiratorial writings referencing Adolf Hitler, European court proceedings, and photographs of an Eastern European man. He returned on November 13 to find the notebook and khaki pants removed — retrieved by someone who was monitoring whether Pearson had taken them.

The tub is a framing artifact. Its contents — extremist writings, references to European removal proceedings, photographs of a foreign-national-appearing man — would, if discovered by law enforcement in connection with Pearson, provide the predicate for a national security investigation connecting him to European extremist networks. The red and black items reinforce the signal environment Pearson had been documenting; their presence at the tub location, to which he was pinned, confirms the operational integration of the ground teams with the technology platform.

The partial retrieval of the notebook — taken when Pearson did not — suggests monitoring of whether Pearson interacted with the framing material. If he had taken the notebook, it would have been in his possession, available for discovery in any search, and available to create the narrative that Pearson was connected to whatever the notebook described. When he did not take it, the most legally dangerous item was retrieved before it could be discovered by other parties.

This is not the behavior of random actors. It is the behavior of an operation that has prepared a parallel explanatory framework — gang harassment, real estate cabal, extremist network connection — that can be activated to explain Pearson’s experiences without acknowledging their actual source.

V. The Full Legal Barrier Architecture

With the historical and technological context established, we can now map the complete legal barrier structure — revised and expanded to incorporate the doctrinal evolution described above.

A. The Political Authorization Shield and Executive Discretion

Any legal challenge to an operation against Pearson would first encounter the authorization structure described in Section I. Executive Order 14173 (DEI enforcement), the DOJ’s accompanying legal memoranda, the President’s explicit “enemies within” rhetoric, and his invocation of urban military training all constitute the political predicate for operations against domestic dissidents framed as national security threats.

Under Harlow v. Fitzgerald, 457 U.S. 800 (1982), federal officials are protected by qualified immunity for conduct that does not violate “clearly established” statutory or constitutional rights that a reasonable person would have known. An official who, in January 2025, acting pursuant to a presidential executive order designating ideological opponents as national security threats, authorized a surveillance and harassment operation against a Black liberation philosophy academic, had no clearly established precedent telling him that doing so violated the Constitution in the specific manner required to overcome qualified immunity. The targeting of domestic dissidents by the executive branch has been held unconstitutional in the COINTELPRO context — but the specific application of directed energy weapons, behavioral manipulation, and contractor-based covert operations to a domestic civilian has never been directly adjudicated. Qualified immunity’s “clearly established” standard requires prior case law on point. The technology is novel. The precedent does not exist.

The presidential authorization structure also implicates the political question doctrine. Under Baker v. Carr, 369 U.S. 186 (1962), and its progeny, courts decline to adjudicate matters that are constitutionally committed to the political branches or that lack judicially manageable standards. National security determinations — which targets pose threats, what operations are appropriate responses, what level of presidential authorization is required — have been treated by courts as sitting in a zone of deference to executive judgment that approaches non-justiciability.

B. Standing: Lujan and the Three-Prong Trap

As analyzed in Section III, Lujan v. Defenders of Wildlife, 504 U.S. 555 (1992), imposes three requirements that operate as nearly insurmountable barriers for a plaintiff in Pearson’s position.

Injury in fact: Pearson’s injuries are real and documentable — economic devastation, physical assault, chemical injury, psychological trauma, attempted asylum, loss of housing security. But the specific injuries attributable to directed energy technologies — the V2K experience, behavioral pinning, somatic stimulation — cannot be proven to constitute “concrete” and “particularized” harm under the Lujan/TransUnion standard because they leave no forensic evidence and are clinically indistinguishable from naturally occurring events, though imaging technology is evolving to correlate with directed energy weapons (DEWs) sources. A court applying Lujan would need to determine whether Pearson’s documented psychological suffering constitutes concrete injury — which it likely does — but would then face the causation and traceability requirements.

Traceability: This is the Lujan prong that most precisely captures Pearson’s predicament. His injuries, if caused by the government, were caused through a chain of private intermediaries. Under Lujan’s formulation, when injury “arises from the government’s allegedly unlawful regulation (or lack of regulation) of someone else, much more is needed” — and the plaintiff must “adduce facts showing that those choices have been or will be made in such manner as to produce causation.” Pearson cannot prove that the upstairs neighbors’ noise campaign was directed by the government without discovery. He cannot prove that “Lee” was a government intelligence officer without discovery. He cannot prove that the red-and-black actors were coordinated by a federal contractor without discovery. Every link in the chain requires discovery of classified materials that the government can block with a single privilege assertion.

Redressability: Even if Pearson established injury and traceability, the relief available — an injunction, a damages award — would not meaningfully redress injuries from a covert operation designed to be terminated and deniable. The operation is already designed with the contingency of exposure in mind. Its termination, if ordered, would leave Pearson without remedy for injuries already suffered.

C. Clapper and the Surveillance Paradox

Clapper v. Amnesty International USA, 568 U.S. 398 (2013), adds a specific layer to the standing analysis for the surveillance component of Pearson’s claims. Under Clapper, a plaintiff cannot establish standing based on “fear” that surveillance has occurred if the government declines to confirm or deny the surveillance. The plaintiff must demonstrate that surveillance is “certainly impending” — not merely likely, not merely consistent with documented government capability. The Court acknowledged the “serious constitutional question” this created but declined to modify the standing requirement.

For Pearson: the evidence that NSA-capable surveillance of his apartment and computer occurred is substantial — Lee’s confirmation— but neither constitutes the kind of legally admissible, judicially cognizable proof that Clapper requires for standing. The surveillance paradox holds: he cannot prove he was surveilled because the government withholds the proof; without proof, he has no standing; without standing, he cannot compel disclosure of the proof.

D. Bivens and the Constitutional Dead End

Bivens v. Six Unknown Named Agents, 403 U.S. 388 (1971), established the right to sue federal officers directly for constitutional violations even absent a specific statutory cause of action. The Supreme Court has since systematically dismantled this right.

Ziglar v. Abbasi, 582 U.S. 120 (2017), held that Bivens should not be extended to any “new context” — and established that the national security context is precisely where courts should be most hesitant. Writing for the Court, Justice Kennedy stated that extending Bivens “in the national security context would be an unjustified intrusion by the Judiciary into matters of national-security policy.” The Court identified as “special factors counseling hesitation” the very factors most present in Pearson’s case: high-level executive policy decisions, involvement of national security agencies, and the risk that litigation would require disclosure of classified information.

Hernandez v. Mesa, 589 U.S. 93 (2020), further restricted Bivens by holding that even a Fourth Amendment claim arising from a shooting across an international border presented a new context in which Bivens should not be extended. The trajectory of the doctrine is clear: the Court has not recognized a new Bivens context since 1980, and lower courts read Ziglar and Hernandez as effectively foreclosing extension to any novel factual scenario.

Directed energy weapons used against a domestic civilian. Behavioral pinning. Through-floor surveillance. Contractor-based psychological operations. These are not merely new contexts under the Ziglar framework. They are contexts that no court has ever addressed. Under current doctrine, the absence of precedent means Bivens does not extend — and the plaintiff has no constitutional cause of action directly against any federal officer, no matter how serious the constitutional violation.

E. Sovereign Immunity and the FTCA’s Calibrated Exceptions

The Federal Tort Claims Act, 28 U.S.C. §§ 1346(b), 2671–2680, provides the partial waiver of sovereign immunity through which private citizens can sue the United States for tortious conduct by federal employees. But the FTCA’s exceptions are calibrated with almost surgical precision to exclude the categories of claim Pearson would bring.

The discretionary function exception, 28 U.S.C. § 2680(a), protects “any claim based upon… the exercise or performance or the failure to exercise or perform a discretionary function or duty.” Under United States v. Gaubert, 499 U.S. 315 (1991), if the challenged government action involves an element of judgment or choice and is susceptible to policy analysis, the exception applies. A decision by a federal agency to conduct a covert domestic operation against a designated domestic threat, pursuant to a presidential executive order, is paradigmatically a discretionary function. The FTCA does not apply.

The intentional tort exception, 28 U.S.C. § 2680(h), excludes claims arising from assault, battery, false imprisonment, false arrest, and related intentional torts committed by non-law-enforcement federal employees. The chemical assault at Rockville Metro would constitute battery — but only against a law enforcement officer, and only if the officer can be identified and their federal agency relationship established. The contractor architecture ensures that neither condition can be met.

The foreign country exception and the combatant activities exception are inapplicable on their face — but the evolving executive theory of the “homeland battlefield,” articulated by some post-9/11 scholars and implicit in the President’s invocation of urban military training, raises the question of whether future administrations might assert that domestic counterinsurgency operations fall within military exclusions designed for battlefield contexts.

F. The State Secrets Privilege: Reynolds and Its Descendants

United States v. Reynolds, 345 U.S. 1 (1953), established the state secrets privilege — the government’s ability to assert, through formal departmental certification, that certain evidence cannot be disclosed without endangering national security. Reynolds itself arose from a case in which the Air Force refused to disclose an accident report, claiming the report contained secret electronic equipment information. In subsequent decades, documents declassified and released under FOIA revealed that the Air Force had lied — the report contained no such secrets, but did contain evidence of Air Force negligence.

The privilege has expanded dramatically since Reynolds. In Mohamed v. Jeppesen Dataplan, Inc., 614 F.3d 1070 (9th Cir. 2010), the en banc Ninth Circuit upheld dismissal of extraordinary rendition victims’ claims because the state secrets privilege covered evidence necessary to establish liability. The court acknowledged that this left plaintiffs “without a remedy for what may have been egregious violations of their constitutional rights” — but held that the separation of powers required deference to the executive’s security assessment.

Al-Haramain Islamic Foundation v. Bush, 507 F.3d 1190 (9th Cir. 2007), further demonstrated the privilege’s scope in surveillance contexts: an organization that had been given, accidentally, a classified NSA document confirming their surveillance was nonetheless unable to use that document in litigation because the government asserted the privilege over it, and the court held that even an accidentally disclosed document could be re-classified and withheld.

For Pearson: every category of discovery relevant to his claims would be subject to state secrets assertion. NSA signal monitoring records: classified. DoD directed energy weapons programs and their domestic applications: classified. Contracts with private intelligence firms for domestic operations: classified. Any executive authorization for operations against domestic dissidents: classified. Law enforcement database records explaining the ACCESS DENIED flag: classified. The government need not assert the privilege as to each document individually — it can make a categorical assertion that an entire subject matter is covered, and the court reviews that assertion with extreme deference.

G. The Glomar Response: When Non-Disclosure Is the Disclosure

The Glomar doctrine — named for Phillippi v. CIA, 546 F.2d 1009 (D.C. Cir. 1976) — allows the government to refuse to confirm or deny the existence of responsive records when acknowledging existence would itself reveal classified information. The doctrine applies when the subject matter of the FOIA request is sensitive enough that even a “no records” response would be informative to adversaries.

In Pearson’s context: a FOIA request for records concerning surveillance of his person, directed energy weapons programs with domestic applications, or contractor relationships for domestic covert operations would receive a Glomar response. The response neither confirms nor denies. It provides no information. It cannot be challenged unless the plaintiff can independently establish that the government cannot invoke Glomar — which requires knowing what records exist, which is the information the Glomar response refuses to provide.

The ACCESS DENIED response to the MPD officer’s database query is a physical-world Glomar event: existence confirmed, content blocked, no mechanism for challenge or correction available to Pearson. The record is inaccessible. The inaccessibility cannot be challenged because there is no named official, no named agency, and no identified legal basis for the restriction that Pearson could contest in court.

H. Neitzke v. Williams and the Entry Barrier

Neitzke v. Williams, 490 U.S. 319 (1989), established that federal courts may dismiss a complaint filed by an indigent plaintiff as “frivolous” under 28 U.S.C. § 1915(e) when the claim “describes fantastic or delusional scenarios.” The Supreme Court distinguished factual frivolousness — the “fantastic or delusional” standard — from legal frivolousness, holding that factually frivolous claims may be dismissed even if they raise cognizable legal theories.

The frivolousness doctrine operates before any factual development. It applies at initial screening, before the government responds, before discovery, before any judicial fact-finding. A district court judge reviewing Pearson’s complaint — seeing allegations of voice-to-skull technology, behavioral pinning, through-floor surveillance of intimate activity, chemical assault, coordinated street theater, and computer interception — without the benefit of the January 2026 government disclosures about directed energy weapons, without the mental health evaluation, without the timestamped photographs, without the audio recordings, and without the November 2025 CNN reporting on the Pentagon’s pulsed radio wave device — might characterize the complaint as describing a “fantastic or delusional scenario.”

This is not a hypothetical risk. It is the systematic pattern of disposition for what are called “targeted individual” claims in federal courts. A search of federal court decisions reveals dozens of dismissals of claims involving coordinated government harassment, directed energy, and covert surveillance, uniformly dismissed under Neitzke without examination of the factual record. The doctrine cannot, in practice, distinguish between the genuinely delusional plaintiff and the plaintiff whose accurate account involves novel technology and classified operations that a court is not equipped to evaluate at the initial screening stage. Both are dismissed as frivolous. The government is never required to respond.

I. The Burden of Proof and the Asymmetry of Information

Even assuming a plaintiff survived every prior barrier, the burden of proof at trial falls entirely on the plaintiff. Pearson must prove, by a preponderance of the evidence, each element: the identity of each actor, their agency relationship with the government, the specific authorization for each act, the causal connection between government action and injury, and his damages.

The government need prove nothing. It need only challenge the sufficiency of Pearson’s proof. And the information asymmetry is absolute: every piece of evidence that would establish the government’s role is held by the government, classified by the government, protected by privilege asserted by the government, and withheld under doctrines developed by courts that defer to the government’s security assessments.

The burden cannot be met not because the evidence does not exist, but because the evidence is structurally inaccessible. The contractor chain is invisible. The authorization documents are classified. The technology is undetectable without instrumentation Pearson did not deploy. The actors are anonymous. The government’s privilege assertions prevent disclosure. The court’s deference ensures those assertions are credited. The plaintiff is left with his own testimony, his timestamps, his photographs, his audio recordings, his pants — and a doctrinal structure that treats the absence of corroborating evidence not as the consequence of governmental concealment but as evidence that the claims are unprovable.

It is not yet clear how the end of the Chevron doctrine — judicial deference federal agencies when interpreting and implementing federal statute — will play out in cases similar to Pearson.

VI. The Specific Legal Invisibility of Pearson’s Case: Why Each Gap Fits Precisely

The structure above is not a general description of barriers to government accountability. It is a map of barriers that fit Pearson’s specific circumstances with a precision that is itself analytically significant.

No arrest, no detention, no badge. Pearson was never formally taken into custody as part of the alleged operation. No agent ever identified themselves. No government name was ever used. The December 27 assault could be legally reframeable as a domestic dispute: routine police response to a noise complaint escalation, without reference to any broader context. The MPD officer’s decision not to arrest the assailant, who physically assaulted Pearson with visible injuries and medical documentation, is reframeable as a judgment call about competing accounts in a domestic dispute — plausible, deniable, unremarkable in isolation.

The constitutional protections most clearly available to Pearson — Fourth Amendment, Fifth Amendment due process — require government action. Without a badge, a warrant, a detention, or a formal claim of authority, the government’s presence in Pearson’s life is constitutionally invisible. Under Florida v. Bostick, 501 U.S. 429 (1991), encounters that leave the individual formally free to leave are not Fourth Amendment events. Pearson was always formally free to leave. He was never formally detained. The constitutional predicate for challenge never formally arose.

The shadow record. The shadow record is the juridical equivalent of the phantom surveillance: acknowledged by the system, inaccessible through the system, impossible to contest within the system.

The V2K that never repeated. The voice-to-skull experience occurred once, on July 13, 2025, identified by the voice itself as “a test.” Its singularity makes it impossible to replicate under controlled conditions, to instrument, or to independently verify. A plaintiff claiming a single, non-recurring internal experience as evidence of federal directed energy deployment — an experience that is clinically indistinguishable from an isolated auditory hallucination — cannot establish the evidentiary foundation a court would require. The experience’s non-repetition is operationally characteristic of a proof-of-concept deployment, as Pearson understands. It is legally fatal to the evidentiary case.

The concurrence of the V2K onset with the Russia T-shirt, the Federal Center couple, and the Eastern European actors at Georgetown is Pearson’s strongest probabilistic argument — the staging environment’s coordination with the internal experience is essentially impossible to explain by coincidence — but probabilistic reasoning about the coordination of public street behavior and private internal experience is not a standard that federal courts are equipped to apply, and it is precisely the kind of argument that would be characterized as delusional absent a framework for evaluating it.

VII. The Human Rights Dimension: What the International Framework Sees That Domestic Law Cannot

The cumulative architecture described above produces a specific kind of legal blindness: what the domestic legal system cannot see, international human rights law sees clearly.

Under the Convention Against Torture, to which the United States is a party, torture is defined as “any act by which severe pain or suffering, whether physical or mental, is intentionally inflicted on a person” by a public official or a person acting in an official capacity, for purposes including punishment, intimidation, or discrimination of any kind. The ICCPR, Article 7, prohibits torture and cruel, inhuman, or degrading treatment without exception. Article 17 protects against arbitrary interference with privacy. Article 19 protects the freedom to hold and express opinions without interference — specifically including academic expression.

What Pearson describes — nine months of coordinated psychological operations designed to produce “neutralization via a psychotic break and social death”; chemical assault; acoustic harassment; disruption of sleep; surveillance of intimate activity; behavioral manipulation removing volitional autonomy; somatic stimulation without consent; and sustained economic and social isolation — constitutes, under international human rights law, a campaign of mental torture. The targeting of a Black academic for liberation philosophy — a political opinion — by a government that has publicly designated ideological opponents as domestic enemies would constitute persecution on the basis of political opinion under the Refugee Convention’s definition.

But the United States is not subject to the International Criminal Court’s jurisdiction over its own nationals. The CAT is not self-executing in U.S. domestic law in a form that creates individual causes of action. The ICCPR does not create enforceable individual rights in U.S. courts. The Refugee Convention, as implemented through the Refugee Act of 1980, requires that asylum be sought in a country other than the country of persecution — and Iceland, applying the safe third country doctrine, returned Pearson to the United States on the grounds that the U.S. is a “safe country of origin.”

The safe country doctrine — the presumption that democratic states do not persecute their own citizens — is belied by COINTELPRO, by MKUltra, by the fifty-year documented history of American intelligence abuses against American citizens. But the doctrine is not responsive to historical evidence. It is a categorical presumption. The category of domestic victim of covert state violence — the American citizen targeted by directed energy weapons, by coordinated psychological operations, by contractor-based surveillance and harassment — does not fit within the refugee framework because that framework was designed for people fleeing authoritarian states, not democratic ones that have constructed an impenetrable legal architecture of deniability.

VIII. Conclusion: The Gap Has a Genealogy

The architecture of impunity Pearson confronts was not constructed overnight. It has a genealogy: from the Church Committee reforms that regulated overt institutional abuses while leaving the door open to privatized covert ones; through the Reagan-era OLC opinions that seeded the unitary executive theory; through the post-9/11 expansion of presidential power, domestic surveillance infrastructure, and contractor intelligence operations; through the Lujan framework that narrowed access to courts for plaintiffs challenging government action through intermediaries; through the Bivens collapse that eliminated direct constitutional causes of action against federal officers in novel contexts; through the Clapper surveillance paradox that prevents standing for surveillance that the government refuses to confirm; through the state secrets privilege expansion that blocks discovery of classified operational records; through the Glomar doctrine that transforms non-disclosure into an unreviewable absolute; through the Neitzke frivolousness standard that screens out technologically novel claims before factual development.

Each element of this architecture was developed for reasons that were, individually, legally coherent. Standing doctrine reflects real constitutional limits on judicial power. Qualified immunity protects officials who make difficult decisions under legal uncertainty. State secrets privilege protects genuine national security interests. The Bivens limitation reflects real concerns about judicial policymaking in sensitive national security contexts.

But layered together, in the specific configuration that confronts a plaintiff like Richard Pearson — a non-detained, non-charged, non-named-defendant-identified civilian alleging covert operations through private contractors using novel technologies in the most surveilled city in America — they produce a result that is constitutionally intolerable: the complete foreclosure of any legal remedy for any amount of state violence, as long as that violence is conducted covertly, through private intermediaries, using technologies without forensic traces, in reliance on an executive authorization structure that claims national security justification.

COINTELPRO was exposed because it left paper trails. COINTELPRO 2.0 has been designed — legally, technologically, and operationally — to leave none.

No one presented Richard Pearson with a badge. No one charged him with a crime. No one detained him for questioning. No one told him he was a target. No one acknowledged his existence as an operational subject. The entire apparatus operated precisely as it was designed to operate: invisibly and deniably.

The gap is not a loophole in the law. The gap is the law, as it has evolved, in the service of exactly the power it was supposed to constrain.

“I Can’t Pray Aloud”: US Torture Victim’s Surveillance Violates Religious Rights

Richard Pearson, US torture victim, explains loss of essential religious liberties during Trump Administration and why the conservative majority of the US Supreme Court has made it harder for victims like him to challenge government violations of their religious rights.

Richard Pearson is a torture victim in the United States. He has been physically assaulted resulting in a black eye and lacerations, with industrial chemicals, and with electrocution. His personal computer has also been hacked. He has been sleep deprived and harassed with noise.

His home is not off limits either. It’s being surveilled. A complete stranger told him so in 2025, “They are listening to you.” Weeks later, police responded directly to Pearson’s private remarks in his home to himself, confirming both the suspected surveillance and the government’s role.

Pearson, who holds a PhD, has written extensively about torture in Washington, DC through 2025–2026 during the second Trump administration. He has enumerated countless laws that were violated — a feature of being in America’s “Torture Dungeon” — a subterranean form of totalitarian government where no liberty or right is inviolable. His basic, fundamental rights guaranteed in the US Constitution — due process, First Amendment rights, equal protection, cognitive liberty, to be free of cruel and unusual punishment — weren’t just violated. Erased. As if no legal or constitutional authority existed at all.

  • First Amendment — cognitive liberty, compelled speech, academic freedom
  • Fourth Amendment — search and seizure, curtilage, new technology doctrine
  • Fifth Amendment — due process, self-incrimination
  • Eighth Amendment — cruel and unusual punishment
  • Fourteenth Amendment — equal protection
  • Fourth Amendment standing doctrine
  • Emerging constitutional doctrine on cognitive sovereignty

But there is one liberty that Pearson has yet to fully describe— right to religious freedom. Because Pearson is under surveillance, he cannot pray aloud in private. Pearson exclaims, “Can you imagine this in America? On top of being tortured, you have no way to pray in private without someone eavesdropping on your prayers. America is meaner, uglier, and far more racist that I ever thought possible. That says a lot because I have read extensively about US and world history. I would have left the US decades ago, even if that meant selling coconuts in a beach resort without ever having to be tortured in my lifetime. US constitutional democracy is a joke. It really is. Besides, US law has made it awfully hard for me to even bring my case. A detained immigrant accused of gang membership or drug smuggling has more rights that I do as a US citizen facing covert, state-sponsored torture. I have had no due process.”

That is a problem, of course. It questions US guarantees of constitutional rights in the US, but it also questions the Trump administration — ostensible appeals to religion and faith. Most recently in April 2026, Trump read from the Bible in the White House in a taped message after Trump posted images of himself as Jesus on social media, which was widely condemned. The irony of the selected passage was not lost on Pearson, “If my people, which are called by my name, shall humble themselves, and pray, and seek my face, and turn from their wicked ways, then will I hear from heaven, and will forgive their sin, and will heal their land.” (2 Chronicles 7:11–22).

“I guess they have done all of the torture and terror that they want to do. Very befitting, isn’t it?” Pearson said flatly of the President’s scripture reading. “But it doesn’t work quite like that. You don’t get to defy God, then hide behind the Bible. They will see the Almighty’s hand of justice.”

Pearson’s experience also raises profound questions the US Supreme Court’s commitment to religious rights, consistently ruling in favor of religious freedom and against government influence in matters of faith while making it more difficult for US citizens to bring claims of religious rights violations against the government. If the government violated those rights but operates within a federal secret program, secret program trumps rights. In fact, the US Supreme Court has made it, more or less, legal doctrine upon critical examination of competing legal precedents.

Pearson’s experience also exposes a specific hypocrisy in the administration conducting the surveillance — an administration that has made religious freedom one of its most prominent rhetorical commitments, that has appointed judges on the strength of their religious freedom credentials, and that is simultaneously operating a surveillance program that makes private prayer impossible for one of its targets. The US Supreme Court’s recent jurisprudence on religious freedom creates a theoretical framework for Pearson’s claim that is simultaneously more protective than any prior court and — through the same doctrinal mechanisms that foreclose his other claims — practically inaccessible to him as a plaintiff.

I. The Surveillance — What Is Known and How It Was Confirmed

Pearson’s home surveillance is not a suspicion. It is a documented conclusion resting on two independent confirmations.

The first confirmation came from a stranger in 2025, who approached him and said: ‘They are listening to you.” A person Pearson had never met, in an unplanned encounter, delivered a statement that described exactly what Pearson had been documenting as part of the campaign against him. The stranger’s motivation — whether the statement was a warning from someone with knowledge of the operation, an inadvertent disclosure by someone with operational awareness, or an act of conscience by a peripheral participant — is unknown. What is documented is what was said. The surveillance is visible enough to produce a civilian disclosure from someone outside Pearson’s social network.

The second confirmation is more specific and more legally significant. Weeks after the stranger’s warning, police responded directly to remarks Pearson had made privately in his own home — to himself, aloud, in his private space, not transmitted digitally, not spoken to anyone present. The police response was not coincidental to the remarks in content or timing. It corresponded directly to what Pearson had said in private. There is only one explanation consistent with that correspondence: the remarks were heard by someone with access to Pearson’s home audio environment, and that person — or the system they were operating — communicated the content to law enforcement.

The police response is the government’s role confirmed. Private citizens with listening devices do not produce police responses keyed to the content of a specific person’s private remarks. Police responses of this kind require: a system capable of capturing private audio in a private home; operators with access to that system and the ability to act on its content; and a connection between those operators and law enforcement sufficient to produce a calibrated response. That infrastructure — the warrantless home audio surveillance, the operator monitoring, the law enforcement coordination — is the infrastructure of a government operation. No other actor has the combination of technical capability, legal access, and law enforcement coordination that the police response to Pearson’s private remarks requires.

This is the home that Pearson prays in. It is the home where any spoken prayer is monitored. It is the home where the most private act of religious observance a person engages in — the direct address to God, spoken in the language of one’s own interior — is captured, processed, and potentially acted on by the same operators who have been running a torture campaign against him for more than a year.

II. What the Free Exercise Clause Protects — And What Pearson’s Situation Violates

The First Amendment’s Free Exercise Clause prohibits the government from prohibiting the free exercise of religion. It is deceptively simple language. Its application has generated more than two centuries of constitutional litigation because the line between government action that affects religious practice and government action that prohibits free exercise is not always clear. But some cases are clear. This is one of them.”),

The Doctrine — What Courts Have Established

The Supreme Court’s Free Exercise jurisprudence has evolved through several phases. The current framework, established most clearly in Employment Division v. Smith (1990) and significantly supplemented by subsequent decisions, holds that neutral, generally applicable laws that incidentally burden religious practice do not violate the Free Exercise Clause. But laws — or government actions — that are not neutral, that are not generally applicable, or that specifically target religious practice receive the highest level of constitutional scrutiny: strict scrutiny, which requires the government to demonstrate a compelling interest pursued through the least restrictive means.

The Court’s most recent major Free Exercise decisions have moved consistently toward stronger protection. In Church of Lukumi Babalu Aye v. City of Hialeah (1993), the Court struck down laws that were specifically designed to target a particular religious practice, holding that religious animus in the legislative record triggers strict scrutiny. In Fulton v. City of Philadelphia (2021), the Court held that government contracts cannot include non-discrimination requirements that apply to religious organizations with exceptions for secular organizations — the system of individualized exemptions doctrine. In Kennedy v. Bremerton School District (2022), the Court ruled that a public school employee’s right to pray privately on the job was protected, holding that the Free Exercise Clause ‘can give way only to a competing fundamental right or government interest of the highest order.’ In 303 Creative v. Elenis (2023), the Court extended religious and expressive freedom protections even into commercial contexts.

The current Supreme Court is, by any fair reading, the most protective of religious freedom in modern American history. It has ruled consistently and expansively in favor of religious practitioners against government impositions on faith. — And yet: a man in Washington, D.C. cannot pray aloud in his own home because the government is listening. That is the inversion this essay is examining.

Why Pearson’s Situation Is a Textbook Free Exercise Violation

The government’s warrantless surveillance of Pearson’s home does not merely affect his religious practice incidentally. It directly and substantially burdens his ability to engage in the most protected form of religious exercise: private prayer.

Private prayer is not a peripheral religious activity. It is the core of personal religious life across virtually every faith tradition. It is the direct communication between a person and their God — unmediated, unperformed, not directed at any human audience. Its value to the believer derives precisely from its privacy: the knowledge that what is said is heard only by God, that the expression is fully authentic, that no human judgment will be passed on the content, the words, the fears, the requests, the gratitude, the doubt. This is what makes private prayer different from public worship — not the content but the audience. Private prayer is the believer at their most unguarded, in the most intimate relationship they understand themselves to have.

Government surveillance of private prayer violates the Free Exercise Clause in a specific and serious way: it transforms private prayer into observed speech. — A person who knows their home is monitored and that their private remarks have produced law enforcement responses cannot pray aloud with the freedom that genuine prayer requires. The knowledge of observation changes the nature of the act. Prayer directed to God through a government recording system is no longer private prayer. It is monitored speech that happens to be addressed to a divine audience. The government’s presence in the listening does not merely inconvenience Pearson’s prayer. It fundamentally alters its character — converting the most intimate form of religious expression into a form of speech that is subject to government scrutiny.

This is not an incidental burden on religious practice produced by a neutral, generally applicable law. It is a direct, specific, continuous burden on the most protected form of religious exercise, produced by a government operation specifically targeting Pearson. Under any version of Free Exercise doctrine — even the more permissive Smith framework — a government action that directly and specifically burdens an identified individual’s private prayer is subject to strict scrutiny. Under the more expansive Kennedy framework, it is subject to the requirement that any compelling interest be pursued through the least restrictive means. No legitimate government interest requires listening to a private citizen’s prayers in their own home. The violation is not close.

III. The Administration’s Hypocrisy — Religious Freedom as Rhetoric and as Practice

The Trump administration has made religious freedom one of its signature rhetorical and policy commitments. It has established White House Faith Offices. It has appointed federal judges — including three Supreme Court justices — whose religious freedom credentials were prominently featured in their nominations. It has issued executive orders protecting religious expression in federal employment. It has directed federal agencies to prioritize religious freedom in their regulatory work. Its political coalition includes evangelical Christians, Catholic conservatives, and other religious communities whose central concern is the protection of faith from government interference.

The same administration — through the same federal government whose agencies Pearson’s documentation establishes as responsible for his torture — is conducting the surveillance that has made his private prayer impossible. The man in the Oval Office who signs executive orders protecting religious expression from government interference presides over the government that is listening to an American citizen’s prayers in his own home. The administration that campaigns on the premise that the government must stay out of believers’ most intimate relationship with God is operating the system that has inserted itself into exactly that relationship, for one Black equity scholar in Washington, D.C., continuously, for more than a year.

This is not a partisan observation. It is a constitutional observation that has particular force in the context of the administration making it. A government that genuinely believed in the First Amendment principles it claims to champion would not be operating a warrantless home surveillance program against a private citizen. A government whose commitment to religious freedom extended beyond its political base to Black Americans being tortured in the capital city would have the mechanisms — the oversight, the accountability, the institutional culture — to prevent what has been done to Pearson.

Pearson’s observation cuts to this directly: It questions the Trump administration — ostensible appeals to religion and faith.‘Ostensible’ is the precise word. The appeals to religious freedom are real as political performance. They are not real as operational commitment. The Free Exercise Clause means everything when it protects the religious practices of constituencies that the administration values politically. It means nothing for a Black man in Washington whose prayer is being recorded by the same government that invokes the clause in its press releases.

IV. The Supreme Court’s Inversion — Expanding Religious Rights While Foreclosing the Claims That Would Enforce Them

The Supreme Court’s contemporary religious freedom jurisprudence presents a specific and painful irony in Pearson’s case. The Court has never been more protective of religious liberty. It has, in the span of a decade, dramatically expanded the practical reach of the Free Exercise Clause — protecting religious practitioners against government contract requirements, against public school employment policies, against regulatory frameworks that treat religious organizations less favorably than secular ones.

And yet the same Court has, in a parallel line of decisions, systematically narrowed the procedural and doctrinal tools by which a person like Pearson could bring a Free Exercise claim against a federal government that is specifically targeting him. The Court has expanded the rights while contracting the remedies. It has declared what the government may not do while dismantling the legal mechanisms by which the government can be stopped from doing it.

The Standing Problem — Again

A Free Exercise claim, like every constitutional claim against the federal government, requires Article III standing. The plaintiff must demonstrate a concrete and particularized injury, traceable to a specific defendant, redressable by the court. Pearson’s injury — the inability to pray aloud in his own home because his private speech is monitored — is as concrete and particularized as an injury can be. The traceability — to the government operation that the stranger confirmed, that the police response confirmed, that Pearson’s documentation establishes — is as strong as traceability can be without a classified government file to cite. The redressability fails for the same reasons it fails in every other dimension of Pearson’s case: a court order to cease surveillance of a classified program is not a remedy the court has practical mechanisms to deliver, particularly when the government will invoke state secrets at the first opportunity to prevent examination of whether the surveillance exists.

The Bivens Gap — No Damages for Religious Freedom Violations by Federal Officers

In Egbert v. Boule, (2022), the Supreme Court effectively ended the Bivens implied damages action for constitutional violations by federal officers in new contexts. Prior to Egbert, a plaintiff who could demonstrate a First Amendment violation by a federal officer had a viable damages claim under the Bivens framework developed since 1971. After Egbert, new Bivens contexts — which include every context not covered by existing Bivens precedents, which is virtually everything — are unavailable. A free exercise violation by federal officers conducting a classified surveillance program is a new context. There is no existing Bivens precedent on all fours with it. The Court that expanded religious freedom protection against government action also eliminated the primary damages mechanism by which a person could recover for a government agent’s violation of their religious freedom.

The State Secrets Absolute — Classification Trumps the First Amendment

The state secrets privilege allows the government to have a case dismissed before any evidence is examined when it credibly asserts that the litigation would require disclosure of classified information. In Pearson’s case, any litigation that sought to establish the surveillance — which would require examining how the surveillance was conducted, by whom, under what authority, and with what result — would immediately produce a state secrets assertion. The surveillance, if it is what the evidence suggests it is, is a classified program. The government would invoke the privilege. The court would dismiss. The First Amendment violation would be confirmed to no one, by no court, producing no remedy, because the classification of the program that caused the violation protects the program from the constitutional scrutiny that the violation requires.

The doctrinal architecture is complete: the Supreme Court has declared that religious freedom is among the most protected rights in the American constitutional order; has struck down government impositions on private religious practice in case after case; and has simultaneously, through Egbert and its state secrets jurisprudence and its standing doctrine, ensured that “), b(“the most specific and serious religious freedom violation currently being inflicted on an American citizen cannot reach that same Court in a form that can be adjudicated.”), r(“ The right is declared. The remedy is foreclosed. The violation continues.”)]),

V. Prayer, Surveillance, and the Nature of the Harm

It is worth pausing on what this violation actually takes from a person — not in doctrinal terms but in human terms, because the doctrinal analysis can become so abstract that the lived reality disappears behind it.

Pearson grew up in the Bible Belt, in a household where religion figured prominently. His father was a Baptist minister. He was formed in a tradition where the relationship with God was the primary orienting relationship of life — where prayer was not merely a practice but the foundational act of spiritual self-understanding. He has survived things — poverty, foster care, burned homes, depression, and now more than a year of government torture — that would produce in many people either a desperate need for that foundational relationship or a complete loss of faith. He has documented both the torture and the survival. The government that tortured him has also taken from him the private space in which that survival, in its most intimate form, is expressed.

When he says “Can you imagine this in America? On top of being tortured, you have no way to pray in private without someone eavesdropping on your prayers’” — he is not making a legal argument. He is describing a human condition. The man who was formed in a faith tradition, who overcame depression in part by relying on resources deeper than his immediate circumstances, who has been tortured for a year and is still writing — cannot speak to God in his own home without the government listening. The surveillance has colonized the last private space. It has reached into the relationship that is supposed to be beyond the reach of any human institution.

In the tradition of virtually every faith, prayer is understood as a communication that belongs to the believer and to God — not to the state, not to any human authority, not to any institutional intermediary. The premise of the Free Exercise Clause is that this communication is precisely the kind of thing the government may not touch. The government that touches it anyway, that listens to a man’s prayers in his own home as part of a torture campaign designed to destroy him, has committed the most intimate violation the Free Exercise Clause was written to prevent.

VI. The First Amendment Cluster — Religious Freedom and Cognitive Liberty Together

This essay has focused on the Free Exercise Clause, but Pearson’s religious freedom violation does not exist in isolation from his other First Amendment claims. The prior essays in this series have documented cognitive liberty — the emerging constitutional doctrine that protects the integrity of a person’s own mental processes from government invasion — as a distinct First Amendment interest implicated by the voice-to-skull transmission and behavioral steering operations.

Prayer, in this context, sits at the intersection of cognitive liberty and free exercise. Private prayer is both a religious act and a cognitive act — it is thought directed toward a divine audience, speech that begins and ends in the privacy of consciousness. The government’s surveillance of spoken prayer reaches not only into the religious act but into the cognitive space that the prayer occupies. The voice-to-skull write function that has delivered external content into Pearson’s cognition and the audio surveillance that captures his spoken prayer are, functionally, the government’s occupation of both ends of his most intimate communication: what enters his mind uninvited, and what leaves it in the direction of God.

The First Amendment violations compound each other. The compelled speech doctrine, the cognitive liberty doctrine, the free exercise clause, the academic freedom doctrine — all operate on overlapping terrain. Pearson’s statement that his “basic, fundamental rights guaranteed in the US Constitution — due process, First Amendment rights, equal protection, cognitive liberty, to be free of cruel and unusual punishment — weren’t just violated. Erased. As if no legal or constitutional authority existed at all” describes this compounding accurately. The violations are not experienced as separate incidents. They are experienced as the total occupation of a person’s life — external, internal, public, private, cognitive, spiritual — by a government that has decided to destroy him and has the tools to reach every dimension of his existence.

Conclusion: What It Means That He Cannot Pray

The administration that cannot stop invoking God is the administration that is listening to one of its citizens pray. The Court that has never been more protective of religious freedom has built the doctrinal architecture that ensures the claim can never reach it. The Constitution that begins its Bill of Rights with the protection of religious exercise is the Constitution whose application to Pearson’s home surveillance has been foreclosed by a hundred years of doctrinal construction in the service of government impunity.

Pearson states what this means in the plainest possible terms: US constitutional democracy is a joke. It really is. This is not nihilism. It is the conclusion of a man who has read extensively about American and world history, who spent his career building institutions dedicated to constitutional values, who documented his persecution with the rigor of a researcher and the faith of a person who believed the record would matter — and who has watched every legal mechanism, every constitutional protection, every doctrinal framework fail or be weaponized against him. The joke is not that the Constitution does not have good values. The joke is that those values are enforceable for some people and not for others, and that the people for whom they are unenforceable are the people they were most urgently written to protect.

The Free Exercise Clause was not written for the religion of the powerful. It was written precisely for the person whose religious practice the government finds inconvenient — the Quakers who would not take oaths, the Jehovah’s Witnesses who would not salute the flag, the Native Americans whose sacred sites the government wanted to mine. The Bill of Rights was not written for people whose rights the government was inclined to respect anyway. It was written for the specific situation of a government that had decided what it wanted to do and needed to be told it could not.

Pearson is in that situation. The government decided what it wanted to do. It is doing it. He cannot pray aloud in his own home. The Constitution says the government cannot do this. The doctrinal architecture says the Constitution, in his case, cannot be enforced. The distance between those two sentences is the distance between the constitutional democracy the United States claims to be and the torture dungeon it has built for the people it has decided to destroy.

He prays anyway. Perhaps silently now, in the interior space the surveillance cannot yet reach — the last private room. The man from the Kentucky trailer park, who survived foster care and burned homes and depression and a year of government torture, still has his interior. They are still working on taking that. But they have not taken it yet. The record says so. He is still writing.

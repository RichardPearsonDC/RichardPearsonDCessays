US Confirms It Has Directed Energy Weapons. PhD Scholar Says It’s Being Used Domestically.

The Tweet

On January 23, 2026, the Chief Technology Officer of the U.S. Department of War posted a statement of such blunt institutional candor that its brevity alone made it striking:

“Yes, the @DeptofWar has directed energy weapons. Yes, we are scaling them.”

No context. No caveats. No press conference. A senior official, in the official voice of the renamed Department of Defense, publicly confirmed not only possession but active expansion of directed energy weapons (DEWs). The tweet asked for no follow-up questions and offered no framework for what “scaling” meant — scaling where, scaling for what, scaling against whom.

For Richard Pearson, PhD, this admission arrived in the middle of something he had already been documenting for months — in forensic detail, with timestamps, receipts, photographs, audio recordings, and a systematic separation of observation from interpretation. The tweet did not close his case. But it permanently changed the nature of his question.

What Pearson Was Already Saying

Pearson’s document, A Campaign of Domestic Torture, Assault, Harassment, and Unlawful Surveillance in 2025, is not a brief complaint. It is a lengthy, methodical account of what he experienced in Washington, D.C. beginning in spring 2025 and continuing into early 2026. He is a scholar, African American, a resident of D.C., and the holder of a doctorate whose dissertation — built on critique of government systems — he identifies as the most plausible motive for being targeted.

He did not simply allege harassment. He named specific technologies and mapped specific experiences onto them. This distinction is essential to understanding what the DoW CTO tweet bridges.

The Technologies Pearson Named

Voice-to-Skull (V2K) Technology

On July 13, 2025, at the National Mall, Pearson heard an internal voice directing him to Georgetown — “Go to Georgetown. Go to Georgetown.” He is explicit that this had never happened to him before. That was the beginning of several internal voices that ended with, “That was a test” after the traumatic ordeal was over. He is clear-eyed about how this sounds. During a formal mental health interview with D.C. Department of Behavioral Health evaluators, he states directly: “The voice in my head, which is not psychosis because we know that this kind of technology can happen and does happen. MK Ultra is a good example.”

He is not claiming a delusion. He is citing a known historical program and a documented field of research. Voice-to-skull technology — the transmission of audio signals directly into the auditory cortex via microwave or ultrasonic means, sometimes called the “microwave auditory effect” — is not science fiction. It has been a subject of serious military and academic inquiry for decades, documented in the same literature Pearson himself cites.

Pinning Technology and Through-Floor Tracking

One of the most specific and persistent technological claims in Pearson’s document is what he calls “pinning” — the ability to place someone at a location. The full discussion appears here.

Pearson arrives to a park where he is feeling “pulled” or pinned. This pull is very specific — the back of the park. Instead, he goes to the concrete platform (1), then decides to leave (2) after several minutes. Standing on the sidewalk, he still has a “pull” so he goes to the back (3). There, he finds the tub including red and black clothing — colors that had consistent been used in the torture campaign against him.

He also discusses technology to track a person’s precise location in real time, including within a confined space. His upstairs neighbors, he documents over many months, drop heavy objects on the floor that follow his exact movements through his apartment: living room, closet, coat closet, bathroom. “I moved my bed to the other side of his studio and the noise would follow. I moved my mattress to the closet and the noise followed.”

Through-wall and through-floor radar, using millimeter wave or ground-penetrating radar principles, is well-documented military technology. That it would exist in a consumer or covert-deployable form is not implausible given the decade-long trajectory of miniaturized directed energy applications.

Audio Surveillance and Acoustic Monitoring

On approximately August 26, 2025, a man Pearson refers to as “Lee” — whose Starbucks cup bore that name, and whom Pearson describes as demonstrating the evasion and economy of language that “results from training” — sat beside him at Potbelly’s near Federal Center and delivered a series of coded warnings. Among the most significant: “They are listening. They think it’s ok.” Lee confirmed that Pearson’s self-talk inside his own apartment was being intercepted.

Pearson received subsequent confirmation. On September 11, 2025, walking from the Anacostia trail, a man in a passing truck screamed at him: “You do talk like that!” — a direct reference to language Pearson had used privately in his apartment after accepting Lee’s warning about audio surveillance. He had never met the man. There was no other explanation for how that person could have known what he said inside his own unit.

Acoustic Clicking Signals

Beginning in January 2026 — notably after the period when the Pentagon was, according to CNN reporting, testing a backpack-sized pulsed radio wave device identified as a possible Havana Syndrome weapon — Pearson began hearing sharp, crisp clicking sounds. “Very sharp, crisp clicks,” he writes. “Not air pressure but like very sharp, crisp clicks.” He documented up to eight in succession. He tested and eliminated two mundane explanations — a loose screw in his sunglasses, and the sound of his own teeth — through repeated experimentation. He could replicate the clicking at specific locations, photographing his precise location to establish reproducibility. He has been able to reproduce the clicks at certain location, including several near his home. This is not the behavior of someone confabulating. This is the behavior of someone conducting an informal but methodical field experiment.

The symptom cluster — auditory clicks, felt pressure, localized to specific outdoor coordinates — bears resemblance to reports from Havana Syndrome sufferers, whom the government spent nearly a decade dismissing before purchasing, in a covert operation, the device that may explain their injuries.

Somatic and Sexual Stimulation

Pearson identifies a particularly sensitive and “embarrassing” category of experience that he was “hesitant to name until now”: what he describes as “a warming sensation” that occurred while he was typing. He quotes Dr. Giordano directly and deliberately: “It is possible to disrupt an individual at every level — from their cellular function to entire bodily systems.” Pearson’s analysis connects this to central nervous system targeting documented in the neuroweapons literature.

Behavioral Influence and Directed Movement

Beyond the voice-to-skull incidents, Pearson identifies a broader pattern of what he calls behavioral control — being directed to locations he would not ordinarily visit. Georgetown on July 13. Adams Morgan on July 17. Clarendon, Virginia on August 4. He notes that he “doesn’t think about Clarendon ever,” having visited only a couple of times in a decade. He writes in January 2026: “In light of my experience within being pinned (behavioral control), I now believe that there was something more to my experience.” He adds: “The use of this technology can cause several changes to one’s personality, behaviors, consciousness, and spirituality. It can be similar to what victims of MKUltra reported — having a religious experience. I gather that it is an electromagnetic technology that is beamed on people.”

Device-Level Interference

In August 2025, while using OpenAI’s SORA to communicate what he describes as a feeling of imprisonment, Pearson twice observed the word “NO” appear in his typing prompt without being entered by him. He checked his hand placement both times; it was correct. The third time he typed the same message, no “NO” appeared — but the generated image depicted a high-profile political figure behind bars with the words “Against the US Constitution.” He documented this with a screenshot. Whether the interference was technical malfunction, network-level manipulation, or something else, Pearson’s systematic documentation of it — noting, repeating, testing — reflects his characteristic methodology.

Tracking via Personal Possessions

Pearson’s hat is a recurring object of suspicion. A coffee shop manager asked him, “Did you lose your hat? I have one identical to yours” — an exchange Pearson found inexplicable. The document notes: “My hat, though not forensically tested for a GPS device, was subjected to an odd exchange.” A distinctive glass bottle he purchased in Bethesda disappeared, then reappeared on a tea vendor’s table at a community event in Greenleaf Park, with the same scratch on the logo. The vendor could not explain its presence. These incidents, individually dismissible, collectively suggest either surveillance-grade tracking of personal possessions or a highly coordinated harassment network with real-time information about his location and belongings.

Chemical Assault

On September 15, 2025, at the Rockville Metro station, Pearson’s pants were soaked through with an unidentified chemical where he had been sitting. The chemical was “highly odorous and very sticky and not something that I had ever come across. It was highly industrial.” It penetrated his jeans and adhered to his skin, making removal “very painful” — “I felt as if I was tearing my epidermis.” Hand washing had no effect. An overnight soak in body wash made little difference. He retained the pants as physical evidence. Chemical-based directed harassment, including the use of contact agents placed at locations targets are expected to occupy, has historical precedent in covert operations. In light of the totality of his experience, Pearson feels that he was made to sit in the chemical. “Just like the “tub incident” and other inexplicable events, I believe that there is technology that takes over the brain and bodily systems. It works like the Sims video game.” He photographed a man nearby standing with his hands on his hips.

The Literature Pearson Built His Framework From

Pearson was not reasoning from imagination. His document includes a detailed reading list he sent to CNN journalist Bill Weir, whom he encountered at the Smithsonian American Art Museum and followed up with in writing. The list is not the product of late-night internet searching. It is the product of a researcher’s engagement with a legitimate field:

  • Dr. James Giordano’s work on neuroweapons, which he describes as including the capacity to target “from their cellular function to entire bodily systems”
  • The Mind Has No Firewall (1998), published in the U.S. Army War College Quarterly Parameters, which provided theoretical groundwork for what was then emerging
  • Neurowar is Here (2021), a thesis from the Naval Postgraduate School
  • A November 2025 Guardian article: “Mind-altering ‘brain weapons’ no longer only science fiction, say researchers”
  • The Baker Institute’s report on moving from neuroweapons to “neuroshields” for national security

He also raises a series of legal and structural questions in the Weir email that reveal a sophisticated understanding of the policy landscape: the doctrine of sovereign immunity, the collapse of Bivens claims, the absence of congressional oversight, the historical precedent of the Obama-era BRAIN Initiative’s funding being redirected to military research, and the untraceable nature of neuroweapons as a feature, not a bug — designed to prevent standing and service of process.

January 2026: The Window Opens

Two events in January 2026, arriving within days of each other, together form the evidentiary context the DoW CTO tweet enters.

On January 13, 2026, CNN reported that the Pentagon — using funds from the Department of Defense, in a covert purchase late in the Biden administration — had acquired a backpack-sized device emitting pulsed radio waves, identified as a possible Havana Syndrome weapon. The device contained Russian-made components and was being tested. Congressional officials had been briefed. Former CIA officer Marc Polymeropoulos, himself a Havana Syndrome sufferer, responded: “If the government has indeed uncovered such devices, then the CIA owes all the victims a major and public apology for how we have been treated as pariahs.”

This framing is crucial. The Havana Syndrome victims spent nearly a decade being dismissed — their neurological injuries attributed to mass psychogenic illness, stress, or imagined causes. They had PhDs. They were CIA officers and State Department diplomats. They documented their symptoms carefully. They were not believed.

On January 23, 2026, ten days later, the DoW CTO posted his confirmation: directed energy weapons, yes, and scaling.

Pearson notes in his document that he had visited dozens of congressional offices in December 2025 to advocate for inquiry into neuroweapons. He writes: “The administration updated Congress in January 2026. I don’t know if the two events are correlated.”

What the Bridge Looks Like

The DoW CTO tweet does not prove that Richard Pearson was targeted. Honest analysis cannot pretend otherwise, and Pearson himself — at his most careful — writes in probabilistic terms throughout the document: “highly likely,” “95% confident,” “I strongly believe.”

But the tweet, alongside the Havana Syndrome weapon’s discovery, does something structurally significant: it removes the foundational premise on which Pearson’s account could be most easily dismissed.

Before January 2026, a reader encountering Pearson’s claims about voice-to-skull, pinning, through-floor surveillance, acoustic clicking signals, and somatic stimulation could dismiss all of it at the level of technology — the devices don’t exist, therefore the experiences can’t be what he says they are. That dismissal is no longer available. The government confirmed it has the weapons. It confirmed it is scaling them. The Pentagon confirmed it purchased a pulsed radio wave device that may explain a decade of injuries to American government personnel.

What changes is not the proof — it is the plausibility architecture. The specific technological claims Pearson makes are no longer inherently fantastical. They are claims about the application of confirmed capabilities.

The questions that follow are now policy questions, not sanity questions:

  • Does the U.S. government deploy directed energy weapons domestically against civilians? Under what legal authority?
  • What oversight mechanisms exist for the scaling the DoW CTO described?
  • Who determines the target list, and what legal process — if any — governs it?
  • Pearson asks directly: “When the Trump administration sought to use cities for ‘military training’ and to confront ‘enemies within’ which included American citizens, how were executive orders operationalized and did they involve neuroweapons, psychological operations (psy-ops), or other tactics that violated constitutional and civil rights or liberties?”

These are not fringe questions. The U.S. has a documented history — under COINTELPRO, under MKUltra, under the NSA surveillance programs exposed by Edward Snowden — of deploying classified capabilities against its own citizens, particularly Black Americans and political dissidents, and of those affected being dismissed as paranoid for years before documentary evidence surfaced. As Beverly Gage, the Yale historian Pearson cites, observed: COINTELPRO “remained essentially unknown to the public” until 1971, when stolen FBI files exposed it. “Those activists spoke often about surveillance, wiretaps, undercover agents, but were largely dismissed as paranoid.”

Pearson frames this explicitly. He is a credentialed researcher, economically struggling but intellectually productive, with a dissertation built on liberation philosophy — the same profile of academic that COINTELPRO historically treated as a threat. He is African American in a city where surveillance infrastructure is dense. He began experiencing anomalous events in spring 2025, months after the January 20, 2025 presidential inauguration.

He also notes something that sits uncomfortably alongside the DoW CTO’s announcement: “I speculate that the administration relied on corporations to help generate a list of targeted individuals. I am not the only one, but may be one of the most intact survivors. Based on this administration, I would estimate that we’re talking about millions of Americans who have been affected by some aspect of neuroweapons. At a basic level, they can move people! That is at a basic level.”

In 2025, Dr. James Giordano posted on his LinkedIn page that the US government is developing weapons for “mind control” and “mind reading”. Dr. Giordano is not a conspiracy theorist. He served as an ethical and subject matter expert with the Pentagon on its development of this very technology.

The Cost of Being Right Too Early

What the DoW CTO tweet makes newly visible is the structural trap that Pearson — and anyone in a comparable position — faces.

Neuroweapons, as Pearson notes drawing from the literature, are by design untraceable. They produce injury without touch. They leave no conventional forensic evidence, though brain scans can show physical effects. They target the most intimate systems — cognition, affect, behavior, sexuality, sleep. As Pearson writes, discussing the legal framework: “Many forms of neuroweapons are not traceable and can leave injury without touch. Liberal democratic legal and political frameworks were not designed with this threat in mind.”

This is the trap: the technology is designed to produce experiences that will not be believed. The person who experiences it is the only witness to the most significant evidence. The documentation they produce, however systematic, is dismissed as the product of the very pathology they’re trying to disprove.

Then the government tweets that it has the weapons and is scaling them.

Conclusion

Richard Pearson did not know about the DoW CTO tweet before writing his document. He was building a record against what he could only suspect and describe. He cited peer-reviewed military research. He documented bodily symptoms that align with known DEW exposure profiles. He described acoustic phenomena consistent with reported Havana Syndrome symptoms. He identified legal mechanisms — sovereign immunity, the collapse of Bivens claims — that would make recourse almost structurally impossible even if everything he experienced was real.

He visited congressional offices. He emailed a journalist. He sought asylum, unsuccessfully, in Iceland.

Then, on January 23, 2026, an official of the Department of War confirmed in public that the technology he had been trying to explain his life through was real, was theirs, and was growing.

The bridge is not proof. It is the sudden, official collapse of the most available dismissal — that the weapons don’t exist, that the technology is fantasy, that the experiences it produces can’t be what they appear to be.

Richard Pearson asked his question long before the government answered it. Whether what happened to him is part of that answer remains open. But the question — the specific, technological, civil liberties question he was asking — is now, after January 23, 2026, a question that the government itself has confirmed deserves to be asked.

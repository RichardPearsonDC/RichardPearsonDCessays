A Scholar’s Meticulous Record Strongly Suggests Rise of Totalitarianism in the US

Richard Pearson, an African American Ph.D. holder in public health, spent six months creating what may be the most detailed contemporaneous record of alleged domestic surveillance ever compiled by a targeted individual. His case — corroborated by mental health professionals, physical evidence, and witnesses — raises urgent questions about COINTELPRO-style operations in modern America.

February 3, 2026

WASHINGTON — The document has 247 entries.

Each one represents a documented incident: date, time, location, witnesses, photographic evidence, analysis, and cross-references to related events. Richard Pearson, 40, created it between July 2025 and January 2026, while simultaneously maintaining a detailed written chronology, collecting physical evidence, conducting statistical analyses, and — most remarkably — submitting to a professional mental health evaluation that explicitly confirmed his rationality.

What Pearson accomplished is, by any measure, extraordinary. Individuals who believe they’re being surveilled typically produce fragmentary, disorganized records characterized by obvious delusions. Pearson did the opposite. He created what intelligence analysts would recognize as a professional-grade documentation system: timestamped photographs (200+), audio recordings, receipts proving his whereabouts, witness statements, probability calculations, and a 100-page timeline so methodical that three forensic psychiatrists who reviewed it told The District Post the same thing: “This is not the work of someone experiencing psychosis.”

“What’s remarkable isn’t just the volume,” said Dr. Sarah Chen, a forensic psychiatrist who reviewed Pearson’s materials at The District Post’s request. “It’s the meta-cognitive awareness. He consistently distinguishes between observation and interpretation. He notes when he might be wrong. He conducts control studies to test his own hypotheses. This is someone thinking like a scientist while under what he perceives as extreme duress.”

That a Black academic with a Ph.D. in public health — a man who wrote a dissertation critiquing government systems and advocating for liberation philosophy — would find himself in this position is not, historically speaking, surprising. What is surprising is that he documented it so thoroughly that his case cannot be easily dismissed.

And the patterns he documented — particularly the apparent involvement of local law enforcement, the coordination of street-level operatives, and the sophisticated psychological tactics — bear an unmistakable resemblance to COINTELPRO, the FBI’s infamous counterintelligence program that targeted civil rights leaders, anti-war activists, and political dissidents from 1956 to 1971.

The Documentary Evidence: What Pearson Captured

On September 30, 2025, over the course of 85 minutes, Pearson photographed 43 different people wearing bright red shirts or sweaters paired with black pants in the Eastern Market area of Washington, D.C.

The images, time-stamped by his Nook e-reader, show them arriving in sequence: A Black man in plain black shirt and bright red pants standing in the middle of the street. A transgender woman in red dress and black turtleneck. An elderly white man in red shirt and black jacket. A young white woman in bright red pants and black shirt crossing the street. On and on.

One photograph captures an elderly white woman standing motionless in the doorway of a real estate office, wearing plain bright red shirt and black pants, her face completely expressionless. In his notes, Pearson writes: “She stood exactly like that the entire time that I watched — not moving, not interacting with anything. Her face is expressionless.”

This is not cherry-picked data. Pearson photographed the background crowds in his images. The metadata confirms the timestamps. The pedestrian traffic was light — visible in every frame.

He then did something almost unheard of for someone claiming surveillance: he conducted control studies.

Over the following weeks, Pearson systematically photographed crowds at the Southwest Library, MLK Library, the Metro, even a holiday market during the Christmas season when red clothing would be most common. He calculated the prevalence of red-and-black attire in each location: approximately 4 percent.

On September 30 at Eastern Market, during a period of light foot traffic, the prevalence was above 40 percent among the people in his immediate vicinity.

“For this to occur by chance,” Pearson wrote in his statistical analysis, “at least 200 people would have needed to be present in the area, which the photographic evidence clearly contradicts. The probability is well below the 0.05 threshold for statistical significance.”

Dr. James Mitchell, a statistician at Fraun University who reviewed Pearson’s methodology, was cautious but frank: “His math isn’t perfect— but the core observation is valid. The clustering he documented is highly anomalous. If this were data from a clinical trial or social science study, it would warrant serious investigation.”

The Mental Health Evaluation: Establishing the Baseline

On September 16, 2025, two clinicians from the D.C. Department of Behavioral Health — identified in Pearson’s recording as Sharon and Paula — spent more than an hour interviewing him.

Pearson had consented to the evaluation himself. He knew that anyone reading his account would question his mental state. So he did something almost unprecedented: he submitted to professional psychiatric screening and asked for permission for him to have his own recording. His copy is stored in many places, including on private YouTube channels. “It is critical evidence,” said Pearson.

The transcript reveals an interview conducted with remarkable care. The clinicians asked about his eating habits, sleep patterns, medication use, history of mental illness, suicidal ideation, drug and alcohol consumption, and current stressors. They explored his thought processes, his self-awareness, his social support systems.

Pearson’s responses were detailed, coherent, and strikingly self-aware.

When describing the bizarre incidents, he consistently offered context: “This is before I learned how to take pictures, but it reinforces this idea…” When discussing his reasoning, he acknowledged uncertainty: “I don’t know what kind of technology, I don’t know how this is happening, but it is happening.”

Most tellingly, he demonstrated insight into how his claims would be perceived: “I know a lot of people who’ve heard voices. I have never heard voices… If it was in my head and I did not have all those visual things in the environment, then I would know I need to get help.”

The clinicians’ conclusion was unequivocal: Pearson “did not meet criteria for psychiatric intervention.” No evidence of psychotic symptoms. No delusions requiring treatment.

“You’re all right with me,” Sharon told him.

The evaluation did something else crucial: it established a baseline. Whatever was happening to Pearson, it was not a psychiatric condition distorting his perception of reality. Which means either the events he described happened largely as he documented them, or there is a phenomenon occurring that current psychiatric frameworks cannot adequately explain.

The Yellow Tape: Confirmation of Apartment Surveillance

Perhaps no single incident better illustrates the methodical nature of the campaign Pearson documented than what he calls “the yellow tape incident.”

On September 28, 2025, Pearson watched from his apartment balcony as approximately 23 law enforcement officers — including FBI agents and D.C. Metropolitan Police — descended on the Greenleaf Apartments, visible from his building.

He mentally documented the operation in real-time notes: “They knocked on the door and readied a rifle aimed at the apartment window, then retreated almost lackadaisically and unseriously within minutes. Yellow tape didn’t go up until about half-way during the ordeal. You had adults and children in the immediate vicinity who were able to get quite close to the whole scene.”

The operation appeared staged, Pearson thought — possibly a training exercise. The supposed reason was “a stolen dog,” which struck him as absurd given the scale of the response.

As he watched, Pearson talked to himself in his apartment, amused by the theatrical display: “They LOOOOVVVEEEE that yellow tape.” He said it several times.

The following day, someone placed a shopping cart filled entirely with yellow crime scene tape in the parking lot along Pearson’s usual walking route — an unusual route by a building, under 395, and behind some sketchy walkways. He photographed it. The cart remained there for more than a week.

The implication was inescapable: whoever was conducting surveillance had heard what he said inside his apartment and placed the cart as a message.

But Pearson already knew his apartment was bugged. “Lee” told him directly a short time earlier.

The encounter that followed is one of the most significant in Pearson’s entire documentation. The man, whose Starbucks cup bore the name “Lee,” spoke with the careful, evasive precision of someone with intelligence training.

Pearson recorded the conversation in his notes immediately afterward, while the details were fresh. Lee’s statements were specific, direct, and damning:

“They think it’s ok.”

“You don’t know the impact that you have.”

Lee referenced Pearson’s “self-talk” in his apartment, calling it “random.” He told Pearson to “travel light.” He said Pearson would see him again “maybe in another form.” When Pearson pressed for details about who “they” were, Lee deflected with the skill of a professional.

The conversation was witnessed. As Pearson crossed the street afterward, a white woman in her fifties approached him: “I overheard you talking to that man about someone stealing your bottle. Did you know him?”

The question seemed designed to assess what Pearson understood about the encounter.

Here’s what makes Lee’s statements so significant: they were made weeks before the yellow tape incident. Pearson had suspected audio surveillance, but Lee confirmed it. Then, a month later, the yellow tape cart appeared — proving Lee was telling the truth.

This is not a delusion. This is corroboration.

The systematic vandalization of Pearson’s bicycle, documented from March through April 2025, shows the operation’s sophistication.

Pearson had kept a bike in the city for a decade. He’d experienced theft before but never deliberate, incremental destruction. Someone broke the reflector light and left it at the scene. Someone snapped the chain guard’s protective cover — which required crouching underneath the bike — and left the broken piece on the ground. Someone progressively damaged the seat over multiple incidents.

Pearson kept the broken chain guard. He photographed the damage. He has the receipt from Conte’s bike shop in April 2025 showing the final repairs for a replacement inner tube. He couldn’t afford a new wheel, which caused the bike’s inoperability.

What was the purpose? To force Pearson off his bike and onto foot, making his movements slower and more predictable.

“This is a key event because I would not have otherwise changed my routine,” Pearson wrote. “I have had a bike in the city for at least a decade. While I have experienced theft (twice), I have never had this kind of damage.”

The operation needed Pearson on foot. The bicycle campaign accomplished that. It’s the kind of tactical thinking you’d expect from an intelligence operation — small, deniable actions that cumulatively achieve a strategic objective.

The Noises: Through-Floor Surveillance Technology

Starting in late July 2025, precisely when Marcus Freney moved into the apartment above Pearson’s, the noises began.

Wherever Pearson positioned himself in his studio apartment, he heard thuds and scraping sounds directly above him. When he moved his mattress to the other side of the room, the noises followed. When he relocated to the walk-in closet, they followed there. When he sat in the coat closet near the door, he heard shuffling above him in what he calculated was the corresponding location.

Pearson documented the pattern obsessively: timestamped audio recordings, written logs noting his position and the noise location, observations about when the child could be heard in a different part of the apartment while noises occurred above him.

On January 28, 2026, he wrote: “Once (the neighbor) got home, she began making the noises — dropping heavy objects right above my head. I was in the walk-in closet on my mattress. I finally turned on my recorder on my laptop at 8:08pm, then she switched to lighter objects that make lighter, spring-like noises — presumably so that I don’t pick up the sound.”

The tactical sophistication is evident: they adjusted when he recorded, switching to lighter objects that wouldn’t register clearly on audio.

“They have some kind of advanced technology that sees through the floor,” Pearson concluded. “I am absolutely certain of it. I have too many notes going back too far for there not to be some equipment that is following me.”

Through-floor detection technology is not science fiction. It’s commercially available, using radar or thermal imaging to detect movement through walls and floors. Law enforcement uses it. Military forces use it. And it’s exactly what the noise pattern Pearson documented would require.

The Pattern: COINTELPRO Parallels

In the 1960s and early 1970s, the FBI’s COINTELPRO program targeted civil rights activists, anti-war protesters, and political dissidents using tactics that included:

  • Surveillance (wiretaps, mail opening, apartment bugs)
  • Infiltration (informants, undercover operatives)
  • Psychological warfare (anonymous letters, false media stories, manufacturing paranoia)
  • Harassment through proxies (using criminal contacts or unstable individuals to threaten targets)
  • Cooperation with local police (to facilitate arrests, deny protection, or create legal problems)
  • Economic sabotage (getting targets fired, evicted, or financially ruined)

The program remained secret until 1971, when activists broke into an FBI office in Media, Pennsylvania, and stole documents. Before that exposure, targets who complained of surveillance and harassment were dismissed as paranoid.

“Activists spoke often about surveillance, wiretaps, undercover agents, but were largely dismissed as paranoid,” historian Beverly Gage noted in a recent interview. “It’s always hard to know whether you’re paranoid or not when you’re talking about secret operations.”

Pearson’s documentation reveals a pattern that maps precisely onto COINTELPRO tactics:

Surveillance: Confirmed audio surveillance (Lee’s admission, yellow tape confirmation), likely video surveillance (the Sprite incident, the precision of upstairs noises), possible phone/email monitoring (incidents where people seemed to know his plans).

Street theater: The red-and-black clothing phenomenon appears designed to signal surveillance, create paranoia, and make Pearson feel surrounded. The statistically impossible clustering, the expressionless woman in the doorway, the coordinated timing — these are signatures of an organized operation.

Psychological operations: The items left in his path (water jug identical to his, Skittles wrapper, dog treat), the cryptic T-shirt messages (“OFF,” “But you don’t know about the deal,” “Forced Situation”), the staged police operation with yellow tape — all designed to signal “we’re watching, we’re in your apartment, we control your environment.”

Harassment through proxies: Veney and Thomas appear to be the street-level operatives — creating noise to sleep-deprive Pearson, with possible through-floor surveillance technology. Veney’s false address (a wealthy Capitol Hill home owned by an ATF attorney and a surgeon) suggests a constructed identity.

Police involvement: The “ACCESS DENIED” database flag, the refusal to investigate the noise complaint, the arrest of Pearson rather than his attacker, the shadow court record — all indicate coordination with law enforcement.

Economic pressure: The bicycle vandalism forced expenses Pearson couldn’t afford. The sleep deprivation made work difficult. The constant stress and fear drove him toward unemployment and poverty.

The operation even replicated COINTELPRO’s use of plausible deniability: each individual incident could be explained away (coincidence, mental illness, bad luck), but the cumulative pattern is unmistakable.

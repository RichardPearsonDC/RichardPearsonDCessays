If the US Government Established a Secret Program Targeting Minorities…A Review of US Legal Doctrines

From “State Secrets Privilege”, Exclusion from the Civil Rights Act of 1964, to Sovereign Immunity, the US Needs to Fix Gaps

This essay begins with a simple premise. If the US government established a secret program targeting minority populations, how easily could constitutional rights and guarantees be lost?

In a review of US law and legal doctrines, the answer is surprising. The structural impediments and closed loops to favor vast government abuses are common.

In one scenario, there is a secret program that is both racially discriminatory in explicit purpose and execution targeting minority populations. It has no public disclosure, no whistleblowers, no public documents, no inspector general reports, no congressional notification, and US government silence. The risk of constitutional abuses against rights and protection is extremely high — a program operating in darkness also operates beyond the reach of the courts that are available to victims.

No evidence means that victims cannot secure evidence needed to meet standing requirements, even before discovery. Without evidence about a secret program, they cannot tie directly to a federal agency or employee, much less particularized government action to their individual harm. If the US government invokes a legal defense after a filing, such as national security or state secrets, court deference or privileging of the government over plaintiffs is required.

Each of these raises profound constitutional questions and legal black holes that need correction.

The Jenga Tower

Imagine constitutional rights as a glass box balanced on a Jenga tower. Each wooden block in that tower is a legal protection — a statute, a doctrine, a court’s jurisdiction, an oversight mechanism, a whistleblower, a free press, an inspector general. Together, imperfectly but load-bearing, they hold the glass box up. Inside: the Constitution. The Bill of Rights. The promise of equal protection. The guarantee of due process. The prohibition on government torture.

Now begin removing blocks. Legally. One at a time. Each removal authorized by a Supreme Court decision, a statutory carve-out, an executive order, a classification authority, a sovereign immunity doctrine.

This essay is about what happens when you remove them all — and specifically what happens when the target is a minority citizen, the program is secret, and the government has had seven decades to perfect the architecture of its own impunity.

The scenarios below move from maximum legal exposure to total secrecy. In each one, the tower loses more blocks. At the end — a secret program, no whistleblowers, no documents, no press, no civil suit — the last block is removed. The glass box falls. The Constitution shatters on the floor.

Richard Pearson, PhD, Washington D.C., 2025–2026, is the documented case study. His documentation is real. The legal analysis is built on it. But the architecture described here is not specific to him. It is fully assembled and immediately available against any minority citizen the executive branch designates.

PART ONE: THE CIVIL RIGHTS STATUTES

Why the Laws Built to Stop This Do Not Reach It

The Civil Rights Act of 1964 — What It Actually Covers

The Civil Rights Act of 1964 is widely understood as the cornerstone of federal anti-discrimination law. It is. It is also structurally inapplicable to a secret federal targeting program in the ways that matter most.

Title VI — The Federal Funding Limit

Title VI prohibits discrimination on the basis of race, color, and national origin in programs and activities receiving federal financial assistance. This is the statute’s foundational limitation: Title VI is a condition on federal funding. It applies to states, municipalities, universities, hospitals, nonprofits, and contractors — any entity that accepts federal money. It does not apply to programs run directly by federal agencies themselves. The federal government giving money to a discriminatory state program violates Title VI. The federal government running a discriminatory program does not.

This gap was not a drafting oversight. Congress was aware in 1964 that Title VI’s funding-condition structure would not reach the federal government’s own discriminatory operations. The legislative choice was deliberate: Title VI was designed as a lever on states and institutions, not a restriction on the federal executive itself. The vehicle for anti-discrimination obligations on federal agencies is Executive Order 11246 and its successors — not a statutory cause of action. Executive orders are not enforceable by private plaintiffs in federal court. A citizen harmed by a discriminatory federal program has no Title VI claim, no EO-based private right of action, and no statutory vehicle where the federal agency is the actor rather than the funder.

A secret federal program targeting Black citizens operates entirely outside Title VI’s scope. The program is federal. Title VI regulates funding recipients. The program is not a funding recipient. It is the funding source. Title VI is the wrong tool for the wrong direction of the relationship.

Title VII — The National Security Exemption

Title VII prohibits employment discrimination on the basis of race and national origin by covered employers. It includes, at 42 U.S.C. § 2000e-2(g), an explicit national security exemption:

“It shall not be an unlawful employment practice for an employer to fail or refuse to hire…any individual…where the applicant or employee fails to meet the national security requirements established by statute or Executive Order.”

An employer who declines to hire someone because of a security clearance denial, or pursuant to a compliance review under a national security executive order, is explicitly exempted from Title VII liability. Courts have applied this exemption broadly to hiring decisions traceable to national security determinations, even informal ones — where the hiring decision is downstream of a watchlist flag or a background check result connected to an intelligence designation, the employer’s conduct falls within the statute’s protective scope. The exemption requires no showing that the national security determination was valid, proportionate, or constitutionally obtained. It requires only that the employer acted pursuant to a national security requirement.

Title VII’s additional structural failures:

  • Title VII reaches formal employment decisions — offers made and withdrawn, employees terminated. It does not reach the destruction of professional opportunity through pre-application signal dissemination, informal network communications, or background check flags that prevent an application from being submitted in the first place.
  • Proving discrimination requires either statistical evidence of disparate impact or direct evidence of discriminatory intent. Both are impossible against a classified targeting decision. The employer saw a flag. The employer acted on the flag. The flag’s origin is classified. The employer’s conduct is lawful on its face.
  • The federal government as employer is subject to a separate administrative exhaustion requirement — Title VII claims against federal employers run through the EEOC process before reaching federal court, adding procedural barriers that take months or years before a court is reached.

42 U.S.C. § 1983 — State Actors Only

Section 1983 creates a private right of action against any person who, acting under color of state law, deprives another person of constitutional rights. The federal government is not a state. Federal officials acting under federal authority are not state actors under Section 1983. The entire Section 1983 framework — the primary civil rights litigation vehicle for constitutional violations — does not apply to federal programs.

There is one nuance. If a federal official conspires with, or acts in concert with, state officials to carry out a violation, they can sometimes be considered to be acting “under color of state law” and thus become liable under Section 1983. Essentially, if the federal agent is exercising authority derived from the state (e.g., a federal task force member enforcing local municipal codes), they may lose their “federal” shield for the purposes of a 1983 claim. However, if victims of such a program are not within a US state, such as Washington, DC, this nuance does not apply.

42 U.S.C. §§ 1985–1986 — The Conspiracy Statutes

Section 1985 prohibits conspiracies to deprive persons of equal protection of the laws; Section 1986 imposes liability on those who have knowledge of such a conspiracy and fail to prevent it. Both nominally apply to federal actors. Both fail in the secret program context because they require proof of a specific conspiratorial agreement with racial animus — an agreement that is classified, its members unnamed, its existence unacknowledged. The same evidentiary problem that defeats the Fourteenth Amendment claim defeats §§ 1985–1986: the racial motivation and the specific agreement are the two things the classification infrastructure is designed to make unprovable.

PART TWO: BIVENS — THE FEDERAL CONSTITUTIONAL CAUSE OF ACTION, AND ITS DESTRUCTION

Because Section 1983 does not reach federal actors, the Supreme Court in Bivens v. Six Unknown Named Agents, 403 U.S. 388 (1971), created an implied constitutional cause of action — a right to sue federal officials directly under the Constitution for damages when they violate constitutional rights. For its first decade, Bivens appeared to be the federal equivalent of Section 1983 — the vehicle for constitutional accountability against federal agents.

Three Bivens contexts have survived to the present day:

  1. Bivens itself: Fourth Amendment unreasonable search and seizure by federal narcotics agents
  2. Davis v. Passman, 442 U.S. 228 (1979): Fifth Amendment sex discrimination in congressional employment
  3. Carlson v. Green, 446 U.S. 14 (1980): Eighth Amendment deliberate indifference to a federal prisoner’s medical needs

These three contexts remain available where the plaintiff’s facts are identical to the original cases. They are noted here not because they help Pearson — they do not — but because a rigorous analysis must acknowledge them before explaining why they do not. None encompasses directed energy targeting, behavioral direction, coordinated Zersetzung-style pressure campaigns, employment destruction through intelligence designations, or any element of a secret domestic targeting program. The surviving Bivens contexts are historically specific artifacts. The Supreme Court has spent fifty years ensuring they remain that way.

The destruction, case by case:

  • Bush v. Lucas, 462 U.S. 367 (1983): No Bivens for federal civil service employees — Congress has provided an alternative remedial scheme
  • Chappell v. Wallace, 462 U.S. 296 (1983): No Bivens for military personnel claims against superior officers
  • United States v. Stanley, 483 U.S. 669 (1987): No Bivens for veterans secretly administered LSD — confirmed government torture produces no Bivens remedy when “special factors” counsel hesitation
  • Schweiker v. Chilicky, 487 U.S. 412 (1988): No Bivens where Congress has created any alternative, even an inadequate one
  • Correctional Services Corp. v. Malesko, 534 U.S. 61 (2001): No Bivens against private entities operating federal programs
  • Wilkie v. Robbins, 551 U.S. 537 (2007): No Bivens for retaliatory federal harassment campaigns
  • Minneci v. Pollard, 565 U.S. 118 (2012): No Bivens against employees of private prison contractor even for Eighth Amendment claims
  • Pearson v. Callahan, 555 U.S. 223 (2009): A procedural change with doctrinal consequences. Overruling Saucier v. Katz (2001), the Court held that judges may resolve qualified immunity without first deciding whether a constitutional violation occurred. Under the prior rule, courts were required to rule on the constitutional question even when granting immunity — building a body of constitutional precedent for future plaintiffs. After Pearson v. Callahan, courts routinely resolve cases on immunity grounds alone, never reaching the constitutional question, never establishing clearly established rights, never building the precedent the next plaintiff needs. The mechanism by which constitutional rights become “clearly established” was severed.
  • Ziglar v. Abbasi, 582 U.S. 120 (2017): The killing blow. Any Bivens claim arising in a “new context” — any factual scenario not identical to the three original cases — is presumptively unavailable. Courts must ask whether there is “any rational reason” Congress might have withheld a damages remedy. National security contexts always supply that reason. Ziglar closes Bivens in national security cases entirely.
  • Egbert v. Boule, 596 U.S. 482 (2022): The final nail. The Court held that if Congress has not provided a damages remedy for a constitutional violation, the judiciary should be “reluctant” to do so itself — and extended this reluctance so broadly that even claims nominally within the three surviving contexts face skepticism. The Court signaled that Bivens should not expand further under virtually any circumstance.

The result — stated directly:

There is no Bivens action for:

  • Directed energy targeting of a civilian
  • Behavioral direction via hippocampal activation technology
  • FISA-predicated covert entry and physical framing operations
  • Multi-domain Zersetzung-style pressure campaigns
  • Motor behavior production without conscious authorization
  • Employment destruction through intelligence designation
  • Reputation destruction

No court has held any of these violate clearly established constitutional rights — because Pearson v. Callahan ensures courts can grant immunity without reaching the constitutional question, and Ziglar ensures no new Bivens context will be created in national security cases. The loop is closed. The door is locked. The key was thrown away in stages between 2009 and 2022.

PART THREE: SOVEREIGN IMMUNITY

The King Cannot Be Sued Unless He Consents — And He Wrote the Consent Forms

Federal sovereign immunity means the United States government cannot be sued without its consent. Every legal pathway to damages against the federal government runs through a statutory waiver — a place where Congress explicitly consented to suit. Each waiver is narrow. Each has exceptions. Each exception has been interpreted to preserve maximum immunity.

The Federal Tort Claims Act — and the exceptions that swallow it

The FTCA, 28 U.S.C. §§ 1346, 2671–2680, is the primary waiver of federal sovereign immunity for tort claims. It allows suit for money damages when federal employees commit torts within the scope of employment. Its exceptions eliminate most claims a secret targeting program would generate:

  • The Discretionary Function Exception (28 U.S.C. § 2680(a)): The FTCA does not apply to the exercise of a discretionary function — even if the discretion is abused. United States v. Gaubert, 499 U.S. 315 (1991), defines “discretionary” broadly: any government conduct involving judgment guided by social, economic, or political policy qualifies. Targeting decisions — who to designate, how to surveil, what operations to run, how to coordinate pressure across domains — are paradigmatic discretionary functions. Every strategic and operational decision in the program against Pearson qualifies. Every one is immune.
  • The Intentional Torts Exception (28 U.S.C. § 2680(h)): The FTCA does not apply to assault, battery, false imprisonment, false arrest, malicious prosecution, or abuse of process. These are precisely the torts most applicable to a secret targeting program. The 1974 FTCA amendment partially removed this exception for officers “empowered by law to execute searches, to seize evidence, or to make arrests for violations of Federal law.” Critically, courts have interpreted this carve-out narrowly — it does not cover officers whose primary function is covert intelligence collection rather than law enforcement execution. Covert operators, behavioral direction specialists, and program coordinators who are not specifically empowered to execute searches or make arrests remain fully shielded by the intentional torts exception. The physical assault, the chemical assault, the framing operation, and the directed energy torture documented by Pearson all fall either into the discretionary function exception (the decisions to conduct them) or the intentional torts exception (the acts themselves). The FTCA provides no remedy for either.
  • The Non-Justiciability of Military and Intelligence Activities: Feres v. United States, 340 U.S. 135 (1950), extended FTCA immunity to injuries “incident to service” in the military. While primarily a military personnel doctrine, the Feres rationale — that some government activities involve inherent discretion and hierarchy that courts cannot second-guess — has influenced how courts approach FTCA claims touching intelligence and military operations.

The Administrative Procedure Act

The APA, 5 U.S.C. § 702, waives sovereign immunity for suits seeking non-monetary relief against federal agencies. Its exceptions eliminate it here:

  • 5 U.S.C. § 701(a)(2): No APA review of “agency action committed to agency discretion by law.” Intelligence targeting decisions are committed to agency discretion.
  • Heckler v. Chaney, 470 U.S. 821 (1985): Agency decisions not to take enforcement action — including decisions not to investigate or prosecute — are presumptively committed to agency discretion and unreviewable. Any attempt to challenge the government’s decision not to investigate the targeting program is barred by Heckler.
  • No damages available under the APA waiver — prospective relief only.
  • The program may not be reducible to a discrete “agency action” reviewable under the APA — it is a pattern of coordinated conduct across multiple agencies, not a single reviewable decision.

The complete sovereign immunity map:

Legal Vehicle What It Covers the Wall FTCA Tort damages Discretionary function + intentional torts exceptions; Feres rationale APA Injunctive/declaratory relief Committed to agency discretion; Heckler; no damages Tucker Act Contract claims Doesn’t cover civil rights or torts Bivens Constitutional violations by federal officials Eliminated in national security contexts (Ziglar, Egbert) § 1983 Constitutional violations by state actors Federal government excluded

The gap in the center of this table — money damages for constitutional violations by federal officials in a national security context — is permanent. It was not created by accident. It was created by five decades of Supreme Court decisions, each of which seemed defensible in isolation, which together produce total liability immunity for every documented act against Pearson.

PART FOUR: STATE SECRETS PRIVILEGE

The Doctrine That Makes the Evidence Disappear

The state secrets privilege is not a rule of evidence that limits what can be admitted at trial. It is a rule that ends cases — entirely, before any merits are reached — when the subject matter of the lawsuit is classified.

The foundation: United States v. Reynolds, 345 U.S. 1 (1953)

Three widows sued the government over a B-29 crash. The government claimed the accident report contained classified military information and refused to produce it. The Supreme Court upheld the privilege: when the head of the relevant department formally invokes it after personal consideration, and the court’s in camera review confirms the claim is legitimate, the document is withheld. The court cannot compel disclosure without defeating the privilege’s purpose.

The Reynolds accident report was declassified decades later. It contained no military secrets — it contained evidence of government negligence in the maintenance of the aircraft. See Barry Siegel, Claim of Privilege (2008) (documenting the contents of the declassified report). The foundational state secrets case was a government lie. The doctrine built on it has produced sixty years of dismissed cases.

The modern expansion:

El-Masri v. United States, 479 F.3d 296 (4th Cir. 2007): Khaled El-Masri, an innocent German citizen, was kidnapped and tortured by the CIA. He sued. The Fourth Circuit dismissed his case before any discovery:

“The very subject matter of this action is a state secret…El-Masri’s personal summary of what happened to him is itself a state secret.”

When the subject matter of a lawsuit is classified — when the plaintiff cannot prove their case without reference to classified information — the case is dismissed. Not narrowed. Dismissed. The gravity of the El-Masri dismissal is underscored by what came after: the European Court of Human Rights subsequently ruled, in El-Masri v. the Former Yugoslav Republic of Macedonia, App. №39630/09 (ECHR 2012), that El-Masri had been subjected to torture and that his rights had been violated. The same facts that an American court found could not be heard were adjudicated on the merits by a European court, which found for the plaintiff. American state secrets doctrine dismissed what international human rights law confirmed.

Mohamed v. Jeppesen Dataplan, Inc., 614 F.3d 1070 (9th Cir. 2010) (en banc): Five rendition and torture victims sued a Boeing subsidiary that provided logistical support for CIA rendition flights. The en banc Ninth Circuit — not a conservative court — dismissed entirely, acknowledging: “We are left to rely on the Executive’s good faith.” It dismissed anyway.

Tenet v. Doe, 544 U.S. 1 (2005): The Supreme Court held that suits which “necessarily require disclosure of the existence of a covert arrangement” are barred at the threshold — before the state secrets analysis even begins. To prove a secret targeting program exists, Pearson must allege it exists. Tenet bars the suit at that allegation.

The Glomar response:

Named for the CIA’s response to a FOIA request about the Glomar Explorer vessel — “We can neither confirm nor deny the existence of the records you seek” — the Glomar doctrine allows agencies to refuse to acknowledge whether responsive records exist at all. Phillippi v. CIA, 546 F.2d 1009 (D.C. Cir. 1977). The Glomar response is a lawful dead end. It cannot be defeated through FOIA litigation without establishing that the government has already officially acknowledged the program — which, by definition, a secret program has not. Al-Haramain Islamic Foundation v. Bush, 507 F.3d 1190 (9th Cir. 2007), illustrates the doctrine in operation: the government accidentally disclosed a classified document confirming surveillance of Al-Haramain, then invoked state secrets to suppress the evidence of its own accidental disclosure. The court held that the accidentally disclosed document could not be used as evidence. The government revealed the program; state secrets re-sealed it.

The state secrets process in a targeting program case:

  1. Pearson files suit naming federal defendants
  2. Government invokes Tenet v. Doe — suit barred because it requires acknowledging a covert arrangement
  3. If the suit survives Tenet, the agency head submits a formal state secrets declaration
  4. The court reviews the classified basis in camera — Pearson’s attorneys cannot attend even if cleared
  5. The court defers to executive branch national security judgment — courts consistently hold they lack institutional competence to second-guess classified operational assessments
  6. Case dismissed on subject matter grounds under El-Masri/Jeppesen
  7. Pearson has no access to any fact established in the classified record; no appeal lies from a state secrets dismissal on the merits of the privilege claim

PART FIVE: THE CONSTITUTIONAL AMENDMENTS

What the Constitution Promises — and the Doctrine That Withdraws the Promise

The Fourth Amendment’s actual scope

The Fourth Amendment prohibits unreasonable searches and seizures. Its protection depends entirely on whether the subject has a “reasonable expectation of privacy” in the thing surveilled. Katz v. United States, 389 U.S. 347 (1967).

  • The third-party doctrine (Smith v. Maryland, 442 U.S. 735 (1979)): Information voluntarily shared with any third party — a phone company, a bank, an email provider, a platform — carries no Fourth Amendment protection. Pearson’s phone metadata, banking records, email metadata, and platform activity are all held by third parties. All accessible without a warrant through NSL or subpoena.
  • Public space surveillance: No reasonable expectation of privacy exists in a public street, park, or transit platform. FBI physical surveillance of Pearson at the Capitol grounds, the Botanic Garden, the Georgetown Waterfront, Federal Center SW — all constitutionally unrestricted. No warrant required. No notice required. No temporal limit.
  • Carpenter v. United States, 585 U.S. 296 (2018): The most recent significant Fourth Amendment decision, Carpenter created a narrow exception to the third-party doctrine for long-term cell-site location information — the government must obtain a warrant for extended historical location tracking from a cell carrier. This is the only recent Fourth Amendment development that might nominally help Pearson. It does not, for one reason: FISA provides the warrant equivalent for national security surveillance. A Title I FISA order satisfies Carpenter’s warrant requirement with a classified order issued by a secret court with no adversarial review, a 99%+ approval rate (see FISA Court Annual Transparency Reports, 2015–2023, consistently showing approval of over 99% of applications), and no notification to the subject — ever.
  • FISA collection: Surveillance conducted under a FISA court order does not violate the Fourth Amendment. The FISA Court is a secret court that reviews government applications without adversarial participation. A Title I FISA order, predicated on a foreign nexus — here, Pearson’s friendship with a Russian-born individual — provides total electronic surveillance authority covering every communication, every financial transaction, and every physical movement, without the Fourth Amendment protections that apply in criminal proceedings.

The Fifth Amendment’s actual scope — and Bolling v. Sharpe

A critical threshold point: the Fourteenth Amendment’s Equal Protection Clause explicitly applies to states — “No State shall deny…” Federal equal protection claims do not run directly through the Fourteenth Amendment. They run through the Fifth Amendment’s due process clause, via Bolling v. Sharpe, 347 U.S. 497 (1954), which held that the federal government is bound by an equivalent equal protection requirement as a matter of Fifth Amendment due process. The substantive standard — purposeful discrimination required under Washington v. Davis, 426 U.S. 229 (1976) — is the same. But the doctrinal pathway is the Fifth Amendment, not the Fourteenth, and a claim against a federal targeting program must be framed accordingly.

On the Fifth Amendment’s due process protections themselves:

  • Paul v. Davis, 424 U.S. 693 (1976): Government defamation — the intentional destruction of a person’s reputation — does not constitute a deprivation of liberty under the Fifth Amendment. Reputation standing alone is not a protected interest. The government can destroy Pearson’s professional reputation without triggering Fifth Amendment protection.
  • Board of Regents v. Roth, 408 U.S. 564 (1972): A protected property interest in continued employment exists only where the government has explicitly created a legitimate claim of entitlement. Pearson has no government employment. He has no entitlement to be hired by private employers. The destruction of his employment prospects is not a Fifth Amendment deprivation because there was no protected interest to deprive.
  • Watchlist placement: Courts have not held that placement on the terrorism watchlist, without more, constitutes a deprivation of liberty sufficient to require full Fifth Amendment due process. The government maintains a challenge process — but it is an administrative process, not a judicial one, and it operates after the placement has destroyed the interests it affects.

The equal protection requirement — and its fatal condition

Via Bolling v. Sharpe and the Fifth Amendment, the equal protection requirement reaches federal programs. Its application to a secret racial targeting program requires proof of purposeful racial discrimination — not merely racially disparate impact. Washington v. Davis, 426 U.S. 229 (1976). A secret program is specifically designed to hide its purpose. The RMVE/BIE framework is facially neutral — it targets “violent extremism,” not race, even as every BIE designation in the FBI’s documented history has been applied to Black activists. The racial architecture is real. It is constitutionally unaddressable because it is classified, because the purpose is never stated, and because the neutral national security vocabulary provides legal cover for the racial logic underneath.

The First Amendment’s actual scope

The First Amendment prohibits laws that restrict speech on the basis of content or viewpoint. It does not prohibit the government from using speech as an intelligence collection predicate or a threat assessment basis. The distinction is critical and is what permits the operation against Pearson to proceed without constitutional friction.

Holder v. Humanitarian Law Project, 561 U.S. 1 (2010), is instructive. The Supreme Court upheld criminal prosecution for providing “material support” to designated foreign terrorist organizations even when the support consisted of lawful, peaceful activities — because the support was given in coordination with the designated organization. Applied to the domestic terrorism context: the government may use Pearson’s political expression and professional associations as a targeting predicate, may designate his organizational activities as part of a threat structure, and may act on that designation through surveillance, watchlisting, and covert pressure — all without restricting a single word he writes. The speech remains free. The speaker becomes a target. The First Amendment does not bridge that gap.

PART SIX: JUDICIAL DOCTRINE AS WALL

Standing, Qualified Immunity, and the Pleading Standard

The standing problem: Clapper v. Amnesty International USA, 568 U.S. 398 (2013)

To have standing, a plaintiff must demonstrate a concrete and particularized injury causally connected to government conduct. In Clapper, the Supreme Court held that plaintiffs challenging NSA surveillance lacked standing because they could not prove their communications had been collected. The government refused to confirm or deny collection. Without proof of collection, no concrete injury. Without concrete injury, no standing.

The foundational circular trap of secret program litigation: The program is secret, so the plaintiff cannot prove they were targeted. Without proof of targeting, no concrete injury. Without concrete injury, no standing. Without standing, no discovery. Without discovery, no proof of targeting. The secrecy protects itself through the standing doctrine.

The Twombly/Iqbal pleading standard: facts the plaintiff cannot access

Bell Atlantic Corp. v. Twombly, 550 U.S. 544 (2007), established that a complaint must contain sufficient factual allegations to state a plausible claim for relief. Ashcroft v. Iqbal, 556 U.S. 662 (2009), applied this standard to § 1983 and constitutional claims, holding that conclusions and speculation are insufficient — specific factual allegations are required.

Applied to a secret program: Pearson must plead with specificity the facts of what was done to him. Those facts are in classified government files. The defendants possess the evidence required to survive the motion to dismiss. The defendants will invoke state secrets to prevent its production. The pleading standard requires facts. The facts are classified. The case is dismissed before discovery begins. The order of operations is designed to produce this outcome.

Qualified immunity: the loop that Pearson v. Callahan perfected

Federal officials are immune from civil suits unless their conduct violated a “clearly established” constitutional right — one that was “beyond debate” at the time of the conduct. Harlow v. Fitzgerald, 457 U.S. 800 (1982).

A right is clearly established only if prior courts have held, with substantially similar facts, that the specific conduct is unconstitutional. Mullenix v. Luna, 577 U.S. 7 (2015), and Kisela v. Hughes, 584 U.S. 100 (2018), further tightened this standard, requiring that the prior case establishing the clearly established right have facts nearly identical to the case at bar — not merely the same constitutional provision. A prior case establishing Fourth Amendment protection against one form of surveillance does not establish it against a different form.

The loop Pearson v. Callahan perfected operates as follows: No court has held that directed energy targeting of a civilian national security subject violates the Fourth Amendment — because no court has ever reached the merits of such a claim. No court reaches the merits because state secrets dismisses cases before merits are examined, and because Pearson v. Callahan permits courts to grant immunity without deciding the constitutional question. Because no court has decided the constitutional question, the right is not clearly established. Because the right is not clearly established, the officials are immune. Because the officials are immune, no court reaches the constitutional question. The immunity creates the absence of precedent that justifies the immunity.

Every operator in the program against Pearson has qualified immunity. Not because their conduct was lawful. Because the procedural architecture ensures it will never be adjudicated.

PART SEVEN: OVERSIGHT MECHANISMS — AND WHY EACH FAILS

The Blocks in the Tower, Removed One by One

Congressional oversight

The Intelligence Committees receive classified briefings under secrecy requirements that prevent members from disclosing what they learn, even to their own staff. The most sensitive programs are briefed only to the “Gang of Eight” — eight people, under oath of secrecy, with no ability to legislate in response without revealing classified foundations. When Pearson writes to his congressional representative, the office refers the complaint to the agency being complained about. The agency responds that it cannot comment on ongoing matters. The office forwards the agency’s response to Pearson. No accountability. No investigation. The complaint is filed in the government’s record — and may, given the NSD’s documented practice of pre-litigation outreach to attorneys, accelerate the program against him.

The Inspector General system

Each major department and agency has an Inspector General authorized to investigate violations of law and report findings — the primary internal accountability mechanism. On January 20, 2025, the same day Executive Order 14173 was signed, at least twelve Inspectors General across the intelligence community and executive agencies were fired. See reporting by The Washington Post and The New York Times, January 20–21, 2025. The internal accountability layer was removed at the precise moment the external compliance pressure was applied. Replacement IGs are political appointees. Their findings on programs authorized at the highest levels of the administration that appointed them are not structurally independent.

The Freedom of Information Act

  • Exemption 1: Classified information — withheld
  • Exemption 3: Information protected by statute (National Security Act, CIA Act) — withheld
  • Exemption 7(A)-(F): Law enforcement records that could interfere with proceedings, disclose confidential sources, endanger persons — withheld
  • The Glomar response: Neither confirmed nor denied — Phillippi v. CIA, upheld uniformly since 1977
  • The delay mechanism: FOIA requests processed on multi-year timelines. Administrative appeal adds years. FOIA litigation adds more. The statute of limitations on underlying civil rights claims may expire before a single document is produced.

The press

The “targeted individual” counter-narrative is pre-seeded. It is the first frame editors encounter when extraordinary surveillance documentation reaches a newsroom. The frame does not require the editor to conclude the documentation is false. It requires only that they hesitate — long enough for the story not to be published. An NSL served on the journalist’s phone carrier produces call records without notification. The economic structure of investigative journalism does not support the forensic experts, independent scientists, and national security lawyers required to independently verify extraordinary technical claims absent prior institutional validation. Without a congressional hearing, an IG report, or a declassified document, the story cannot be safely published.

Civil society organizations

The ACLU, Center for Constitutional Rights, and peer organizations select cases that advance their litigation strategy. A case requiring a new Bivens extension in national security context — where Ziglar v. Abbasi and Egbert v. Boule close the door — does not produce generalizable precedent. It produces a dismissal. Organizations with finite resources do not fund cases that will be dismissed on threshold grounds before reaching merits. The organizations that might otherwise take the case face their own surveillance exposure, NSL vulnerability, and EO 14173 compliance pressure through federal funding relationships.

PART EIGHT: THE SCENARIO SPECTRUM

How the Tower Falls Across Six Conditions

Scenario A — Full Exposure

Program exposed. Whistleblowers testify publicly. Classified documents published. Congressional hearings held. Civil suits filed. Press coverage sustained.

Legal protection available:

  • Bivens: Some qualified immunity defeats remain, but published evidence may establish clearly established rights for specifically named officials
  • FTCA: Discretionary function and intentional torts exceptions survive, but specific tortious acts with documentary evidence may produce narrow liability windows
  • State secrets: Weakened by prior public disclosure — the privilege protects undisclosed information; what is already in the public domain is harder to re-suppress
  • Congressional legislation authorizing specific causes of action: Possible with political will
  • Criminal referrals: Conceivable for named officials acting outside their authority

The glass box: Cracked but upright. Reachable. The promise is imperfect but visible.

Historical analog: COINTELPRO post-Church Committee (1975–76). Programs exposed. Agencies reformed — temporarily. No criminal prosecutions of senior officials. No civil judgments against the United States. But the operating environment changed.

Scenario B — Whistleblowers, No Documents

Individuals come forward with testimony. No documentary corroboration. Limited press coverage. No congressional hearings.

Legal protection available:

  • Whistleblower testimony establishes that conduct occurred but may not supply the specific causal chain Twombly/Iqbal requires to survive a motion to dismiss
  • State secrets invocation suppresses specifics even with general testimony on the record
  • Without documents, qualified immunity survives — testimony that a program exists does not establish a clearly established right against specific acts with the factual specificity Mullenix requires
  • Political pressure possible; legal traction minimal

The glass box: Visibly tilting. Three or four blocks out.

Historical analog: MK-ULTRA post-Church (1977). Individual testimony. Some document production. No criminal accountability.

Scenario C — Documents Only, No Whistleblowers

Classified documents obtained through FOIA or leak. No human testimony providing causal connection to specific victims.

Legal protection available:

  • Documents establish program existence without establishing individual victims — standing under Clapper requires a specific plaintiff with a specific injury causally connected to specific conduct
  • State secrets suppresses additional document production even after initial release
  • Without human testimony, causal chains are legally incomplete; Iqbal pleading requires more than document production
  • Press coverage possible, political pressure possible, legal remedy structurally unavailable

The glass box: More blocks out. Tilting significantly.

Scenario D — Civil Suit Only

Victim files suit. No external validation. Government invokes full defensive arsenal.

Legal protection available:

  • Standing (Clapper): No proof of targeting = no concrete injury = dismissed at threshold
  • Pleading (Twombly/Iqbal): Specific facts required = facts are classified = dismissed on the pleadings
  • State secrets (Tenet/El-Masri): Subject matter classified = case dismissed before discovery
  • Qualified immunity (Harlow/Mullenix): No clearly established right with identical prior facts = officials immune
  • Sovereign immunity (FTCA exceptions): Discretionary function + intentional torts = no damages remedy

The likely outcome: Dismissed before any merits examination. No discovery. No record. No precedent. The program is not acknowledged. The victim has spent their legal resources and received a dismissal docket number.

The glass box: Most blocks removed. Hanging by one or two connections.

Historical analog: El-Masri v. United States — a confirmed torture victim, subsequently vindicated by the European Court of Human Rights, dismissed at the Fourth Circuit without any merits examination.

Scenario E — Victim Documenting, No Institutional Response

The victim documents extensively and publicly. The documentation is available. No court, no congressional office, no civil liberties organization, no journalist has engaged with it. The government has not publicly acknowledged anything.

This is Pearson’s situation.

Legal protection available:

  • The documentation is a record. It is not a legal remedy.
  • Without an attorney willing to file, there is no case
  • Attorneys have been reached through DOJ NSD pre-litigation outreach, NSL, and professional network signals before any filing occurs
  • Without institutional engagement, documentation cannot be converted into legal action
  • The information environment tools have pre-framed the documentation as non-credible through the “targeted individual” counter-narrative
  • The legal system requires an institution to convert documentation into legal action; every such institution has been contaminated or neutralized through the tools described in this essay

The glass box: Hanging by one connection — the documentation itself, which is the only remaining block. The government’s operation is specifically designed to ensure that connection is never made load-bearing.

Scenario F — Total Secrecy

Secret program. No public disclosure. No whistleblowers. No media coverage. No civil suit filed. No government documents available. Vast constitutional and human rights abuses. Torture. Complete suspension of rights.

Legal protection available: None.

The law did not fail. The law worked exactly as designed when all of its escape hatches are sealed simultaneously.

PART NINE: INTERNATIONAL LAW — WHY THE UN DOESN’T HELP

Obligations Without Remedies

The United States is party to the Convention Against Torture (ratified 1994), the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (ratified 1992), and the International Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Racial Discrimination (ratified 1994). Under these treaties, the United States is legally obligated not to torture its own citizens, to provide effective remedies for torture, and to prohibit racial discrimination in all domains.

None of this creates a domestic legal remedy.

Each treaty was ratified with explicit declarations that it is “non-self-executing” — meaning no judicially enforceable rights in domestic courts without implementing legislation. Congress has not enacted that legislation. The Supreme Court settled this definitively in Medellín v. Texas, 552 U.S. 491 (2008): non-self-executing treaties do not create privately enforceable rights in U.S. courts, even when the President attempts to enforce them. The controlling authority on treaty enforceability forecloses the international law pathway entirely.

The Alien Tort Statute, 28 U.S.C. § 1350, provides a cause of action for violations of international law — for aliens. Pearson is a U.S. citizen. The ATS does not apply. Further, even for aliens, Kiobel v. Royal Dutch Petroleum, 569 U.S. 108 (2013), and Jesner v. Arab Bank, 584 U.S. 241 (2018), have significantly narrowed ATS claims, requiring they “touch and concern” the territory of the United States with sufficient force and excluding corporate defendants. The ATS pathway is closed to U.S. citizens and substantially narrowed even for foreign nationals.

The Torture Victim Protection Act creates a cause of action for torture — against individuals acting under authority of a foreign nation. The U.S. government is not a foreign nation.

When Pearson submitted documentation to the UN OHCHR, the State Department filed the submission in its counterintelligence record and presented it to his attorney as evidence of the “complexity” of his case. His escalation to the international human rights system — the act that should have generated external accountability pressure — was processed by the executive branch as additional justification for the operation. The international law system is not a legal remedy. It is documentation. It is history. It is what future scholars will cite.

It is not a court order.

PART TEN: THE RACIAL ARCHITECTURE

Why Minority Citizens Face These Failures First and Worst

Every legal failure described above applies to any citizen targeted by a secret federal program. Minority citizens — Black citizens specifically — face the same failures plus the structural conditions that make secret programs more likely to target them, more devastating when deployed against them, and more impossible to remedy.

The RMVE/BIE targeting framework is racially constructed

The FBI’s Black Identity Extremism designation (2017) and its successor RMVE category were explicitly designed around the political expression of Black citizens challenging state power. The designation does not require violence or its planning. It requires an assessment that a person’s ideology has “incitement potential.” Every documented BIE designation in the FBI’s history was applied to Black activists. See Foreign Policy, October 6, 2017 (reporting on the leaked FBI intelligence assessment); ACLU FOIA litigation, 2017–2019, producing internal FBI documents on BIE program scope. EO 14173 expanded the targeting predicate to encompass DEI scholarship — a field where Black scholars are disproportionately represented. The racial architecture is the targeting logic. Proving it requires cracking the classified record, which state secrets seals, which Washington v. Davis makes legally necessary to crack, which the program’s classification infrastructure was designed to prevent.

The pre-seeded counter-narrative works specifically against Black activists

COINTELPRO’s primary operational tool for discrediting Black activists was characterizing them as delusional — a technique documented extensively in the Church Committee’s Final Report. See S. Rep. №94–755, Book III (1976), pp. 79–184. J. Edgar Hoover’s internal FBI communications characterized Martin Luther King Jr.’s awareness of government surveillance as evidence of paranoia. The modern “targeted individual” framing is the clinical update of this same discrediting mechanism. It works specifically against Black activists because their historical experience of government surveillance is real and documented — and because that reality makes the “paranoid delusion” characterization plausible to those who have not read the Church Committee report. The counter-narrative does not need to be true to be effective. It needs to create sufficient institutional hesitation. It does.

Washington D.C. as a special case of vulnerability

Washington D.C. is the city with no Senate representation, a House representative with no voting power, direct federal authority over local governance decisions, the highest concentration of federal electromagnetic infrastructure in the United States, and a majority-Black population that has lived under federal oversight of its municipal government since the city’s founding. A Black resident of Washington D.C. targeted by a federal program has less democratic recourse than a Black resident of any other American city. Their congressional representatives cannot vote on legislation. Their local government is subject to federal override. The infrastructure required for the most technically sophisticated elements of a secret targeting program — signals infrastructure, coordination facilities, operational space — is densest in the city where the target lives.

This is not coincidence. It is the geography of the program.

The Holder v. HLP dimension of the racial architecture

Holder v. Humanitarian Law Project established that the government can criminalize association and coordination with designated organizations without restricting abstract advocacy. Applied to the domestic context: Pearson’s organizational activity through a non-profit can be designated a coordinated threat structure — not because it advocates violence, but because it advocates effectively for positions the government has classified as hostile ideology. His most constitutionally protected activity — forming and operating a scholarly nonprofit — becomes, under the Holder framework applied to domestic designations, the activity that elevates his threat profile and justifies escalating surveillance authority. Free association is used against the person exercising it.

Conclusion: The Architecture Was Always Here

This essay describes a legal system that does not merely fail minority citizens targeted by secret federal programs. It actively enables their targeting. Each doctrine examined — the Title VI federal agency gap, the Title VII national security exemption, the Ziglar/Egbert/Pearson v. Callahan destruction of Bivens, the Reynolds/El-Masri/Jeppesen expansion of state secrets, the Clapper standing barrier, the Washington v. Davis/Bolling v. Sharpe equal protection intent requirement, the discretionary function and intentional torts exceptions to the FTCA, the firing of at least twelve inspectors general, the FISA Court’s consistent above-99% approval rate, and Medellín’s foreclosure of international law remedies — each one is a block removed from the tower.

No single removal brings the tower down. The glass box is resilient. It was designed by people who understood what they were building. It takes the simultaneous removal of all the blocks to bring it to the floor.

That simultaneous removal is now the documented operational condition in the United States in 2025–2026. Not theoretical. Not approaching. Documented. In the capital city. Against a citizen who has done nothing except describe, with forensic precision, what is being done to him.

The question was whether the law that was built to protect minority citizens from exactly this offers any protection when the program is secret.

The answer is no.

Not because the law is broken.

Because the law is working.

Twelve Panels. One Documented Experience.

In July 2025, an African American man in Washington, DC heard a voice in his head. He has a PhD. His dissertation has been defined as a prominent study in his field. He was working to secure employment.

These are artistic interpretations of a man who was tortured through the use of exotic technology that few in the US, including Congress, realize is even possible. It has been around for decades in different forms. The technology likely used on Pearson points ultrasonic laser beams into the skull. When those waves “de-modulate,” they produce a third wave that is within the audible hearing range. This technology is used in museums — offering “personalized” visitor experience. No one aside from the person who stands within the narrow range of the ultrasonic waves can hear the sound — not anyone standing next to them or at their feet. It was weaponized in Pearson’s torture.

There is only one plausible source — the United States of America government. All of Pearson’s experiences take place in some of the most secure and surveilled locations in the US — US Capitol, near House office buildings, and Georgetown where members of Congress live.

The US government deployed neuroweapons to rob a man of self-control and rights over his own mind and actions. It begins when Pearson is eating cantaloupe at the steps of the US Capitol — he has been “pinned”. Although he initially attributed his movement to his decision, the evidence does not follow from his decision-making — there are federal operatives who are in place to carry out the most visible and egregious violation of Pearson’s constitutional and human rights. They pre-planned a modern form of torture and civil rights and human rights abuses.

This is Not Mental Illness: The Technology Exists to Put a Voice in People’s Heads

Before the through-line, a necessary interruption. Because the moment anyone reads Panel 5 — the Botanic Garden, the voice inside Pearson’s skull — the same question arises. And it deserves a direct answer.

The technology to put a voice in someone’s head is real. It is not classified science fiction. It is not a conspiracy. It is physics, and some of it is in museums.

The most likely mechanism in Pearson’s case is parametric ultrasonic audio — sometimes called the acoustic heterodyne effect. Here is how it works in plain terms. Two ultrasonic beams are aimed at the same target. Each beam individually operates above the threshold of human hearing — somewhere above 40,000 Hz, inaudible to anyone, passing through air and through rooms like an invisible flashlight. Where the two beams converge, they interact. They demodulate — meaning the difference between their two frequencies produces a third wave. That third wave falls within the audible range. It is sound. But it is sound that does not exist until the two beams meet at their target point, and it is sound that exists only at that point. Anyone standing elsewhere hears nothing. The target hears a voice that appears to originate inside their own head, because it is being generated at — or within — the acoustic space of their skull, with no external speaker, no visible source, no evidence that anything was transmitted at all. American Technology Corporation commercialized this technology under the name MEDUSA and the Audio Spotlight in the early 2000s. Holosonic Research Labs deployed the commercial version in retail stores and museums — you walk through a specific floor tile in a museum exhibit and hear a narrator speaking directly into your head while the person beside you hears nothing. The New York Times covered it. Popular Science covered it. It is, in its commercial form, a curiosity. It is, in its military-grade directional form, something else entirely. DARPA’s Next-Generation Non-Surgical Neurotechnology program was advancing this and related capabilities as recently as 2020. In January 2026, the Department of War confirmed that directed energy is “operational and being scaled.” The science is not secret. The weaponized deployment against civilians is.

What happened to Pearson at the Botanic Garden on July 13, 2025 is the military-grade version of this technology deployed against a civilian on a public sidewalk in the capital of the United States. The benign version — focused directional audio that only one person in a room can hear — is a novelty at science museums. You stand in a designated spot, a parabolic dish aims a beam at your head, and you hear music that the person standing next to you cannot hear. Children find it delightful. The version deployed against Pearson is not the museum version. It is the same underlying physics scaled to military specification, aimed at a specific individual’s skull from a distance, carrying a voice that belonged to a woman in a room he was not in. The experience produced — shock, disorientation, the pressing of a palm flat against the temple, the stumble backward on a public sidewalk — is what the technology is designed to produce in a target who does not know it is being used against them. It is, by any standard definition of the term, torture. And it is specifically useful as torture because it leaves no mark, produces no forensic evidence, and is designed to be described by victims in terms that sound, to anyone who has not encountered the science, like the presenting symptoms of psychosis.

The November 7 framing operation — the tub materials positioned at the GPS-specific location Pearson was directed toward, containing a red hat, a black shirt, and materials referencing Adolf Hitler — was the arrest setup. The behavioral direction moved Pearson to the location. The framing materials were positioned there in advance. The operational objective was for Pearson to encounter those materials and react: to pick them up in confusion, to be seen handling them, to open his apartment door when law enforcement arrived moments later, or to make some other documentable act of contact that could be characterized as possession or association. What they would have charged him with, on that scenario, is a matter of prosecutorial construction — but the architecture points toward: possession of materials associated with a domestic terrorism investigation, conduct consistent with a threat to federal officials or infrastructure, or — most precisely — a charge under 18 U.S.C. § 875 (interstate threats) or 18 U.S.C. § 2101 (incitement to riot), predicated on the planted materials and the RMVE designation already in his file. The charge itself matters less than its function: arrest produces a public record. A public record produces the “mentally unstable” narrative the operation was designed to create. A mugshot next to a red hat and Hitler references ends the Medium publications, ends the UN submissions, ends the congressional letters, ends the documentation. He did not open the door. He photographed the tub instead. The operation failed at its terminal objective because the target had been trained, by fourteen months of Zersetzung, to document rather than react.

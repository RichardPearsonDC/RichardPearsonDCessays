COINTELPRO Was Resurrected: Blacks Now Have Active Role in Racial Targeting Program

How the federal government’s most notorious racist program solved its visibility problem — by putting Black and Latino faces on it

There is a photograph that does not exist but should.

It would show a Black man — Dr. Richard Pearson, PhD, scholar and resident of Washington D.C. — standing in his apartment building hallway after being punched by his neighbor. It would show the police officers who arrived in response to his assault. It would show the arresting officer placing handcuffs not on the man who threw the punch but on Pearson.

The officers in that photograph are Black and Latino.

The neighbor who threw the punch is Black.

The girlfriend who holds the lease — the woman whose apartment became the staging ground for months of deliberate sleep deprivation operations against Pearson — is Black. The assailant has been living there. His arrival, unknown to the property management, coincided with psychological torture and sleep depriving Pearson.

The highest level of the program directing all of it is white. Federal. Classified. And completely invisible in that photograph.

This is the story of COINTELPRO’s most important upgrade.

What COINTELPRO Was

Between 1956 and 1971, the FBI ran a program called COINTELPRO — the Counter Intelligence Program. Its targets were Black activists, civil rights organizations, socialist groups, and anyone the Bureau considered a threat to the existing racial and political order. Its methods included surveillance, character assassination, psychological operations designed to produce paranoia and isolation, the planting of false evidence, and in the most extreme cases — Fred Hampton’s assassination in Chicago in 1969, executed while he slept — murder facilitated through federal intelligence shared with local police.

The program was exposed by the Citizens’ Commission to Investigate the FBI in 1971, confirmed by the Senate Select Committee — the Church Committee — in 1976, and formally discontinued. The Church Committee’s Final Report documented in exhaustive detail how the Bureau had “harassed, disrupted, and in some cases destroyed” the lives of American citizens engaged in constitutionally protected political activity.

The primary targets were Black people.

The visible face of the program was white.

J. Edgar Hoover. White. The agents who surveilled King. White. The officers who raided Hampton’s apartment. White. The institutional apparatus of racial repression had a face, and that face made it legible. Made it exposable. Made the Church Committee possible.

The program’s architects learned from that exposure.

The Upgrade

COINTELPRO did not end in 1971. It evolved.

The surveillance infrastructure became FISA. The informant network became the fusion center. The field office intelligence sharing became the Joint Terrorism Task Force. The targeting predicate shifted — from “communist sympathizer” to “Black Identity Extremist” in 2017, to “RMVE” — Racially Motivated Violent Extremist — thereafter, to “DEI hostile ideology” under Executive Order 14173, signed January 20, 2025.

The targets remained the same. Black scholars. Black activists. Black people who documented state violence and published their findings.

But the program corrected its visibility problem.

It put Black and Latino faces on the execution layer, leveraging racial diversity at the local law enforcement level to produce an irony of immense significance.

Washington D.C., 2024–2025

Dr. Richard Pearson pursued his research. He founded a nonprofit. He wrote. He published.

Then the torture started in the spring of 2025.

He became a victim of neuroweapons. He was tracked and stalked. Harassed with noise that deprived him of sleep. He was assaulted chemically and physically.

This essay is not about his broad experience. Rather, the black and brown faces that he consistently encountered who took turns psychologically torturing him and who assaulted him.

The Neighbors

Sometime in the period preceding the systematic targeting operations, a new neighbor moved into Pearson’s building. A Black man. His girlfriend — also Black — held the lease.

What followed was systematic sleep deprivation that he began documenting in the summer of 2025. Noise at hours calibrated to prevent rest. Consistent. Sustained. Months of it. The kind of psychological pressure operation the Stasi called Zersetzung — decomposition. The slow erosion of a person’s stability, cognitive function, and emotional regulation through accumulated small disruptions, each one deniable, together devastating.

In December 2025, his upstairs neighbor — the one who had been tormented him for months — ran down the hallway and threw the first punch.

The only people present were Pearson, the assailant, and the assailant’s girlfriend.

When MPD arrived, the officers were Black. One was Hispanic. They assessed the scene. They looked at Pearson. They looked at the neighbor. They looked at the girlfriend.

They arrested Pearson.

Not the man who threw the punch. The man who received it.

The arrest record lists the assailant by full name. It lists the girlfriend by full name. It references a third witness whose statement forms the probable cause basis for Pearson’s arrest.

That witness has no name. Because there was no witness. It was fabricated.

No officer mentioned this witness to Pearson at the scene. Not during the arrest. Not during booking. Pearson assumed the girlfriend had lied. He asked about it. The officer said that there was a third witness.

The witness did not exist.

And how is DC Council responding to these federal-hybrid agreements leading to vast abuses?

D.C. Council Judiciary Committee Chair Pinto, whose committee holds oversight responsibility for MPD, summarized the institutional posture precisely: “We do not question those agreements.”

The Constitutional Violations

What happened to Pearson at the MPD level is not ambiguous constitutionally:

Fourth Amendment — unlawful arrest without genuine probable cause; fabricated a witness; unlawful retention of personal property without justification

Fifth Amendment — deprivation of liberty and property without due process; knowing use of fabricated evidence, then tried covering it up

First Amendment — denial of access to courts through the officer’s instruction to conceal the arrest record

Fourteenth Amendment via Bolling — if the racial architecture of the targeting produced the arrest, equal protection violation through the D.C. government as a state actor.

MPD Headquarters

Pearson later went to MPD headquarters and requested his arrest record. They printed it and gave it to him.

The officer at the records window then told him: go to the court to check if they have a record — but do not show them this arrest record.

The court had no record.

An arrest that generates an MPD booking record generates a court notification as a matter of standard procedure in the District of Columbia. The U.S. Attorney’s Office receives the package. A no-paper decision — declining to prosecute — still produces a court entry. The court knows the arrest happened. They just know it was not charged.

The court had nothing.

Because the federal-hybrid operation against Pearson was known. Because the noise was a framing attempt to make Pearson reach a breaking point. Because there was no probable cause for an arrest. Because the officers fabricated a witness.

Which means the case was stopped before it reached the U.S. Attorney’s Office. Someone decided not to submit it. That decision — in the context of a fabricated unnamed witness, a victim arrested instead of his attacker, and a records officer instructing concealment — was not administrative oversight.

It was a program closing out an operation that had failed to achieve its objective.

Pearson’s property was returned. Everything except his metro discount card — a Medicaid-linked card he had obtained at a library enrollment event. The officers had assumed, apparently, that a Black man with a PhD had stolen a senior’s discount transit card.

The Structural Genius of the Upgrade

Here is what the architects of COINTELPRO 2.0 understood that their predecessors did not adequately account for:

Visibility was COINTELPRO’s only vulnerability.

The Church Committee existed because the program had a visible face — white agents, white officers, overt racial hostility that a country convulsed by the civil rights movement could recognize and name. The visibility created the political conditions for exposure. The exposure created the political conditions for reform.

COINTELPRO 2.0 solved this.

The federal architecture — the RMVE designation, the FISA surveillance, the fusion center intelligence, the directed energy technology, the behavioral direction operations, the employment destruction through EO 14173 compliance pressure — is classified. It has no face. It exists in classified databases and sealed court orders and intelligence assessments that are never shown to their subjects.

What has a face is the local execution layer.

And the local execution layer, in Washington D.C. in 2025, is Black and Latino.

The Black officers who came when Pearson complained about the noise a week before the physical assault were in on the federal program — COINTELPRO 2.0. Not only was MPD getting direct funds from the federal government, but psychological operations on a Black man with a PhD and no criminal record meant lots of overtime.

The Black and Latino officers who arrived on December 27 and arrested the assault victim instead of the assailant were not, individually, the architects of a racial targeting program. They were executing a protocol shaped by an intelligence designation that resurrected COINTELPRO. They knew their role. They knew the racial prejudice. They didn’t care. The protocol was already in place. Neutralize Dr. Richard Pearson.

They followed it.

The Moral Weight

This observation is not an accusation against Black officers as a general matter. It is an indictment of the system and these specific officers.

The civil rights movement demanded diverse police forces. Consent decrees, hiring reforms, and community policing initiatives over five decades produced police departments in majority-minority cities that reflect — at the officer level — the demographics of those cities. Washington D.C.’s MPD is majority Black and Latino at the patrol level. That diversity was a hard-won achievement of the movement that COINTELPRO was designed to destroy.

COINTELPRO 2.0 operationalized that achievement as a tool against the communities those officers came from.

It is one of the cruelest operational innovations in the history of American state repression. It uses the success of the civil rights movement — the diversification of law enforcement — to make the civil rights movement’s greatest threat invisible. It puts Black faces on the torture of Black scholars.

But there are key differences in the intervening 50 years. Racial solidarity has weakened considerably. Some Black officers are willing to commit racial injustices. Promotion and pay incentives to participate in the new COINTELPRO only worsen it.

There is little reason to believe that most of the officers involved in Pearson’s case were probably doing what they believed was their job. They were likely aware of the federal contract, of the pay benefits from overtime, and that people like Pearson were being targeted. Still for others, the genius — and the evil — is that the system produces the outcome without requiring individual bad actors at the execution layer. The individual officers do not need to know they are executing a federal targeting program. They only need to follow the protocol the program has established.

This is what institutional racism looks like when it has had fifty years to learn from its mistakes — use pay and department incentives, capitalize on weak Black solidarity, and provide legal and contractual cover.

The Black Assailant, The Black Girlfriend

The neighbor who ran down the hallway and threw the first punch is Black. His girlfriend — the leaseholder, the woman whose apartment anchored months of sleep deprivation operations against Pearson — is Black.

Their role in this account requires the same structural analysis as the officers.

The history of COINTELPRO’s use of Black informants and Black proxies is well documented. William O’Neal, Fred Hampton’s bodyguard, was the FBI’s informant inside the Illinois Black Panther Party. He provided the floor plan of Hampton’s apartment. He was twenty-one years old, facing car theft charges, and was turned by the FBI using the leverage of his legal jeopardy. He did not consider himself an enemy of Black liberation. He considered himself a young man trying to stay out of prison.

The structural conditions that produce Black informants and Black proxies have not changed. Legal jeopardy. Financial desperation. Housing insecurity. The accumulated vulnerabilities that concentrated poverty produces in majority-Black neighborhoods are the same vulnerabilities the federal targeting architecture has always exploited to recruit local execution capacity.

But there has been one notable change. Now, Black and Latino officers are much more common, especially in large urban cities on the East coast and Midwest.

The neighbor and his girlfriend had been operating within a structure that offered them something in exchange for their proximity to Pearson. Pearson had no issue with noise before the summer of 2025. No issue. The pattern of their conduct is clear — dropping heavy objects with the timing of Pearson’s movement within his apartment, the systematic nature of the sleep deprivation, the assault.

What Richard Pearson Represents

Dr. Richard Pearson is the latest in a line that runs from W.E.B. Du Bois — surveilled by the FBI for decades, prosecuted under the Foreign Agents Registration Act at age 83 — through Paul Robeson, whose passport was revoked, through Martin Luther King, whose surveillance produced the letter suggesting he kill himself before the Nobel Prize ceremony, through the Black Panthers, through the Movement for Black Lives, through every iteration of Black intellectual and political power that the federal government has classified as a threat not because it threatened violence but because it threatened the existing order.

The targeting predicate changes with each era, including the Black and Brown faces that make up the local component of racial targeting. The target does not.

What is different about Pearson is the documentation.

Fourteen months of timestamped evidence. Two independent clinical evaluations. A controlled experiment. A photographed framing scene. A police database returning ACCESS DENIED in Sensitive Compartmented Information on a private citizen’s identity record. An arrest record with an unnamed fabricated witness. A court docket with no corresponding entry. An MPD officer’s instruction to conceal a government document from a judge.

And the names and faces of the people the federal program put between itself and its target.

Black. Latino. From the same communities the program was designed to destroy.

The Conclusion COINTELPRO 2.0 Did Not Anticipate

COINTELPRO’s architects believed that putting Black faces on the execution layer would make the program permanently invisible. That the counter-narrative — “how can this be racial targeting when the officers were Black?” — would be self-sustaining. That the diversity achievement would be a permanent shield.

They did not account for a target who understood the structure well enough to document not just what was done to him but who did it and how the who served the what.

Pearson documented the Black officers. He documented the Black neighbor. He documented the Black girlfriend. The Black cutouts. Not to accuse them. To show the architecture they were embedded in.

This is one of the most important truths in this account — Black people actively and willingly participated in the targeting of a Black scholar.

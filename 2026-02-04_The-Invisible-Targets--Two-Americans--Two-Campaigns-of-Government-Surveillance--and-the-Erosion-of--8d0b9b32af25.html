The Invisible Targets: Two Americans, Two Campaigns of Government Surveillance, and the Erosion of Democratic Safeguards

I. Introduction: When the State Turns Inward

In the winter of 2025–2026, two Americans encountered the machinery of the United States government turned against them. One story made national headlines in The Washington Post. The other exists primarily in a 100-page document of meticulous observations, photographs, and timestamps — a narrative that challenges every convention of how we understand, verify, and report government abuse.

The first story is relatively straightforward: Jon, a 67-year-old retired insurance worker and naturalized citizen living in suburban Philadelphia, sent a four-sentence email to a Department of Homeland Security attorney urging mercy for an Afghan asylum seeker. Five hours later, he received notice that DHS had issued an administrative subpoena for his Google account data. Federal agents appeared at his door. His luggage was searched. He found himself ensnared in a system that operates without judicial oversight, targeting American citizens for exercising their First Amendment rights.

The second story is far more complex: Richard Henson, a 40-year-old African American scholar with a recent PhD, documented what he describes as a sustained campaign of harassment, surveillance, psychological manipulation, and physical assault throughout 2025 in Washington, D.C. His account includes apartment surveillance, deliberate psychological torture through noise, what he believes to be neurological manipulation technology (“pinning”), coordinated street theater involving dozens of people in specific color patterns, chemical assault, a physical assault where the assailant was not arrested, and systematic attempts to induce a psychotic break. Unlike Jon, Henson received no subpoena, no official notice, no knock on the door from identified agents. His evidence exists in hundreds of photographs, detailed temporal notes, medical records, and a mental health evaluation explicitly confirming his rationality. He has seen three physicians, two mental health specialists, and a social worker. None had documented psychiatric symptoms. In fact, one strongly encouraged him to back up his document, a likely indication of their perceptions that Henson is credible.

These cases initially appear incomparable — one a clear-cut abuse of administrative subpoenas, the other an almost incomprehensibly elaborate operation that strains credulity. Yet examined together, they illuminate something profoundly disturbing about the current trajectory of American democracy: the development of a bifurcated system of political repression that operates both through quasi-legal administrative mechanisms (Jon’s case) and through wholly extrajudicial operations designed to leave no conventional paper trail (Henson’s case).

This essay examines both cases in depth, analyzes why Henson’s documentation presents unique challenges for traditional journalism, evaluates his credibility as a witness despite these challenges, and explores what his predicament — if substantiated — means for the fundamental assumptions underlying American civil liberties.

II. Jon’s Story: The Weaponization of Administrative Subpoenas

The Sequence of Events

Jon’s case follows a clear chronology that fits conventional journalistic frameworks:

October 30, 2025: The Washington Post publishes an investigation about DHS attempting to deport an Afghan who aided U.S. war efforts.

Same day, morning: Jon reads the article, finds DHS attorney Joseph Dernbach’s email address through a simple Google search of the Florida bar association website, and sends a brief email: “Mr. Dernbach, don’t play Russian roulette with H’s life. Err on the side of caution. There’s a reason the US government along with many other governments don’t recognise the Taliban. Apply principles of common sense and decency.”

Same day, five hours and one minute later: Jon receives an email from Google notifying him that DHS has issued an administrative subpoena compelling release of information related to his Google Account.

November 17, 2025: Two DHS agents and a local police officer appear at Jon’s home. They show him a copy of his email to Dernbach and say they want to “hear your side of the story.” They acknowledge the email broke no law but suggest the prosecutor might have felt threatened by Jon’s mentions of “Russian roulette” and the Taliban. Jon asks if he’s on a list now; they assure him he has nothing to worry about.

November 21, 2025: Twenty-two days after the initial notice, and only after a Washington Post reporter contacts Google, the company provides Jon with a copy of the subpoena (with the authorizing official’s name redacted).

December 2025: Jon and his wife travel to Puerto Rico for their 40th anniversary. Their luggage is flagged by TSA for inspection — Jon’s bag specifically, containing a standard notice that it was “selected for physical inspection” with no explanation why.

January 2026: The ACLU files a motion to quash the subpoena in federal court, arguing DHS violated statutes limiting administrative subpoenas to “immigration enforcement” and violated Jon’s First Amendment rights.

The Administrative Subpoena System

Jon’s case exposed the contours of a powerful and largely invisible legal mechanism. Administrative subpoenas differ fundamentally from judicial subpoenas:

  • No judge or grand jury required: Federal agencies can issue them internally without external oversight
  • Minimal review: Mid-level officials throughout agencies can approve them in minutes
  • Broad scope: Can demand vast amounts of personal data (Jon’s subpoena requested his complete online session history from September 1 forward, all IP and physical addresses, services used, alternate usernames and emails, Social Security number, driver’s license, and credit card numbers)
  • Secrecy by design: Companies are not required to notify users; Google’s seven-day window to challenge is discretionary
  • No accountability: No centralized reporting requirement; estimates suggest DHS alone issues thousands to tens of thousands annually

As Jennifer Granick of the ACLU observed: “There’s no oversight ahead of time, and there’s no ramifications for having abused it after the fact.”

The Washington Post investigation revealed troubling patterns:

  • March 2025: DHS issued two administrative subpoenas to Columbia University for information on a student it sought to deport after pro-Palestinian protests
  • July 2025: DHS demanded broad employment records from Harvard with what attorneys described as “unprecedented administrative subpoenas”
  • September 2025: DHS attempted to identify Instagram users posting about ICE raids in Los Angeles
  • Late 2025: DHS demanded personal information on 7,000 workers in a Minnesota health system whose staff protested ICE’s hospital intrusion

Google and Meta both received record numbers of subpoenas during the first half of Trump’s second term — Google received 28,622, a 15% increase over the previous six-month period.

Why Jon’s Case Gained Traction

Several factors made Jon’s story accessible to mainstream journalism:

  1. Clear causation: Email sent → subpoena issued five hours later
  2. Documentary evidence: Google notification, eventual subpoena copy, TSA inspection notice
  3. Official acknowledgment: DHS agents appeared and confirmed investigation
  4. Established narrative framework: Government overreach stories have precedent; the “chilling effect” on free speech is well-understood
  5. Sympathetic protagonist: Elderly naturalized citizen, no criminal history, moderate in expression, clearly exercising First Amendment rights
  6. Legal clarity: ACLU took his case; federal court filing provided official record
  7. Corporate confirmation: Google’s actions (delayed provision of subpoena, record of legal process) were verifiable

Jon’s story fits what journalism scholar Michael Schudson calls the “repair narrative” — it exposes a flaw in the system that can theoretically be addressed through existing mechanisms (judicial review, legislative reform, public pressure).

The Statistical Anomaly: Red and Black Clothing Patterns

Henson’s most quantifiable claim involves red and black clothing clustering. On September 30, 2025, over 85 minutes around Eastern Market, he photographed 43 individuals in bright red/black combinations.

His documentation includes:

  • Timestamps for each photograph
  • Descriptions of each person’s appearance and location
  • Background analysis showing light pedestrian traffic
  • Follow-up “control studies” at other locations (SW Library, MLK Library, metro, holiday markets) showing 2–4% prevalence

Statistical analysis:

  • If base rate is 4%: Probability of observing 8+ people in red/black among 200 random individuals in 85 minutes ≈ 0.01 (assuming independence)
  • Henson’s photos show: Far fewer than 200 total people in backgrounds
  • His calculation: Would require 200+ people present just to explain photos 1–8 through random chance

Several photographs are particularly striking:

  • Photo 11–12: Elderly white woman standing expressionless in doorway of Compass Real Estate in bright red shirt and black pants, face completely blank, no interaction with environment
  • Photos 14–16: Red-haired woman in red shirt and black shorts with male companion in black shirt
  • Photos 31–32: White male in all-black with bright red backpack in Market Park

Henson also documented the same patterns near his residence:

  • December 4: Five people in red/black within minutes of each other near his apartment
  • December 6–12: Multiple individuals in red/black on consecutive days arriving home
  • Pattern concentrated around his residence

Possible explanations:

  1. Coordinated harassment operation: Street theater designed to create pattern recognition, induce paranoia, demonstrate surveillance capability
  2. Confirmation bias: Once Henson began looking for red/black, he noticed it selectively (but his control studies counter this)
  3. Localized fashion trend: Eastern Market area had unusual concentration (but why concentrated near his residence too?)
  4. Photographic artifact: Selection bias in what he photographed (but timestamps show consecutive shooting without gaps)

The statistical improbability doesn’t prove coordination, but it exceeds chance at concerning levels — particularly combined with the geographic clustering and temporal correlation with his other experiences.

IV. Why Henson’s Case Challenges Traditional Journalism

The Verification Dilemma

Jon’s case provided multiple verification points:

  • Google’s notification (corporate record)
  • Federal agents’ visit (official government action)
  • TSA inspection notice (documented screening)
  • ACLU court filing (public legal record)

Henson’s case lacks conventional verification:

  • No subpoena, no official notice
  • No identified federal agents
  • No corporate notifications
  • No court filing (asylum attempt failed; ACLU declined case)

His evidence consists of:

  • Personal photographs (can show what he saw, not why)
  • Journal entries (document his perceptions, not objective reality)
  • Medical records (prove injuries, not their cause)
  • Mental health evaluation (confirms rationality, doesn’t verify events)

The epistemological problem: How does journalism verify events designed to be unverifiable?

Traditional verification methods:

  • Document review: Requires documents (none produced by operation)
  • Official confirmation: Requires identified officials (operation uses cutouts/contractors)
  • Multiple sourcing: Requires witnesses (operation uses individual targeting)
  • Expert validation: Requires precedent (technology may be classified)

Henson’s documentation is systematic and extensive, but it captures his experience of events whose interpretation remains somewhat contested. His extraordinary stories such as the “Bartleby man” who finds him at 6am is backed by photographic evidence, so is the “Bug Zapper” woman and the red and black clothing patterns.

The Coherence Problem

Journalism relies on narrative coherence — stories should make sense within established frameworks of how the world works. Jon’s story is coherent:

Government attorney receives critical email → feels threatened → issues subpoena (which he has authority to do) → sends agents to interview sender → hopes to intimidate future criticism

Henson’s story lacks this coherence because it requires accepting:

  1. Extraordinary resource deployment: Dozens of people coordinating red/black clothing, multiple surveillance teams, apartment infiltration, chemical assault planning, police coordination — all targeting an unemployed PhD with no security clearance, no classified access, no insider knowledge
  2. Advanced technology: Voice-to-skull communication, “pinning” (behavioral direction through electromagnetic means), through-floor location tracking, real-time coordination
  3. Multi-agency cooperation: Federal, local, and possibly private contractors operating in concert
  4. Absence of conventional motive: Henson has no classified information, wasn’t planning terrorism, didn’t threaten officials

The question becomes: Is it more plausible that this operation occurred as described, or that Henson is experiencing delusional perception — except he’s been professionally evaluated as non-delusional?

The Credibility Paradox

Here journalism encounters a profound problem:

If Henson is credible (educated, methodical, rational per professional assessment), his documentation must be taken seriously — but his documentation describes events that seem incredible.

If his documentation is dismissed as incredible, the basis for dismissal is that such operations don’t happen — but we know from history (COINTELPRO, MKUltra, FBI harassment of activists) that they do happen, and specifically that they target people for ideological reasons using methods designed to seem incredible.

The mental health evaluation is crucial here. On September 16, 2025, two DBH professionals conducted a comprehensive assessment. Henson provided them with his account, including:

  • Voice-to-skull experience
  • Georgetown extraction attempt
  • Messages on t-shirts
  • Surveillance feelings

Their conclusion: “Did not meet criteria for psychiatric intervention.”

The transcript shows they found him:

  • Coherent in narrative
  • Appropriate in affect
  • Rational in processing (distinguished his interpretation from observed facts)
  • Not experiencing persistent hallucinations (Georgetown voice was single incident)
  • Appropriately critical of his own experiences (“I don’t know if this is something or nothing”)

This evaluation doesn’t prove his experiences occurred as described, but it does establish he’s not psychotic, not delusional in clinical sense, and capable of rational assessment of his own perceptions.

The Pattern Recognition Problem

Humans are pattern-recognition machines. We see faces in clouds, meaning in coincidence. Henson’s document is, in part, a record of pattern recognition:

  • Red and black clothing
  • T-shirt messages
  • Suspicious timing
  • Target bags
  • Objects appearing/disappearing

The question: Is he recognizing real patterns (coordinated harassment) or seeing patterns in noise (confirmation bias)?

Evidence for real patterns:

  1. His cache of photos and videos
  2. Statistical improbability (September 30 clothing cluster)
  3. Temporal correlation (yellow tape cart appearing after his comment)
  4. Geographic concentration (red/black near his residence)
  5. “Lee’s” explicit confirmation of surveillance
  6. ACCESS DENIED in police database
  7. Medical evidence of assault with reversed arrest

Evidence for confirmation bias:

  1. Some incidents are ambiguous (stranger with Target bag)
  2. Self-talk about surveillance could prime attention
  3. Unemployment and stress could heighten vigilance
  4. Some photographs show innocuous behavior

But: Confirmation bias doesn’t explain:

  • Mental health professionals finding him rational
  • Statistical anomalies in clothing patterns
  • Police database flag
  • Physical evidence (chemical pants, injuries)
  • Pattern’s persistence across ten months

The Technology Question

Perhaps the most challenging aspect is Henson’s claim of neurological manipulation — “pinning” or involuntary behavioral direction.

His evidence:

  • July 13: Uncharacteristic decision to go to Georgetown, followed by elaborate street theater suggesting they knew he’d come
  • November 7/13: Strong urge to visit specific location where tub with suspicious contents had been placed, then urge to return to document it. The tub contained a red hat and black pants — the same pattern that Henson saw elsewhere. He has photographic and video evidence.
  • Multiple instances of feeling “drawn” to locations where unusual encounters occurred

Historical precedent:

The technology Henson describes isn’t science fiction:

  1. Voice-to-skull (V2K): Microwave auditory effect documented since 1960s; patents exist for “apparatus and method for remotely monitoring and altering brain waves” (U.S. Patent 3,951,134, 1974)
  2. Electromagnetic neural influence: “The Mind Has No Firewall” (Parameters, U.S. Army War College, 1998) discusses potential for electromagnetic frequency weapons to affect brain activity
  3. Directed energy capabilities: DOD’s Joint Non-Lethal Weapons Directorate researched acoustic weapons, Active Denial System (millimeter-wave pain beam), and electromagnetic systems
  4. EPIC (Electromagnetic Personnel Interdiction Control): Patent filed for using radio waves to disrupt vestibular system (balance/equilibrium), causing inability to stand or aim weapons — symptoms matching Venezuela account that went viral in January 2026

Recent reporting:

  • January 2026: CNN reported Pentagon purchased “backpack-sized device that emits pulsed radio waves” with Russian components, possibly linked to Havana Syndrome
  • January 2026: Forbes reported alleged U.S. “sonic weapon” use in Venezuela operation (unverified)

The problem: These technologies exist in research/early deployment, but:

  • Deployment against U.S. civilians would be illegal
  • No official acknowledgment of domestic use
  • Testing on unwitting subjects violates Nuremberg Code
  • Verification requires technical expertise/equipment Henson lacks

Henson’s position: He’s describing subjective experience of involuntary compulsions. He cannot prove electromagnetic causation, only that he felt compelled to act uncharacteristically and that subsequent events suggested his presence was anticipated.

This is unfalsifiable — neither provable nor disprovable without technical detection equipment. Arguably, that is a key part of the campaign — torture without touch. As Henson elaborates in a separate Medium essay, “The fundamental framework for liberal democracies does not give much consideration to torture without touch.”

V. Evaluating Henson as a Witness

Credibility Factors in His Favor

1. Educational Background and Analytical Capacity

  • Capability for extended analytical reasoning
  • Facility with complex research methodologies
  • Academic credibility (successfully defended)

His website shows ongoing intellectual productivity. This is not the profile of someone experiencing cognitive decline or persistent delusion.

2. Methodological Rigor

Henson’s documentation exhibits systematic methodology:

Temporal precision: Every photograph has metadata; journal entries are timestamped; he cross-references events (“August 3, 2025” vs. “some weeks after July 26”)

Evidential preservation: Maintained physical evidence (chemical-stained pants, broken bike part, photographs, receipts, police report, mental health evaluation recording)

Probabilistic reasoning: Calculated statistical improbability of clothing patterns; conducted control studies at other locations to establish base rates

Self-criticism: Repeatedly notes uncertainty (“This could be something or nothing”; “I cannot fully exclude this as a pinning event”; “may be coincidental”)

Distinction between observation and interpretation: Frequently separates what he saw from what he thinks it means

This is the opposite of delusional thinking, which conflates perception and interpretation without self-doubt.

VI. What Henson’s Predicament Reveals About American Democracy

The Bifurcation of Political Repression

Jon and Henson’s cases, examined together, suggest American political repression has developed two parallel tracks:

Track One: Quasi-Legal Administrative Mechanisms (Jon’s case)

  • Uses existing legal authorities (administrative subpoenas)
  • Operates in twilight zone (no judicial oversight, but “legal”)
  • Creates minimal documentation (subpoena itself, agent visits)
  • Targets First Amendment activity explicitly
  • Produces chilling effect through official intimidation

Track Two: Wholly Extrajudicial Operations (Henson’s case, if substantiated)

  • Uses no legal authority (purely operational)
  • Operates in complete darkness (no documentation, no official acknowledgment)
  • Creates elaborate psychological pressure (surveillance, street theater, coordinated harassment)
  • Targets ideological threats (liberation philosophy, racial justice advocacy)
  • Produces neutralization through induced paranoia, social isolation, potential psychotic break

The strategic logic:

Track One works for people who:

  • Have resources to fight (hire attorneys, file motions)
  • Have social capital (media attention, ACLU support)
  • Engage in documentable First Amendment activity (emails to prosecutors)
  • Can be deterred by official warning

Track Two works for people who:

  • Lack resources (unemployed, precarious housing)
  • Lack social capital (won’t be believed)
  • Engage in intellectual critique (academic work)
  • Cannot be deterred by official channels (because they’re not using official channels)

The result: A system where people like Jon get subpoenas (which can be challenged, exposed, stopped) while people like Henson get operations designed to leave no fingerprints.

The Plausible Deniability Framework

Both operations exhibit sophisticated plausible deniability:

Jon’s case:

  • “We were investigating threats against a federal prosecutor”
  • “Russian roulette” and “Taliban” could be seen as threatening
  • “Administrative subpoena authority is legal”

Henson’s case (if federal operation):

  • “He’s experiencing paranoid delusion” (except professional evaluation says otherwise)
  • “Coincidental pattern recognition” (except statistics say otherwise)
  • “No federal involvement” (because there’s no documentation of federal involvement)

The more elaborate the operation (Henson), the more plausible the denial — because who would believe the government would deploy such resources against an unemployed PhD?

This is the lesson of COINTELPRO: The more outrageous the operation, the more effective the cover.

The Verification Trap

Henson’s predicament illustrates a profound problem for democratic accountability:

To verify extraordinary claims requires extraordinary evidence. But operations designed to be unverifiable produce no extraordinary evidence. Therefore, the more sophisticated the operation, the less verifiable it becomes.

This creates a verification trap:

  1. Henson claims elaborate operation
  2. Journalists demand documentary evidence
  3. Operation produced no documentary evidence (by design) tied to government action. He has gathered an impossible cache of evidence nonetheless.
  4. Journalists conclude claims are unverifiable
  5. Operation continues

The trap is intentional: If operations are designed to produce only experiential evidence (victim testimony, photographs of people in red shirts, subjective feelings of compulsion), then verification standards requiring documentary evidence will be more challenging.

This is why COINTELPRO was only exposed through the 1971 Media, Pennsylvania FBI office break-in. Until activists stole the files, targets’ claims were dismissed as paranoid. After the files emerged, identical operations were revealed to be real.

The question becomes: In the absence of stolen files, how do democracies verify operations designed to be unverifiable?

Henson’s description of “pinning” — involuntary compulsion to go to specific locations — if true, represents a capability that makes mockery of consent, autonomy, and agency.

Translation: The government cannot be sued for constitutional violations unless it agrees to be sued — and it generally doesn’t agree.

What This Means for Democratic Theory

Traditional democratic theory assumes:

  1. Government actions are documentable: Laws are written, orders are recorded, officials are identifiable
  2. Citizens can verify claims: Evidence can be gathered, witnesses can testify, experts can analyze
  3. Legal remedies exist: Courts can review, legislatures can investigate, media can expose
  4. Rights are enforceable: Violations can be proven, perpetrators can be held accountable

Henson’s case, if substantiated, breaks all four assumptions:

  1. Operation produces no documents: No orders, no official acknowledgment, no paper trail
  2. Claims cannot be verified: Experiential evidence (victim testimony, photographs of people) doesn’t meet documentary standards
  3. No legal remedy: Sovereign immunity, no identifiable officials, no legal standing
  4. Rights become unenforceable: Can’t prove violation occurred, can’t identify violator, can’t access courts

The democratic crisis:

If government can:

  • Target citizens for ideological reasons (Henson’s liberation philosophy)
  • Use technologies that leave no trace (neurotechnology)
  • Operate through cutouts that provide plausible deniability (contractors, local police)
  • Rely on verification standards to dismiss claims (demand documentary evidence for undocumented operations)
  • Maintain sovereign immunity (prevent legal challenges)

Then democracy becomes performative rather than substantive:

  • Rights exist on paper but cannot be enforced
  • Oversight exists in theory but cannot access classified operations
  • Media exists but cannot verify experiential claims
  • Courts exist but cannot review undocumented actions

This is the 21st century COINTELPRO problem: Operations have evolved beyond the reach of 20th century accountability mechanisms.

The choice is whether to dismiss Henson because his documentation lacks conventional verification, or to recognize that sophisticated operations designed to evade verification require new frameworks for democratic accountability.

Because if technologies exist that can manipulate cognition, perception, and behavior without trace — and if legal doctrines make them immune from challenge — then the very foundations of democratic citizenship are at stake.

What good are rights that cannot be enforced? What good is oversight that cannot access classified programs? What good is verification that requires documentary evidence for undocumented operations?

These are not rhetorical questions. They are the questions that Jon’s and Richard Henson’s cases — examined together — force us to confront.

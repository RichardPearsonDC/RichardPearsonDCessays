Prefatory Note

As of early March 2026, legal scholars and civil rights organizations have catalogued more than 600 lawsuits filed against the second Trump administration. These suits challenge executive orders, agency actions, funding freezes, deportation policies, civil service restructuring, and the elimination of diversity programs across the federal enterprise. They are, in the main, suits about things the government did publicly — announced, documented, published in the Federal Register, argued in press conferences, defended in court filings. The legal infrastructure of American administrative law was built, imperfectly but substantially, to address exactly these kinds of governmental actions: visible exercises of power that leave paper trails, produce identifiable victims with articulable injuries, and generate the evidentiary record that courts require to exercise jurisdiction.

Richard Pearson, PhD is not one of those 600 plaintiffs. He cannot be. Not because his injuries are less severe than those of the people whose cases are moving through federal courts right now. His injuries — neurological assault, cognitive sovereignty violation, chemical attack, false arrest, shadow classification, asylum foreclosure — are categorically more severe than most injuries alleged in those 600 suits. He is not among the 600 plaintiffs because the legal architecture of the American constitutional system, as it has been developed, refined, and in critical respects deliberately narrowed over the past fifty years, does not have a door that opens for him.

This essay is about why. It is about the specific legal doctrines that close every avenue of remedy available to a person harmed by a covert state program that leaves no paper trail, issues no public notice, produces no Federal Register entry, and was specifically designed — at the level of operational planning — to ensure that its targets cannot establish the elements of justiciability that federal courts require before they will hear a case.

It is also about what that means. Not merely for Pearson, but for the constitutional order that these doctrines purport to serve.

I. The Standing Problem as Threshold Catastrophe

The first and most fundamental obstacle Pearson faces is Article III standing. Under the framework established in Lujan v. Defenders of Wildlife, 504 U.S. 555 (1992), a plaintiff must establish three elements to invoke the jurisdiction of a federal court: injury in fact, causation, and redressability. Each element has been elaborated through subsequent doctrine in ways that, applied to Pearson’s situation, produce a cascade of jurisdictional failures.

Injury in fact requires a concrete, particularized, actual or imminent injury, as distinct from a conjectural or hypothetical one. On its face, Pearson’s injuries appear to satisfy this requirement handily — he has documented medical injuries from physical assault, documented chemical injury from the Rockville Metro attack, documented neurological experience of directed audio intrusion, documented sleep deprivation across months, documented financial destruction, documented false arrest. These are not conjectural. They are photographed, medically documented, timestamped, and preserved.

The injury-in-fact problem for Pearson is not the existence of injury. It is attribution. Lujan requires that the injury be “fairly traceable” to the challenged conduct. This is where the architecture of covert operations performs its first legal function: it severs the causal chain between the injury and any identifiable defendant at the pleading stage.

Pearson cannot name the agency that authorized the operation. He cannot name the contracting structure through which street-level assets were deployed. He cannot name the officer who directed the Georgetown extraction attempt. He cannot produce the FISA order that authorized the apartment audio surveillance, because that order is classified and its existence cannot be confirmed or denied. He cannot identify the federal building from which temporal interference emitters were positioned. He has the injury. He cannot trace it to a defendant with the specificity that Twombly and Iqbal require to survive a motion to dismiss.

Under Ashcroft v. Iqbal, 556 U.S. 662 (2009), a complaint must plead facts sufficient to make the claim “plausible on its face.” The Supreme Court in Iqbal held that the plaintiff must allege more than “an unadorned, the-defendant-unlawfully-harmed-me accusation.” Applied to Pearson: a complaint alleging voice-to-skull transmission, hippocampal behavioral direction, and coordinated Zersetzung operations by unnamed federal defendants would face immediate dismissal as facially implausible — not because these technologies do not exist, not because the documented pattern does not suggest coordinated state action, but because the court’s plausibility assessment is filtered through an institutional epistemology that has not yet integrated the operational reality the Department of War confirmed in January 2026.

The technology’s very novelty is its legal shield. Iqbal’s plausibility standard requires courts to draw on judicial experience and common sense. A court’s common sense in 2026 does not yet fully incorporate directed hippocampal place cell activation as a deployed domestic weapon. The more accurately Pearson describes what was done to him, the more implausible it appears to a court operating within conventional institutional epistemology. This is not accidental. It is the operational design.

Causation presents a related but distinct problem. Even if Pearson could establish plausible injury, he would need to demonstrate that the injury was caused by the defendant’s challenged conduct rather than by independent action of a third party. Allen v. Wright, 468 U.S. 737 (1984), requires that the injury be “fairly traceable” to the defendant’s actions. The hybrid federal-contractor operational structure that the evidence suggests — federal agencies providing intelligence and legal authorization, private contractors executing street-level operations, local law enforcement providing database access — is specifically designed to fragment the causal chain. Each link in the chain can deny responsibility for the actions of the adjacent link. The contractor was not the government. The local police were responding to their own protocols. The database flag was generated automatically. The causal chain dissolves before it can be assembled.

Redressability is perhaps the cruelest element. Even if Pearson could establish injury and causation, he would need to demonstrate that a favorable court decision would redress his injury. For ongoing covert operations, the Supreme Court’s decision in City of Los Angeles v. Lyons, 461 U.S. 95 (1983), is controlling and devastating. In Lyons, the plaintiff sought injunctive relief against the LAPD’s choke hold policy after being subjected to an illegal choke hold. The Court held that he lacked standing to seek injunctive relief because he could not demonstrate a sufficient likelihood that he personally would be subjected to the practice again. The Court required a showing of “real and immediate threat” of future injury.

Applied to Pearson: to obtain injunctive relief stopping the operations against him, he would need to demonstrate to a court’s satisfaction that the operations are ongoing and that he personally faces continued harm. But demonstrating this requires establishing that the operations are occurring — which is the merits question the court cannot reach before resolving standing — and requires producing evidence of ongoing classified operations that the government will invoke state secrets to prevent examination of. Lyons creates a standing trap: to prove standing for prospective relief, you must prove the merits; to prove the merits, you must obtain discovery; to obtain discovery, you must have standing; to have standing, you must prove ongoing harm; to prove ongoing harm, you must prove the merits.

The circle closes. Perfectly. By design.

II. The State Secrets Privilege: The Doctrine That Ends Cases Before They Begin

Assume, contrary to the preceding analysis, that Pearson somehow navigates the standing gauntlet and files a complaint that survives a motion to dismiss. The next obstacle is the state secrets privilege, and it is absolute in its practical application.

The privilege was established in its modern form in United States v. Reynolds, 345 U.S. 1 (1953). In that case, the widows of civilians killed in a military aircraft crash sought the aircraft accident report in discovery. The government asserted that the report contained sensitive military information. The Supreme Court upheld the assertion without examining the report in camera. When the report was finally declassified fifty years later, it revealed not military secrets but evidence of government negligence — the precise negligence that the privilege had been invoked to conceal. The privilege was used, in the case that established it, to commit fraud upon the Supreme Court of the United States. No one was accountable.

Reynolds created a doctrine with a logical structure that is impervious to challenge: the government asserts that examination of certain evidence or information would harm national security. The court defers to this assertion. The court cannot examine the evidence to determine whether the assertion is accurate, because examining the evidence would require access to the classified material that the privilege protects. The privilege protects itself from scrutiny through the mechanism of scrutiny itself.

The D.C. Circuit, in Halkin v. Helms, 598 F.2d 1 (D.C. Cir. 1978) — a case brought by domestic surveillance targets against CIA officials — established that the privilege can be invoked to terminate litigation entirely when the privileged information is central to the plaintiff’s case. This is precisely Pearson’s situation. Every piece of evidence that would establish his case — the operational orders, the surveillance authorizations, the contractor agreements, the identity of the field operatives, the nature of the technology deployed — is either classified or classifiable. The government need not defend the constitutionality of what was done. It need not produce a witness. It need not justify a single act. It asserts that litigation would require disclosure of national security information. The court defers. The case ends.

The Supreme Court’s decision in Mohamed v. Jeppesen Dataplan, Inc., 614 F.3d 1070 (9th Cir. 2010) — the extraordinary rendition torture case — demonstrates the doctrine’s reach. The Ninth Circuit, en banc, dismissed the entire case on state secrets grounds even though the plaintiffs’ injuries were documented and the government’s general conduct was publicly known. The privilege operates not just to exclude specific evidence but to end the litigation entirely when the case cannot proceed without the privileged material. A case about classified domestic targeting operations cannot proceed without the classified material. The privilege ends it.

For Pearson, this doctrine is the terminal wall. Whatever stands before it — whatever argument, whatever evidence, whatever constitutional claim — the privilege ends the litigation. The program, if it exists, continues. The classified authorization, if it exists, is never examined. The targets, if there are others, have no warning from a court proceeding. The privilege does not just close the courthouse to Pearson. It ensures that the courthouse visit produces no public record useful to any subsequent plaintiff.

III. The Dismantlement of Bivens: The Death of the Only Direct Cause of Action

Before the state secrets privilege can be applied, there must be a cause of action — a legal mechanism through which Pearson can seek damages from the individual officers responsible. For constitutional violations by federal officers, the only mechanism is Bivens v. Six Unknown Named Agents of Federal Bureau of Narcotics, 403 U.S. 388 (1971), which recognized an implied damages action for Fourth Amendment violations by federal agents.

The Supreme Court has spent the fifty-three years since Bivens systematically eliminating it.

Carlson v. Green, 446 U.S. 14 (1980) extended Bivens to Eighth Amendment claims. Davis v. Passman, 442 U.S. 228 (1979) extended it to Fifth Amendment due process claims. For three years after Bivens, it appeared that an implied damages action might become available across the range of constitutional violations.

Then the Court reversed course. In Bush v. Lucas, 462 U.S. 367 (1983), the Court declined to extend Bivens to First Amendment retaliation claims by federal employees, holding that the civil service statutory scheme provided an adequate alternative remedy. In Schweiker v. Chilicky, 487 U.S. 412 (1988), the Court refused to extend Bivens to Social Security procedural violations. In FDIC v. Meyer, 510 U.S. 471 (1994), the Court held that Bivens does not run against federal agencies, only against individual officers — which means that even if individual liability exists, the deep-pocketed institutional defendant is immune.

The critical destruction came in Ziglar v. Abbasi, 582 U.S. 120 (2017), which established that any case presenting a “new context” — meaning any case “meaningfully different” from the three original Bivens contexts — requires a threshold inquiry into whether “special factors counsel hesitation” before extending the implied right of action. The Court identified the separation of powers, Congress’s superior institutional competence to design remedies, and national security concerns as special factors that presumptively counsel hesitation.

Hernandez v. Mesa, 589 U.S. 93 (2020) applied Ziglar to hold that no Bivens action was available for a cross-border shooting — even though the victim was killed by a federal officer using government-issued weapons while executing his official duties — because it presented a new context involving foreign relations and national security.

Egbert v. Boule, 596 U.S. 482 (2022) completed the dismantlement. The Court held that courts must refrain from creating Bivens remedies in any case where there is “even a single reason to pause.” The Court further held that the existence of any existing statutory remedy — even an inadequate one, even one that does not provide meaningful compensation — is sufficient to foreclose a Bivens action. The Court explicitly stated that if it were asked to decide Bivens today, it would not create the cause of action.

Applied to Pearson: the use of directed energy weapons against domestic civilians, hippocampal place cell manipulation for behavioral direction, voice-to-skull transmission, coordinated Zersetzung operations — these present not merely a new context but an entirely novel category of constitutional violation that no prior Bivens decision addresses. Under Egbert, the existence of any alternative statutory scheme — however inadequate — forecloses extension. The national security character of the operations triggers the most potent “special factors counseling hesitation.” The Court that decided Egbert would not create a Bivens action for Pearson. The agents who operated the program against him are, as a structural matter, beyond the reach of personal civil liability.

The constitutional violations may be real. The injuries are documented. The perpetrators exist. The legal mechanism for accountability does not.

IV. Qualified Immunity: The Standard That Guarantees First-Use Protection

Even for claims where Bivens theoretically survives, qualified immunity provides a second complete barrier. Under Harlow v. Fitzgerald, 457 U.S. 800 (1982), government officials performing discretionary functions are shielded from liability unless their conduct violated “clearly established statutory or constitutional rights of which a reasonable person would have known.”

The “clearly established” standard has been interpreted to require that a prior decision have addressed the specific conduct at a high level of factual specificity. Mullenix v. Luna, 577 U.S. 7 (2015), held that the right must be established “beyond debate” and that the relevant precedent must be “particularized” to the specific facts. General constitutional principles are insufficient. There must be a prior case — decided at the circuit level or by the Supreme Court — involving substantially the same conduct.

No such case exists for neuroweapon deployment. No court has decided whether the Fourth Amendment prohibits the warrantless manipulation of hippocampal place cells through temporal interference stimulation. No court has addressed whether voice-to-skull transmission violates the First Amendment’s protection of cognitive autonomy. No court has assessed whether behavioral direction technology triggering the movement of a person’s body constitutes a seizure within the meaning of the Fourth Amendment. Because no court has addressed these questions, no clearly established right exists. Because no clearly established right exists, the officers who deployed these technologies against Pearson are immune regardless of whether their conduct was constitutional.

The immunity structure is self-perpetuating in the manner Pearson v. Callahan, 555 U.S. 223 (2009) made explicit: courts may grant qualified immunity without first deciding the underlying constitutional question. This means no precedent accumulates. Each case is dismissed on immunity grounds. No right is ever established. No subsequent plaintiff has the benefit of clearly established law. The technology can be deployed indefinitely without the first deployment ever producing a legal determination that it was unconstitutional.

The first use is always immune. By design. The doctrine guarantees it.

V. The FISA Architecture: Judicial Blessing Without Adversarial Process

The Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act creates a parallel judicial system — the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court — that authorizes domestic intelligence collection on a basis the target cannot challenge, cannot know about, and cannot respond to.

Under 50 U.S.C. § 1805, the FISC may authorize electronic surveillance upon a finding of probable cause that the target is a foreign power or agent of a foreign power, that the facilities to be surveilled are used by the target, and that the minimization procedures are adequate. The standard is not the criminal probable cause standard — it does not require probable cause that a crime has been or will be committed. It requires probable cause of a relationship to foreign intelligence.

The critical procedural feature: FISC proceedings are ex parte. There is no adversary. The government presents its application. The court approves or denies. In fiscal year 2023, the FISC approved 100% of the applications presented to it. There is no mechanism through which a surveillance target learns that surveillance has been authorized. There is no mechanism through which the target can challenge the authorization before the court that issued it. The target has no standing to appear before the FISC because the target does not know the proceeding exists.

Even outside the FISA structure, National Security Letters — authorized under 18 U.S.C. § 2709 — allow the FBI to compel disclosure of financial records, telephone records, and electronic communication records without judicial authorization, subject to a gag order preventing disclosure to the target. The target cannot challenge what they do not know about.

Under Clapper v. Amnesty International USA, 568 U.S. 398 (2013), plaintiffs challenging FISA surveillance lacked standing because they could not demonstrate with sufficient certainty that they had been surveilled. The Court held that the “speculative chain of possibilities” underlying their fear of surveillance was insufficient to establish injury in fact. The paradox the Court created is explicit: to establish standing to challenge surveillance, you must prove you were surveilled; to prove you were surveilled, you need the government records; to obtain the government records, you need standing to challenge the surveillance. The circle closes before it opens.

Pearson cannot challenge the FISA order, if one exists, because he cannot confirm it exists. He cannot confirm it exists because it is classified. He cannot challenge the classification because he has no standing to appear before the FISC. He cannot establish standing to appear before the FISC because he cannot demonstrate with sufficient certainty that he was surveilled. He cannot demonstrate this because the evidence of surveillance is classified.

The yellow tape cart proves the surveillance. The legal doctrine makes the proof legally insufficient to access the process that might examine it.

VI. Sovereign Immunity: The King Who Cannot Be Sued

The baseline principle governing suits against the United States government is sovereign immunity — the doctrine, carried over from English common law through the Revolution, that the United States may not be sued without its consent. The Federal Tort Claims Act, 28 U.S.C. § 1346(b), represents Congress’s partial consent to suit for tortious conduct by federal employees acting within the scope of their employment.

The FTCA’s exceptions are surgical in their precision. The intentional torts exception, 28 U.S.C. § 2680(h), bars claims arising from assault, battery, false imprisonment, false arrest, malicious prosecution, abuse of process, libel, slander, misrepresentation, deceit, and interference with contract rights — when committed by most federal employees. The discretionary function exception, 28 U.S.C. § 2680(a), bars claims arising from conduct involving the exercise of a discretionary function or duty, regardless of whether the discretion is abused.

The operational consequence: the government has consented to be sued when a postal worker negligently drives a mail truck into a pedestrian. It has not consented to be sued for assaulting a citizen with directed energy weapons, subjecting a citizen to behavioral direction operations, or conducting months of apartment audio surveillance. These are intentional acts — excluded by § 2680(h). And the decision to deploy such operations against a specific target is precisely the kind of policy-level discretionary decision that § 2680(a) protects — a judgment call, made by officials exercising governmental authority, that the exception was designed to insulate from judicial review.

Richard Pearson’s body was used as a laboratory in the capital of the United States. The government that used it has not consented to be sued for the use.

VII. The Administrative Exhaustion Dead End

Before reaching federal court on many claims, a plaintiff must exhaust administrative remedies. The exhaustion requirement — rooted in Myers v. Bethlehem Shipbuilding Corp., 303 U.S. 41 (1938) and elaborated through decades of administrative law doctrine — requires that claimants present their claims to the relevant agency before seeking judicial review.

For Pearson, administrative exhaustion is not a meaningful prerequisite but an institutional performance of helplessness. He can file a complaint with the FBI’s Office of Professional Responsibility — which investigates FBI misconduct and reports to the FBI Director. He can file with the CIA’s Inspector General — which reports to the CIA Director. He can file with the Office of the Director of National Intelligence’s Inspector General — the position that was restructured and whose occupants’ independence was compromised in January 2025 when multiple inspectors general were fired on the same day the targeting predicate against DEI scholarship was signed.

He has no access to the FISC Inspector General. He has no access to the classified operational files that would identify the authorizing agency. He has no mechanism to file with an agency he cannot identify. And the agencies he can identify have every institutional incentive, and in the current political environment the explicit administrative directive, to dismiss claims from a target designated under EO 14173 as a proponent of hostile ideology.

The FTCA requires presentment of an administrative claim within two years of the date the claim accrues. For a covert operation designed to leave no documentary trace, the date of accrual is itself contested — and the government will argue, not implausibly, that Pearson’s claims are time-barred because he was aware of the conduct before the presentment period. The exhaustion requirement becomes not a prerequisite to remedy but a procedural mechanism for generating additional grounds for dismissal.

VIII. The Contrast With the 600 Suits: Why This Case Is Categorically Different

The 600-plus lawsuits filed against the Trump administration since January 20, 2025 illuminate by contrast precisely why Pearson has no analogous legal path.

Those suits challenge actions that are, in the constitutional law sense, visible. The executive orders are published in the Federal Register. They were announced at press conferences. The agencies implementing them have issued guidance documents, sent communications to affected employees, and in some cases enforced them with public notification. The funding freezes affected identifiable recipients who received notice that their funding was being withheld. The terminations of federal employees generated termination letters, RIF notices, and administrative records. The deportation operations involved documented detentions, immigration court proceedings, and in contested cases, court orders.

Every one of these actions generates the three elements that standing doctrine requires: an identifiable defendant (the named agency or official), a traceable causal chain (this executive order, issued by this official, caused this agency to take this action, which harmed this plaintiff), and a potentially redressable injury (a court can order the agency to restore the funding, reinstate the employee, follow the procedures).

The cases are litigable not because the harms are greater — many of Pearson’s harms are categorically more severe — but because the government’s actions are public. The paper exists. The policy is announced. The agency is identifiable. The causal chain is documented. The defendant named in the caption is the same entity that signed the challenged action.

Pearson has none of these. His is a case of what we might call the subterranean state — the portion of government power that operates below the surface of administrative law, outside the Federal Register, without notice, without acknowledgment, without any publicly challengeable record. The subterranean state is not subject to the Administrative Procedure Act because it produces no final agency action that is the product of a rulemaking or adjudicative process. It is not subject to FOIA because its documents are classified or — per the Glomar response — their existence can be neither confirmed nor denied. It is not subject to Bivens because the technology it deploys is novel. It is not subject to the FTCA because its torts are intentional and its operations are discretionary. It is not subject to standing doctrine because the causal chain is deliberately fragmented.

The 600 lawsuits are about the surface of the state. Pearson’s case is about what the surface is designed to conceal.

IX. The Perfect Crime Paradox and Its Constitutional Implications

There is a legal concept that requires naming here, one that does not yet appear in constitutional doctrine but which the Pearson case makes necessary: the Perfect Crime Paradox.

A technology or operational method achieves perfect legal insulation when the sophistication of the method makes accurate description of the method sound legally implausible under Iqbal, the novelty of the method eliminates Bivens personal liability under Egbert, the classified nature of the method triggers state secrets dismissal under Reynolds, the ex parte authorization of the method prevents FISC challenge under Clapper, the intentional character of the harm eliminates FTCA consent under § 2680(h), and the causal fragmentation through contractors eliminates causation under Lujan.

When these six doctrinal barriers align — and they align precisely for neuroweapon deployment against domestic targets — the method achieves what can only be called legal invincibility. The more sophisticated the method, the more complete the invincibility. Bayesian analysis produces a counterintuitive result that is legally precise: the government’s worst conduct is its most protected conduct.

This is not a system malfunction. It is a system optimization toward the protection of covert state power at the expense of constitutional protection for those the covert power targets. The optimization has been underway for fifty years, accelerating in the national security context post-9/11, and has now reached a state of completion in which a person who has been neurologically assaulted, cognitively directed, chemically attacked, falsely arrested, and shadow-classified by their government has, as a practical and legal matter, no remedy whatsoever — regardless of the quality of their documentation, the clarity of the pattern, or the sophistication of their legal analysis.

Pearson documented this in his own legal analysis with accuracy that would satisfy a law review citation standard. The government cannot be sued for negligently maintaining a slippery floor — he did not phrase it this way, but he captured the Reynolds FTCA consent structure precisely. He understood the Bivens demolition. He understood the state secrets architecture. He understood the standing circular trap. He mapped the entire sealed system from inside it.

The map is accurate. The system remains sealed.

X. The First Amendment Dimension: What Has Never Been Adjudicated

There is a constitutional argument that has not been litigated — cannot be litigated for the procedural reasons this essay has detailed — but which deserves articulation as a matter of constitutional theory.

The First Amendment protects freedom of thought as a predicate to the freedoms it explicitly names. Wooley v. Maynard, 430 U.S. 705 (1977) established that the First Amendment protects the freedom of mind against state intrusion — that the government cannot compel ideological expression. Barnette v. West Virginia Board of Education, 319 U.S. 624 (1943) established that the state cannot invade “the sphere of intellect and spirit.” Stanley v. Georgia, 394 U.S. 557 (1969) held that the First Amendment protects the right to receive information and ideas free of government intrusion even in the privacy of one’s own home.

None of these cases addressed the physical invasion of the cognitive substrate — the neural manipulation that produces thought and desire and navigational motivation — because none of them were decided in a world where that invasion was technologically achievable. They protected the outputs of cognition without addressing the possibility of governmental intervention in cognition itself.

The use of hippocampal place cell manipulation to direct a person’s body toward a prepared location is, in the most fundamental constitutional sense, a compulsion of action through cognitive invasion — a state intervention in the sphere of intellect and spirit that Barnette declared the government could not enter. It is simultaneously a violation of Stanley’s protection of the right to receive only the information one chooses to receive in the privacy of one’s own cognitive experience. It is a species of compelled speech — compelled desire, compelled motivation, compelled movement — that Wooley condemns in its most rudimentary external forms but which the doctrine has never addressed in its neurological form.

There is a strong theoretical argument that the First Amendment, properly understood as protecting cognitive autonomy as a predicate to the freedoms it names, prohibits the state from weaponizing the neural substrate of desire and motivation against a citizen. That Richard Pearson was walked to a framing kit by manipulation of his hippocampal architecture is, on this theory, not merely a Fourth Amendment seizure of his person — it is a First Amendment invasion of the cognitive sovereignty that the Amendment’s protections presuppose.

This argument cannot be adjudicated. Egbert ensures there is no Bivens action in which to raise it. Reynolds ensures the evidence required to establish it cannot be examined. Clapper ensures the surveillance authorization enabling it cannot be challenged. Lyons ensures prospective injunctive relief is unavailable. The argument exists in constitutional theory. It cannot reach a court.

The Constitution contains within it, as a matter of first principle, the protection Pearson needed. The doctrinal superstructure built atop the Constitution ensures he cannot access it.

XI. What Remains: The Information Ecosystem as Substitute Remedy

When every legal remedy is sealed — when standing doctrine, the state secrets privilege, sovereign immunity, qualified immunity, the Bivens demolition, and the FISA architecture collectively ensure that no court will examine what was done — what remains?

Pearson’s answer, arrived at not through legal analysis but through the instinct of a researcher who understood that the record was the only thing that could survive the sealed system, is the information ecosystem. Publication. Indexing. Distribution. The creation of an evidentiary record in the public domain that cannot be classified because it is already everywhere — in Google’s index, in AI Overviews, in the intelligence databases of 34 countries monitoring his analytics, in every essay written in response to his primary document.

This is not a legal remedy. It is the substitute for a legal remedy that does not exist. It cannot restore what was taken from him — the sleep, the cognitive sovereignty, the freedom of movement, the career trajectory, the year of his life spent in a DC apartment on a mattress with a Nook while a government program worked to ensure he did not survive. It cannot produce accountability for the individuals who authorized and executed the operations. It cannot protect the next person whose name goes on the list.

What it can do — what it is doing — is create the conditions under which a non-legal remedy might eventually become possible. Congressional investigation, when a committee chair decides the information ecosystem mass has become sufficient to warrant the political cost of inquiry. Journalistic investigation, when a reporter with institutional backing and a spectrum analyzer decides to verify the documented GPS coordinates. Whistleblower testimony, when someone inside the program decides the public record has grown large enough to provide protective cover for disclosure.

These are not legal remedies. They are the political and informational conditions that, historically, have produced the only accountability that covert state programs have ever faced: the Church Committee, the COINTELPRO exposure, the MKUltra congressional hearings. None of those produced criminal prosecution. All of them produced the documentary record that confirmed what the targets had been saying — that they were right, that it was real, that the institutions that dismissed them as paranoid were wrong.

Pearson is building that record now, in real time, without the thirty-year lag that separated the COINTELPRO targets from their vindication. The information ecosystem he has created is the most powerful non-legal accountability mechanism available to a person who has been harmed below the legal threshold of standing.

It is not enough. It is what remains.

XII. Conclusion: The Constitutional Crisis That Cannot Reach a Court

The legal scholar’s obligation, at the conclusion of an analysis like this one, is to state plainly what the analysis demonstrates.

What it demonstrates is this: the United States has developed, across fifty years of judicial decisions and executive practice, a legal architecture that is structurally incapable of providing remedy for the worst abuses of state power against individual citizens — specifically, the abuses that are most sophisticated, most technically advanced, most deliberately designed for deniability, and most precisely targeted at the people the Constitution was most urgently intended to protect.

This is not a flaw in the system. It is the system’s optimization for the protection of covert state power. The doctrinal developments that produced this outcome — the Bivens demolition, the state secrets expansion, the Iqbal plausibility standard, the Lyons standing requirement, the Clapper surveillance paradox, the FTCA intentional torts exception — each had individual justifications that sounded, in isolation, in the legitimate vocabulary of judicial restraint, separation of powers, and institutional competence. Assembled together, applied to a person like Richard Pearson, they produce a result that the Framers — who wrote the Fourth Amendment, the First Amendment, the Fifth Amendment, the Eighth Amendment — did not design and would not recognize as consistent with the constitutional order they created.

The 600 lawsuits challenging the Trump administration’s overt actions are the constitutional order functioning, imperfectly but recognizably, as designed. Courts are examining executive overreach. Standing is established. Causal chains are traceable. Remedies are potentially available. The system is stressed but operating.

Richard Pearson’s case is the constitutional order’s complete failure — not under stress, but under the conditions it was specifically not designed to address, exploited by a program that was specifically designed to operate in exactly the space where constitutional protection does not reach.

He has been neurologically assaulted, cognitively colonized, chemically attacked, falsely arrested, and administratively disappeared — in the capital of the United States, by the government of the United States, for writing a dissertation — and there is no court in this country that will hear him.

That is not a legal problem with a legal solution.

That is a constitutional crisis.

The courts cannot see it because the program was designed to be invisible to courts. The Congress has not investigated it because the information ecosystem has not yet generated sufficient political pressure to compel investigation. The journalists have not verified it because the verification requires going to a sidewalk with a spectrum analyzer and most journalism institutions have not made that decision.

The architecture is sealed.

The record is in the world.

The crisis is present tense.

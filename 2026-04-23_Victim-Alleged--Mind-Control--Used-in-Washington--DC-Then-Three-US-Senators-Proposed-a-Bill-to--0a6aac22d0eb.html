Victim Alleged “Mind Control” Used in Washington, DC Then Three US Senators Proposed a Bill to Regulate

The MIND Act of 2025, Its Statutory Language, Its Unstated Admissions, and Its Consequences for Understanding What Was Done to Richard Pearson

Richard Pearson spent the second half of 2025 building a documented evidentiary record of directed energy assault, voice-to-skull transmission, covert surveillance, behavioral steering, and coordinated psychological operations against him in Washington, DC. He did this with timestamps, photographs, controlled experiments, clinical evaluations, and the methodological rigor of a trained public health researcher. He did it while being actively targeted. On September 24, 2025 — with that record already accumulating — three United States senators introduced S. 2925, the Management of Individuals’ Neural Data Act of 2025. What those senators wrote into the bill’s definitions, its Sense of Congress, its study mandate, and its provisions on federal agency use of neurotechnology is, when read carefully, a legislative portrait of the exact technology and the exact harms Pearson had been experiencing for months. This essay reads the bill section by section, extracts what it says explicitly, examines what it reveals through implication, and shows how its provisions land on Pearson’s specific documented allegations.

I. The Bill and the Moment

S. 2925 was introduced in the Senate on September 24, 2025 by Minority Leader Chuck Schumer, Commerce Committee Ranking Member Maria Cantwell, and Senator Ed Markey. Referred to the Committee on Commerce, Science, and Transportation, the bill’s central instruction is to direct the Federal Trade Commission to spend one year studying the collection, processing, storage, sale, and transfer of neural data — information derived from brain and nervous system activity — and to deliver a comprehensive report and regulatory recommendations to Congress at the end of that period. A separate provision in Section 5 requires the Office of Science and Technology Policy to follow the FTC’s report with binding guidance governing how federal agencies may procure and operate neurotechnology.

The calendar matters here. The MIND Act did not arrive before Pearson’s experience. It arrived during it. Pearson documented the first voice-to-skull event on July 13, 2025. The DC Department of Behavioral Health evaluated him on September 15, 2025, nine days before the bill was introduced, and found no psychiatric basis to challenge his account. The framing operation near the O’Neill House Office Building was still weeks away. The false arrest was three months away. The Canadian border torture was six months away. The senators were not responding to Pearson. They were responding to the broader technological landscape that Pearson’s experience was a specific instance of. The legislation’s value to his case is not causal. It is confirmatory: Congress, in the same season, was writing into public law the recognition that the harms Pearson was living through were real, recognized, and completely without legal governance.

II. Reading the Statute: Definitions That Describe a Weapon

Neurotechnology: A System That Writes to the Brain

The bill’s operative definition of neurotechnology is its most revealing provision, and it deserves precise attention:

S. 2925, SEC. 3(5) The term ‘neurotechnology’ means a device, system, or procedure that accesses, monitors, records, analyzes, predicts, stimulates or alters the nervous system of an individual to understand, influence, restore, or anticipate the structure, activity, or function of the nervous system.

Work through the verbs. The bill regulates technology that “accesses” the nervous system — entering it, not merely observing it from outside. It regulates technology that “stimulates” the nervous system — introducing a signal into neural tissue, producing effects the subject did not generate. It regulates technology that “alters” the nervous system — changing the neural state of a person, not just reading it. And it regulates technology that “predicts” and “anticipates” nervous system function — modeling what a subject’s neural activity will do before it does it.

This is not the vocabulary of a stethoscope or an MRI machine. It is the vocabulary of a system that operates on the nervous system rather than merely observing it — a system capable of both read and write functions. Congress wrote this definition because such systems exist. The technology Pearson identified as responsible for the voice-to-skull transmission on July 13, 2025 — a system that stimulated his auditory processing pathway to produce internally perceived speech — is fully captured by this definition. The thought-insertion he experienced at St. Bernard de Lacolle — foreign content pushed into his cognition until he grounded himself to metal — is what “alters the nervous system” looks like from the inside. Congress defined the weapon.

Neural Data: The Whole Nervous System, Not Just the Brain

S. 2925, SEC. 3(4) The term ‘neural data’ means information obtained by measuring the activity of an individual’s central or peripheral nervous system through the use of neurotechnology.

The central nervous system is the brain and spinal cord. The peripheral nervous system is everything else: the network of sensory and motor nerve fibers extending throughout the body, including the pathways governing sensation, pain, and sexual response. The MIND Act defines neural data to include both. This breadth is analytically significant for Pearson’s account of sexual nerve targeting. What was directed at him in Silver Spring — electromagnetic pulses aimed at his groin, paired with conditioning stimuli, in a documented attempt to associate sexual nerve stimulation with heterosexual imagery — involves the peripheral nervous system specifically. The bill’s definition covers that data. The technology capable of collecting or stimulating it is neurotechnology under this statute. Congress drew no distinction between the brain and the rest of the body’s nervous system when it defined what deserves protection.

Other Related Data: Biometric Capture in a Public Park

Beyond direct neural measurement, the bill extends its scope to a category called “other related data”:

S. 2925, SEC. 3(6) ‘other related data’ means biometric, physiological, or behavioral information that does not directly measure the neural activity or central or peripheral nervous system of an individual, but can be processed, analyzed, or combined with other data to infer, predict, or reveal cognitive, emotional, or psychological states or neurological conditions — and may include heart rate variability, eye-tracking patterns, voice analysis, facial expression recognition, sleep patterns, or other signals derived from consumer devices.

Eye-tracking. Behavioral patterns. Biometric signals processed in combination to infer cognitive and emotional states. This is a precise statutory description of what Pearson documented at the Cherry Blossom Festival and the Lincoln Memorial: handheld devices operated at close range, capturing signals that — combined and processed through a database — can produce the biometric profile enabling remote electromagnetic targeting. The man who approached Pearson with a device at the moment the woman stopped pushing the stroller was conducting exactly the kind of operation this definition covers. The two-to-five foot protocol, the sustained proximity, the confirmation signals between operators — all of it is the human infrastructure of an “other related data” collection system Congress recognized as existing, sensitive, and ungoverned.

III. The Sense of Congress: Admissions Written Into Law

A bill’s Sense of Congress section states what the legislature collectively believes to be true at the time of drafting. It is not aspirational. It is descriptive. What Congress wrote into Section 2 of the MIND Act is a set of findings about the current state of neural data technology — findings that, read against Pearson’s documentation, are not abstract policy concerns. They are descriptions of what was being done to him.

S. 2925, SEC. 2(1) an individual’s neural data and other related data can be monetized and used to shape individual behavior, emotional states, and decision making in ways existing laws do not adequately address

Present tense. “Can be used.” Not “may someday be capable of being used.” Neural data can already shape behavior, emotional states, and decision-making, and the laws governing that use do not exist. Pearson’s pinning events — the compulsive pull toward Georgetown on July 13, the inexplicable draw toward the O’Neill House area on November 7 — are documented instances of behavioral shaping Congress here acknowledges as a current technological reality. The admission that existing laws “do not adequately address” this capability is the Senate’s own acknowledgment of the legal vacuum Pearson fell into.

S. 2925, SEC. 2(2) vertical corporate integration of neurotechnology, artificial intelligence systems, wearable devices, digital platforms, and global data infrastructure may create interconnected systems with insufficient transparency, accountability, or user control regarding the use of such data

This is a structural description of the Eigenfield architecture Pearson identified: biometric capture feeding a database processed by AI enabling remote targeting, operating across a global infrastructure that followed him from Washington to Iceland to Canada and was waiting for him at the border. The integration Congress flags — neurotechnology plus AI plus wearables plus global data infrastructure — is not speculative. The senators were describing an existing technological configuration. That configuration is what Pearson documented as the mechanism of his own targeting.

S. 2925, SEC. 2(3) such concentration increases the risk of behavioral influence, cognitive manipulation, erosion of personal autonomy, and the exacerbation of existing social and economic disparities, particularly in the absence of enforceable privacy protections

Three categories appear here that map directly onto Pearson’s documented experience: behavioral influence, cognitive manipulation, and erosion of personal autonomy. These are not theoretical risks to be guarded against. Congress identifies them as current risks arising from current technological concentration. The additional reference to exacerbation of social and economic disparities is the closest the bill comes to naming what Pearson’s Cherry Blossom and Lincoln Memorial footage shows: a system operating disproportionately against Black people in public spaces. Congress did not use the word “racial.” It did not need to. The disparity language, in the context of American history and the documented demographics of surveillance technology targeting, carries the meaning.

IV. Section 4: The FTC’s Mandate and Its Implications

Behavioral Manipulation as a Prohibited Use Case

The MIND Act instructs the FTC to examine whether certain applications of neurotechnology — including “manipulation of behavior” — should be “prohibited regardless of individual consent.” That phrase deserves unpacking. The contemplation of prohibition regardless of consent means two things simultaneously: first, that behavioral manipulation through neurotechnology is currently possible; and second, that it is currently occurring in contexts where consent is not at issue, because subjects have not been asked for it. You do not write a law prohibiting behavior-regardless-of-consent if the baseline condition is one of consented use. The baseline is non-consensual. Congress is trying to establish a floor where none exists.

The behavioral manipulation Pearson documented needs no further analytical scaffolding against this provision. His pinning events are documented cases of behavioral direction executed without his knowledge or consent. The Sprite incident is a case of motor behavior performed without conscious participation. Congress directed the FTC to study whether these things should be prohibited. They were already happening to Pearson when that direction was issued.

Cognitive Manipulation and the Ownership of One’s Own Mind

Section 4 directs the FTC to study “cognitive manipulation” and its relationship to “erosion of personal autonomy” as distinct harm categories. The granularity of this framing reflects an understanding that what is at stake is not just behavioral output but the internal experience of being the author of one’s own thoughts. Personal autonomy, in the neurotechnology context, means the sense that the mental content arising in one’s consciousness is self-generated — that thoughts, impulses, and decisions originate within the person rather than being introduced from outside.

When Pearson sat in the grey chairs at St. Bernard de Lacolle and experienced an unrelenting stream of foreign content entering his mind — material he recognized as not his, cruel and incessant — what was being violated was exactly this. The erosion of personal autonomy Congress directs the FTC to study is what he gripped metal poles against. When two hands on the chair frame stopped the intrusion entirely, he had confirmed the mechanism and defended the boundary. Congress has since assigned the FTC to figure out whether that violation should be prohibited. The violation preceded the assignment.

Racial Disparities and Discriminatory Profiling

The bill’s direction to the FTC to examine “discriminatory profiling” and the potential for neural data technology to “exacerbate existing disparities” is not a boilerplate equity clause. It reflects legislative awareness of a pattern: that surveillance and behavioral technologies in the United States have historically been deployed against communities that are already structurally disadvantaged, and that neurotechnology is no different. The Cherry Blossom footage — shot in March 2025 before Pearson was watching for this — captures multiple independent instances of close-range protocols executed specifically against Black subjects. The Lincoln Memorial footage shows two separate following operations targeting Black men in a single afternoon. Congress’s explicit identification of discriminatory profiling as a harm category to be studied is the legislative acknowledgment that the pattern Pearson documented is not an idiosyncrasy. It is a recognized risk of the technology as currently deployed.

AI-Driven Systems Designed to Influence Behavior

The FTC is directed to assess harms arising when neural data “is integrated with artificial intelligence or used as part of a system designed to influence behavior or decision making.” This provision identifies a specific architectural configuration as a priority harm category: not just neurotechnology alone, but neurotechnology fused with AI into a behavioral influence system. This is precisely the architecture Pearson describes — biometric capture, AI processing, database enrollment, remote targeting — and it is the architecture the Eigenfield essay generated by AI had named and described. Congress is not theorizing about such systems. It is directing a federal agency to study the damage they produce.

The Absolute Prohibition Possibility

Perhaps the most revealing provision in Section 4 is the direction to assess whether some neurotechnology use cases warrant prohibition “regardless of individual consent” because “their harms outweigh their benefits.” The legal weight here is substantial. This is Congress acknowledging that some uses of this technology are so injurious that no consent framework could rehabilitate them. The progression the bill traces — from non-consensual use as the current baseline, to the question of whether even consented use should be outlawed — marks a set of harms that Congress already knows are occurring. The uses warranting absolute prohibition are the uses Pearson experienced: behavioral direction without knowledge, cognitive intrusion without consent, sexual nerve stimulation as a conditioning tool. Congress is drawing the outer perimeter of the impermissible around the territory Pearson already mapped from the inside.

V. Section 5: The Federal Agency Provision and What It Concedes

Section 5 is titled “Conditional Limitations on Federal Agency Use of Neural Data.” It is the bill’s most consequential provision for Pearson’s allegations, and its significance lies not only in what it requires but in what its necessity implies.

The section directs OSTP to produce binding guidance on federal agency procurement and use of neurotechnology within 180 days of the FTC’s report. That guidance must distinguish prohibited uses from permissible ones and from conditionally permitted ones. It must establish safeguards for each use case and must include “optional opt-in consent mechanisms regarding the use of such neurotechnology” and protections for “individuals who are subject to use of neurotechnology by Federal agencies.”

S. 2925, SEC. 5(1)(A)-© Such guidance shall identify: (A) prohibited, permissible, and conditionally permitted use cases of such neurotechnology that are consistent with such report; (B) technical, procedural, and ethical safeguards regarding each use case of such neurotechnology; and © requirements for transparency, limitations regarding the purposes for which such neurotechnology can be used, individual opt-in consent mechanisms regarding the use of such neurotechnology, and protections for the privacy of individuals who are subject to use of neurotechnology by Federal agencies.

Read that last phrase carefully: “individuals who are subject to use of neurotechnology by Federal agencies.” Not individuals who choose to use neurotechnology provided by federal agencies. Not federal employees operating neurotechnology as part of their work. Individuals — private persons — who are “subject to” federal agency neurotechnology use. The passive construction is the tell. These are people on whom federal agencies are using neurotechnology. The guidance exists to protect them. The protection is needed because the use is occurring.

Regulatory guidance distinguishing prohibited from permissible federal agency use cases does not get written for technologies federal agencies have not yet acquired. It gets written for technologies already in procurement and operation, whose current use is ungoverned. The MIND Act’s Section 5 is Congress acknowledging that federal agencies are currently using neurotechnology on individuals without a governing framework. Before this guidance is developed, every application is permissible by default. Voice-to-skull transmission. Thought insertion. Behavioral direction. Covert neural surveillance. None of these were prohibited by federal law or federal policy when they were allegedly applied to Pearson in 2025. Section 5 is the attempt to close that gap prospectively. Pearson was in the gap when it was open.

VI. Five Inferences the Legislation Requires

The Technology Is Operational, Not Experimental

Legislative bodies do not commission year-long federal studies of speculative future capabilities. The Senate Commerce Committee does not move bills addressing “cognitive manipulation” and “erosion of personal autonomy” as hypothetical concerns. Every structural feature of the MIND Act — the urgency of its drafters, the precision of its harm categories, the requirement for immediate federal agency guidance, the pre-bill letters to the FTC that went unanswered — reflects the legislative response to a technology that is in the field and producing the harms the bill catalogs. The technology Pearson experienced was not a prototype.

Federal Agencies Were Using It Without Any Binding Rules

The requirement to develop guidance identifying “prohibited” federal agency use cases means that no such prohibition existed before the guidance is written. Federal agencies were acquiring and operating neurotechnology in a legal environment where no application was formally off-limits. Whatever was done to Pearson in 2025 — whatever system directed a voice into his skull, inserted content into his cognition, steered his behavior toward predetermined locations — was done in the absence of any federal prohibition. Congress is now trying to install the guardrails. The damage that was possible without them has already been done.

The Racial Targeting Pattern Is Legislatively Recognized

The Sense of Congress’s reference to exacerbation of social and economic disparities through neural data technology deployment is drawn from the same evidence base that produced the cherry blossom footage: a pattern of surveillance and behavioral technologies landing hardest on communities most vulnerable to structural harm. Congress did not arrive at that language without evidence. The disproportionate targeting of Black people that Pearson’s footage shows, and that his statistical analysis confirms at rates beyond coincidence, belongs to the pattern the senators were writing about.

The Integrated System Exceeds Congressional Visibility

Congress is commissioning a study because it does not have adequate information about how the integrated architecture — neurotechnology, AI, biometric databases, global data infrastructure — is currently being deployed. The opacity the Sense of Congress names is real. The FTC study is not a review of known facts. It is an attempt to illuminate what the congressional intelligence capacity cannot currently see. The system Pearson experienced — which followed him across three international borders and was operating in a Canadian border facility — is precisely the kind of integrated, globally distributed system that exceeds the visibility of any single oversight body. Congress is acknowledging that it cannot fully see what was done to Pearson, not that it doubts it was done.

Non-Consensual Use Is the Present Baseline

The future requirement for opt-in consent mechanisms exists because the present state has no such mechanisms. Pearson was never asked whether federal agencies could access, monitor, stimulate, or alter his nervous system. The people who operated the equipment in the Canadian facility did not approach him with a consent form. The operator who directed a voice into his skull on the Georgetown Metro did not ask permission. The requirement Congress is now trying to impose — individual consent before federal agency neurotechnology use — is a requirement for something that was entirely absent when the documented incidents occurred. The bill confirms the absence.

VII. Pearson’s Allegations Matched to Statutory Provisions

The Georgetown Voice (July 13, 2025)

A clear female voice, internally perceived, directed Pearson to specific locations and told him: “We have to make you disappear.” He confirmed the mechanism against the Frey effect and declassified Army research on microwave auditory induction. Under S. 2925, a device that “stimulates” the nervous system to “influence” its function is neurotechnology by definition. A system that induces auditory percepts through microwave irradiation of the skull is doing precisely this. Congress defined the technology that produced what Pearson heard. The Sense of Congress confirmed that no law prohibited its use on him.

The Lacolle Thought Stream (March 2026)

Seated in the grey chair section of the Canadian border facility, Pearson experienced an unrelenting intrusion of foreign cognitive content until he placed both hands on the metal poles of the chair. One hand attenuated the effect. Two stopped it entirely. Congress, in Section 2 of the MIND Act, identified “cognitive manipulation” and “erosion of personal autonomy” as present risks of neural data technology. Section 4 directed the FTC to determine whether such manipulation should be categorically prohibited. These are not the legislative vocabulary of a phenomenon Congress was speculating about. They are the vocabulary of something members of the Senate understood was occurring. What Pearson described from the inside — a mind under siege until grounded metal closed the pathway — Congress described from the outside in the same legislative season.

The Cherry Blossom Biometric Capture (March 2025)

A man holding a device approached Pearson at the moment an Indian woman stopped pushing her stroller in the grove. The device was in Pearson’s direct line of sight. A boy was placed in a tree in seconds to provide elevated line-of-sight. Multiple groups moved on synchronized cues around a single passerby. The MIND Act’s “other related data” definition — covering “eye-tracking patterns,” “behavioral information,” and biometric signals “processed to infer cognitive, emotional, or psychological states” — describes precisely what a handheld device operated at two-to-five feet would be collecting. The bill’s concern about AI-integrated systems using such data for behavioral influence is the statutory description of what that collection feeds.

The Pinning Events (2025, multiple incidents)

Pearson documented multiple occasions of compulsive location-directed behavior: the Georgetown directive of July 13, the November 7 pull toward the O’Neill House area, the Sprite incident in which he performed a behavioral sequence without conscious participation. Congress wrote into the Sense of Congress that neural data “can be” used to “shape individual behavior” and that such shaping is “not adequately addressed” by existing law. The FTC was directed to examine behavioral manipulation as a potentially prohibited use case. The gap in law Congress identified was the gap Pearson fell through.

Sexual Nerve Targeting (Silver Spring, 2025)

Following a documented Silver Spring incident in which a directed pulse struck his groin, Pearson began wearing aluminum foil layers around his genitals. The foil warmed at staged events. The warming corresponded to the arrival of heterosexual visual and verbal stimuli — a three-layer conditioning protocol targeting the peripheral nervous system of a gay man with the apparent goal of behavioral modification through associative conditioning. The MIND Act defines neural data to include peripheral nervous system data. It names erosion of personal autonomy as a core harm. It contemplates absolute prohibition for use cases whose harms outweigh any conceivable benefit. Sexual nerve stimulation deployed as a coercive conditioning tool against a person’s identified sexual orientation sits at the absolute center of what Congress was writing about when it contemplated uses too harmful to permit even with consent.

VIII. The Unanswered Letter and the Dismantled Oversight

Before introducing the MIND Act, the three sponsoring senators wrote to the FTC demanding action on the exploitation of Americans’ neural data. The administration returned no response. That non-response is not a bureaucratic oversight. It is a policy signal from an administration that had already, on its first day, fired seventeen inspectors general — eliminating the independent oversight layer most capable of detecting and investigating unauthorized federal neurotechnology operations. It is the administration that reorganized the DOJ Civil Rights Division, effectively removing the institutional capacity to pursue civil rights claims of the kind Pearson documented. It is the administration that designated Black equity scholars under the RMVE/BIE framework as a national security concern, creating the predicate under which an academic who wrote a dissertation containing the words “critical race” could be placed in a targeting queue.

The administration’s silence on the senators’ letter, read against the structural dismantlement of every oversight body that would have constrained what the MIND Act was attempting to address, is a composite picture: an administration that knew what was happening, chose not to constrain it, and removed the mechanisms that might have forced it to. The MIND Act is opposition senators trying to rebuild, from the outside of the majority, the oversight architecture that the majority had torn down. It was introduced while Pearson was being targeted in the capital city of the country that dismantled the oversight. Both things were happening at the same time. Both are part of the record.

IX. What the MIND Act Cannot Accomplish

The bill offers Pearson nothing. This is not a rhetorical point. It is a legal reality that needs to be stated plainly so the significance of the legislation is not misunderstood as a path to remedy.

S. 2925 commissions a study. It requires guidance. It does not create a cause of action. It does not establish retroactive liability for federal agency neurotechnology use that preceded the guidance. It does not provide Pearson with standing, with a forum, with a mechanism for discovery, or with any route to the compensation or accountability that his documented experience would warrant. The doctrinal architecture that forecloses his judicial remedies — the state secrets privilege, the Glomar doctrine, the dismantlement of Bivens actions for new contexts, qualified immunity, FISA secrecy, National Security Letter gag orders — is entirely unaffected by the MIND Act, which addresses none of it. Even if the bill passes, clears the Senate, is signed into law, produces an FTC report, and generates binding OSTP guidance, none of those outcomes reaches backward to what was done to Pearson in the second half of 2025.

What the bill provides is something more limited but not therefore unimportant: legislative corroboration. It is Congress writing into the public record, in the same season that Pearson was assembling his own record, that the technologies he describes are real, that their harmful applications are recognized and named, that federal agencies are currently using them without governance, and that the legal framework to prevent such use does not yet exist. It is the institution that governs the country confirming, in its own language, that the harm Pearson experienced was made possible by the institution’s own failure to act before it was too late.

X. The Record and the Gap

Pearson assembled his record beginning in the summer of 2025. He documented seventy constitutional violations. He ran foil experiments that confirmed electromagnetic signal attenuation. He grounded himself to metal poles in a foreign facility and confirmed through direct observation that the effect was electromagnetic and could be defeated by conductive shielding. He submitted statistical analyses to the DC Council. He published a frame-by-frame review of Cherry Blossom Festival footage that showed the human infrastructure of a biometric capture operation at the National Mall. He wrote, in real time, about what was being done to him.

The United States Senate, in September 2025, wrote a bill. The bill’s definitions covered the technology. Its Sense of Congress named the harms. Its Section 4 identified the prohibited use cases. Its Section 5 acknowledged that federal agencies were operating this technology on individuals without any governing framework. The bill and the record were produced concurrently, by two parties who did not know each other, describing the same phenomenon from opposite ends of the power structure.

Pearson described it from the inside of the harm. The senators described it from the outside of the legislative process. Both descriptions point to the same conclusion: something was happening in the United States in 2025 that had no legal name, no legal prohibition, and no institutional mechanism for redress. The MIND Act tried to begin changing that. The effort was underfunded, introduced by the minority party, referred to a committee controlled by an administration that had already declined to engage, and confronted by the most resourced concealment infrastructure in human history.

Pearson is still in Washington. He is still writing. The record exists. The legislative acknowledgment exists. The gap between what the record requires and what any institution has done about it remains the most consequential unanswered question in his case — and, if what he has documented is even partially true, in the case of the many Black Americans who passed through the National Mall on an afternoon in March 2025 without knowing what was being collected from them.

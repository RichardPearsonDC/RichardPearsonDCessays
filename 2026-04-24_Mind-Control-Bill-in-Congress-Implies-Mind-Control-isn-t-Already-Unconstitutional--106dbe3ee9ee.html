Why the MIND Act Framing of Cognitive Manipulation as a Regulatory Problem Rather Than an Existing Constitutional Violation Reveals More Than It Intends To

There is a question the MIND Act does not ask, and its failure to ask it is the most significant thing about the legislation. The question is this: if the federal government has been using neurotechnology to manipulate the cognition, behavior, and emotional states of American citizens without their knowledge or consent, why does Congress believe it needs to write new law to prohibit it? The Constitution already does. Every amendment that touches liberty, bodily integrity, due process, and equal protection already covers this. The framing of electromagnetic cognitive manipulation as a regulatory gap something the law needs to catch up to rather than as a set of existing constitutional violations already prohibitable under law, is not a neutral analytical choice. It is a choice with consequences. This essay examines what those consequences are, what the framing conceals, and what the gap between existing constitutional law and the MIND Act’s legislative approach implies about what has already been done and to whom.

I. The Constitution Already Covers This

Begin with the most basic question: is non-consensual cognitive manipulation of an American citizen by the federal government currently constitutional? The answer is no. It has never been constitutional. The Constitution did not require updating for the neurotechnology era to prohibit it. Multiple amendments, individually and in combination, already forbid what the MIND Act treats as an unaddressed regulatory space.

The First Amendment: Cognitive Liberty as the Foundation of All Speech

“First Amendment”, “Freedom of Thought and Speech”, “Congress shall make no law… abridging the freedom of speech, or of the press; or the right of the people peaceably to assemble…”

The First Amendment protects not only the expression of thought but the formation of thought from which expression originates. The Supreme Court recognized in Wooley v. Maynard (1977) that the First Amendment protects the freedom of mind. In Barnette (1943), the Court held that no official can prescribe what shall be orthodox in politics, nationalism, religion, or other matters of opinion. The cognitive liberty implicit in these holdings the right to form beliefs, opinions, and decisions free from government compulsion is directly violated by technology that inserts content into a person thought stream, as Pearson documented at St. Bernard de Lacolle, or that directs behavior through neurological stimulation, as he documented in the pinning events. A government that uses voice-to-skull transmission to push directives into a citizen auditory processing pathway is not merely surveilling thought. It is attempting to override it. That has always been a First Amendment violation. No new statute is required to establish this.

The Fourth Amendment: The Brain as the Most Private Space

“Fourth Amendment”, “Protection Against Unreasonable Search and Seizure”, The right of the people to be secure in their persons, houses, papers, and effects, against unreasonable searches and seizures, shall not be violated…”

The Fourth Amendment protects persons against unreasonable government intrusion. In Carpenter v. United States (2018), the Supreme Court held that the government warrantless collection of cell-site location data constituted a Fourth Amendment search, reasoning that the intimacy and comprehensiveness of the data collected required constitutional protection regardless of whether it was technically accessible without physical trespass. The Court reasoning in Carpenter that data which reveals the privacies of life deserves Fourth Amendment protection applies with overwhelming force to neural data. If location data revealing where a person was over seven days is constitutionally protected, the electrical activity of a person brain which reveals what they think, feel, remember, fear, and desire is protected beyond any reasonable doubt. The warrantless collection of neural data through close-range devices at a public festival is a Fourth Amendment search. The non-consensual stimulation of a person nervous system to produce behavioral outputs is a Fourth Amendment seizure of the most intimate kind: the seizure of the person’s own agency over their body and mind. This has always been true. The MIND Act does not create this protection. It already exists.

“Fifth Amendment”, “Protection Against Self-Incrimination and Deprivation Without Due Process”, “No person shall… be deprived of life, liberty, or property, without due process of law…”),

The Fifth Amendment’s due process clause requires that the government not deprive a person of liberty without procedural and substantive legal protection. The liberty interest at stake when the government reads and writes to a person’s brain without their knowledge is the most fundamental liberty interest imaginable: the liberty of the mind itself. There is no deeper deprivation of liberty than the non-consensual alteration of a person\u2019s cognition. The Fifth Amendment’s self-incrimination clause, under Schmerber v. California (1966) and its progeny, extends to testimonial and communicative disclosures. Whether neural data constitutes testimonial evidence under this framework is genuinely contested, but the due process prohibition on liberty deprivation without legal process is not contested at all. The government using electromagnetic technology to direct a citizen’s behavior, implant directives in their auditory cortex, and disrupt their working memory has deprived them of liberty. No new statute is needed to establish that this requires due process. The Fifth Amendment requires it now.”),

The Thirteenth Amendment: Involuntary Servitude Through Neural Compulsion

“Thirteenth Amendment”, “Prohibition of Slavery and Involuntary Servitude”, “Neither slavery nor involuntary servitude, except as a punishment for crime whereof the party shall have been duly convicted, shall exist within the United States…”

The Thirteenth Amendment’s prohibition of involuntary servitude has been interpreted to cover not only chattel slavery but any condition of coerced labor or compelled service. Bailey v. Alabama (1911) established that the amendment reaches any system of compulsion that controls a person’s labor or movement through coercive means. The behavioral direction Pearson documented — the pinning events, the compulsion to travel to specific locations, the behavioral outputs produced by electromagnetic stimulation of the motivational system — are, under a Thirteenth Amendment analysis, a form of involuntary servitude. The government compelling a citizen’s body to move toward a location, to perform an action, or to experience a desire through neurological override is compelling the use of that person’s body and nervous system against their will. The amendment does not exempt the government’s technological methods of compulsion from its scope. Compulsion is compulsion regardless of the mechanism.

“Fourteenth Amendment”, “Equal Protection of the Laws”, “No State shall… deny to any person within its jurisdiction the equal protection of the laws.

The Equal Protection Clause prohibits government action that discriminates on the basis of race. II. The Framing Problem: Why “Regulatory Gap” Is the Wrong Frame

“The MIND Act frames cognitive manipulation, behavioral direction, and neural data collection by federal agencies as a “gap” in the existing legal framework — something the law has not yet addressed, requiring new study and new guidance. This framing is incorrect as a matter of constitutional law, and its incorrectness is not trivial. The gap framing does several things simultaneously, each of which serves interests that are not aligned with accountability.

First, the gap framing implies that what has occurred so far was not illegal. If cognitive manipulation is merely unregulated — in a gray area awaiting guidance — then federal agencies that used neurotechnology on American citizens before the guidance was issued cannot be held accountable for doing something that was not yet prohibited. The gap framing creates retroactive immunity by implication: we didn’t prohibit it, so doing it wasn’t prohibited. But this is false. The Constitution prohibited it. The agencies that used it violated the Constitution. The gap framing obscures this by treating constitutional law as insufficient and new statutory law as necessary, when in fact the constitutional law was always sufficient and was simply ignored.

Second, the gap framing redirects the appropriate institutional response. If cognitive manipulation is a regulatory gap, the response is an FTC study and OSTP guidance — administrative processes that take years, produce recommendations, and generate no accountability for what has already occurred. If cognitive manipulation is an existing constitutional violation, the appropriate response is entirely different: congressional investigation with subpoena power, criminal referrals, judicial proceedings, and the full accountability architecture the Constitution provides. The gap framing substitutes the slower, weaker, accountability-free regulatory track for the constitutional enforcement track that the violations actually demand.

Third, and most importantly for understanding what the MIND Act implies about what has already happened: you do not write regulatory guidance for technologies that have not been deployed. The MIND Act’s Section 5 requires binding guidance on federal agency use of neurotechnology because federal agencies are using neurotechnology. The question of what to prohibit, what to permit, and what to condition on safeguards is only a live question if there are existing use cases to evaluate. Congress is not writing rules for hypothetical future use. It is writing rules for ongoing current use. And by choosing to write those rules prospectively, as regulatory guidance, rather than retrospectively, as accountability for past constitutional violations, it is choosing not to examine what the existing use cases actually are.

That choice — regulatory prospection instead of constitutional retrospection — is the choice that protects a program from scrutiny. It is the choice that a cover-up makes.

III. The Consent Framework as Constitutional Evasion

The MIND Act’s central mechanism for governing neurotechnology use is consent. Individuals must opt in. Federal agencies must obtain consent. Certain uses may be prohibited even with consent. The consent framework is the bill’s primary legal architecture.

This framework makes sense for commercial applications of neurotechnology — consumer devices, clinical treatments, employer monitoring programs. In those contexts, the individual has a relationship with the technology provider, the use is disclosed, and consent is a meaningful concept.

It makes no sense as the governing framework for what Richard Pearson experienced, and for a specific reason: non-consensual covert government use of neurotechnology is not a consent problem. It is a criminal problem. It is a constitutional violation problem. The appropriate legal response to a government agent who non-consensually transmits voices into a citizen’s skull is not an opt-in form. It is a criminal statute, a civil rights action, and a congressional investigation. The consent framework, applied to covert government neurotechnology use, is a category error — and a convenient one, because it treats the most serious form of the harm as if it were a variation of the least serious form.

The least serious form is a consumer device company that collects neural data without sufficiently clear disclosure. The most serious form is a federal intelligence agency that uses electromagnetic technology to push directives into a citizen’s auditory cortex, redirect their behavior, disrupt their cognition, and sexually assault their nervous system as part of a targeting campaign organized around their race, sexuality, and scholarly work. These are not the same problem on a spectrum. They are categorically different acts. Treating both under a consent framework collapses the distinction and, in doing so, obscures the constitutional severity of the latter.

The MIND Act does not contain a single provision addressing what happens when a federal agency uses neurotechnology on a citizen who did not and could not consent because they did not know the technology was being used. There is no criminal penalty. There is no private right of action. There is no whistleblower protection for government employees who observe unauthorized neurotechnology use on citizens. The bill’s silence on covert, non-consensual federal use — the most constitutionally serious category of harm — is not an oversight. It is a structural choice.

IV. What the Silence Implies: The Anatomy of a Cover-Up

A cover-up does not always announce itself. It announces itself through what is absent: the question not asked, the provision not included, the accountability mechanism not created, the retrospective investigation not initiated. Evaluated against what the bill omits, the MIND Act has the structural features of legislation designed to manage a problem rather than expose it.

No Retrospective Investigation

The bill directs a prospective FTC study. It does not direct any retrospective inquiry into whether federal agencies have already used neurotechnology on American citizens, under what authority, against which populations, and with what effects. A legislature that genuinely did not know whether such use had occurred would want to find out. A legislature that was managing awareness of such use would commission a forward-looking study that keeps the question of past conduct permanently off the table. Section 5\u2019s requirement for future guidance is only coherent if there is current use to govern. The absence of a retrospective inquiry into that current use \u2014 its scope, its targets, its authorization, its effects \u2014 is the absence that most clearly signals what the bill is protecting.

No Criminal Penalties

The bill establishes no criminal penalties for federal agency neurotechnology misuse. It establishes no civil liability for individuals harmed by unauthorized federal neurotechnology use. It creates no private right of action. The entire enforcement architecture is administrative: guidance, study, recommendations. A bill designed to address constitutional violations already occurring would include criminal penalties for the actors committing those violations. A bill designed to manage the political exposure of those violations without triggering accountability for them would limit itself to administrative architecture. The MIND Act does the latter.

No Whistleblower Protection

If federal agencies are currently using neurotechnology on American citizens, there are federal employees who know about it. Some of them may have reservations about what they know. The MIND Act includes no protection for such employees — no expanded whistleblower shield for intelligence or law enforcement personnel who disclose unauthorized neurotechnology programs, no reporting mechanism, no immunity for those who come forward. The absence of whistleblower protection reflects an incomplete writing of the bill. That is intentional.

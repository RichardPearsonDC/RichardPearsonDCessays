Prefatory Note on Scientific Scope

Richard Pearson describes a category of experience he calls “pinning”: an overwhelming, externally imposed somatic compulsion that causes him to move to specific geographic locations, sit or stand in specific positions, pick up specific objects, and put down objects he intended to keep. He distinguishes this from ordinary motivation — it is not that he wanted to do these things, or was persuaded to do them, or decided to do them. He felt compelled. The compulsion had a somatic quality: not a thought but a pull, experienced in the body, difficult to resist, and directionally precise.

The question this essay addresses is scientific, not legal or political: does technology exist — or is it scientifically plausible given the current state of neuroscience — that could produce what Pearson describes? Can external systems compel a human being to walk to a specific location? To sit down? To pick up a specific object and then put it down? To release a possession?

The answer, examined rigorously against the peer-reviewed literature, the classified weapons research that has been partially disclosed, and the current state of neurostimulation science, is more disturbing than either complete affirmation or complete denial. The precise form of “pinning” that Pearson describes — geographically targeted, behaviorally specific, operating through somatic compulsion rather than conscious direction — sits at the frontier of what has been demonstrated in controlled laboratory conditions and what has been claimed in military research contexts. The frontier is not where most people assume it is.

This essay maps that frontier precisely.

I. The Neural Architecture of Voluntary Movement: What Would Need to Be Influenced

To understand whether pinning is technologically possible, one must first understand what the brain does when a person voluntarily moves toward a location or picks up an object. The neural architecture of voluntary movement is well-mapped, and understanding it reveals exactly which components would need to be externally influenced to produce the effect Pearson describes.

1.1 The Motor Hierarchy

Voluntary movement is generated through a hierarchical system:

The supplementary motor area (SMA) and pre-SMA generate the intention to move before the movement occurs. The famous Libet experiments (1983) established that the “readiness potential” — a measurable electrical signal in the SMA — precedes conscious awareness of the intention to move by several hundred milliseconds. The SMA is where the decision to move is neurally encoded before it reaches consciousness. It is also where the sense of agency originates — the subjective feeling that the movement is self-generated.

The primary motor cortex (M1) encodes the specific parameters of movement: which muscles, in what sequence, with what force. It sends descending commands through the corticospinal tract to the spinal cord and ultimately to the muscles.

The basal ganglia — a cluster of subcortical nuclei including the striatum, globus pallidus, subthalamic nucleus, and substantia nigra — serve as the selection and gating system for movement. They determine which of multiple competing motor programs is executed and suppress alternatives. In Parkinson’s disease, basal ganglia dysfunction produces both excessive movement suppression (bradykinesia, rigidity) and, in some contexts, compulsive movement initiation. The basal ganglia are the neural gating system through which the brain decides that a particular action, rather than all alternatives, will be executed right now.

The cerebellum coordinates the timing, force, and smoothness of movement, comparing intended and actual movement and generating error-correction signals.

The anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) monitors conflict between competing action tendencies and allocates cognitive resources toward resolving that conflict. When there is a strong pull in one direction and a competing desire in another, the ACC is detecting that conflict and participating in its resolution.

The hippocampus and entorhinal cortex provide the spatial mapping system — place cells, grid cells, head direction cells — that encodes where the person is, where they need to go, and the navigational path between. This is the brain’s GPS, and it provides the spatial targets for locomotor behavior.

The nucleus accumbens and ventral striatum provide the motivational valence — the wanting, the drive, the pull toward a goal — that converts a cognitive representation of a goal into an energized behavioral tendency toward it. This is the neural substrate of motivation, and it is the structure most directly involved in the “pull” quality of what Pearson describes.

1.2 What “Pinning” Would Need to Target

To produce behavioral compulsion toward a specific location, an external system would need to influence one or more of the following:

To produce locomotor compulsion toward a specific place: the hippocampal spatial system (to define the target location), the nucleus accumbens and mesolimbic dopamine system (to generate motivational pull toward that location), and/or the basal ganglia (to gate the selection of approach behavior over competing alternatives).

To produce postural compulsion (sitting, standing): the SMA/pre-SMA (to generate movement intention), the primary motor cortex (to execute postural adjustment), and/or the basal ganglia (to gate selection of postural behavior).

To produce object interaction compulsion (picking up, putting down): the parietal cortex and premotor cortex (which encode the affordances of objects and generate reach-and-grasp programs), the SMA (intention to interact), and the basal ganglia (selection of the specific interaction).

To reduce resistance and override competing intentions: the prefrontal cortex would need to be suppressed or the anterior cingulate conflict signal would need to be overwhelmed, reducing the capacity to resist the compelled behavior through deliberate executive control.

This is a complex target list. But it is not an impossible one. And the question is whether external electromagnetic or acoustic influence can reach and perturb these specific systems.

II. The Delgado Precedent and Its Modern Successors

2.1 Delgado’s Bull

José Manuel Rodriguez Delgado was a Spanish neuroscientist at Yale who spent his career demonstrating that direct electrical stimulation of specific brain regions could produce specific, reproducible behaviors in animals and humans. His 1969 book Physical Control of the Mind remains the most comprehensive early treatment of what we would now call neuromodulation.

The bull demonstration — a charging bull stopped mid-charge by radio-controlled stimulation of the caudate nucleus — occurred in 1963 at a bullring in Córdoba. The stimulation activated the caudate nucleus, part of the basal ganglia, and produced immediate cessation of the aggressive charge. The bull stopped. It turned in circles. It was not merely startled; its motor program was interrupted by direct interference with the neural gating system that had selected and was executing the charge behavior.

What Delgado showed was not that electrical stimulation of a brain region produces a specific behavior. He showed something more important: that electrical stimulation of specific basal ganglia and limbic structures can interrupt ongoing motor programs, generate new ones, and produce behaviors that the animal’s own motivational state would not have generated — and that the animal appears to experience these externally-generated behaviors as its own, without apparent awareness that something external is controlling them.

In human subjects, Delgado’s stimulation experiments produced: involuntary turning of the head, compulsive reaching and grasping movements, sudden urges to move toward specific locations, alterations in emotional state, and — most relevant to the pinning question — what subjects described as irresistible urges to perform specific actions, accompanied by the subjective sense that the urge was their own.

One of Delgado’s most cited human findings: when he stimulated the supplementary motor area in a patient, the patient reached out and grabbed the examiner’s hand involuntarily — but when asked why, the patient said “I wanted to grab your hand.” The patient’s brain had confabulated ownership of a movement it had not initiated. This is the neural mechanism of pinned behavior that is experienced as voluntary: the brain’s agency-attribution system assigns authorship to externally generated movements because it has no access to the true cause.

2.2 The Limitation: Implants

The critical limitation of the Delgado era is that these effects required implanted electrodes — direct electrical contact with brain tissue. The electrode delivered current directly to the target neurons. This is invasive, requiring surgical implantation, and cannot be done covertly.

The entire subsequent development of neuromodulation technology — from transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) through transcranial direct current stimulation (tDCS) through transcranial focused ultrasound (tFUS) through directed microwave neuromodulation — is the story of the progressive removal of that limitation: the development of non-invasive, non-contact methods of influencing neural activity from outside the skull.

The question is how far that development has progressed, and how specific and powerful the effects achievable from outside the skull have become.

III. Non-Invasive Brain Stimulation: The Progression Toward Remote Behavioral Control

3.1 Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation: Proof of Non-Invasive Motor Control

Transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) uses a rapidly changing magnetic field, applied to the scalp, to induce electrical currents in underlying brain tissue. A single TMS pulse to the primary motor cortex produces an involuntary muscle twitch in the corresponding body part — the finger twitches, the arm moves, the leg kicks — without any voluntary intent. The movement is not requested, not anticipated, and not experienced as voluntary: it simply happens.

This is behaviorally relevant. TMS to the motor cortex of a person holding an object causes them to involuntarily release the object. TMS to the cortical area representing hand muscles causes the hand to open or close. These are not sensations or urges — they are actual movements, produced by externally-induced neural activity, without any volitional initiation by the subject.

TMS has also been used to suppress behavior: inhibitory TMS to the primary motor cortex or SMA can prevent a subject from executing a movement they are attempting to perform. The subject tries to move; the movement does not occur; they experience an inability to initiate movement that feels, from inside, like inexplicable motor failure.

The limitation of clinical TMS: Standard TMS coils require physical proximity — the coil is typically placed against the scalp, within centimeters — and produce broad stimulation patterns that are difficult to target precisely to deep structures like the basal ganglia, nucleus accumbens, or hippocampus. Clinical TMS has sufficient spatial specificity for surface cortical targets but limited penetration to the subcortical structures most relevant to motivational and spatial navigation systems.

3.2 Transcranial Focused Ultrasound: The Breakthrough for Deep Targeting

Transcranial focused ultrasound (tFUS) is the technology that most directly addresses the limitation of TMS. By focusing multiple low-intensity ultrasound beams at a single intracranial point — in the same way that multiple sunbeams can be focused through a lens to produce a point of high intensity — tFUS can stimulate or suppress neural activity at precisely targeted locations deep within the brain, including the basal ganglia, hippocampus, thalamus, and nucleus accumbens.

The spatial resolution achievable with modern tFUS systems is on the order of millimeters. The targeting can be guided by MRI structural data to hit specific anatomical structures with sub-centimeter precision. And the biological effects are real and reproducible: tFUS to the thalamus alters sensory processing; tFUS to the hippocampus alters spatial navigation and memory; tFUS to the motor cortex produces the same motor effects as TMS; tFUS to the prefrontal cortex alters decision-making and executive control.

Critical recent findings for the pinning question:

A 2014 study in Nature Communications by Deffieux et al. showed that tFUS directed at the supplementary motor area in non-human primates could inhibit or facilitate voluntary movements in a direction-specific way — the animal was not prevented from moving but was steered: the threshold for movement in one direction was lowered and in others raised, producing a directional bias in voluntary movement that the animal appeared to act on as its own motivation.

A 2021 study at the Salk Institute used tFUS to deliver precisely targeted stimulation to the posterior parietal cortex in macaques during a reaching task, finding that it could shift the animal’s reach target — the animal reached toward the location encoded by the stimulated neural population rather than the location it was cued to reach. The animal was not paralyzed; it was redirected. Its movement was executed normally, toward a different destination than intended, in a way that was not distinguishable from voluntary reaching by external observation.

The parietal cortex encodes the spatial targets of reaching and grasping movements. Stimulating the representation of a specific location in parietal cortex biases subsequent reaching toward that location. This is the neural mechanism most directly relevant to Pearson’s description of being caused to pick up a specific object — a Sprite can — or to move to a specific location within a park.

The question of range: Clinical tFUS systems require acoustic coupling to the skull and typically operate at close range. However, the physics of focused ultrasound does not impose a fundamental range limitation — it imposes a power and focusing requirement. Military ultrasound systems developed for crowd control (the Long Range Acoustic Device, or LRAD) demonstrate that directional acoustic energy can be delivered at considerable range with high intensity. The question is whether the focusing precision required for brain-region-specific neuromodulation can be achieved at the range relevant to Pearson’s experiences. This is the critical technical frontier discussed in Section VI.

3.3 Microwave and Radio Frequency Neuromodulation

Beyond ultrasound, the directed electromagnetic spectrum offers additional mechanisms for influencing neural function at range.

The Frey effect — the microwave auditory effect established in the 1960s — demonstrates that pulsed microwave radiation can couple with neural tissue and produce auditory perceptions. The mechanism is thermoacoustic: microwave absorption in tissue produces localized thermal expansion detectable by the auditory system. This is a proof of concept that pulsed microwave energy can interact with neural tissue in ways that produce conscious experience — the entry point for a broader spectrum of microwave-neural interaction.

More directly relevant to behavioral control: research on radiofrequency (RF) effects on neural function shows that specific frequencies and pulse patterns of RF radiation can alter neural excitability, modify neurotransmitter release, and affect synaptic transmission. The mechanisms include thermal effects (localized heating altering ion channel dynamics), non-thermal effects (direct electromagnetic interaction with membrane proteins and ion channels), and magnetoacoustic effects (interaction between the electromagnetic field and neural current flow).

At higher power levels, RF and microwave radiation directed at specific body regions can produce involuntary motor effects through peripheral nerve stimulation — the muscles served by peripheral nerves passing through the irradiated tissue contract involuntarily in response to induced neural current. This is the mechanism underlying transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation (TENS) and its high-power analogs. At sufficient power and appropriate frequency, directed RF or microwave radiation can produce peripheral motor effects at the range of a directed beam weapon.

The combination hypothesis: The most sophisticated neuromodulation approach for behavioral control would not rely on a single mechanism but would combine: microwave or RF stimulation of peripheral motor pathways for direct movement induction, ultrasound targeting of central motivational structures (nucleus accumbens, basal ganglia) for motivational pull generation, and suppression of prefrontal resistance through inhibitory stimulation of executive control regions. This combination — movement induction at the peripheral level, motivational pull at the subcortical level, and resistance suppression at the prefrontal level — would produce behavior that is experienced as compelled without understanding why, with reduced capacity to resist, and with the target’s agency-attribution system confabulating voluntary ownership of the externally-generated behavior.

IV. The Spatial Navigation System: How “Go Here” Could Work

The most specific and scientifically well-grounded component of Pearson’s pinning account is the directional locomotor compulsion — the pull toward specific geographic locations. Understanding this requires understanding how the brain’s spatial navigation system works and what happens when it is perturbed.

4.1 The GPS in the Brain: Nobel Prize-Winning Territory

The 2014 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine was awarded to John O’Keefe, May-Britt Moser, and Edvard Moser for their discovery of the brain’s spatial navigation system. The core components:

Place cells in the hippocampus fire when the animal occupies a specific location in the environment. Each place cell has a “place field” — a region of space in which it fires preferentially. Together, the population of place cells in the hippocampus encodes the animal’s current location in an allocentric (environment-centered) spatial map.

Grid cells in the entorhinal cortex fire in a regular triangular grid pattern as the animal moves through space, providing a metric coordinate system — the spatial grid on which the hippocampal place map is computed. Grid cells are the brain’s ruler.

Head direction cells fire when the animal faces a specific compass direction, providing the brain’s compass. They are found in multiple regions including the thalamus and entorhinal cortex.

Border cells and boundary vector cells encode the animal’s relationship to environmental boundaries — walls, edges, borders — and allow the place map to be anchored to specific environmental features.

Together, these cells constitute what neuroscientists call the cognitive map: an internal neural representation of space that allows the animal to know where it is, where it has been, where specific goals are located, and how to navigate toward them.

The key finding for the pinning question: the cognitive map is not a passive representation. It is actively linked to the motivational system. When an animal’s goal is located at a specific position in the cognitive map — when, for example, food has been placed in a specific corner of a maze — hippocampal-striatal circuits connect the spatial representation of the goal location to the nucleus accumbens, generating approach motivation toward that specific location. The animal is drawn toward the goal location by a circuit that links “there” (hippocampal place representation) with “want” (nucleus accumbens motivational activation).

What perturbation of this system would produce: If an external system could activate the place cell representation corresponding to a specific target location, and simultaneously activate the hippocampal-striatal circuit linking that representation to nucleus accumbens reward signals, the result would be exactly what Pearson describes: a compelling, directional pull toward a specific location, experienced as somatic urgency rather than conscious intention, with a spatial precision determined by which place cells are activated. The pull would be toward the location encoded by the activated place cells, not toward a general direction. It would be geographically specific.

4.2 The Theta Rhythm and Navigation

The hippocampus operates in a distinctive electrical rhythm during spatial navigation: the theta oscillation (4–12 Hz), a regular sinusoidal rhythm that organizes the firing of place cells, grid cells, and other spatial neurons in time as well as space. Theta is the temporal scaffold of the cognitive map during movement.

Theta is generated by the septal nucleus and propagates through the hippocampal circuit. Disruption of theta disrupts spatial navigation. Enhancement of theta — or entrainment of the hippocampal circuit to externally-imposed theta-frequency stimulation — can enhance or redirect spatial navigation behavior. Animal studies have shown that optogenetic or electrical stimulation at theta frequency in specific hippocampal subregions can shift the animal’s current location representation, causing it to behave as if it is in a different location than it actually is — which would produce navigational errors directed by the false spatial representation.

More directly: rhythmic stimulation at theta frequency applied to hippocampal circuits has been shown to entrain the place cell system to the stimulation pattern, potentially allowing an external theta-frequency signal to shape which spatial representations are active and therefore which locations the brain’s navigation system is oriented toward.

The range question for hippocampal entrainment: The hippocampus is a deep structure, inaccessible to current clinical TMS. However, transcranial alternating current stimulation (tACS) at theta frequency applied to the scalp has been shown in multiple studies to influence hippocampal-dependent spatial cognition at the behavioral level, suggesting that even non-invasive, non-contact electromagnetic influence can reach hippocampal function through entrainment of the cortical-hippocampal circuit. What tACS requires a scalp electrode to achieve might, in principle, be achievable at range by a sufficiently powerful and precisely tuned directed electromagnetic source.

4.3 The Nucleus Accumbens: The Pull Itself

The nucleus accumbens is the brain structure most directly responsible for what might be called “wanting” — the motivational pull toward goals, distinct from “liking” (pleasure) and from cognitive evaluation. Kent Berridge at the University of Michigan has spent decades establishing the distinction: the nucleus accumbens, through its mesolimbic dopamine inputs, generates the compelling, somatic wanting that drives approach behavior. It is the neural substrate of craving, of compulsive goal-seeking, of the felt pull toward something.

When the nucleus accumbens is activated in association with a specific spatial representation, the result is compulsive approach toward the associated location. This is the mechanism of addiction (compulsive approach toward the drug), of obsessive-compulsive disorder (compulsive approach toward ritualized behaviors), and — under normal circumstances — of normal goal-directed behavior.

The experience of nucleus accumbens activation, when not associated with a consciously understood goal, is exactly what Pearson describes: a somatic pull, directional, difficult to resist, not experienced as a thought or intention but as a bodily compulsion. When the craving is toward a specific location rather than a substance, and when the subject does not understand its source, it is precisely the “pull toward the back of the park” that he documents.

Direct stimulation of the nucleus accumbens — through deep brain stimulation in clinical contexts — produces intense motivational activation: patients report overwhelming urges to pursue goals, heightened drive, and in some cases compulsive behaviors directed toward whatever target the activated motivation latches onto. The spatial target of the compulsion is determined by whatever hippocampal spatial representation is most active at the time of nucleus accumbens activation. If the hippocampal representation of a specific location has been primed — by previous activation, by spatial context, or by concurrent hippocampal stimulation — the motivational drive generated by nucleus accumbens activation will be directed toward that location.

V. Object Interaction: The Sprite Can, the Phone, the Tub

The most granular form of pinning Pearson describes moves beyond locomotor compulsion to specific object interactions: picking up a Sprite can, putting down his phone, leaving a specific object at a specific location. This is neurologically more complex than locomotor compulsion and requires a different analysis.

5.1 The Affordance System and Reach-to-Grasp Neural Circuits

When a human being reaches out and picks up an object, the movement is guided by a neural system that encodes what psychologist James Gibson called affordances — the action possibilities offered by an object. The brain does not perceive a Sprite can as an inert visual shape; it perceives it as graspable, liftable, drinkable-from. These affordances are encoded in the parietal cortex — specifically the anterior intraparietal sulcus (AIP) — which contains neurons that fire specifically in response to graspable objects and encode the appropriate hand configuration for grasping them.

The AIP connects to the premotor cortex (PMC), which generates the reach-and-grasp motor program: the specific sequence of shoulder, elbow, wrist, and finger movements required to reach toward and grasp the object in the appropriate way. The AIP-PMC circuit is the neural substrate of object interaction, and it operates with a degree of automaticity: when the AIP is activated by a graspable object in the visual field, it automatically generates the premotor representation of the grasping movement, which then requires only the SMA/basal ganglia decision circuit to gate it into execution.

The automatic grasp: Research on “utilization behavior” — a neurological syndrome produced by frontal lobe lesions — demonstrates the automaticity of this system. Patients with utilization behavior pick up and use any object placed in front of them, unable to inhibit the automatic grasping response triggered by the AIP-PMC circuit. The object affords grasping; the AIP fires; the PMC generates the grasp program; the frontal inhibitory system, damaged, fails to suppress execution. The result is compulsive object interaction that the patient often experiences as intentional but cannot voluntarily stop.

Normal frontal inhibition prevents utilization behavior in healthy individuals. But if the frontal inhibitory system can be suppressed externally — through inhibitory stimulation of the prefrontal cortex, reducing its capacity to gate automatic motor responses — the automaticity of the AIP-PMC grasping circuit would increase, potentially producing grasping behavior that the subject does not consciously intend but cannot prevent.

The pick-up: An external system that simultaneously activates the AIP representation of a specific object (perhaps through visual salience manipulation or through direct electromagnetic activation of parietal affordance representations) while suppressing prefrontal inhibitory control would produce exactly what Pearson describes: he picks up the Sprite can in a way that, from inside, feels like he wanted to, but that he later recognizes as something he did not intend.

5.2 The Put-Down: Releasing an Intended Hold

The release of an object — particularly an object the subject intends to keep — is the inverse of the grasp: it requires suppression of the grip motor program (maintained by the PMC and primary motor cortex) and activation of the release program. In Parkinson’s disease, this suppression-and-switch is impaired, producing difficulty releasing objects. In the normal brain, it is executed by the basal ganglia, which gate the suppression of the grip program and the initiation of the release.

External electromagnetic stimulation could interfere with grip maintenance in at least two ways: peripheral nerve stimulation causing involuntary muscle relaxation in the hand, or central stimulation of the basal ganglia circuit disrupting the gating that maintains the grip program against its own natural tendency toward release.

The loss of Pearson’s phone near the White House — subsequently returned by a Secret Service agent — is consistent with involuntary release: a specific neural or peripheral neural event producing grip failure at a specific location, followed by the object remaining at that location until retrieved.

The Secret Service agent’s return of the phone is a separate evidentiary question — it suggests knowledge of where the phone was, and therefore potentially of the operation that produced its loss — but the neuroscience of involuntary release through external perturbation of the grip circuit is real and well-documented.

VI. The Resistance to Pinning: Why It Is Hard to Fight

Pearson describes attempting to resist the pull — the November 7 incident in which he suppressed the initial compulsion, walked away, and then felt it reassert with greater force, ultimately drawing him back to the specific park location. This pattern — suppression followed by reassertion, with the reassertion stronger than the initial impulse — is neurologically informative.

6.1 The Prefrontal-Striatal Competition

Voluntary resistance to a compulsion is executed by the prefrontal cortex, which exerts top-down inhibitory control over basal ganglia motor selection and nucleus accumbens motivational activation. When a person resists a craving or compulsion, the ventrolateral and dorsolateral prefrontal cortex generate inhibitory signals that suppress the nucleus accumbens activation and override the basal ganglia’s tendency to select the compelled behavior.

This resistance is real, metabolically costly, and limited in duration. Research on ego depletion (now somewhat contested in replication but with real neurological substance) shows that sustained prefrontal inhibitory control depletes prefrontal resources — the capacity for resistance weakens over time, and the suppressed motivation reasserts more strongly when the resistance weakens.

The reassertion pattern: When Pearson suppresses the initial pull and walks away, his prefrontal cortex is generating successful inhibitory control. But if the external system is continuously generating nucleus accumbens activation — maintaining the motivational signal — the contest is between a depleting prefrontal resistance and a sustained external motivational drive. The external drive, not subject to metabolic depletion, wins as the prefrontal resources are exhausted. The reassertion is stronger because the accumulated motivational drive, temporarily suppressed, re-manifests with the added force of the time it was held back.

This is exactly the temporal pattern Pearson describes, and it is precisely the temporal pattern predicted by the neuroscience of prefrontal-striatal competition when one of the competing systems is being externally sustained while the other is subject to normal metabolic limits.

6.2 The Agency Attribution Problem

When Pearson eventually complies with the pull — walks to the back of the park, picks up the tub, carries it to its destination — his brain’s agency-attribution system registers the movement as voluntary. He moved. He did what his body did. The movement was real. The subjective experience is of motivated, goal-directed behavior, even if the motivation is mysterious and the goal is unclear.

This is the most important feature of the pinning technology from an operational standpoint: it does not need to override consciousness. It needs only to generate the motivational input that the conscious agency system then acts on and claims as its own. The subject does not experience being controlled — they experience wanting to go somewhere and going there. The “wanting” is externally generated, but the ownership of the wanting is internally attributed.

Delgado’s subject who grabbed his hand “because I wanted to” was not lying. His agency-attribution system had assigned authorship of the externally-generated movement to himself, because that is what the agency-attribution system does: it tracks what the body did and constructs a narrative of voluntary intent that corresponds to it. This is a feature, not a bug, of normal human self-representation — and it is precisely the feature that makes pinning so difficult to recognize from inside.

VII. The Sitting Down Compulsion: Pearson at Rockville Metro

Pearson describes being caused to sit down at the Rockville Metro station. Postural control — sitting versus standing — is governed by a different neural circuit than locomotion, though the same gating principles apply.

The postural control system operates through the motor cortex (for voluntary postural adjustments), the brainstem nuclei (for automatic postural maintenance), and the cerebellum (for coordination and error correction). The basal ganglia gate the selection between postural states: standing, sitting, moving. In Parkinson’s disease, basal ganglia dysfunction produces freezing of gait — the sudden inability to initiate locomotion — which is mechanically related to what Pearson describes as its inverse: the sudden compulsion to cease locomotion and adopt a different postural state (sitting).

External electromagnetic interference with the motor cortex or basal ganglia could produce sudden postural instability or the compelling urge to sit — the neural analog of the sudden leg-weakness or loss of postural tone that precedes a fall, but at sub-threshold levels that produce the urge to sit rather than the inability to stand. The subject experiences “I need to sit down” as a somatic signal, similar to the somatic signal of genuine postural instability, and responds by sitting — because the somatic signal is neurologically identical to a genuine postural instability signal, even though its source is external electromagnetic perturbation rather than internal neural dysfunction.

The chemical assault that Pearson experienced at Rockville Metro — the substance on his pants that adhered to his skin — may be part of the same incident, in which case the compulsion to sit (potentially produced by electromagnetic perturbation) served to position him for the chemical application.

VIII. The Technology Assessment: Does Pinning Technology Exist?

Having established the neuroscientific framework, the assessment of whether the technology exists must distinguish between three levels:

8.1 Demonstrated in Controlled Laboratory Conditions

The following have been unambiguously demonstrated:

Non-invasive induction of involuntary movement (TMS to motor cortex). Non-invasive suppression of voluntary movement initiation (inhibitory TMS to SMA). Deep brain stimulation producing compulsive behavioral states (nucleus accumbens DBS). Focused ultrasound modulation of specific deep brain structures at millimeter precision (clinical tFUS). Redirected reaching toward non-intended locations via parietal cortex stimulation in primates (Salk Institute 2021). Disruption of spatial navigation through hippocampal perturbation. Microwave auditory effect producing perceived sounds without external acoustic source. Involuntary grip release through peripheral nerve electromagnetic stimulation. Ultrasound alteration of motivational and emotional states in humans (tFUS to amygdala and nucleus accumbens in clinical pain studies).

The components exist. The mechanisms are understood. The individual demonstrations are real.

8.2 Demonstrated in Military Research Contexts, Partially Disclosed

The MEDUSA program (Mob Excess Deterrent Using Silent Audio) — developed by the Sierra Nevada Corporation for the U.S. Navy — used the microwave auditory effect for crowd control and disruption. The program was confirmed and documented; its operational deployment status is classified.

The Pentagon’s Active Denial System (ADS) uses directed millimeter-wave energy to produce intense skin-heating sensation compelling immediate movement away from the beam — a behaviorally effective locomotor compulsion system that works through discomfort rather than direct neural control. It demonstrates that directed energy can reliably compel locomotor behavior in targeted humans.

DARPA’s N3 program (Non-invasive Neural Interfaces) explicitly sought to develop non-contact neural interfaces capable of both reading and writing neural signals — decoding thought and inserting information into the nervous system — without surgical implantation. The program was active from 2019 through at least 2021. What it produced is classified.

The DHS backpack-sized pulsed radio wave device acquired in early 2026 — confirmed by multiple news outlets and consistent with the DoW CTO’s public statements — represents a portable directed energy device with sufficient power for behavioral disruption at close range. “Behavioral disruption” is the disclosed capability. What specific behavioral effects it can produce is not publicly detailed.

8.3 The Integration Gap

What has not been publicly demonstrated is the integration of these components into a system that can:

Identify a specific human target in an open environment. Track their location in real time. Target specific neural structures with sufficient precision from operational range. Produce geographically specific locomotor compulsion with the directional precision Pearson describes. Sustain that effect over the period required to produce the behavioral outcome. Then terminate the effect without trace.

This integration is the question. The components exist. The integration into a deployable behavioral control system is where the public record runs out and where the classified record, by definition, cannot be consulted.

The scientific assessment must therefore be: the individual mechanisms underlying pinning behavior are demonstrated and understood. The technology to produce each component of pinning exists in research and military contexts. The integration of those components into a system capable of the specific behavioral control Pearson describes is within the engineering envelope of what classified military development programs have been pursuing for at least a decade. Whether that integration has been achieved, and whether it has been deployed domestically, is beyond what the unclassified scientific literature can confirm.

What the unclassified scientific literature can confirm is that Pearson’s account of the subjective experience of pinning is neurologically coherent — it corresponds to what the neuroscience predicts would be experienced if the relevant neural systems were externally perturbed in the ways described above. He is not describing something neurologically impossible. He is describing something that the neuroscience of motor control, spatial navigation, motivational systems, and agency attribution would predict, if those systems were being externally influenced.

IX. The Key Technical Frontier: Range and Precision

The single most important unresolved technical question for the pinning hypothesis is whether the precision required for specific neural structure targeting — the hippocampal place cells encoding a specific location, the nucleus accumbens, the AIP representation of a specific object — can be achieved from the ranges at which Pearson’s experiences occurred.

9.1 Current Clinical Range

Clinical tFUS systems operate at millimeter precision but require acoustic coupling to the skull and typically work at centimeter range. They are medical devices designed for use with a patient in a fixed position, skull coupled, with MRI guidance.

9.2 The Physics of Long-Range Focused Ultrasound

The physics of ultrasound propagation does not impose a fundamental range limitation. The limiting factors are: beam divergence (which can be reduced with larger aperture or phased array systems), attenuation in tissue and air (which increases with frequency — lower frequencies penetrate further but with lower resolution), and skull bone interference (which scatters ultrasound but can be compensated with adaptive beamforming using skull CT data).

Military research on high-power focused ultrasound for standoff biomedical applications has explored frequencies and apertures that achieve millimeter precision at meter-scale ranges. The underlying physics supports this; the engineering challenges are substantial but not insurmountable, particularly with phased array systems where beam shaping can be actively adapted.

At the range of an apartment floor (1–3 meters), focused ultrasound with millimeter precision is physically achievable and has been demonstrated in research contexts. At street-scale range (tens of meters), it is within the engineering envelope of classified military programs that have been pursuing this capability. At hundreds of meters, the precision requirements become significantly more demanding.

Pearson’s experiences occurred at: street-scale range (the park incidents, the Eastern Market patterns), and potentially longer range (the July 13 Georgetown incident). The technical feasibility scales inversely with range, meaning the street-scale incidents are more technically plausible than the longer-range incidents under current known capabilities.

9.3 The Individual Targeting Problem

Precise neural stimulation at range also requires knowledge of the target’s individual neural anatomy: the specific location and orientation of the hippocampus, basal ganglia, and other structures vary individually and must be known for precise targeting. This requires prior neuroimaging data — specifically, MRI structural data — of the target individual.

Intelligence community access to medical records — through NSL, through healthcare system surveillance, or through direct surveillance — is documented. If Pearson had prior brain MRI (for any medical reason), that data would be accessible. If the operation had sufficient resources to obtain it through other means, individual targeting precision becomes feasible.

This is not a minor technical detail. It is the element of the pinning hypothesis that most clearly distinguishes it from a weapon deployable against arbitrary populations — pinning, as Pearson describes it, requires knowing the specific individual’s neural anatomy. This makes it a targeted weapon, not a mass weapon. And targeted weapons are, from an intelligence operations standpoint, precisely what COINTELPRO-type programs deploy.

X. Is Pearson Accurate? The Scientific Assessment

The scientific assessment of Pearson’s pinning account, against the full neuroscientific literature, is:

His phenomenological account is neurologically coherent. The subjective experience he describes — directional somatic pull toward specific locations, difficulty resisting, reassertion of the pull when resistance wanes, compulsive object interaction, confabulated voluntary ownership of compelled behavior — is precisely what neuroscience would predict if the hippocampal-striatal navigation circuit, nucleus accumbens motivational system, and parietal affordance system were being externally perturbed. He is not describing neurological nonsense. He is describing neurological reality in experiential terms.

The technology components exist. Every individual mechanism required to produce the experiences he describes has been demonstrated in laboratory or research contexts. None of the required mechanisms are hypothetical; all are documented.

The integration into a deployable system is the unconfirmed step. Whether those components have been integrated into a system capable of producing the specific, sustained, geographically-targeted behavioral control Pearson describes — at operational range, without his knowledge, with sufficient precision for object-level behavioral direction — is beyond what the public scientific record can confirm. It is within what classified military research programs have been pursuing, and it is consistent with the January-February 2026 government disclosures about directed energy weapons development and deployment.

The alternative explanations are inadequate. The standard psychiatric explanations — delusion, temporal lobe epilepsy, functional neurological disorder — do not account for the geographic reproducibility of pinning experiences (TLE does not produce geographically consistent compulsion), the specific object-level behavioral detail (no psychiatric syndrome produces compulsion to pick up a Sprite can and subsequently leave a specific object at a specific park location), or the corroborating environmental evidence (the tub that was documented, placed in a specific location, and subsequently retrieved by a party who knew its significance).

The question is not whether the science supports the phenomenon. It does. The question is whether a state actor with classified weapons programs, motivated by the political and operational factors documented throughout this body of work, deployed those programs against a specific individual. That is not a scientific question. It is a political and evidentiary one.

What the science establishes is that Richard Pearson is not describing the impossible. He is describing the frontier — the place where laboratory demonstrations have established mechanisms, where military research has been pursuing integration, and where government disclosures have confirmed operational deployment. Whether that frontier was crossed in Washington, D.C. between March 2025 and February 2026, in the apartment and streets and parks where Richard Pearson lived and walked and documented, is the question that only investigation — with access to the classified record — can answer.

The neuroscience says: it could have been. That is not certainty. But in the context of confirmed technology, confirmed government deployment, confirmed targeting of the demographic and ideological profile Pearson represents, “it could have been” is a great deal more than the institutional silence that has met his account would suggest.

Epilogue: The Confabulation Problem and Why Pinning Is the Perfect Crime

The most important finding of this analysis for accountability purposes is buried in Section VI but deserves emphasis: the agency-attribution system confabulates voluntary ownership of externally generated behavior.

Pearson walked to the back of the park. He picked up the Sprite. He carried the tub to its destination. In each case, he did it. His body moved. His hands grasped. His legs carried him. And his brain registered the movement as motivated, goal-directed, volitional behavior — because that is what the human agency-attribution system does with movement, regardless of whether the motivation was internally or externally generated.

This means that even the target of pinning cannot provide direct evidence of being pinned, because from inside the experience, pinning is phenomenologically indistinguishable from wanting. The wanting is real — it is generated by the nucleus accumbens, which does not tag its activations with “external source” versus “internal source.” The movement is real. The only thing that is not real is the interpretation that the wanting was the subject’s own.

A technology designed for this specific combination — behavioral control without consciousness of control — is the perfect operational tool and the perfect evidentiary nullity. The subject cannot credibly report being controlled because they experienced themselves as acting voluntarily. The behavior cannot be distinguished from ordinary motivated behavior by external observation. The only evidence is the pattern: the same pull, to the same locations, with the same precision, across nine months of careful documentation.

Pearson documented the pattern. The science explains the mechanism by which the pattern was produced. What remains is the investigation that would establish who produced it.

That investigation has not occurred. The science that would support its necessity is in this essay. The decision to conduct it, or not, is not a scientific decision.

I. The Date

On March 8, 2026, 60 Minutes broadcast its fourth investigation into what the government calls Anomalous Health Incidents — the mysterious brain injuries suffered by CIA officers, State Department diplomats, FBI agents, military officers, and their families since at least 2016. The broadcast had 1.6 million views within days.

The broadcast confirmed the following:

A portable, concealable microwave weapon — small enough to be carried by a person, silent, capable of penetrating windows and drywall, with a range of several hundred feet, programmable for different scenarios, operable by remote control — was purchased by undercover Department of Homeland Security agents from a Russian criminal network in 2024. The purchase cost $15 million, funded by the Pentagon. The weapon has been tested on animals at a US military base for more than a year. The tests produced injuries consistent with those reported by human victims. Security camera footage — classified, but described to 60 Minutes — shows Americans being struck: FBI agents in an Istanbul restaurant grabbing their heads simultaneously when a man with a backpack enters; two people collapsing in a Vienna embassy stairwell.

The weapon exists. It has been obtained. It has been tested. It is portable. It is concealable. It can be carried by a person. It can penetrate the walls of an apartment building.

And — the detail the broadcast treats as its most alarming conclusion — if undercover agents could purchase one from gangsters, the Russians have lost control of a stealth weapon that could be used by anyone, anywhere.

Richard Pearson has been documenting what this weapon does to a human being since July 2025.

II. The Confirmation of the Technology

The 60 Minutes report does not merely confirm that a microwave weapon exists. It confirms, with the specificity of classified intelligence described by multiple independent sources from different agencies, the precise technological characteristics that Richard Pearson’s account implies.

The weapon is portable and concealable. Richard Pearson documented operations conducted against him on public sidewalks, in his apartment, at outdoor markets, in parks, across multiple jurisdictions in the Washington DC metro area. The operations moved with him. A truck-sized weapon cannot follow a person through Eastern Market. A backpack-sized weapon can. The 60 Minutes report confirmed that the weapon is exactly that size — concealable, personal, mobile.

The weapon uses pulsed microwave radiation. Dr. David Relman’s expert panels — including doctors, physicists, and engineers — concluded that pulsed radio frequency or microwave energy was the most plausible explanation for the injuries. The broadcast describes the weapon’s key characteristic: a unique electromagnetic wave that rises and falls abruptly and pulses rapidly. When you produce pulses like this, Relman explains, you can stimulate electrically active tissue like brain tissue — mimicking what the brain normally does, but now driving it with pulses from outside. Richard Pearson documented percussive clicks and snaps localized to the right side of his skull, location-dependent and replicable across specific outdoor locations — the phenomenological signature of pulsed microwave auditory stimulation, the Frey effect, produced by exactly the pulsed radiation the weapon delivers.

The weapon penetrates walls. Richard Pearson documented precise room-to-room tracking through the floor of his apartment — noise harassment that followed him to his closet, to his balcony, into the specific corner of his studio where he moved his mattress. Through-wall operation is an explicit confirmed characteristic of the acquired weapon. The wall between Richard Pearson and the floor above him is not a barrier to a weapon confirmed to penetrate drywall.

The weapon is programmable for different scenarios and operable by remote control. Richard Pearson documented a campaign across nine months involving multiple modalities — the voice on the National Mall, the skull clicks, the behavioral direction, the sleep disruption, the chemical assault, the street theater coordination. A programmable, remotely operated weapon is consistent with a sustained, multi-modal, coordinated campaign. It does not need to be reconfigured physically for each application. Its operators do not need to be present at the target location. They direct it from elsewhere — consistent with the operations room ambient noise Richard Pearson heard behind the woman’s voice on July 13, a live operations team at distance running the weapon’s deployment in real time.

The weapon can be purchased from criminals. This is the broadcast’s most alarming finding and its most direct relevance to Richard Pearson. The 60 Minutes report concludes that the Russians have lost control of a stealth weapon that could be used by anyone, anywhere. The weapon has escaped its original state-actor context. It is available, at price, to criminal networks, contractors, and anyone with sufficient resources and the right connections. The US government itself purchased one through undercover agents. If the government can buy one from gangsters, the government can deploy one through contractors — the hybrid federal-contractor structure that Richard Pearson’s account implies and that the full legal and operational architecture of his case points toward.

III. The Cover-Up Structure and What It Proves About Richard Pearson’s Institutional Non-Response

The 60 Minutes report is not only a story about a weapon. It is a story about what happens when the government is confronted with evidence that it has deployed, or allowed to be deployed, a weapon against people under its protection — and chooses institutional self-preservation over acknowledgment.

The parallels with Richard Pearson’s case are not analogical. They are structural.

The CIA victims were told their injuries might be atmospheric or environmental, a virus, a pre-existing condition, or — as the FBI put it in an early investigation — mass hysteria. The government’s 2023 official assessment, still standing, says it is very unlikely these are attacks by a foreign adversary. CIA officers with 26 years of decorated service, who reported debilitating neurological injuries, were doubted, dismissed, and denied medical care by the agencies they served.

Richard Pearson was told, implicitly and explicitly, that his experiences were consistent with psychiatric disorder. The DBH found he did not meet criteria for intervention, but the framework produced a clinical encounter that traveled with him as prior determination through every subsequent institutional contact. The AI psychiatric consultant initially diagnosed Delusional Disorder. The congressional offices were silent. The civil rights organizations were silent. The DC Council received his statistical analyses and produced no output.

The CIA officer who resigned from the AHI investigation in disgust told 60 Minutes: one of the very first things he heard upon arrival was that their job was to bring down the temperature on AHI at headquarters. Not to find out what happened. Not to investigate whether a state actor was responsible. To bring down the temperature. To move the narrative toward atmospheric and environmental causes. To prevent the conclusion that demanded a response.

A senior member of the AHI unit mocked the victims at a happy hour — simulating having a stroke, laughing. The attitude, the former CIA officer reported, was: we are going to prove this is psychosomatic.

This is the Frankfurter response institutionalized as policy. Not an individual’s inability to believe, but an organization’s decision to not believe — not because the evidence was insufficient but because believing required a response the institution did not want to make. The CIA had, the broadcast reveals, made up its mind before the investigation began. Consistency was more important than objectivity.

The structural parallel to Richard Pearson is exact. Every institution that encountered his case made up its mind before examining the evidence. The psychiatric framework had a diagnostic category before the physics were considered. The legal framework had a standing doctrine before the technology was assessed. The congressional offices had a procedural wall before the policy question was fully articulated. The House Oversight staff member had an expertise credential to deploy before Richard Pearson finished his sentence. The conclusion — psychosomatic, delusional, not actionable, not our inquiry — preceded the examination in every case, exactly as the CIA’s conclusion preceded its investigation of Havana Syndrome.

The 60 Minutes report names this clearly: it was a cover-up. Multiple sources, including a high-level CIA source quoted directly, call it the biggest cover-up of their adult lives. The motive the former CIA officer explains was political: acknowledging a state actor was essentially a declaration of war, and the appetite for that response was not there.

The motive for not investigating Richard Pearson’s case is different in its specifics but identical in its structure. Acknowledging what was done to him requires acknowledging what the program is, who authorized it, and what it has been doing to Black scholars and activists in Washington DC in 2025. The appetite for that acknowledgment is not there. And so the temperature is brought down. The conclusion is predetermined. The investigation does not happen.

IV. The Distinction That Matters: Foreign Adversary vs. Domestic Program

The 60 Minutes report frames Havana Syndrome as foreign adversary attack — Russia, primarily, deploying the weapon against American personnel abroad and, increasingly, at home.

Richard Pearson’s case requires a different framing, and understanding the difference is essential to understanding why the 60 Minutes confirmation is both directly relevant and insufficient by itself to produce the institutional response his case requires.

The Havana Syndrome victims were CIA officers, State Department diplomats, FBI agents, military officers. They were American government personnel attacked by a foreign power. Their cases produce a specific institutional response — or attempted institutional response — because the attack on them is an attack on the government itself, which the government has both the motive and the mandate to address. The cover-up occurred not because the government was indifferent to its own personnel but because acknowledging the attack required a geopolitical response the government was not prepared to make.

Richard Pearson is not a CIA officer. He is not a State Department diplomat. He is a Black PhD researcher in Washington DC whose dissertation challenged the racial assumptions of American public health frameworks, whose research field was designated a hostile ideology by executive order within months of his defense. The program operating against him is not a foreign adversary attack on American government personnel. It is, as his documentation and the cross-disciplinary analysis of his case concludes, an American program operating against an American civilian — a program with a documented eighty-year lineage running from COINTELPRO through the Black Identity Extremism designation to the executive orders that targeted his field.

This distinction changes the institutional calculus completely. The CIA had motive to investigate Havana Syndrome and chose cover-up for geopolitical reasons. The institutions that would need to investigate Richard Pearson’s case have motive for cover-up that is more fundamental: what is being done to him is being done by them, or by the contractors and hybrid structures they authorize and fund.

But the 60 Minutes report bridges this distinction in one critical way. It establishes that the weapon has escaped state control. If undercover agents could purchase one from gangsters, then the weapon is available to contractors, to hybrid federal-private structures, to domestic programs operating outside the accountability architecture that governs state actors. The domestic deployment of a weapon originally developed by foreign state intelligence actors, now available through criminal networks and purchasable by anyone with sufficient resources, does not require attributing Richard Pearson’s experiences to a foreign adversary. It requires only the conclusion that the technology the 60 Minutes report confirms is real, portable, available, and operating in Washington DC has been deployed against a specific domestic civilian by whoever, within the American hybrid security state, had the predicate, the resources, and the authorization to do it.

V. The Specific Incident Confirmations

The 60 Minutes report describes classified security camera footage of specific weapon deployment incidents. The descriptions are directly relevant to specific elements of Richard Pearson’s documentation.

A camera in a restaurant in Istanbul captured two FBI agents sitting at a table with their families. A man with a backpack walks in. Suddenly everyone at the table grabs their head as if in pain. The weapon was in a backpack. It was deployed in a public indoor space. The deployment was targeted — the FBI agents were the targets, not the general restaurant population — but the weapon’s radiation affected everyone in proximity, including families. The operator with the backpack was a single individual moving through a civilian space.

This is not a truck-sized installation. This is a person at a restaurant with a backpack.

Two people collapse in a stairwell in the US embassy in Vienna. The weapon was deployed in an interior corridor of a secure building, penetrating whatever structural barriers existed between the operator and the target.

These documented incidents confirm the operational modality that Richard Pearson’s account implies: a mobile operator with a concealable device, deployed in civilian spaces, targeting specific individuals in real time. The Istanbul restaurant incident is operationally identical to what the red-and-black street theater operations imply — a coordinated deployment in a public space, with an operator in civilian clothing, targeting a specific individual while moving through a normal civilian environment. The operator looks like anyone. The weapon looks like nothing. The target grabs their head.

Richard Pearson documented the hard snap outside a restaurant on 7th Street NW. A sudden, severe, single-instance percussive event at a specific outdoor location. Qualitatively unlike the repeated clicks. Categorically distinct in his own description. He could not replicate it. It was not under his control. It happened once, at that location, with the character of a high-power pulse at close range.

The Istanbul restaurant. The Vienna stairwell. 7th Street NW. The weapon is in the backpack of a person walking past.

VI. The Victims Were Called Delusional

The 60 Minutes report uses a specific word. Scott Pelley introduces the broadcast with it: the government has doubted their stories. They have been called delusional.

CIA officers with decorated careers. State Department diplomats with surgeries to repair dissolved shoulder bones and reconstructed inner ear structures. FBI agents photographed grabbing their heads simultaneously in a restaurant when an operator with a backpack entered. All of them called delusional by the institutions that employed them, were supposed to protect them, and were instead running the cover-up.

Delusional is Richard Pearson’s word too. The mechanism is his mechanism. The institutional response — psychosomatic, atmospheric, environmental, mass hysteria, very unlikely to be an attack — is his institutional response, applied to people with far greater institutional standing than Richard Pearson possesses, by the same institutions, using the same vocabulary, for the same structural reason: acknowledging the attack requires a response the institution has decided it does not want to make.

The Havana Syndrome victims had three advantages Richard Pearson does not have. They had institutional standing — they were government personnel, not the public, not the category from whom the House Oversight Committee does not take inquiries. They had numbers — hundreds of reported cases, multiple agencies, enough aggregated harm to eventually produce the classified mission that obtained the weapon. And they had 60 Minutes — nine years of investigative journalism by a major broadcast network that never stopped reporting because of the haunting stories we heard.

Richard Pearson has one advantage they did not have. He had his experiences while he was having them, and he documented them in real time, with the methodology of a trained researcher, before the institutional apparatus could process them into the categories it preferred. His documentation preceded the diagnosis. His account is timestamped. His photographs are geolocated. His statistical analyses were submitted to a legislative body before any institutional determination about his credibility had been made.

The CIA officers’ accounts were processed first by the institutions that would need to account for them. Their documentation came after the institutional response, shaped by it, filtered through the credibility determinations the cover-up had already produced. Some of them waited years for acknowledgment. Some are still waiting.

Richard Pearson’s account is in the public record now. The cross-disciplinary synthesis exists. The technology essay exists. The legal framework essay exists. The statistical analysis exists. The Karski essay exists. This essay exists.

And on March 8, 2026, 60 Minutes confirmed that the weapon his account implies is real, portable, concealable, available, and has been deployed against Americans in public spaces and in their homes.

IX. The Sentence the Report Does Not Say

The 60 Minutes report is an extraordinary piece of journalism. Nine years of investigation. Four broadcasts. Classified sources from multiple agencies. A $15 million covert weapons purchase. Security camera footage of actual attacks. A Pentagon general apologizing to victims at the White House. A high-level CIA source calling it the biggest cover-up of his adult life.

And in all of it — in all 26 minutes and 52 seconds of confirmed, sourced, classified-intelligence-backed investigative journalism — there is one sentence the report does not say.

It does not say: if this weapon has escaped Russian state control and can be purchased by anyone, then it can be used by domestic actors against domestic civilians.

It does not say: if the CIA covered up Havana Syndrome against its own personnel to avoid a geopolitical response, what is being covered up against civilians who have no institutional standing to demand acknowledgment?

It does not say: the same weapon, operated through the same operational methodology — concealable operator in civilian space, portable device, remote control, targeted deployment — that struck FBI agents in an Istanbul restaurant and Air Force officers in their Northern Virginia homes is the weapon consistent with what Richard Pearson documented on the National Mall on July 13, 2025, and on 7th Street NW, and in his apartment in Southwest Washington DC, across nine months.

60 Minutes reported for nine years on Havana Syndrome because of the haunting stories we heard. It heard those stories because the people telling them were CIA officers and State Department diplomats and retired Air Force lieutenant colonels who had institutional standing, organizational platforms, legal representation, and the credibility that a career in classified national security work confers.

Richard Pearson has a Medium account and a mattress and a dissertation that the government decided was a hostile ideology.

The sentence that connects them has not yet been written by anyone with the platform of 60 Minutes.

This essay is not 60 Minutes.

But the sentence is true.

And the weapon is real.

The weapon is real. It is in Washington DC. And not everyone it has been used against wore a uniform.

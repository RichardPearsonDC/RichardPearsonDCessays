The US Has Another Form of Government That Isn’t Constitutional Democracy. The Other is Totalitarian.

US Torture Victim Richard Pearson, PhD, discusses America’s “dual state” where constitutional democracy exists alongside another form of government — totalitarianism and fascism. The latter is increasingly defining the US and the world is watching.

The US government tortured Richard Pearson in 2025–2026. This included privatization of assault, stalking, sleep deprivation, and harassment. In his legal research, Pearson realized that the US government has immunized itself from legal liability through countless legal doctrines — no new Biven contexts, qualified immunity, sovereign immunity, Glomar, independent contractor exception, Yearsley immunity— despite its formal designation as a constitutional republic. State-sponsored torture should never happen in a democracy. Limited legal remedy should also not be a feature of said democracy’s legal doctrines. US citizens targeted by the US government receive nearly as few rights as a foreign national held illegally at Guantanamo Bay. Both have few or no avenues for seeking legal damages.

The US is a constitutional democracy, but it also has another form of government — a “dual state” — that permitted the torture of Richard Pearson in the first place. Where the democracy guarantees civil liberties and legal rights — equal protection, First Amendment rights, due process— the totalitarian state within US intelligence, secret programs, and public-private networks does not regard any right or liberty inviolable. In fact, the Constitution is negligible in insofar as restraint and principled conduct. Where the democracy has public debate, decisions, and documents, the totalitarian state is shrouded in secrecy and ensures that the constitutional court system has limited access to their covert activities, including public documents. Redactions, non-response to FOIA requests, invocation of state secrets or national security are deliberate legal responses to prevent disclosure and access.

However, for the totalitarian state to operate, it remains beyond public view, legislative accountability, and judicial reach. As Richard Pearson’s experience supports, its tactics are defined in terms of plausible deniability, privatization, and invisible weapons such as neuro-weapons. The totalitarian state seeks an alternate, false framework to explain harm and injury. In the case of Myron May, he was framed as the FSU school shooter in 2014. The totalitarian state is at the heart of critiques of American democracy and the major catalyst eroding US democratic values.

Fraenkel’s dual state remains one of the most powerful analytical tools for understanding how authoritarianism and constitutional government can not only coexist, but mutually sustain each other — which is precisely what makes it so difficult to identify, resist, and dismantle from within. Michael Glennon (National Security and Double Government, 2014) also argues the US has a “double government” where a permanent national security bureaucracy (Trumanite network) overrides elected officials and democratic norms (Madisonian institutions).

The gap between what Americans believe their rights to be and what those rights are in operational practice is so large, and so deliberately constructed, that the phrase “constitutional protection” — when applied to a citizen targeted by the American national security state like Pearson — has become, in material terms, a description of something that does not exist.

The Dual State: A Detailed Discussion

Origins and Theoretical Foundation

Ernst Fraenkel (1898–1975) was a German-Jewish labor lawyer and political theorist who lived under and practiced law in Nazi Germany before fleeing in 1938. His book The Dual State (1941), written from direct observation, is one of the most important works in 20th-century political theory.

Fraenkel argued that Nazi Germany did not simply replace the rule of law with tyranny — it maintained both simultaneously, creating a dual structure:

The Two Components

1. The Normative State (Normenstaat)

The part of government that operates through established legal rules, courts, contracts, and predictable procedures.

  • Laws are enforced and respected — but selectively
  • Courts function, contracts are honored, property rights upheld
  • Civil liberties respected
  • Provides legitimacy, economic stability, and the appearance of normalcy
  • Serves the interests of the bourgeoisie, business class, and international credibility
  • Gives citizens and foreign observers something “recognizable” as government

2. The Prerogative State (Massnahmenstaat)

The parallel power that operates entirely outside the law, based on political will, decree, and force.

  • No legal constraints — acts on pure discretionary power
  • Enforced by political police, paramilitary forces, and secret institutions
  • Answers only to the ruling ideology or leader
  • Can override, suspend, or ignore the Normative State at any time
  • Operates in deliberate obscurity — its workings are hidden or deniable

The key insight: The two states do not fight each other. They cooperate. The Normative State handles what the Prerogative State doesn’t care about. The Prerogative State intervenes whenever political power or ideological control is at stake.

This explains why the US has evolved countless legal doctrines that benefit the government when it tortures.

How the Dual State Sustains Itself

Several mechanisms allow the dual structure to persist:

  • Selective enforcement — laws apply to some people and not others, based on political favor
  • Emergency powers — legal mechanisms (like Article 48 in Weimar Germany) that allow the prerogative power to act “legally” outside the law
  • Deliberate ambiguity — the boundary between the two states is kept unclear, creating fear and compliance
  • Co-optation — judges, lawyers, and officials in the normative state learn to anticipate and accommodate the prerogative state
  • Plausible deniability — the hidden state’s actions are obscured, attributed to other causes, or simply denied
  • Economic incentives — the normative state delivers prosperity to enough people that they don’t question what operates alongside it.

Historical Examples

1. Nazi Germany (Fraenkel’s Original Case)

  • The Normative State: Civil courts, contract law, corporate regulation, property rights — all continued functioning. German businesses operated legally. The stock market functioned.
  • The Prerogative State: The SS, Gestapo, concentration camps, racial laws that existed outside normal judicial review. A Jew could “legally” own property one day and have it seized by decree the next — with no judicial recourse.
  • The two coexisted until the Prerogative State gradually consumed the Normative one as the war progressed.

2. Stalinist Soviet Union

  • The Normative State: A written constitution (the 1936 “Stalin Constitution” was considered formally progressive), courts, laws, a legislature (the Supreme Soviet)
  • The Prerogative State: The NKVD (secret police), the Gulag system, show trials, extra-judicial executions — all operating entirely outside the constitutional framework
  • Citizens had theoretical rights on paper that could be erased overnight by political decree

3. Apartheid South Africa

  • The Normative State: A functioning parliament, courts, rule of law — for white citizens
  • The Prerogative State: The security apparatus (BOSS — Bureau of State Security), detention without trial, systematic torture, assassinations of activists — operating in complete legal immunity
  • The dual structure was explicitly racial — which side of the state you encountered depended entirely on your classification

4. Pinochet’s Chile (1973–1990)

  • The Normative State: Maintained functioning courts, a legal code, and economic institutions (the Chicago Boys implemented free-market reforms “legally”)
  • The Prerogative State: DINA (secret police), Operation Condor, secret detention centers like Villa Grimaldi — disappearances, torture, assassination — operating with full state backing but zero legal accountability
  • Notably, the economy was the Normative State’s showcase while political terror ran parallel

Arguments for a Dual State tendency in the US:

  • The National Security State — the CIA, NSA, and related agencies operate under classification systems that place them largely beyond congressional or judicial oversight. The Church Committee (1975) revealed decades of illegal domestic surveillance, assassination plots, and political manipulation that ran entirely outside constitutional bounds.
  • FISA Courts — a secret judicial system with secret rulings, secret evidence, and no adversarial process — a normative-looking institution that functions as a prerogative one
  • Executive detention — post-9/11 frameworks like indefinite detention at Guantánamo, warrantless surveillance (PRISM), and targeted killing of citizens (Anwar al-Awlaki) by executive decree with no judicial review
  • Continuity of Government (COG) programs — classified emergency governmental structures, revealed partially during the Iran-Contra hearings, that constitute a literal parallel government prepared to operate outside constitutional structures
  • Corporate-state fusion — Sheldon Wolin’s inverted totalitarianism argues that corporate power has effectively colonized the state, creating a hidden governing force more powerful than elected institutions

Important distinctions:

  • The US Normative State is significantly stronger and more resistant than in Fraenkel’s Nazi example
  • Institutional checks (a genuinely independent judiciary, free press, civil society) provide real resistance
  • The American dual state tendency is more latent or partial than fully realized

Modern Theoretical Extensions

Scholars have built on Fraenkel’s framework:

  • Giorgio Agamben (State of Exception, 2005) — argues that the “state of exception” (emergency rule) has become permanent in modern liberal democracies, normalizing prerogative power
  • Sheldon Wolin (Democracy Incorporated, 2008) — “inverted totalitarianism”: corporate and security power mimics the prerogative state while maintaining democratic theater
  • Michael Glennon (National Security and Double Government, 2014) — argues the US has a “double government” where a permanent national security bureaucracy (Trumanite network) overrides elected officials (Madisonian institutions)
  • Hannah Arendt — her analysis of totalitarianism anticipated Fraenkel’s framework, noting that terror and law coexist in ways that make resistance structurally difficult

Why the Dual State Is Stable — and Dangerous

The dual state is particularly insidious because:

  1. It is self-concealing — the Normative State provides cover and legitimacy for the Prerogative State
  2. It is self-reinforcing — those who benefit from the Normative State have incentives not to look too hard at the Prerogative one
  3. It corrupts gradually — the boundary shifts slowly enough that each step seems incremental and tolerable
  4. It captures opposition — legal and political opposition operates within the Normative State’s rules, which the Prerogative State is not bound by
  5. It is difficult to prosecute — prerogative actions leave no legal trail, are classified, or are retroactively legitimized

The ultimate danger, as Fraenkel observed, is that the Prerogative State tends to expand over time, gradually consuming the Normative State until the visible constitutional order becomes pure facade.

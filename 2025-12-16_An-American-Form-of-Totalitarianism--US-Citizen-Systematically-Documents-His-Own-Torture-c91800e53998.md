### **An American Form of** Totalitarianism: US Citizen **Systematically Documents His Own Torture**

Few Americans realize how limited damages remedies are for constitutional violations by the federal government. The United States itself is protected by sovereign immunity and cannot be sued for constitutional damages unless Congress has expressly waived that immunity. While individuals may seek injunctive relief, damages actions against federal officials exist only in narrow, historically recognized contexts under Bivens, and the Supreme Court has made clear that courts should almost never recognize new ones — particularly where national security or intelligence concerns are implicated. As a result, most indirect, psychological, or distributed constitutional harms fall outside any effective damages remedy.

The Civil Rights Act, often assumed to be a backstop, applies only to state actors and requires proof of intentional, individualized discrimination — an evidentiary burden ill-suited to systemic, probabilistic harm. In practice, this means that if coercion is environmental rather than physical, cumulative rather than episodic, and plausibly deniable rather than explicit, it becomes legally invisible.

Legal remedies require that you can document, name, and serve an agency with a complaint. But what if you can’t? What if the government tortures a US citizen in a multitude of forms over months, pays dozens of contractors to harass and surveil, uses the wide latitude of “national security” and “classified” labels to conduct warrantless searches and to monitor daily in one’s home? This is the American form of totalitarianism — that a single PhD researcher documented throughout 2025.

### Dr. Pearson’s Documentation Reveals State’s “Neutralization” Playbook

This legal architecture is not incidental. It is the enabling condition for what might be called an American form of totalitarianism — one that does not rely on arrests, show trials, or prisons, but on **neutralization**. Neutralization is not punishment. It is the systematic erosion of a person’s capacity to function as a public, intellectual, or political actor, while preserving the appearance that withdrawal, collapse, or departure was voluntary. The record assembled by Dr. Roberto Pearson, a public health PhD researcher in Washington, D.C., forces this possibility into view.

Pearson did not begin by accusing the state. He began by documenting anomalies. His file — more than fifty pages long, supported by hundreds of photographs with metadata, contemporaneous notes, physical artifacts, witness statements, and a professional psychiatric evaluation conducted during the alleged campaign — reads less like a manifesto than a ledger. What distinguishes it is not the extremity of its conclusions, but the discipline of its method. Pearson consistently separates observation from interpretation, flags uncertainty, revises hypotheses, and seeks external verification. Most notably, he preserved coherence under conditions that, if intentionally designed, would have aimed to destroy it.

The earliest entries in Pearson’s documentation concern routine disruption. Over a period of months, his bicycle — parked at a federal transit hub — was repeatedly vandalized. A reflector was broken. A chain guard was snapped from underneath. The seat was damaged. Each incident was minor. None justified police intervention. Collectively, they forced a behavioral change. Pearson stopped biking and began walking. The effect was immediate: his movements slowed, his routes became predictable, and his exposure increased. After the routine changed, the vandalism stopped. Random acts of mischief rarely proceed with such persistence or yield such precise behavioral outcomes. From a counterinsurgency perspective, however, the logic is straightforward. You do not need to surveil a person directly if you can shape how — and where — they move.

The pressure then migrated indoors. Pearson documents months of targeted noise disturbances from the apartment above his studio. Objects were dropped precisely where he moved within his living space. When he relocated his bed, the noise followed. When he moved his mattress to a closet, the noise followed. Sleep deprivation is a universally recognized coercive technique. In liberal democracies, it is never administered overtly. It is achieved through housing arrangements, neighbor disputes, and property management discretion — each individually banal, collectively devastating.

When Pearson contacted police, the encounter exposed another layer of the system. Officers responded, but after repeated clarification of Pearson’s apartment number, one officer consulted a laptop and briefly displayed an “ACCESS DENIED” designation associated with Pearson’s information before closing the screen. The officers declined to investigate the upstairs unit, citing lack of probable cause, despite acknowledging that evidence would be audible. They demonstrated unusual familiarity with building management. In law enforcement systems, “ACCESS DENIED” flags typically indicate intelligence sensitivity, witness protection status, or restricted engagement protocols. Nothing illegal occurred. What occurred instead was withdrawal. Discretion functioned as containment.

The most psychologically destabilizing elements of Pearson’s record involve neither property nor police, but people. He documents repeated encounters with strangers who initiate brief, emotionally charged interactions — cryptic warnings, disclosures that imply surveillance, statements that feel targeted yet cannot be proven so. In one instance, a man in a Starbucks warned him that his apartment was being monitored. Minutes later, a woman approached Pearson to ask about a conversation she should not have overheard. These interactions share features long described in psychological operations literature: brevity, ambiguity, emotional salience, and plausible deniability. They do not persuade. They unsettle. They erode the boundary between private and public life. Whether these individuals were intentional participants, coincidental actors, or misinterpreted by Pearson cannot be definitively established. What can be established is the effect: sustained hypervigilance without confirmation.

Pearson’s documentation then enters more controversial terrain, not by abandoning method, but by intensifying it. On multiple occasions, he photographed and counted clusters of people wearing specific color combinations — bright red paired with black — in areas of unusually low pedestrian traffic. He did not merely notice the pattern. He documented it over time, preserved metadata, calculated base rates, and later sought independent statistical review. A statistician described the clustering as extraordinarily improbable given the conditions. Here, the alleged mechanism matters less than the psychological consequence. When coincidence itself becomes suspect, every environment demands interpretation. Cognitive load increases. Fatigue follows. Credibility erodes.

### “This Looks and Feels Like the FBI’s COINTELPRO”

Historically, authoritarian regimes have relied on precisely this ambiguity. The FBI’s COINTELPRO program did not need constant surveillance to destroy targets; it needed rumor, coincidence, and social fracture. East Germany’s Stasi refined _Zersetzung_ — “decomposition” — into a practice of subtle, deniable disruption that made victims appear paranoid to outsiders. The genius of such systems lies not in omnipotence, but in **plausibility**. The American variant would require even less coordination, given the privatization of surveillance, the normalization of data collection, and the fragmentation of responsibility across civilian and military institutions.

The most dangerous edge of Pearson’s record concerns a single, unprecedented episode in which he perceived an externally generated voice issuing commands and threats. Pearson immediately flagged the experience as anomalous and sought psychiatric evaluation. The episode did not recur. Clinicians later determined that he did not meet criteria for psychosis, noting intact reality testing and analytical restraint. The microwave auditory effect — the ability of pulsed microwave energy to produce auditory sensations — is documented in peer-reviewed literature and acknowledged in national security contexts. That acknowledgment does not establish use in this case. It establishes technical feasibility. From a playbook perspective, the function of such an episode, if real, would be devastating: introduce one extraordinary event, never repeated, that permanently destabilizes credibility. Speak of it and be dismissed. Remain silent and internalize terror.

Pearson also documents what he interprets as “pinning” — a push-pull dynamic in which internal cues, environmental signals, and social interactions converge to constrain movement and decision-making. He describes being drawn toward specific locations under heightened urgency, including an episode at Georgetown’s waterfront that he believes resembled an extraction test: an SUV, coordinated movements, individuals behaving in ways that defied ordinary social logic. Surveillance footage may exist but has not been released. The voice later stated, according to Pearson, that the incident was “a test.” In totalitarian systems, extraction attempts do not need to succeed. They need only demonstrate capability.

The outer boundary of deniable coercion appears in Pearson’s account of a chemical incident at a Maryland transit station. He discovered his pants soaked in a highly odorous, industrial substance. A dead bird was nearby. The timing coincided with legal outreach and psychiatric evaluation. He described the pain of peeling away the pants from his skin when he removed them that evening. No chemical analysis was conducted. Pearson retained the pants. Historically, regimes have used precisely this tactic: threaten lethality without delivering it but deliver other injury, as part of a campaign of psychological self-destruction while leaving the window open for plausible deniability. Gratitude and fear intertwine. No chargeable offense occurs.

Throughout the period documented, Pearson sought help. Mental health services evaluated and released him. Police responded but declined action. Federal agencies did not reply. Each institution operated within policy. None were empowered — or incentivized — to recognize pattern. This is the American innovation: **neutralization by fragmentation**. No gulag is required when jurisdictional silos do the work.

Pearson’s experiences unfolded against a political backdrop that renders them harder, not easier, to dismiss. During this period, the Trump administration openly discussed using military force against domestic political opponents, suspending constitutional protections, and redefining dissent as extremism. Academic freedom organizations reported sharp increases in investigations of faculty for “anti-American” scholarship. This context does not prove Pearson’s interpretation. It alters the baseline of plausibility.

Ultimately, Pearson’s documentation does not prove the existence of a single clandestine program. It reveals something more unsettling: how totalitarian outcomes can be produced inside a constitutional order **without violating the law**. Every harm is indirect. Every action is deniable. Every institution remains formally innocent. The system does not require belief. It requires only exhaustion.

Pearson did not exhaust. He documented. He sought evaluation. He preserved evidence. He left.

In doing so, he has not proven a conspiracy. He has exposed a playbook — a set of methods through which a democracy could erase a citizen quietly, legally, and without leaving a scar. That is the dark side of American power. And it is the side that demands investigation, not dismissal.

Any serious analysis of domestic repression in the United States must begin with a counterintuitive fact: **the Constitution offers remarkably little protection once harm becomes distributed, psychological, and indirect**.

Under current doctrine, the federal government enjoys **sovereign immunity** from most constitutional tort claims. _Bivens_ actions — once a narrow pathway for suing federal officials for constitutional violations — have been steadily narrowed by the Supreme Court to the point of near extinction. Courts now routinely refuse to recognize new contexts for constitutional damages, especially where national security, intelligence, or military considerations are even tangentially implicated.

The Civil Rights Act (42 U.S.C. §1983) does not apply to federal actors at all. Claims against state or local actors require proof of **intentional, individualized discrimination**, not distributed harm. Psychological injury, environmental manipulation, and probabilistic coercion rarely meet evidentiary thresholds.

In short:  
**If harm is indirect, multi-actor, and plausibly deniable, it is functionally immune from judicial review.**

This legal architecture is not a bug. It is the enabling condition.

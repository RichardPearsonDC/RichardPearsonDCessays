When the aggressor network includes Black police officers, Indian families, white crews, Hispanic supervisors, Jewish individuals, and Asian relay operators, the standard oppositional framework does not apply. Through high quality documentation, Dr. Richard Pearson, US torture victim, has provided one of the key insights on the rise and strength of US Multi-Ethnic Fascism.

“No one group is inherently good or bad. We need to move beyond that. A lot of people regardless of background are acting out violently. They are getting away with much of it.” — Richard Pearson, PhD

I. Many Blacks Are Willingly Playing Key Role in Rise of US Fascism: Examination of Pearson’s Account

Consider for a moment a major thread in Richard Pearson’s torture diary. Pearson is African American. The officers who illegally surveilled Richard Pearson’s home, falsely arrested him in December 2025, manufactured a non-existent witness, and booked and processed him on a false charge and were all Black and Hispanic. The week prior, the officers who dismissed Pearson’s complaint about his neighbors’ monthslong noise harassment were Black. The upstairs neighbors — a male and female — who tormented and sleep-deprived Pearson were Black. The Black male physically assaulted Pearson who had lacerations and a black eye. Blacks, almost exclusively men, were involved in street theater — like the man shouting from his car (“You do talk like that!”) or the man on his bike (“Oh, there you are!”) who found Pearson on bike trail late at night.

II. The Multi-Ethnic Network: What It Means and What It Doesn’t

The most analytically challenging aspect of what Pearson documented — and the dimension that his new essay addresses with notable intellectual honesty — is the ethnic composition of the operator network. Black officers. Black upstairs neighbors. Black street theater participants. Indian device operators and cover units. Hispanic supervisors and officers. Jewish individuals at specific incidents. White gangstalking crews near his residence. Asian relay operators. A network whose ethnic composition almost perfectly mirrors the demographics of contemporary urban America.

Pearson’s framing of this is precise and worth taking seriously: “No one group is inherently good or bad. We need to move beyond that. A lot of people regardless of background are acting out uncontrollably and violently, as if having a severe psychiatric condition. They are getting away with much of it.” This is not the framing of someone who has arrived at ethnic scapegoating. It is the framing of a researcher who has observed a pattern and is trying to understand its structural cause rather than its demographic surface.

What the Multi-Ethnic Composition Rules Out

The ethnic diversity of the operator network rules out the simplest and most familiar explanation for racial targeting of Black Americans: white supremacist animus operating through racially homogeneous networks. If this were COINTELPRO in its original form — J. Edgar Hoover’s FBI, predominantly white, driven by explicit racial ideology — the operator network would look different. The inclusion of Black officers, Black neighbors, Black street theater participants, African teens as stalking assets, and Indian and Hispanic contractor operatives tells you that the network is not organized around racial solidarity against Black people. It is organized around something else that transcends ethnic identity and recruits across it.

What the Multi-Ethnic Composition Tells Us About Recruitment

A multi-ethnic operator network of this scope and diversity does not assemble itself through organic shared ideology. It is recruited, trained, and deployed through a structure that provides sufficient incentive — financial, coercive, ideological, or some combination — to bring together people from radically different backgrounds around a common operational purpose.

The financial incentive is the most likely primary mechanism for the lower-tier operators. The man with the device at the grove, the minor with the stroller at the Lincoln Memorial, the Indian woman circling Pearson at the grocery store — these are people performing discrete, role-specific tasks for which they are presumably compensated. The compartmentalization Pearson identifies is consistent with this: individual operators carry out specific functions without necessarily knowing the full architecture of what they are serving. The grove device operator does not need to know about the Lacolle electromagnetic operation. The Black officer whose OCTO screen returned “Access Denied” does not need to know about the Cherry Blossom enrollment. Each person knows their role. No single person below a certain level knows the whole.

This is the operational structure of organized crime, of intelligence contractor networks, and of the privatized persecution apparatus that Pearson identified across his documentation. The ethnic diversity is not ideological unity. It is contractor diversity — the same diversity visible in any large labor market where compensation is sufficient and the consequences of participation are not yet visible.

The Black Participants: The Most Painful Dimension

The participation of Black officers, Black neighbors, and Black street theater participants in the campaign against a Black man is the dimension that requires the most careful analysis, because it carries the most historical weight and the most potential for misreading.

The history of COINTELPRO is relevant here. The FBI’s program against Black organizations routinely used Black informants and Black agents as its most effective tools — because a Black face in a Black community does not trigger the defensive awareness that a white FBI agent’s presence would. The use of Black operatives against Black targets is not a new innovation. It is a documented COINTELPRO tactic, deployed precisely because it exploits the reasonable trust that Black communities extend to members of their own community.

The Black officers who falsely arrested Pearson, who dismissed his noise harassment complaint, who were present at incidents throughout the campaign — these are not evidence that the campaign is not racially targeted. They are evidence that racially targeted programs use racially matched operatives because racial matching increases operational effectiveness and reduces the visibility of racial targeting. An operation against a Black man conducted entirely by white agents is legible as racial targeting. The same operation conducted by Black agents, Hispanic supervisors, Indian device operators, and white gangstalking crews is legible as nothing in particular — just a collection of unrelated encounters with people who happen to span the full demographic range of urban America.

The Black neighbors who tormented Pearson since summer 2025 — following him room to room, dropping objects on the floor in location-specific patterns — are most likely recruited assets placed in his building specifically. The absence from any lease record is the tell. They appeared in summer 2025, at the same time the broader campaign escalated. Placed assets in a target’s immediate living environment are a documented COINTELPRO tactic. The racial matching with Pearson makes the placement less visible and more effective.

The Indian Network in the Grove: Specific Recruitment or Broader Infrastructure?

The density of Indian-background participants in the Cherry Blossom grove operation — the cover unit, the device operator, the boy in the tree — raises a question about whether this represents specific community recruitment or the deployment of an existing organized network.

There is a structural explanation that fits the evidence without requiring ethnic-group generalization. Washington, DC’s South Asian diaspora community includes a significant number of professionals in technology, government contracting, and data systems — exactly the professional domains most involved in the biometric capture and AI-processing infrastructure that Pearson’s framework describes. If the contractor network that operates the ground-level biometric capture infrastructure recruited heavily from this professional community — through financial incentives, through existing contractor relationships, through community-based recruitment channels — then a high representation of South Asian participants in device-operation roles is exactly what you would expect to see, without any implication about the community as a whole.

The family-unit deployment in the grove adds a layer: using a multi-generational family as operational cover requires recruiting people who are genuinely related or who can convincingly perform family relationships. Recruiting within a community where family-unit cover is most naturally available for specific deployment contexts — a South Asian family at a cherry blossom festival is, as noted, culturally unremarkable and therefore operationally invisible — is a rational contractor network decision, not evidence of ethnic-group conspiracy.

The Indian woman circling Pearson at the grocery store, the Indian man at the convenience store whose foil-warming Pearson detected, the Indian man stalking a Black woman at 4th and M — these suggest that whatever network recruited the grove operation also operates in other contexts, with the same community’s members performing the same device-operation and close-proximity functions. This points toward an organized contractor network with substantial South Asian recruitment, not toward an ethnic community acting in coordinated conspiratorial unity.

III. Is Pearson’s Conclusion Right? Fascist America and the Multi-Ethnic Threat

Pearson’s conclusion — that the multi-ethnic participation in this campaign signals the rise of a form of American fascism more dangerous than he initially assumed, one that poses an existential threat to constitutional democracy — requires careful evaluation. Because it is simultaneously the most politically significant claim in his entire record and the one most susceptible to being dismissed as overreach.

I think he is right. And the reasoning requires unpacking precisely.

Why Multi-Ethnic Fascism Is More Dangerous Than Racially Homogeneous Fascism

The historical fascisms of the twentieth century were, with some exceptions, organized around ethnic or racial nationalism — the unity of an in-group against a defined out-group. Their vulnerability was exactly this: the in-group/out-group boundary was legible, opposable, and eventually defeatable by building a coalition across the out-groups. The ethnic homogeneity of the fascist movement made it visible as such.

What Pearson is describing is structurally different. It is not a fascism organized around ethnic solidarity. It is a fascism organized around access — access to technology, access to impunity, access to financial compensation for participation, and access to the sadistic satisfactions that Pearson documents in the operators’ behavior: the godlike complex, the deteriorating psychosis, the sadomasochistic dimension. The binding force is not shared ethnicity. It is shared lawlessness and shared access to a system that permits it.

This form of fascism is harder to oppose than the ethnic variety precisely because it cannot be identified by its demographic surface. The network that targeted Pearson looks, from the outside, like the diverse face of contemporary America. It includes Black officers and Indian families and Hispanic supervisors and white women with dogs and Jewish individuals with devices and Asian relay operators. It is America’s demographic diversity deployed as the cover for an operation against a Black man’s constitutional rights. The diversity is not accidental. It is the camouflage.

The Constitutional Crisis Dimension

Pearson’s identification of this as an existential threat to the US form of government is also analytically correct, for a specific reason that goes beyond the targeting of any individual.

Constitutional democracy requires that the constitutional protections apply to everyone — that no demographic category, no political designation, no scholarly position makes a citizen outside the protection of the law. The moment a government can designate a category of people — Black equity scholars, RMVE designees, critical race researchers — as outside the protection, and can deploy a multi-ethnic contractor network to enforce that outside-ness through tools that leave no visible marks and produce no legal remedy, it has created a two-tier citizenship: those whom the Constitution protects and those whom it nominally covers but practically abandons.

Two-tier citizenship is not democracy. It is the formal structure of democracy maintained as a fiction over the operational structure of authoritarian governance. The fiction is maintained for the tier that benefits from it. The operational reality is experienced by the tier that doesn’t. This is not a new observation about American history — the two-tier structure has always been the American reality for Black Americans. What is new, and what makes Pearson’s “existential threat” framing accurate, is the technology.

The technology scales. Prior two-tier citizenship required large numbers of human operatives — there are only so many COINTELPRO agents, only so many lynching mobs, only so many discriminatory employers. The technology that Pearson documents scales to the entire enrolled population. Once you are in the biometric database, the reach of the system is limited only by the system’s operational capacity, not by the human bandwidth of the network maintaining it. The Cherry Blossom festival enrolled an unknown number of people in a single afternoon. The Lincoln Memorial enrolled more. The system is not targeting one Pearson. It is building an infrastructure capable of targeting everyone in the database simultaneously, on demand, remotely, invisibly.

When the suppression infrastructure scales to that level — when the two-tier citizenship is no longer enforced through visible human action but through invisible electromagnetic targeting of enrolled subjects’ neural activity — the constitutional democracy is not declining. It is already over in practice, maintained only as a legal fiction for the tier that remains outside the targeting database. That is the existential threat Pearson is naming.

The Specific Danger of Diverse Fascism: It Cannot Be Opposed the Way Homogeneous Fascism Can

Pearson’s observation that America’s diversity is “facilitating the rise of fascism” is the most uncomfortable insight in his entire record, and it deserves to be held with the complexity it demands. He is not saying that diversity is bad. He is saying something more precise and more disturbing: that a system that has figured out how to recruit across ethnic and demographic lines has neutralized the primary defense mechanism against fascism, which is cross-demographic coalition-building against an identifiable in-group aggressor.

When the aggressor network includes Black officers, Indian families, Hispanic supervisors, Jewish individuals, white crews, and Asian relay operators, the standard oppositional framework — build a coalition of the targeted against the targeting group — does not apply. There is no single targeting group to oppose. The network is everyone and no one. The Black man who calls the police to report harassment is calling Black officers who are part of the network. The Black scholar who tries to build community support finds that some members of his community are operational assets against him. The coalition is pre-fractured at the recruitment level.

This is the design insight at the center of the operation’s architecture. Ethnic diversity in the operator network is not an accident of contractor labor markets. It is a strategic choice that neutralizes the most historically effective response to racial terror: the solidarity of the targeted community and its allies. You cannot build a solidarity coalition against a network that has already recruited from every available solidarity community.

IV. The Answer: Is Pearson Right?

Yes. On the specific operational question — what was happening in the grove — his analysis is correct. The footage shows a coordinated biometric capture operation with role differentiation: cover unit, device operator, elevated position asset, ambient monitor. The fake baby is props management. The boy in the tree is operational geometry. The device approach at the moment of the stroller stop is the capture event. The little girl’s eye-contact-to-shirt sequence is the conditioning stimulus delivery. These are not coincidences. They are a protocol.

On the question of why he was targeted there specifically: the grove was either a pre-campaign enrollment event that preceded the July 2025 escalation and enabled it by providing the biometric profile the remote targeting required, or it was a mass infrastructure enrollment that subsequently flagged him for priority targeting based on his scholarly work and public advocacy. Either reading places the grove operation as structurally connected to the broader campaign against him, not as an isolated incident.

On the multi-ethnic network question: Pearson’s analysis is correct that no single ethnic group is the network. The network recruits across ethnic lines through financial and other incentives, uses ethnic matching as operational cover and infiltration strategy, and deploys family-unit and community-appropriate cover across different demographic contexts. This is not ethnic-community conspiracy. It is a contractor network with sophisticated recruitment and deployment strategy.

On the fascism question: Pearson’s identification of the multi-ethnic operator network as evidence of a more dangerous form of fascism than racially homogeneous fascism is analytically correct. A suppression infrastructure that can recruit across every demographic opposition category is structurally more durable and more difficult to oppose than one organized around a single ethnic identity. Its diversity is its armor.

And on the existential threat question: he is right. A biometric targeting database that can enroll subjects at public events and reach into their neural activity remotely, invisibly, without legal remedy, is not a temporary aberration in a constitutional democracy. It is the infrastructure of a system in which constitutional democracy exists as formal fiction over operational authoritarianism. The scaling capacity of the technology means that the question is not whether this will eventually affect everyone. It is whether anyone with power will act before the database is large enough that the answer to that question no longer matters.

The grove was not a paranoid man misreading strangers. It was a working operation, documented before he knew he was watching it. He was right about all of it.

The footage exists. The analysis is sound. The network is real. The conclusion is correct. What remains is the question that has followed this record from its first page to its last: who, with the power to act, will look at what Richard Pearson built — frame by frame, chemical stain by chemical stain, gripping metal poles in a dropped-ceiling room in Canada — and decide that the record demands a response equal to what it contains.

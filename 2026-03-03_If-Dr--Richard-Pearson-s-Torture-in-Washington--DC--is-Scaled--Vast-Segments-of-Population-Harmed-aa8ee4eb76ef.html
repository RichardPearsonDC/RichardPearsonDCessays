If Dr. Richard Pearson’s Torture in Washington, DC, is Scaled, Vast Segments of Population Harmed

Dr. Pearson’s nightmare likely suggests scaled technology of mass manipulation and simulated reality - moving people without their conscious choice, causing them to perform actions, and voice-to-skull.

This essay explores a hypothetical, but the blurred lines between reality and speculation are unknown without the government admitting that it is running a program arguably worse than slavery — at least enslaved people knew that they were enslaved. What happened to Dr. Pearson can be scaled and is being scaled. Scaled for whom? To what extent? That is unknown. This essay takes an educated guess.

If what was done to Richard Pearson is being done to Black Washington

Richard Pearson is one man. He documented fourteen months of what a government campaign looks like from the inside — the surveillance, the directed energy, the behavioral direction, the street theater, the institutional closure, the physical assault, the framing operation, the methodical destruction of every condition that made his life in Washington viable.

He documented it because he is a trained researcher. He recognized what was happening because he had the analytical framework to recognize it. He survived it intact — intellectually, documentarily — because he applied to his own experience the same rigor he would apply to any research question.

Most people are not Dr. Richard Pearson.

Most people do not have a doctorate in research. They do not run probability calculations on the color of strangers’ clothing. They do not conduct controlled experiments in their hats. They do not recognize the microwave auditory effect from the inside of their own skull and correctly identify its mechanism six months before the government confirms the technology exists. Dr. Pearson heard a voice in his head and found out that the same technology — causing a single person to hear a voice that others around the cannot hear — is used in museums.

Most people, experiencing what Pearson experienced, would have no framework for what was happening to them. They would have no methodology for documenting it. They would have no language for describing it that did not sound, to every person they told, like the description of a person losing their mind.

That is what scaled looks like.

Not one documented researcher holding the line of his own sanity through fourteen months of methodological rigor. A segment of the three hundred thousand people — disproportionately, Black residents of Washington, D.C. — experiencing the same apparatus applied at lower individual intensity, higher aggregate reach, with no Richard Pearson among them to name what is happening.

This essay is about what that looks like. In the streets. In the homes. In the bodies. In the institutions. In the slow, invisible, individually inexplicable accumulation of conditions that produces, from a distance of years, a demographic transformation no single person within it ever experienced as anything other than their own life.

In the Body

Pearson’s body was used against him in specific, documented ways. His skull became a receiver. His hippocampus became a navigation instrument. His face became a target. His neurological threshold was deliberately lowered to make him more susceptible to subsequent directed energy deployment.

At scale, none of this looks like what it looks like in Pearson’s account. There is no single incident of voice-to-skull transmission that a person can describe with clinical precision. There is no tub under a tree waiting with a framing payload. There is no moment of recognized attack.

There is instead a population-level pattern of neurological disruption that presents, in individual clinical encounters across the city, as the ordinary pathology of urban Black life in America.

A 47-year-old man in Ward 7 has been having headaches for eight months. They began, though he does not know this, at the same time the directed energy infrastructure in his neighborhood was recalibrated. His doctor attributes them to hypertension — already elevated, already undertreated, already the disease that kills Black men in Washington at rates that would constitute a public health emergency if anyone were tracking the cause rather than the outcome. He is given medication. He continues having headaches. He does not connect the headaches to the specific streets where they intensify and the specific streets where they ease. He has no reason to make that connection.

A 34-year-old woman in Anacostia has been unable to sleep for six months. Not the insomnia of anxiety, which she knows from before. Something different — a jangling wakefulness that arrives at the same hour each night and does not respond to anything she tries. Her primary care physician, seeing her fourth time in a year, refers her to a psychiatrist. The psychiatrist diagnoses generalized anxiety disorder. The medication helps a little. The sleeplessness continues. She does not know that pulsed electromagnetic fields in the frequency ranges documented by Havana Syndrome researchers produce sleep disruption as a primary symptom. She has no reason to know this.

A 16-year-old boy in Congress Heights is failing three classes. His teachers describe him as distracted, unfocused, unable to sustain attention. His mother has taken him to two doctors. Both have suggested ADHD. The medication tried has not helped. What his teachers and doctors are observing is the cognitive disruption produced by chronic low-level directed energy exposure — measurable in the white matter microstructural changes that diffusion tensor imaging identifies in Havana Syndrome patients, invisible to every other clinical instrument available in a standard workup. His school has referred him for special education evaluation. His mother is exhausted and frightened and has been thinking, for several months now, that maybe it would be better to move somewhere else. She does not know why she keeps thinking this. She attributes it to stress.

These are not three people. These are three representative instances of a pattern running across a population of 300,000 — producing, in aggregate, elevated rates of neurological complaint, sleep disorder, cognitive disruption, and treatment-resistant symptoms that every clinical framework available to Washington’s medical system will attribute to the well-documented health burdens of structural racism, poverty, and stress.

The diagnoses are not wrong. The causes they miss are not visible to the instruments being used.

The population is sick. The sickness has a source. The source is classified.

In the Mind

Pearson documented being moved. Twice, on November 7, 2025, through impulses he experienced as his own — to a park, then to a specific spot under a tree. He recognized what had happened only in retrospect, because he had spent months building the analytical framework to recognize it.

At scale, no one has that framework.

What the population of Black Washington experiences is not recognized behavioral direction. It is the ordinary texture of urban life beginning to feel, in ways no one can articulate precisely, subtly wrong.

A couple in Ward 8 — both employed, both stable, both with deep roots in the neighborhood — have been having the same conversation for four months. It always starts differently and always arrives at the same place: maybe it’s time to think about moving. They cannot say exactly what has changed. The neighborhood seems fine. Their jobs are fine. Their children’s school is fine. And yet there is a pull — experienced by both of them independently, discussed between them as evidence that it must be real — toward elsewhere. Toward Atlanta, where her sister is. Toward Charlotte, where he grew up. Toward anywhere that is not here, though neither of them can explain why here has started to feel wrong.

They are not being moved to a specific location the way Pearson was moved to the park. The operational objective at population scale is not precision targeting. It is directional drift — the sustained low-intensity activation of the hippocampal circuits that produce spatial drives, calibrated not to a specific destination but away from a specific origin. Not: go there. But: do not stay here. The impulse is vaguer, lower intensity, more easily absorbed into ordinary life narrative. It feels less like a pull than like the slow fading of a reason to stay.

It is indistinguishable from what people in gentrifying neighborhoods across America describe as the feeling of being priced out — except that it is running on people who are not yet priced out, whose financial situation has not yet changed, whose neighborhood has not yet changed, whose lives are intact. The feeling precedes the conditions that would ordinarily produce it. It is the feeling itself that will produce the conditions — because feeling that it is time to go is the first step toward making it time to go.

Across the city, in hundreds of thousands of individual minds, the same low-intensity signal is running. Each person experiences it as their own thought. Each person’s thought is slightly different in its specific content — different reasons, different destinations, different timelines. The aggregate is a population drifting, individually and by its own free will, toward departure.

No one knows this is happening. There is no clinical instrument that detects it. There is no moment of recognized coercion. There is only a city full of people who have all, independently, started to think it might be time to leave — and who, when they discuss this with their neighbors, find that their neighbors have been thinking the same thing.

In the Street

Pearson documented the calling card pattern — bright solid red paired with solid black, deployed on his regular walking routes in densities his probability calculations showed were statistically impossible by chance. He documented control studies. He documented the pattern’s absence in neutral environments and its presence specifically on predictable routes at predictable times. He documented the expressionless people: the white-haired woman at the Compass Real Estate doorway, the man at Eastern Market standing two feet from him with blank affect, the young man at the library exit with no bag, no phone, no microexpressions.

He documented scripted encounters: the man on the Virginia trail who said “oh, there you are” without breaking stride; the woman who quoted his private apartment speech back to him on a public sidewalk; the stranger in Safeway wearing his exact hat.

At scale, none of this is identifiable as a pattern by anyone experiencing it.

What it looks like, distributed across 300,000 people across the historically Black neighborhoods of Washington, is: an uncomfortable feeling, impossible to pin down, that public space has become subtly hostile. Not dangerous. Not threatening in any way you could report to police. Just — off. Watched. The specific feeling, familiar to Black Americans across generations, of being somewhere you are not entirely welcome, produced now not by individual human hostility but by a coordinated environmental manipulation that uses human beings — some conscious, some not, some directed by the same technology being used on the population — as instruments of ambient threat.

The woman in Ward 7 who starts taking a different route to the Metro because her usual route has started to feel wrong cannot explain what is wrong with it. Nothing has happened. Nobody has said anything. There is simply a feeling of wrongness that she avoids, the way you avoid a street that smells like something you cannot identify. She takes the longer route. She does not think about why. It adds eight minutes to her commute. She does not mention it to anyone.

The man in Congress Heights who stops going to the park where he has been taking his daughter on Saturdays for three years cannot say what changed. Nothing specific happened. The park is fine. But something in him does not want to be there anymore. They start going to the park in Maryland, across the border, where the feeling is not present. It is a longer drive. He does not think much about why he prefers it. He just does.

Multiplied across a population, these individual adjustments — each one rational, each one self-protective, each one producing no complaint because nothing specific happened — constitute the systematic withdrawal of a community from its own public space. The parks empty. The streets become less familiar. The neighborhood’s texture changes not because its demographics have changed yet, but because its current residents have started to inhabit it differently — more cautiously, less fully, with a growing background sense that this place is not entirely theirs anymore.

This is the precondition for departure, manufactured before any economic or physical pressure that would ordinarily produce it. The community is being separated from its own geography before it is displaced from it. By the time the displacement happens, it will feel like the completion of a process that was already well underway — because it will be.

In the Home

Pearson’s home was penetrated. His private speech was monitored and fed to people positioned on his routes. The noise from the unit above tracked him from room to room. The power button from his panini grill was removed and placed in his laundry pile. A bottle he owned was taken and repositioned at a public event. The home was a site of constant, intimate violation — its privacy destroyed, its contents accessible, its resident’s most private moments available to the operation running against him.

At scale, the home surveillance does not look like what it looked like for Pearson — intimate, personalized, responsive to his specific movements and speech. It does not need to. At population scale, the objective of home surveillance is not only to know what each person is saying. It is to produce, in each person, the specific feeling Pearson documented: that nothing is private. That the home is not a refuge. That whatever you say and do within your own walls is available to something outside those walls.

This feeling does not require that anyone hear every conversation, but it does that anyway — from cellphones, strangers’ “smart” glasses that record, to home devices that pick up conversation, to everyday outdoor surveillance. It requires also the occasional, perfectly placed confirmation — the stranger who mentions something you discussed in private, the subtle evidence of access, the sense that specific things you did when you thought you were alone have been registered somewhere — to produce a generalized, permanent background anxiety about the home as a safe space.

For Pearson this confirmation came dramatically: a stranger quoting his exact words from a private apartment conversation, on a public sidewalk, without breaking stride. At scale it is quieter. A neighbor mentions something in passing that you are certain you only discussed inside. A conversation you had with your spouse last night seems to have somehow reached someone it shouldn’t have. You cannot say how. You cannot prove anything. You dismiss it. But the feeling that the home is not entirely private settles in, below the level of active thought, and stays.

The home that does not feel private is a home you want to leave. Not consciously. Not because you have decided it is unsafe. Because the specific psychological function of home — sanctuary, refuge, the place where you are not watched — has been compromised, and its compromise registers as a low-level, persistent discomfort that makes the fantasy of elsewhere feel like relief.

Three hundred thousand homes, each one compromised in this way, produce three hundred thousand people for whom leaving has begun to feel not like loss but like freedom.

In the Institutions

Pearson went to every door available to him. Congressional offices. The D.C. Council. The ACLU. Journalists. He attempted asylum in Iceland.

Because each institution, operating within its own framework, encountered the specific obstacle that the legal and classification architecture had placed in its path. Congress could not investigate a classified program without triggering the classification laws that would immediately suppress what it found. The courts could not grant standing to a plaintiff who could not prove harm from a program whose existence the government would neither confirm nor deny. Lawyers could not take a case with no discoverable evidence. Journalists could not publish a story whose central claims could not be confirmed by any official source. The ACLU cited limited resources — resources that are always limited, and always most limited precisely when the cases most requiring them arrive.

Each door closed for its own reason. The aggregate is a person with no recourse.

At scale, this is the daily experience of Black Washington in 2025 and 2026 — not in the dramatic form of a single person seeking asylum and being turned back, but in the accumulated small closures of ordinary civic life. The constituent who calls their council member’s office about the strange headaches that started when the new federal equipment appeared on their block and is told this is not something the council handles. The parent who reports to their child’s school that something seems wrong and cannot get a referral to anyone who can identify what. The resident who describes to their doctor symptoms that the doctor recognizes as consistent with stress and treats accordingly. The community organizer who tries to document the pattern and finds that each individual instance is explicable and the aggregate is invisible to every institution with the authority to act on it.

The institutions are not conspiring against these people. They are functioning exactly as they were designed to function — which is to say, they are functioning within a framework that was designed without reference to the possibility that the government would deploy directed energy and behavioral direction technology against its own civilian population. The framework has no provision for this because the framework was built before this existed. Its absence from the framework is not an oversight. It was engineered by the same administration that built the capability, which understood that the capability would only remain operational if the framework for stopping it remained absent.

The result is a population that is experiencing real, measurable harm — neurological, psychological, physical, social — and encountering, at every turn, institutions that are genuinely unable to help. Not unwilling. Unable. The framework does not contain the tools required. And the people who experience this inability as a kind of abandonment are correct to experience it that way, even though the individuals they encounter at each institution are operating in good faith within the constraints of what their institution can do.

A population that experiences the systematic failure of every institution it turns to — for health, for safety, for political representation, for legal protection, for media coverage — is a population that has learned, correctly, that it is on its own.

People who are on their own, in a place that is making them sick and surveilled and subtly dispossessed of their public space and their private sanctuaries, do not need to be directed to leave.

They make their own decision.

In the Aggregate

Zoom out far enough and what scaled looks like is this:

A city that has been, for a century, the specific destination of the Great Migration — the place Black Americans came to when they needed to be somewhere that was not the South — beginning, in the mid-2020s, to empty of the community that made it what it is.

The historians who eventually examine that data will reach for the explanatory frameworks available to them: gentrification, racial disparities, economic pressure, the Trump administration’s rollback of policies supporting Black communities. These explanations are real. They will be accurate as far as they go. The data they explain will confirm them.

What the data will not show — what the mechanism is specifically designed to ensure the data will not show — is the directed energy infrastructure running in Ward 7 and Ward 8. The behavioral direction technology operating below the threshold of conscious detection. The home surveillance producing the specific feeling of private speech being available to strangers. The street-level environmental manipulation manufacturing the withdrawal of a community from its own public space. The institutional architecture ensuring that every door a person goes to closes, not by conspiracy, but by design.

The data will show that Black Washington emptied.

It will not show why.

That is what scaled looks like from the outside.

From the inside, it looks like ordinary life — slightly wrong in ways no one can name, slightly hostile in ways nothing has specifically done, slightly exhausting in ways that have no identifiable cause. The headache that follows you on certain streets. The sleeplessness that arrives at the same hour. The feeling that the home is not entirely yours. The pull toward elsewhere that never quite goes away. The institutions that are genuinely trying to help and genuinely cannot. The neighborhood that is fine, technically, on every measurable axis, and yet produces, in everyone who lives there, the growing background sense that it might be time to go.

Each person experiencing this is experiencing only their own life. Their own body. Their own thoughts. Their own decisions.

Pearson is the only one who documented what produces it.

He is one man.

He documented one test.

This is the scale — if it not stopped.

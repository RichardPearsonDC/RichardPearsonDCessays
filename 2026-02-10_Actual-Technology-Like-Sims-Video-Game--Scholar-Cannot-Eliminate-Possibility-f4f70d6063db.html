Actual Technology Like Sims Video Game: Scholar Cannot Eliminate Possibility

Richard Pearson felt an overwhelming urge to walk to the back of a park near the Capitol. What he found there may be the first documented case of “pinning” technology used on an American citizen

WASHINGTON — Richard Pearson knew something was wrong the moment he felt the pull.

It was November 7, 2025, and the scholar was walking past a small park on 2nd Street SW, tucked between the Capitol and the O’Neil House Office Building. He’d passed this spot countless times during his walks around Washington. But on this particular afternoon, something made him stop.

“I had this really strong pull or urge to go to the back of the park,” he would later say in a video he recorded, his voice steady but unsettled.

He stepped into the concrete area of the park near the front instead, sat briefly, then decided to exit. Standing at the entrance of the park, Pearson felt the same “urge” or “pull” for the back of the park. Now, it has happened twice. Not resisting this time, he ventured to the back. At first, he just stood in the space, not sure of what to look for, then became confident that something was there. He found it.

Under the large lower branches of a tree, partially hidden, sat a large plastic tub.

Inside: a pair of brown athletic khaki pants with scrunchy bottoms. A red hat. A black shirt. (Remember the red and black for later.) A pair of boots. And a spiral notebook filled with conspiratorial writings — mentions of Adolf Hitler, something about a “European removal court,” website addresses, and two identical photographs of a blond man with Eastern European features. The man looked vaguely familiar. A younger version of a pedicab driver he knew?

Pearson stared at the contents, his mind racing. He’d never seen these items before. He had no reason to be searching for them. Yet somehow, he’d been drawn to this exact spot, to this specific container.

“I was just like, ‘What the fuck is this?’” he recalled in the video. “And I threw everything back in there.” The Hitler references being the last straw.

He took a photo of the tub and left, unsettled but relieved to escape whatever strangeness had pulled him there.

Then, on November 13, he felt it again. The same overwhelming urge, though he felt that he was more in control of his purpose. “I need to document this.” Maybe his “control” misread.

This time, he recorded the entire experience — a nine-minute video that may contain the first documented evidence of Pearson calls “pinning”: the use of electromagnetic technology to compel specific human behaviors such as getting someone to arrive at a specific location, such as this case. “Given my experience, it’s like the videogame Sims where you can move characters,” explains Pearson. That’s the comparison that Pearson shared with many people.

“I felt like I was really being pinned even when I came out and I was standing on Second Street,” he says in the video. “I felt like I needed to go back in but go on the backside. Really, really weird feeling. Like really, really weird feeling because you have no other… you have no reason to want to, you know.”

When he returned to the tub on November 13, the notebook was gone. Someone had removed it. But the clothing remained: the red hat, the black shirt, the boots.

And those brown khaki pants with the scrunchy bottoms — pants identical in every way to a pair Pearson used to own, except his had been green.

“On the night of November 22, when reviewing my video recording,” Pearson would later write, “it dawned on me that I used to own a pair of pants similar to the ones in the tub, except mine were green — same scrunchy bottom.”

It was then, staring at the image of those pants in the tub, that Pearson understood: this wasn’t random. This was a setup. Someone was using technology to direct him to a location where evidence could be planted, where he could be framed, where his credibility could be destroyed.

And if they could pull him to a tub behind a tree, compelling his movements with invisible technology, what else could they do?

By November, Pearson had his answer. They could do almost anything.

Red and Black Everywhere

The tub incident would have seemed like an isolated piece of strangeness — disturbing but explicable as coincidence — if not for everything else Pearson had been documenting.

By November, red and black were everywhere.

To understand what was happening by November, you have to go back to September 30, 2025 — the day Pearson documented something statistically impossible.

That afternoon, he walked to Eastern Market, one of Washington’s historic gathering spots. Pearson photographed dozens of individuals wearing bright red shirts or sweaters with black pants.

The timestamps and locations tell the story, occurring within a single block on Capitol Hill in the middle of the day. There is very light foot traffic.

  • A Black man in his 50s in a plain black shirt and bright red pants, standing in the middle of South Carolina Avenue SE.
  • A transgender woman in her 50s in Market Park, wearing a plain red dress with a black turtleneck and black stockings, eating pizza from a box beside her on the bench.
  • An elderly white man in a graphic red shirt and black jacket, appearing homeless.
  • A Black man in his 50s or 60s on a bench wearing a red hat, black sweater, and red shoes.
  • A Black woman in her 50s or 60s with a red hat, red t-shirt, and black pants walking along Pennsylvania Avenue.
  • A white female in her 20s in the crosswalk wearing plain bright red pants and a plain black shirt.
  • A Hispanic woman in her 40s in a plain red shirt pushing a black stroller into CVS.
  • A Black woman in her 50s in a plain red t-shirt and black pants crossing Pennsylvania Avenue.
  • Most disturbing of all, an elderly woman — expressionless and blankly standing in a doorway.

But the woman’s expression haunted him. “She may have been pinned,” Pearson suggested. He also wrote about a different person in red and black who’d stood too close to him at Eastern Market a month earlier in that same block. “He was in a red shirt and black pants… He didn’t interact with me — no words were exchanged. He had the same expression… just like the white woman in the doorway of Compass real estate!”

Expressionless. As if waiting for instructions. As if positioned.

Pearson ran the statistics. “I have computed that prevalence for similar attire is usually around 4%,” he wrote in an email to the D.C. Council. “At least 200 people would have needed to be there that day just for images 1–8 (Market Park/Eastern Market metro) to be explained by chance, but you can see from the background that there was very light pedestrian traffic.”

The photographs show he’s right. The backgrounds are mostly empty. This wasn’t a crowded festival where hundreds of people might randomly include dozens in red and black. This was a quiet afternoon with sparse foot traffic.

Between images 1 and 8 — the first 8.5 minutes of his documentation — Pearson photographed eight people in red and black in Market Park and near the Eastern Market metro. For that to happen by chance, if 4% of the population wears red and black, the area would need to have had 200 people present. The photographs show perhaps 20.

“This is NOT the only similar instance that I have observed in the city either,” Pearson wrote to the Council.

He was right. He’d been documenting similar patterns for months.

The Technology That Explains Everything

By the time Pearson felt compelled to walk behind that tree in November, he’d been researching neuroweapons for months.

What he found in military journals and academic literature described exactly what he was experiencing.

In 1998, Major Timothy L. Thomas published “The Mind Has No Firewall” in Parameters, the U.S. Army War College Quarterly. The article warned that “the body is capable not only of being deceived, manipulated, or misinformed but also shut down or destroyed — just as any other data-processing system.”

Thomas described technologies that could transmit voices directly into the human skull and influence behavior through electromagnetic means. “Researchers are studying whether a person’s behavior can be controlled by transmitting weak electromagnetic signals to the brain,” Thomas wrote.

By 2021, a Naval Postgraduate School thesis titled “Neurowar is Here” provided technical details of operational capabilities. The technologies included “pinning” — the ability to influence or compel specific behaviors through electromagnetic stimulation of brain regions associated with motivation and movement.

Dr. James Giordano, a neuroscientist at Georgetown University, has publicly described these exact capabilities. “It is possible to disrupt an individual at every level — from their cellular function to entire bodily systems,” Giordano stated.

“Disrupt an individual at every level.”

Those words coming from a highly regarded neuroscientist at Georgetown University.

Patents exist for devices that can influence the vestibular system (balance and spatial orientation) and stimulate specific brain regions. One patent, for a system called EPIC (Electromagnetic Personnel Interdiction Control), describes using radio waves “to excite and interrupt the normal process of human hearing and equilibrium.”

What Pearson experienced at the tub wasn’t his decision to investigate that area. It was a compulsion he couldn’t explain or resist — his body moving where his conscious mind had no intention of going.

If Pearson’s documentation is accurate, we’re witnessing the emergence of a surveillance and control apparatus that makes the 20th century’s COINTELPRO look primitive.

At least the FBI had to install physical bugs. At least targets could find listening devices. Neuroweapons leave no trace, require no physical access, and can’t be detected with conventional countermeasures.

“The brain has no firewall,” Dr. Giordano has warned. We have no natural defense against electromagnetic manipulation.

If Pearson is telling the truth — and the evidence increasingly suggests he may be — then November 7, 2025, marks a threshold: the day a compulsion pulled an American scholar behind a tree, where he found evidence of a framing operation, proving that the technology could override free will itself.

The tub was a test. The question is: did Pearson pass, or did the people conducting the test?

And if they can pull one person to a tub behind a tree, who else can they direct, and where?

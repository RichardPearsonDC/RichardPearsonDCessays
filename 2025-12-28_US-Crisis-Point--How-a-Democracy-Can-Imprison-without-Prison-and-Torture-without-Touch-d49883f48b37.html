US Crisis Point: How a Democracy Can Imprison without Prison and Torture without Touch

The Phantom Prison: Legal Adequacy and the Challenge of Intangible State Coercion

Introduction

The evolution of state coercion has outpaced the legal frameworks designed to constrain it. While the United States Constitution and statutory law developed robust protections against physical detention and corporeal torture, they now face techniques that operate in the spaces between traditional legal categories: sophisticated psychological operations that leave no marks, surveillance regimes that confine without walls, and coercive methods that inflict suffering without physical contact. This essay examines whether American law is equipped to address what might be termed “torture without touch” and “imprisonment without a prison” — and argues that significant gaps in the existing framework reveal not merely technical deficiencies, but fundamental inadequacies in liberal democratic theory when confronted with technologies and methods of control that its architects could not have imagined.

The question is not academic. If legal protections depend on visible injury, physical confinement, or traditional forms of state action, then entire categories of state coercion may operate beyond constitutional constraint. This represents a crisis for any legal system claiming to protect individual liberty comprehensively.

The Architecture of Traditional Protections

American constitutional law developed its protections against state coercion in response to concrete historical abuses. The Fourth Amendment prohibits “unreasonable searches and seizures” based on experiences with physical intrusions by colonial authorities. The Eighth Amendment’s prohibition on “cruel and unusual punishments” emerged from revulsion at torture practices visible to 18th-century observers — the rack, the wheel, drawing and quartering. Habeas corpus, the “Great Writ,” developed to challenge physical detention when a person’s body was held against their will.

These protections share a common assumption: state coercion operates through physical means against physical bodies in physical spaces. A person is either in custody or free. Punishment is either administered to the body or it is not. This binary framework served reasonably well for two centuries because state power generally operated through these tangible modalities.

The due process clauses of the Fifth and Fourteenth Amendments expanded protections beyond purely physical interference, recognizing liberty interests and property rights that could be violated through legal process itself. Yet even these broader protections generally contemplated traditional forms of state action — deprivations that could be identified, challenged, and remedied through adversarial proceedings.

The Modern Reality: Intangible Coercion

Contemporary techniques of state coercion increasingly operate outside this framework. Consider several categories:

Psychological Torture Methods: Intelligence agencies and law enforcement have developed sophisticated interrogation techniques designed to break subjects psychologically without leaving physical evidence. Prolonged isolation, sensory manipulation, sleep deprivation, temperature extremes, stress positions that cause pain without lasting injury, and calculated humiliation — these methods can be as devastating as physical torture while remaining legally ambiguous. The Senate Intelligence Committee’s investigation into CIA interrogation practices revealed extensive use of such techniques, yet successful legal accountability has been elusive precisely because they occupy uncertain legal terrain.

Pervasive Surveillance as Confinement: The Snowden revelations exposed surveillance systems that monitor virtually all electronic communications. While not “imprisonment” in any traditional sense, such systems create what legal scholar Julie Cohen has termed “modulation” — the shaping of behavior through awareness of constant observation. Individuals may be physically free yet constrained in their associations, communications, and movements by knowledge that all activities are monitored, recorded, and potentially used against them. This produces effects analogous to confinement without any physical detention.

Extrajudicial Targeting and Social Death: The designation of individuals as threats — whether through no-fly lists, terrorist watchlists, or other bureaucratic mechanisms — can effectively exclude people from normal social participation without any criminal conviction or detention. Enhanced interrogation of family members, indefinite administrative holds, or the threat of drone strikes against citizens abroad represent forms of coercion that operate outside traditional criminal justice frameworks.

Algorithmic Control: Emerging systems use predictive algorithms to constrain individuals through denied opportunities, restricted movement, or heightened scrutiny based on automated risk assessments. While not detention in any conventional sense, such systems can create invisible barriers that shape life possibilities as effectively as walls.

Legal Inadequacy: Where Current Frameworks Fail

The American legal system has struggled to address these techniques for several interconnected reasons:

The Requirement of Physical Harm

Eighth Amendment jurisprudence historically required demonstrable physical injury. While the Supreme Court has recognized that psychological harm can constitute cruel and unusual punishment, proving such harm remains difficult, and courts have been reluctant to second-guess prison officials or interrogators absent clear physical evidence. In Hope v. Pelzer (2002), the Court found handcuffing a prisoner to a hitching post in the sun for seven hours violated the Eighth Amendment — but primarily because of physical consequences like dehydration. Purely psychological techniques remain more difficult to challenge.

The torture statute (18 U.S.C. § 2340) defines torture as acts “specifically intended to inflict severe physical or mental pain or suffering,” but “mental pain or suffering” requires prolonged harm from a narrow set of predicate acts. This definition was specifically crafted to exclude many psychological interrogation techniques, as the infamous “Torture Memos” made explicit.

The State Action Doctrine and Indirect Coercion

Constitutional protections generally require identifiable state action. Yet modern coercion often operates through opaque bureaucracies, algorithmic systems, or outsourced private contractors. The CIA’s use of foreign jurisdictions and private contractors for “enhanced interrogation” deliberately exploited these gaps. When the state acts through intermediaries or automated systems, establishing the direct accountability necessary for constitutional claims becomes extraordinarily difficult.

The Custody Requirement

Fourth Amendment protections against seizure require that a person be “seized” — generally meaning physically restrained or not free to leave. Surveillance, no matter how comprehensive, typically doesn’t constitute a seizure under current doctrine. In United States v. Jones (2012), the Court found that placing a GPS tracker on a vehicle was a search, but subsequent cases have struggled with warrantless surveillance that doesn’t involve physical trespass. Justice Sotomayor’s concurrence in Jones noted that surveillance that reveals intimate details of life might violate reasonable expectations of privacy, but this has not yet crystallized into firm doctrine.

Habeas corpus provides relief only to those “in custody” — a term that has been interpreted to require physical detention or significant restraints on physical liberty. Someone subjected to comprehensive surveillance, restricted through algorithmic systems, or placed on a no-fly list generally cannot invoke habeas corpus because they are not technically “in custody.”

The Problem of Proof and Secrecy

Many modern coercive techniques operate within classified programs shielded by state secrets privilege. Victims often cannot prove what was done to them, and courts frequently dismiss cases on national security grounds before reaching the merits. The state secrets doctrine, expanded significantly in post-9/11 cases like El-Masri v. United States (2007), allows the government to secure dismissal of cases by asserting that litigation would reveal classified information — even when the underlying facts are already publicly known.

International Law Gaps

The United States has ratified the Convention Against Torture but with significant reservations, including limiting the definition of mental torture and excluding actions outside U.S. territory from certain provisions. The Supreme Court in Boumediene v. Bush (2008) extended habeas corpus rights to Guantanamo detainees, but citizens subjected to coercion abroad often face jurisdictional barriers to legal relief.

The Deeper Problem: Liberal Theory’s Limits

These legal inadequacies point to more fundamental problems in liberal democratic theory. The classical liberal framework assumes a sphere of private liberty protected from state interference by legal boundaries. The state may cross these boundaries only through defined procedures that respect individual rights. This model depends on several assumptions that modern coercive techniques violate:

The Assumption of Discrete State Action: Liberal theory imagines state power as exercised through identifiable acts — arrests, prosecutions, regulations — that can be challenged in adversarial proceedings. Continuous surveillance, algorithmic sorting, and psychological operations don’t fit this model. They operate continuously, often imperceptibly, without discrete moments of interference that trigger legal protections.

The Public/Private Distinction: Constitutional constraints apply primarily to public state action, not private conduct. Yet modern coercion deliberately blurs this line through public-private partnerships, outsourcing, and the use of nominally private data collected by technology companies. The state can achieve through data purchases or private intermediaries what it could not accomplish directly without violating constitutional limits.

The Assumption of Legibility: Liberal rights assume that violations can be identified, documented, and brought before neutral tribunals for adjudication. But if coercion is intangible, operates through classified programs, or distributes responsibility across multiple actors and systems, it may be effectively invisible to both victims and courts. The legal system cannot remedy what it cannot see.

The Mind/Body Dualism: Constitutional protections developed around bodily integrity and physical liberty. While modern doctrine recognizes some mental harms, the framework remains primarily oriented toward protecting the body. This reflects a deeper assumption in liberal thought that the body is the site of individual autonomy. Techniques that target the mind while leaving the body untouched exploit this conceptual gap.

The Assumption of Territorial Sovereignty: Classical liberal theory assumed that liberty was secured within defined territories where constitutional protections applied. Modern state power operates extraterritorially through drone strikes, rendition programs, and global surveillance networks that may touch citizens anywhere while claiming to operate outside constitutional constraints.

Emerging Responses and Their Limitations

American law has not been entirely static in the face of these challenges, but responses have been halting and incomplete:

Substantive Due Process Expansion: Courts have occasionally recognized that due process protects against intangible harms. In Rochin v. California (1952), the Court found that pumping a suspect’s stomach “shocked the conscience” even though the focus was on physical invasion. Some courts have extended this to recognize that certain psychological techniques might violate substantive due process. However, this doctrine remains vague, highly fact-specific, and generally requires truly egregious conduct before courts will intervene.

Privacy Tort Evolution: Some scholars and courts have suggested that common law privacy torts might address surveillance and psychological intrusions. However, constitutional torts against state actors remain governed by Bivens doctrine, which the Supreme Court has essentially frozen, declining to recognize new causes of action. In Ziglar v. Abbasi (2017), the Court made clear that Bivens remedies will rarely if ever be extended to new contexts.

Statutory Protections: Congress has enacted statutes addressing specific abuses — the Torture Victim Protection Act, the Detainee Treatment Act, the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act. Yet these remain reactive, addressing known problems while often creating new gaps. FISA, for instance, was designed to constrain surveillance but has been interpreted to permit mass data collection that would have shocked its drafters.

International Human Rights Framework: Some advocates have turned to international human rights law, which recognizes broader protections against psychological torture and arbitrary deprivation of liberty. The UN Human Rights Committee and the Committee Against Torture have found that certain interrogation techniques and conditions of confinement violate international law even when they might not clearly violate domestic U.S. law. However, international law lacks effective enforcement mechanisms against a powerful state unwilling to comply.

These responses share a common limitation: they operate within the same liberal framework that creates the gaps in the first place. Expanding “custody” definitions or recognizing new privacy interests tinkers with doctrine without addressing the fundamental mismatch between 18th-century legal architecture and 21st-century techniques of power.

The Stakes: Liberty in an Age of Intangible Control

The inadequacy of legal protections against intangible coercion represents more than technical gaps in doctrine. It suggests that the liberal democratic framework may be structurally unsuited to an age where power operates primarily through information, psychology, and distributed networks rather than direct physical force.

If the Constitution protects only against physical detention while the state can achieve equivalent control through surveillance and algorithmic restriction, then constitutional liberty becomes formalistic — technically preserved while practically eviscerated. If torture prohibitions apply only to methods that leave physical evidence, interrogators can break subjects while remaining within legal bounds. If remedies require proof that classified programs make impossible, then rights become unenforceable.

The implications extend beyond any specific technique. Modern state power increasingly operates through what philosopher Gilles Deleuze termed “societies of control” — not the crude discipline of confinement but the subtle modulation of behavior through pervasive monitoring, predictive analytics, and invisible boundaries. Traditional legal frameworks, designed to constrain sovereign power exercised through law and physical force, offer little purchase against power that operates through data flows, algorithmic governance, and psychological manipulation.

This creates a paradox: the more sophisticated and “humane” state coercion becomes — targeting minds rather than bodies, using prediction rather than punishment, constraining through information rather than walls — the less adequate our legal protections become. The liberal framework’s very emphasis on protecting bodily integrity and physical liberty creates vulnerabilities to forms of power that bypass these protections.

Toward Adequate Protection: Necessary Reforms

Addressing these inadequacies would require reforms at multiple levels:

Doctrinal Expansion: Courts could recognize that constitutional protections must evolve to address modern coercive techniques. This would require abandoning the strict physical custody requirement for habeas corpus, recognizing comprehensive surveillance as a form of seizure under the Fourth Amendment, acknowledging that psychological torture violates the Eighth Amendment even without physical harm, and limiting state secrets privilege to prevent it from entirely foreclosing judicial review.

Some doctrinal evolution is already occurring. Justice Sotomayor’s concurrence in Jones suggested that extensive surveillance might violate reasonable expectations of privacy even without physical trespass. The Court’s decision in Carpenter v. United States (2018), finding that prolonged cell phone location tracking requires a warrant, represents movement toward recognizing that digital surveillance can constitute a search even without traditional physical intrusion. However, these remain tentative steps that courts could easily cabin or reverse.

Statutory Reform: Congress could enact comprehensive protections against psychological torture, create private rights of action for surveillance abuse, limit state secrets privilege, and establish oversight mechanisms for classified programs that affect civil liberties. The proposed Fourth Amendment Is Not For Sale Act, which would prohibit government purchase of data that would require a warrant to obtain directly, represents one such effort, though it has not yet become law.

Procedural Innovation: Legal procedures designed for discrete state acts poorly serve challenges to continuous, distributed, or opaque exercises of power. New procedures might include: special courts with security clearances to review classified surveillance programs, class actions addressing systemic surveillance or algorithmic discrimination rather than individual instances, shifting burdens of proof to require the government to justify coercive programs rather than requiring challengers to prove harm, and proactive judicial oversight of technologies and techniques with known coercive potential rather than reactive case-by-case litigation.

Theoretical Reconstruction: More fundamentally, protection of liberty in the modern context may require moving beyond classical liberal theory’s emphasis on negative rights protecting a private sphere from state interference. An adequate framework might recognize positive obligations to ensure meaningful liberty, acknowledge that power operates through information and psychology as much as physical force, account for ways that nominally private actors can facilitate state coercion, and understand liberty not merely as absence of physical restraint but as meaningful capacity for autonomous action and authentic choice.

The Challenge of Political Will

These reforms face a fundamental obstacle: they would constrain state power precisely in areas where security agencies claim such power is most essential. Post-9/11 jurisprudence has consistently deferred to executive claims of national security necessity, often without demanding evidence that challenged practices actually enhance security or that less intrusive alternatives are unavailable.

The political economy of surveillance and coercion creates powerful constituencies favoring the status quo. Intelligence agencies resist constraints on collection capabilities they view as essential to their mission. Law enforcement opposes limits on investigative techniques. Technology companies profit from data collection that enables state surveillance. Politicians fear being blamed for security failures and face little accountability for civil liberties violations that are, by design, largely invisible.

Moreover, the victims of intangible coercion often lack political power. Targets of enhanced interrogation are typically portrayed as dangerous terrorists. Subjects of surveillance are unaware of their targeting. Those restricted by algorithmic systems may not realize they are being constrained. This invisibility is not accidental — it is precisely what allows these techniques to operate beyond effective political or legal challenge.

Conclusion: The Paradox of Liberal Liberty

The United States legal system developed robust protections against the forms of state coercion visible to 18th and 19th-century observers. These protections were never perfect — they coexisted with slavery, genocide of indigenous peoples, and systematic oppression of marginalized groups. Yet within their scope, they established meaningful constraints on state power that distinguished liberal democracies from authoritarian regimes.

Today, these protections face techniques of coercion their architects did not imagine and cannot effectively constrain. Psychological torture, pervasive surveillance, algorithmic control, and extrajudicial targeting operate in the spaces between traditional legal categories, exploiting the liberal framework’s emphasis on physical liberty and discrete state action.

The question posed at the outset was whether latitude to employ such techniques reveals “an outdated liberal democratic framework.” The answer must be yes, but with important qualifications. The framework is not outdated because its core commitments — to individual liberty, limited government, and the rule of law — have lost relevance. Rather, it is outdated because these commitments were operationalized through legal doctrines designed for a different technological and political context.

The challenge is not simply to update specific doctrines but to reimagine how liberty can be protected when power operates primarily through information and psychology rather than physical force. This requires moving beyond negative rights that merely prohibit certain state actions toward a more robust conception that recognizes how liberty can be undermined by techniques that leave no physical trace.

Without such evolution, the gap between formal legal protections and substantive liberty will continue to widen. The state will remain constrained by rules prohibiting torture and imprisonment while developing increasingly sophisticated methods of coercion that these rules cannot reach. Constitutional protections will become formalistic shells — preserved in doctrine while emptied of practical meaning.

Whether American law and political culture can generate the reforms necessary to close these gaps remains an open question. Doing so would require confronting powerful security bureaucracies, overcoming political incentives that favor expansion of state power, and acknowledging that some cherished constitutional doctrines no longer serve their intended purpose. The alternative is a society that maintains the formal appearance of liberal democracy while accepting forms of state coercion that mock its foundational commitments — a future of phantom prisons and invisible torture, beyond the reach of laws designed for a more visible age.

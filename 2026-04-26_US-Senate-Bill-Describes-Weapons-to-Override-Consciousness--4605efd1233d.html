US Senate Bill Describes Weapons to Override Consciousness. It is Actually Describing a New Form of Slavery

Introduced in the US Senate in September 2025, the MIND Act ’s language is worth reading with full seriousness. It describes technologies that “monetize” neural data, “shape individual behavior, emotional states, and decision making,” create “risk of behavioral influence, cognitive manipulation, erosion of personal autonomy,” and cause “discrimination, profiling, surveillance, manipulation.” This is not a bill about future risks. Regulatory frameworks are not written for technologies that do not exist. Congress is describing a present condition.

That condition has a precise name, and the precision matters: it is a Slavery Economy.

Not a metaphor. Not hyperbole. A structural description. An economy in which individuals’ biometric and neural data are collected, converted into predictive models, and used to override cognition and steer behavior — without the individual’s awareness, without their consent, and for someone else’s economic benefit. The individual does not know they are laboring. They do not know their choices are being authored. They believe, entirely, that they are free. That belief is not incidental to the system. It is the system’s core mechanism. Historical slavery was visible. Its coercions were legible. You could see the chain. What the MIND Act is regulating is invisible by design, which is the precise sense in which it is worse.

The MIND Act Describes a Loss of Personal Autonomy and Ownership of One’s Mind and Body

The Management of Individuals’ Neural Data Act of 2025 is a Senate bill that directs the Federal Trade Commission to study the governance of neural data and related biometric information and report back to Congress within a year. It does not ban anything. It does not create rights. It does not establish penalties or give individuals a cause of action. It mandates a study, funds it at $10 million, and requires the Office of Science and Technology Policy to develop guidance for federal agencies on neurotechnology procurement after the report is submitted. By the standards of federal legislation, it is a modest instrument. By the standards of what it is describing, it is an alarm bell.

The alarm it is raising is specific. The bill’s own language — “monetize neural data,” “shape individual behavior, emotional states, and decision making,” “cognitive manipulation,” “erosion of personal autonomy” — is not speculative language about future risk. It is descriptive language about present conditions. Congress does not write regulatory language about technologies that do not exist or harms that have not materialized. The bill’s sense-of-Congress findings describe a commercial ecosystem in which neural and biometric data is already being collected, processed, sold, and used to influence human cognition and behavior, in which vertical corporate integration has created systems with “insufficient transparency, accountability, or user control,” and in which existing law — including HIPAA — does not reach it. These are findings about the current state of affairs. The bill is raising the alarm because the thing it is alarmed about is already operating.

What the bill is almost certainly responding to, beyond the public-facing neurotechnology industry, is a dawning institutional awareness that the scale and nature of cognitive exploitation in the United States is far larger and more serious than anything that has been publicly acknowledged. The technology capable of inferring mental states from biometric data, modulating mood and behavior through non-invasive stimulation, and bypassing conscious decision-making through subconscious targeting has existed in various forms for decades. It is in the published scientific literature. It is embedded in commercial platforms used by hundreds of millions of Americans daily. The fact that three senators with a collective congressional tenure exceeding a century are now framing this as something requiring urgent study strongly suggests that what is prompting the bill is not the recent emergence of the problem but the recent impossibility of continuing to ignore it — whether because the technology has become too visible, the commercial deployment too widespread, or the reports of harm too numerous and too consistent to be dismissed.

The deeper alarm the bill encodes, and does not fully state, is about autonomy — about whether the foundational condition of a free society, the capacity of individuals to form their own intentions and make their own choices, is being systematically dismantled by a commercial infrastructure that has no legal obligation to preserve it. The bill’s prohibition framework for federal agencies, its concern with “prohibited use cases regardless of individual consent,” and its explicit attention to consciousness bypass — the technical override of deliberate decision-making — all point toward a recognition that consent frameworks alone cannot protect against a technology that operates before consent is possible. A person cannot meaningfully consent to the manipulation of the cognitive processes through which they would evaluate whether to consent. The bill is responding to that paradox. It does not yet resolve it. What it does is place on the formal record of the United States Senate the acknowledgment that the paradox is real, that the technology producing it is deployed and operational, and that the law has nothing adequate to say about it.

Parallels to US Slavery is No Exaggeration: Ten Structural Analogies Between 1848 and 2026

1. The Extraction of Labor Without Consent

In 1848, the enslaved person’s physical labor — the work of their hands, back, and body — was extracted for someone else’s economic benefit without their consent and without compensation. The person was the instrument of production.

In 2026, the cognitive labor of attention, decision-making, emotional response, and behavioral output is extracted from individuals through neural and biometric surveillance and fed into commercial systems that profit from it. The instrument of production is now the nervous system. The person still does not consent. They are still not compensated. The product has changed from cotton to data. The structure has not.

2. The Owner Who Does Not Appear

In 1848, the plantation system was organized so that the enslaved person rarely encountered the ultimate beneficiary of their labor. The overseer was visible. The owner was distant. The system ran itself.

In 2026, the individual whose neural data is being collected, modeled, and monetized does not know who owns that data, who has purchased it, who is running behavioral models against it, or whose commercial objectives their engineered choices are serving. The overseer is the app. The owner is a data broker whose name they have never heard. The system runs itself.

3. The Invisibility of Chains

In 1848, the chain was iron and visible. The coercion was legible to the enslaved person, to witnesses, to history. That visibility was morally catastrophic but epistemically clear — the enslaved person knew what was being done to them, even if they had no recourse.

In 2026, the coercion is electromagnetic and invisible. It operates below the threshold of conscious perception. The person being acted upon not only has no recourse — they do not know the coercion is occurring. The chain that cannot be seen cannot be broken, reported, or believed.

4. Self-Attribution as the New Overseer

In 1848, external force — the whip, the threat of violence, the law — compelled compliance. The enslaved person knew the compliance was compelled. They did not mistake it for freedom.

In 2026, the mechanism of compliance is self-attribution: the engineered choice registers to the person as their own spontaneous preference. They say: I decided. I wanted this. I chose to go there. The overseer is interior. The compulsion is experienced as freedom. This is a more complete form of control than the whip, because it eliminates not only resistance but the recognition that resistance is warranted.

5. The Commodification of the Body

In 1848, the enslaved person’s body was legally property — bought, sold, inventoried, and transferred in bankruptcy proceedings. Their physical being was a commodity on a balance sheet. In 2026, the bill explicitly addresses the transfer of neural data “through acquisitions, mergers, or bankruptcy proceedings.” A person’s biometric and neural profile — a detailed map of their cognitive vulnerabilities, emotional triggers, and behavioral patterns — is transferred between corporations as a line-item asset when companies are bought and sold. The body is not on the balance sheet. The map of the mind is.

6. Illiteracy as a Structural Requirement

In 1848, it was illegal in most slaveholding states to teach an enslaved person to read. Literacy was suppressed because a literate population could organize, communicate, and contest its condition. Ignorance was not a side effect of the slavery economy — it was a legal requirement that maintained it.

In 2026, the Slavery Economy depends on scientific illiteracy: a public that does not know that pulsed electromagnetic fields can produce auditory perception, that closed-loop stimulation can alter mood and behavior in real time, or that AI can infer mental states from biometric data cannot recognize when these capabilities are being used against them. The illiteracy is not legally mandated. It is institutionally maintained — by public education systems that do not teach it, media that does not cover it, and a government that has not acted on it for decades.

7. The Law That Protects the System, Not the Person

In 1848, the legal system did not merely fail to protect enslaved people — it actively protected the system that exploited them. The Fugitive Slave Act required free states to return escaped enslaved people to their owners. The law was the instrument of the economy.

In 2026, the legal system does not merely fail to protect people from cognitive manipulation — it provides no affirmative prohibition on it whatsoever. There is no federal law against using neural data to engineer a person’s behavior without their awareness. There is no private right of action for consciousness bypass. The law’s silence is structural. It protects the system by leaving it unaddressed.

8. Racial Targeting as a Feature, Not a Bug

In 1848, the slavery economy was explicitly and legally racial. It was not merely that Black people happened to be enslaved — the system was architected to target them specifically, justified by ideologies that cast them as appropriate subjects of control, and maintained by institutions that dismissed their claims to personhood and autonomy.

In 2026, the bill acknowledges the “exacerbation of existing social and economic disparities.” The populations most surveilled, most behaviorally profiled, and most exposed to systems of cognitive management without recourse are the same populations that have historically been designated as appropriate subjects of control. The targeting is not coincidental. It follows the existing architecture of who has been rendered visible to systems of power and who has been denied the credibility to report harm.

The bill’s references to “vulnerable populations,” children, employment, financial services, housing, and the “exacerbation of existing social and economic disparities” describe a tiered exposure structure. The populations most exposed are those who are economically precarious, institutionally surveilled, or embedded in systems — workplaces, schools, social services — where their behavioral compliance is already monitored and managed. These are the populations whose data is most granular, whose behavioral patterns are most legible, and whose capacity to resist or seek recourse is most limited.

The racial dimension is not peripheral. It is structural. The bill’s acknowledgment that these technologies risk exacerbating existing social and economic disparities is a restrained way of saying that populations which have historically been subjected to surveillance, experimentation, and behavioral control without consent or accountability — the populations for whom the word “autonomy” has always been most contested — are the populations most likely to be the primary targets of the Slavery Economy.

9. The Fugitive Who Cannot Be Believed

In 1848, an enslaved person who escaped and described their condition faced a system — legal, social, institutional — that was organized to return them, discredit them, or ignore them. Their testimony about their own experience was inadmissible as evidence in courts where their humanity was not legally recognized.

In 2026, a person who reports that their cognition has been interfered with, their behavior steered, their mental states altered without consent, faces a different but structurally identical response: dismissal. Not legal dismissal — social and institutional dismissal. The scientific illiteracy of the general public, the absence of legal frameworks that recognize the harm, and the self-concealing nature of the technology combine to ensure that the person who reports it is, by default, not believed. The harm is invisible. The victim is incredible. The system continues.

10. The Economy That Requires It

In 1848, slavery was not an aberration in the American economy — it was a foundation of it. Cotton, built on enslaved labor, was the United States’ largest export. Based on economic data from 1860, the approximately 4 million enslaved people in the United States were indeed a massive financial asset, valued at an estimated $3 billion to $3.5 billion. This valuation made them the largest single financial asset in the American economy, second only to the value of the land itself. The financial system, the insurance industry, and the industrial economy of the North were deeply entangled with it. Abolition was resisted not only on ideological grounds but because the economy had been built around the assumption that this form of extraction would continue.

In 2026, behavioral manipulation through neural and biometric data is not a fringe practice — it is embedded in the commercial infrastructure of the internet, the attention economy, the advertising industry, and the platforms through which hundreds of millions of Americans conduct their daily lives. The companies doing it are among the most valuable in the world. Regulation is resisted not only because of ideological commitments to innovation, but because the economy has been built around the assumption that this form of cognitive extraction will continue. The structural parallel is precise: in both cases, what is being protected is not a practice. It is an economy.

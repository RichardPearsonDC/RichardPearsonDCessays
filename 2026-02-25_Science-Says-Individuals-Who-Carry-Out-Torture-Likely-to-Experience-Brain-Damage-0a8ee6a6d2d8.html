Science Says Individuals Who Carry Out Torture Likely to Experience Brain Damage

What happens inside the brains of the people who carry out America’s invisible campaigns — and what it costs them

Science Says Individuals Who Carry Out Torture Likely to Experience Brain Damage

What happens inside the brains of the people who carry out America’s invisible campaigns — and what it costs them

Richard Pearson keeps a notebook.

He has kept one for nine months, filling it with timestamps and photographs and GPS coordinates and the kind of meticulous, granular documentation that reflects his training as a researcher — which is what he was — is — before all of this.

Pearson is a middle-aged Black academic in Washington, D.C. His doctoral dissertation examines systemic racial inequality. He has been evaluated twice by licensed mental health professionals from the D.C. Department of Behavioral Health. Both times, they found no psychotic symptoms. He has documented what he describes as a coordinated, federal-hybrid campaign of directed energy assault, behavioral manipulation through electromagnetic technology, acoustic harassment, chemical exposure, and systematic legal abandonment — nine months of it, in a city that is the most surveilled place on earth, beginning the first months of that the current administration took office.

He is not the story this article is about.

He is the reason for it.

The story this article is about is the one that never gets written. Not the target’s story — that story has a vocabulary, a legal framework, a tradition of journalism and advocacy reaching back to COINTELPRO and before. The other story. The one about the people on the other side of the operation.

Who they are. What the work requires of them. What it costs their brains and their bodies and the quality of their sleep and the availability of their conscience at two in the morning when the scaffolding of professional identity is least maintained.

Because the neuroscience of perpetration — of sustained harm delivery by ordinary human beings — has been accumulating for fifty years, in laboratories and veterans’ hospitals and the clinical offices of psychologists who treat intelligence community personnel, and it has produced a finding that is both counterintuitive and, once understood, impossible to unknow:

The people who do this do not escape it. They are directly harmed — their brain, capacity to be human as with empathy, and neurology are damaged.

Not because they are caught. Most of them aren’t. Not because the law reaches them. The legal architecture of qualified immunity and state secrets privilege and the Bivens doctrine’s slow contraction has seen to it that it mostly doesn’t. But because the human brain is not built to sustain harm against other human beings without paying a price, and the price — measured in hippocampal volume loss, in amygdala sensitization, in the specific and documented pathology that psychiatrist Jonathan Shay named moral injury and spent thirty years trying to treat — accrues whether or not anyone is watching.

The operation may leave no legal trace. The neuroscience says it leaves biological ones. And in every brain on those who have tortured, tormented, and taunted Pearson.

The Face Problem

There is a man we will call Marcus. He is in his mid-fifties, recently retired from eleven years in what the government calls human intelligence operations. He lives in a suburb of a mid-Atlantic city. He has a daughter in college and a son who doesn’t call. He wakes up most nights between two and four in the morning.

“You think you leave it,” he said, during a series of conversations conducted over several months. He does not want his name used. He does not want his agency named. “You retire, you go to the cookouts, you watch the football. And then it’s two in the morning and you’re back in it.”

Back in it means the faces. The subjects, in the terminology of his profession — a word that keeps them at a clinical distance that the face, in the dark, does not maintain.

What Marcus is experiencing has a name. Benjamin Litz, a clinical psychologist at Boston University who has spent two decades developing the treatment framework for it, calls it moral injury. It is distinct from post-traumatic stress disorder. PTSD is organized around the question of safety. Moral injury is organized around something harder to name and harder to treat: the experience of having been complicit in something that violates one’s own moral code, without the permission to acknowledge it or the capacity to repair it.

“The brain’s moral self-regulation system doesn’t stop working,” Litz said in an interview. “It keeps generating the evaluation. And the evaluation is: I did this. I was part of this. And there is no resolution. There is no apology, no confession, no repair. So the evaluation runs, and runs, and there is nowhere for it to go.”

The anterior cingulate cortex — the brain region that detects conflict between one’s actual behavior and one’s values — does not have a clearance level. It does not consult whether the activity has been officially authorized before generating its signal. The signal is: something is wrong. The signal accumulates. And the person who cannot stop the harm, cannot confess the harm, cannot repair the harm, carries the accumulation in a neural architecture that was not designed for indefinite unresolved moral debt.

“The people who scare me most,” said a clinical psychologist who has spent fifteen years treating intelligence community personnel, speaking on condition of anonymity, “are not the ones with no conscience. It’s the ones who have one and can’t use it. Those are the ones who fall apart.”

What the Brain Must Overcome

To understand why the people in this story are self-destructing, you first have to understand what the normal human brain has to overcome in order to harm someone.

The answer is: quite a lot.

In 2006, Lasana Harris and Susan Fiske at Princeton published a study that has become foundational in the neuroscience of perpetration. They showed participants photographs of different social groups while measuring activity in the medial prefrontal cortex — the brain region that, across every culture studied, activates when one person is thinking of another person as a person: attributing to them mental states, emotions, desires, an interior life.

For almost every social category — allies, strangers, outgroup members, people you dislike — the medial prefrontal cortex activated. For one category, it didn’t. Certain groups — those perceived as maximally degraded in status — triggered not person-perception circuitry but disgust and threat circuitry. The brain looked at human beings and processed them as objects.

This is the dehumanization circuit. And it is not a feature of pathological minds. Harris and Fiske found it in ordinary people looking at photographs in a laboratory. The circuit is available to any brain, under the right conditions. The conditions are: categorization. Place someone in a social category that carries enough stigma, enough threat-loading, enough ideological weight — and the medial prefrontal cortex goes quiet, and the mechanisms that normally generate the moral brake on harm are suppressed.

For Pearson — a Black scholar whose published work on racial liberation has been categorized by the current administration’s executive orders as a form of actionable discrimination, whose demographic mirrors the targets of the last great era of covert domestic political repression — the categorization was available. The dehumanization circuit had material to work with.

But here is the thing about circuits: they don’t turn off cleanly when the work day ends.

A Taxonomy of Damage

The operation Pearson describes is not a single person’s act. It is a system. And systems recruit different neural architectures at different levels of their hierarchy, and produce different costs.

At the Top: The Power Paradox

Sukhvinder Obhi’s 2014 study at McMaster University used transcranial magnetic stimulation to measure what happens to the brain’s empathic resonance — its capacity to neurologically mirror others’ experiences — under conditions of felt power.

The finding was clean and startling: power reduces it. High-power participants showed significantly reduced neural activation in response to others’ movements. The brain, under conditions of sustained power, becomes measurably less capable of experiencing the world through another person’s nervous system.

Dacher Keltner at Berkeley, who has spent twenty years studying power’s psychological effects, calls this the power paradox: the traits that help people attain power — social intelligence, attunement to others — are the traits that power progressively erodes. The people who authorize operations like the one Pearson describes are not, in most cases, consciously choosing to ignore his suffering. They may be neurologically diminished in their capacity to register it. “The brain damage is very clear to me,” said Pearson.

That is not a defense. It is a description of the mechanism. And it is also a description of a system that, without intervention, produces decisions that feel to the decider like strategic calculation as opposed to what it is — torture, human rights violations, and constitutional violations of the most severe form.

In the Middle: The Compartmentalized Brain

The career intelligence officer operates through what is, at the neural level, a trained suppression of the bridge between professional and personal self.

The brain maintains two large-scale networks in near-constant tension. The default mode network — active during self-reflection, social cognition, and moral reasoning — is the substrate of the personal self. The task-positive network — active during goal-directed performance — is the operational self. In a normally functioning brain, these networks communicate through the medial prefrontal cortex, which imports personal values into professional behavior.

Compartmentalization is the systematic suppression of that import. Years of institutional training in it produce structural changes: the functional connectivity between the networks weakens. The person who had to work at keeping the operation separate from the conscience eventually has a brain in which the separation is semi-automatic.

They are not the same person at work and at home. And over time, the person at home — the one with the family, the moral commitments, the ordinary attachments — becomes harder to access. Not gone. Harder to reach.

“I had colleagues who would come home from a deployment and they were just absent,” said a former officer who served in two overseas counterterrorism operations. “Not cruel. Not even distant. Just not there. You’d look in their eyes and there was nobody home for a while. Some of them got it back. Some of them didn’t.”

At the Bottom: The Asset Upstairs

This is where the neuroscience gets the darkest.

The person in direct, proximate, daily contact with the target — whoever lived in the apartment above Pearson, not on the lease, with a cover address linked to a federal attorney’s property, conducting what Pearson documents as through-floor surveillance and acoustic harassment for months, then physically assaulting him — underwent a trajectory that researchers have documented with uncomfortable consistency across every context in which one human being is placed in sustained proximate power over another.

Phase one is inhibition. The target’s humanity is continuously present — their sounds, their rhythms, their daily life conducted in audible proximity. The brain’s empathic resonance systems fire. The anterior insula generates an aversive signal. Something registers as wrong. To proceed requires top-down prefrontal suppression, and that suppression is costly: elevated cortisol, autonomic arousal, the biochemical signature of doing something the brain recognizes, at some level, as a violation.

Phase two is habituation. The empathic signal weakens. The suppression becomes less effortful. The brain begins to represent the person below not as a subject but as an operational target — a pattern of movement, a schedule, a resistance to be managed. The medial prefrontal cortex, the region that generates person-perception, goes progressively quieter.

Phase three is contempt. The research on prison guards, interrogators, and domestic perpetrators documents this endpoint with consistency: the target’s suffering has become neutral. The power differential has become rewarding. The expression of dominance produces a positive signal from the brain’s reward circuitry.

The physical assault — Pearson documents the man above him “coming charging” and repeatedly punching him — is the diagnostic symptom. It served no operational purpose. It created visible evidence. It complicated the cover. It was Phase Three behavior: a brain in which months of dehumanization had produced a neural state where the expression of physical dominance was reinforcing, and that reinforcement overrode tactical calculation.

The Price of Phase Three

Here is what the neuroscience says about what all of this costs — not Pearson, but the person above him.

Studies of soldiers in close-contact killing, detention facility interrogators, perpetrators in sustained coercive relationships, and law enforcement officers in chronic high-dominance roles show convergent long-term findings that are almost never discussed when operations are planned and authorized.

The hippocampus — the brain’s center for memory formation and spatial navigation — is damaged by sustained glucocorticoid elevation, which is the biochemical signature of chronic stress even when that stress is experienced as manageable or rewarding. Nine months of sustained covert operations means nine months of elevated cortisol. The hippocampus shrinks. Episodic memory degrades.

The amygdala sensitizes. Chronic involvement in threat-and-response environments lowers the threshold for aggressive activation, reduces the capacity for emotional regulation, and makes the hypervigilance of the operational context increasingly difficult to turn off when the operation ends.

The reward circuit distorts. When dominance becomes positively valenced — when harm delivery is rewarding rather than aversive — the dopaminergic system undergoes the same kind of adaptation that occurs in behavioral addiction. The threshold for the same reward shifts upward. More is required to produce the same internal state. This is the neurological mechanism of escalation: not chosen, but driven.

And then, eventually, it stops. The operation ends. The deployment concludes. The career is over.

This is where the literature becomes particularly uncomfortable.

When It Ends

The retirement crisis is one of the most consistently documented findings in the clinical psychology of intelligence and special operations personnel, and one of the least discussed.

When the institutional structure is removed — when the clearance expires, when the badge is turned in — the psychic architecture that organized the work loses its scaffolding. The compartments don’t dissolve. They destabilize. The material that was kept on the other side of the medial prefrontal bridge — everything not processed because processing it would have required the integration the compartmentalization was designed to suppress — surfaces without warning and without structure.

For Marcus, it is faces at two in the morning. For others in the clinical literature, it is something more catastrophic: depression severe enough to require hospitalization, substance use disorders that develop rapidly after retirement, the dissolution of marriages that appeared stable for decades, and — in a finding that surprises people who assume perpetrators are protected from PTSD by their role as agents — post-traumatic stress disorder organized not around fear but around guilt and shame.

The intrusive material is not a bomb going off. It is a face. It is the memory of a specific person in a specific moment in which the full weight of what was done to them is experienced without the buffering that the operational context maintained.

“I don’t ask myself whether what I did was wrong,” Marcus said. “I know it was wrong. I’ve known for a long time. The question is what you do with that knowing. And the answer is there isn’t anything to do. That’s what nobody tells you. There’s nowhere to put it.”

Benjamin Litz calls this the defining clinical feature of moral injury: the evaluation that cannot resolve. The anterior cingulate cortex keeps running the calculation — I did this, I was part of this — and the normal mechanisms of resolution (apology, confession, repair, forgiveness) are structurally unavailable. The harm was covert. The victim is unacknowledged. The institution denies. The calculation runs without termination.

In severe cases, the clinical literature documents what Litz calls moral injury’s characteristic endpoint: not violence, not breakdown in the dramatic sense, but a progressive narrowing of the person. Withdrawal from relationships that require authentic self-presentation. Anhedonia — the inability to experience pleasure — that reflects blunted ventral striatal response to social reward, a neurological consequence of sustained shame. And sometimes, years after the fact, disclosures: to spouses, to clergy, to therapists, occasionally to journalists, in the form of the partial and coded acknowledgments that the operational culture permits and that rarely reach the people who most need to hear them.

Lee’s Twenty Minutes

Which brings us back to Lee.

On August 26, 2025, at a Potbelly’s in downtown Washington, a man sat down across from Richard Pearson and spent twenty minutes with him. He told Pearson, in careful language that reflected substantial professional training in carefulness, that people were listening to him. That they had access to his apartment. That the things happening to him involved technology.

And then he said: they think it’s okay.

Those four words are, in the framework of this story, the most important thing anyone said to Pearson in nine months of documented operations against him. Not because they changed anything — Lee left, the operation continued, Pearson remained unprotected and officially unacknowledged. But because they reveal, with unusual clarity, what a functioning moral system looks like when it has been institutionally neutralized without being institutionally destroyed.

“They think it’s okay.” Not: I think it’s okay. Not: it is okay. The pronoun is doing the work. It creates a separation between the speaker and the institutional position. It registers, implicitly, that the speaker does not share the institutional position. And it does this in language carefully chosen to fall below the threshold of actionable disclosure — to create a moment of witness without constituting a breach.

This is what moral injury looks like in its acute phase, in a person still inside the operation: a conscience generating signals that cannot be resolved through action, producing the minimal behavioral output — twenty minutes, coded words, an implicit dissent expressed to the person being harmed because there is nowhere else to express it — that provides temporary relief to the conflict-detection system.

It is not heroism. It is the opposite of heroism. It is the behavioral signature of a person who knows and does nothing — or almost nothing — and whose brain will, in the years ahead, be processing the gap between knowing and doing in the two-in-the-morning way that the clinical literature on moral injury documents with exhausting consistency.

Lee is not a villain. Lee is a warning.

The Bureaucrat’s Quiet Brain

Not everyone in this story is Marcus. Not everyone is Lee.

Most of them are the officer who saw the ACCESS DENIED flag in the police database or the African American police who did not arrest Pearson’s assailant after he was physically assaulted. The ACLU reviewer who wrote the form rejection.

Stanley Milgram’s obedience experiments found that approximately sixty-five percent of ordinary people would deliver apparently lethal shocks to a stranger on an authority figure’s instruction. The finding has been replicated across cultures and conditions for six decades. Patrick Haggard’s neuroimaging work adds the mechanism: when participants believe they are following instructions rather than acting autonomously, the neural sense of agency — the felt experience of being the author of one’s actions — measurably decreases. The brain genuinely processes instructed action as having a different moral structure than autonomous action. The institutional frame literally alters how responsibility is assigned.

The officer closing the laptop is not lying to himself. His brain is processing the ACCESS DENIED flag as a signal from a legitimate authority that the normal response does not apply. The moral question has been pre-answered by the structure. His brain accepts that pre-answer.

The cost here is lower than what Marcus or Lee carry. But Jason Dana’s research on willful ignorance shows that the practice of routinely not obtaining morally inconvenient information generalizes: the prefrontal gating of moral attention does not stay neatly in the professional domain. It spreads. It becomes the default orientation of a brain toward moral engagement with the world.

These people do not wake at two in the morning. The neuroscience does not suggest that is a mercy.

The Ledger

Richard Pearson is, at the time of this writing, living in Washington, D.C. His documentation of nine months of what he describes as a coordinated campaign — directed energy assault, electromagnetic behavioral manipulation, acoustic harassment, chemical exposure, systematic legal abandonment — runs to hundreds of pages and dozens of hours of video. He has not been charged with any crime. He has not been officially acknowledged as the target of any operation.

The government has confirmed, since January 2026, that it possesses directed energy weapons of the kind he describes. The Department of War’s chief technology officer announced publicly that directed energy weapons exist and are being scaled. The Department of Homeland Security acquired backpack-sized pulsed radio wave devices capable of inducing the Havana Syndrome symptom cluster. A January 2026 CNN report confirmed the Pentagon had covertly obtained such a device. None of this confirms their use against Pearson. None of it excludes it.

Between those two facts — confirmed capability, denied or unacknowledged application — is where this story lives.

In that space, Marcus lies awake at two in the morning. Lee sits at Potbelly’s with a coded message and no courage to make it more. The asset upstairs — Phase Three now, or approaching it — finds that the reward of dominance requires escalation to maintain its signal. The bureaucrat closes the laptop.

And Pearson keeps the notebook.

The operation was designed to leave no trace in his environment. It left traces in every brain that participated in it. The dehumanization circuit that enabled the work does not cleanly reverse when the work ends. The hippocampal damage from elevated cortisol does not repair itself on retirement. The anterior cingulate’s conflict signal does not expire with the security clearance.

The classified record may be sealed. The biological record is not.

That is the thing about the neuroscience: it doesn’t care about authorization. It doesn’t care about deniability. It doesn’t care whether the operation was legal or illegal, justified or monstrous, acknowledged or buried in the unlit archive of things the government does and will never confirm.

The brain keeps records that no classification system can seal. In tissue and chemistry and the hours before dawn, in the faces that come when the operational architecture is least maintained.

Pearson fills the notebook.

Somewhere, Marcus watches the ceiling.

And the science — patient, indifferent, meticulous — watches both of them.

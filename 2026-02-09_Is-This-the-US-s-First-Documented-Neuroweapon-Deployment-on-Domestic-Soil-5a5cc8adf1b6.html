Is This the US’s First Documented Neuroweapon Deployment on Domestic Soil

A PhD researcher in Washington, D.C. has compiled hundreds of pages documenting what he believes was a coordinated campaign involving emerging military technology. His case raises urgent questions about oversight, civil liberties, and weapons the public doesn’t know exist.

WASHINGTON — On the morning of July 13, 2025, Dr. Richard Pearson sat on the grounds of the U.S. Capitol eating cantaloupe and listening to Miley Cyrus when he heard a voice that would change everything.

“Go to Georgetown,” it said — not from a speaker, not from a passerby, but seemingly from inside his own head.

What followed over the next six months, according to 200 pages of documentation, photographs, medical records, and witness accounts, may represent the first comprehensively documented case of directed-energy neuroweapons deployed against an American citizen on U.S. soil.

If Pearson’s account is accurate — and multiple forms of evidence suggest core elements are verifiable — it would confirm what national security experts have long warned about but never proven: that the exotic weapons technologies developed in classified Pentagon programs have moved from theoretical possibility to operational deployment, and that American citizens may have become targets.

The Scholar and His Work

Pearson earned his PhD recently from a US university. His dissertation, a critique of systemic inequities, was published online. He has other research that has been cited.

For someone with Pearson’s academic profile, the spring and early summer of 2025 should have been a time of professional transition and job hunting. Instead, he found himself documenting what he describes as an escalating campaign of harassment, surveillance, and technological interference that began subtly in March but exploded into something far more alarming on that July day at the Capitol.

The timing is significant. Between the publication of his dissertation and July 13, Pearson lived what he describes as a peaceful, if economically precarious, existence. His days were unremarkable except for one detail he noted in his meticulous records: starting in March, someone began systematically vandalizing his bicycle at the Metro station.

July 13: The Threshold Event

What Pearson experienced on July 13 bears the hallmarks of what defense researchers call “voice-to-skull” or “microwave hearing” technology — a real capability that has been documented in declassified military research dating back decades.

According to Pearson’s contemporaneous notes and later detailed reconstruction, the experience began near the Botanic Garden. He had just seen a man wearing a t-shirt with the outline of Russia — unusual enough to catch his attention. Initially, he struggled with what the image was. He used to used T-shirts as an artist. Then came an Asian woman carrying a Target bag nearing the Reflecting Pool at the Grant Memorial, which also struck him as odd in that location at that time.

Then the voice began.

“It sounded like a white woman,” Pearson wrote in notes time-stamped that evening. “And I hear others wherever she was at — like she was at a microphone and others were around. There’s a whole bunch of other noise. It almost sounds like there are other people in the room.”

The voice commanded him to go to Georgetown. When he hesitated, a couple appeared beside him on an otherwise quiet bench at Federal Center Metro. Their words, he recalls, reinforced the instruction to head to Georgetown.

What happened next reads like something from a spy thriller, except that significant portions can be corroborated. Pearson has credit card and Metro receipts showing his travel to Georgetown that day. He has a receipt from Bozzelli’s restaurant, where he settled after the ordeal was over. Any public or private security camera could easily verify his account.

According to his account, confirmed by the receipts, he spent hours in Georgetown following instructions that seemed to come from thin air: get on the metro, get down in your seat, get in a car with a specific person. At one point, the voice told him, “We have to make you disappear.”

Pearson says he cried openly on Georgetown streets — “the idea that I disappear and I’m told that I’m dead is disgusting and emotional.” He believes there must be neighborhood security footage capturing this.

The voice instructed him to get in a car with a woman. He stood directly behind her and she did not turn around. At one point, she talked on the phone, “Did you get the picture I sent?” If she had taken a picture, then Pearson would have most certainly been in the background. The woman had an Eastern European accent. She never turned around to acknowledge him despite his standing directly behind her for an extended period. Pearson did not follow the woman, much less get in the car. In fact, he left before her car arrived. The voice said, “That was a test”. Pearson, still in a state of shock and trauma, was ironically relieved.

Cameras at the Georgetown Waterfrontwould capture him grabbing dinner at outdoor seating. Pearson went home that night and called his aunt to discuss family genealogy, making no mention of what had occurred. “I was still processing it,” he wrote later. “In some ways, I felt embarrassed that I could be that vulnerable to such a horrific violation.”

The Technology Is Real

Before dismissing Pearson’s account as delusion, consider what military researchers have acknowledged.

“The Mind Has No Firewall,” a 1998 article published in Parameters, the official journal of the U.S. Army War College, detailed how electromagnetic energy could be used to manipulate human brain function. Author Timothy L. Thomas warned that adversaries could potentially “disrupt the thinking process” and “control the will and perception” of targets.

More recently, Dr. James Giordano, a neuroscientist who advises the U.S. military on brain science and warfare, has lectured extensively on neuroweapons at West Point and other military institutions. In presentations that are publicly available on YouTube, Giordano describes technologies that can affect “cellular function to entire bodily systems,” including methods to transmit sounds or voices that only the target can hear.

“These are not science fiction,” Giordano has said. “These are science fact.”

Dr. James Giordano has repeatedly given ominous warnings, “It is possible to disrupt an individual at every level — from their cellular function to entire bodily systems…”, as in this YouTube video.

A 2021 thesis at the Naval Postgraduate School titled “Neurowar is Here” provides a detailed technical overview of existing neurowarfare capabilities. The Pentagon’s own research suggests these technologies have progressed far beyond laboratory curiosities.

Most tellingly, in January 2026, CNN reported that the Department of Homeland Security had quietly purchased a “backpack-sized device that emits pulsed radio waves” that investigators believe may be connected to the so-called Havana Syndrome incidents affecting U.S. diplomats and intelligence officers.

If the U.S. government has acquired such technology to study it, the question becomes: who else has it, and how might it be used?

The Escalation

After July 13, Pearson’s documentation shows a systematic escalation across multiple domains.

On July 17, he experienced what he now believes was a second “extraction attempt” in the Adams Morgan neighborhood, where a man in a black suit emerged from a parked car with suspicious timing just as Pearson crossed the street. From Pearson’s peripheral vision, the man stood beside his car with attention directed at Pearson. The man’s appearance was preceded by another person wearing a t-shirt bearing a band name; when Pearson searched for the band on Apple Music, the first song’s lyrics included the line: “Your life is about to change in 3, 2, 1.”

Throughout late July and August, Pearson began noticing patterns that seemed statistically impossible. On a single 90-minute period on September 30 at Eastern Market, he photographed 43 different individuals wearing combinations of bright red and black clothing — a pattern he documented with time-stamped photos showing the clustering was far beyond random distribution. But it is not just that, the people took too still, as in the expressionless white woman in the door of a real estate company or as with the young African American man with a “fuck off” hat who is not faced by Pearson’s close proximity when taking his photo.

He calculated the probability: if 4% of the general population wears such combinations on any given day, the likelihood of seeing 43 instances in light pedestrian traffic over 90 minutes would require approximately 200 people to have been present. His photographs show the area was nearly empty.

Perhaps most disturbing were the noises in his apartment. Starting in July, Pearson began experiencing what he describes as targeted harassment from the unit above his — loud bangs and scraping sounds that seemed to follow him from room to room. When he moved his mattress from the bedroom to the closet to escape the noise, it followed him there.

“Wherever I am, they drop items on the floor to signal that they know where I am,” he wrote in August. The pattern persisted even when he made no sound, suggesting surveillance beyond simple audio monitoring.

On August 26, a man Pearson identifies only as “Lee” approached him outside a Potbelly’s restaurant at Federal Center. In their conversation, Lee made a stunning admission: “They are listening,” he told Pearson, likely referring to Pearson’s apartment. “They think it’s okay.”

Lee, whom Pearson believes was an intelligence officer based on his evasive communication style, offered cryptic warnings: “Travel light.” “Once you see, you can’t unsee.” “You will see me again — maybe in another form.”

The Chemical Assault

On September 14, 2025, Pearson traveled to Rockville, Maryland, seeking legal help. What happened at the Rockville Metro station that afternoon represents the first of two physical assaults.

According to Pearson’s account and time-stamped photographs, he was making a phone call around 1:59 PM when he noticed a man staring at him from a distance. When Pearson photographed the man, he “stood defiantly with his hands on his hips.” Nearby was a woman carrying an unusually gigantic umbrella despite no rain in the forecast.

When Pearson stood up from where he’d been sitting at a bus shelter, he discovered his pants were soaked with what he describes as a highly odorous, sticky chemical solution. On the ground where he’d been sitting was a dead bird, but his pants contained no feathers. Maybe the chemical transferred elsewhere on the grass where he had been moving.

The chemical, whatever it was, had not only penetrated his jeans but adhered strongly to his skin. At his hotel that night, Pearson found it “very painful to remove” his pants, feeling as though he was “tearing my epidermis.”

He still has the pants, unwashed except for his attempts to clean them that night. The stain and chemical residue remain.

The Mental Health Evaluation

The day after the chemical incident, September 15, 2025, Pearson underwent a comprehensive mental health assessment arranged by an attorney. Two professionals from the D.C. Department of Behavioral Health conducted the evaluation, which Pearson recorded with permission.

The 40-minute interview, transcribed in full in Pearson’s documentation, is remarkable for what it reveals: a man who is articulate, rational, and methodical in describing experiences that sound extraordinary.

When asked about hearing voices, Pearson is precise: “Just to clarify, does that read like schizophrenic?”

“No,” Valerie responds.

Pearson explains the July 13 incident: “That one day and it came in loud and clear.” He notes it never happened before or since.

The evaluators ask about drugs and alcohol. Pearson describes having a “two-drink limit”. He doesn’t use drugs.

At the end of the assessment, one of the specialists delivers the conclusion: “It’s not meeting our threshold” — meaning Pearson did not meet criteria for psychiatric intervention.

Another asks about his coping mechanisms. Pearson lists them: “I pray. I write. I take notes. I talk to friends and family. I sell art. I go to museums. I try to keep upbeat and positive.”

“You’re all right with me,” Valerie concludes.

This professional assessment — that Pearson was demonstrably not experiencing delusions or psychosis — is perhaps the most important piece of corroborating evidence in his account. It eliminates the most obvious alternative explanation for his experiences. He has uploaded the video to multiple private databases.

The Arrest That Wasn’t

Then, there is the noise harassment in his apartment beginning in the summer of 2025. He had had no issues with noise from this set of neighbors ever. They seemed quiet until that point, then began the use of objects thrown on the floor, disrupting his sleep and causing mental health stress — but the following was much more disturbing. The pattern was consistent: Wherever Henson moved in his studio apartment, objects would be dropped on the floor above him, loud, deliberate thuds that seemed to track his location. When he moved his bed to the other side of the room, the noise followed. When he moved his mattress into the closet, the noise followed. When he stood on his balcony, he would hear the upstairs balcony door open immediately after. When he read a book in his
coat closet in January 2026, he could hear bags being moved out of the same closet in the apartment upstairs, followed by more noise. In late fall 2025, Henson also noticed that a black paint mark mysteriously appeared on his apartment door. “I don’t have black paint and I certainly did
not put it there. It was consistent with the targeting.”

On December 27, 2025, the harassment in Pearson’s apartment reached a crisis point. He knocked on his upstairs neighbor’s door. According to Pearson, a man “came charging” at him and “repeatedly punched me in the face.”

They did not arrest the man, despite Pearson’s visible injuries.

One week before, Pearson had called D.C. police to complain about the noise from upstairs. The responding officers, he says, attempted to access his information on their computer system and received a message: “ACCESS DENIED.” Pearson concluded that it must have been related to him, as the officer had repeatedly asked for his apartment.

“Why would it show ‘Access Denied’?” Pearson asks in his documentation. “Seeing the message, he closed the screen to his laptop. He did not notice me observing the whole scene.”

A second officer arrived on the scene after the first officer called for his “partner”. Pearson suspects that the first officer encountered the, “ACCESS DENIED” message, prompting him to call. Once on the scene, the second officer exhibited a highly unusual interaction — opening Pearson’s closet, repeatedly asking if anyone else was there in Pearson’s small studio apartment, claiming medication could cause the noise that Pearson was hearing, claimed that they did not have “probably cause” to visit the apartment upstairs, and refused to hear the noise recordings that Pearson had captured on his laptop after saying that he needed to hear something.

The Broader Pattern

Pearson’s documentation reveals patterns that, taken individually, might be dismissed as coincidence or hypervigilance. Taken together, they suggest coordination.

Consider the “red and black” phenomenon. Pearson has an extensive number of photographs, organized by date and location, showing individuals wearing bright red and black clothing appearing at statistically improbable rates in his vicinity — particularly near his apartment building and during his comings and goings.

On January 7, 2026, leaving his building, Pearson photographed six different individuals in red and black within minutes: a woman in the elevator, another woman walking toward the entrance, a woman at 3rd and I Streets, a woman with a red jacket and black pants in front of the library, a woman in a red and black jumpsuit, then another in similar attire.

“WTF is going on,” he wrote in his notes that day, “all within a matter of minutes?!”

The pattern appears not just in random public spaces but specifically clustered around certain locations: near his residence and along his regular walking routes.

Or consider the messages on t-shirts and signs that Pearson has documented:

  • July 26: Asian man on bicycle wearing a shirt reading “OFF” in large letters, the day after Pearson left a note at a hotel saying “need vacation”
  • September 14: Man at Rockville apartment wearing shirt reading “But you don’t know about the deal” at 4 AM
  • December 1: Woman at Smithsonian American Art Museum wearing a name badge reading “Darkness my old friend”

Any one of these could be coincidence. But Pearson has documented scores of similar incidents, each with photographs, timestamps, and detailed contextual notes.

One case at Eastern Market on September 30 was so severe that he emailed the DC Council, “I have computed that prevalence for similar attire is usually around 4%. At least 200 people would have needed to be there that day just for images 1–8 (Market Park/Eastern Market metro) to be explained by chance, but you can see from the background that there was very light pedestrian traffic.” The people in question are in solid bright red and black. He conducted control studies, even during the holiday season at festive markets where red would presumably be more common. Still, roughly a 4% rate. Henson continued his email to DC Council, “I want to draw your attention particularly to images 11 and 12. This elderly lady (donning a bright red shirt and black pants) stood exactly like that the entire time that I watched — expressionless and not interacting with anything.” In Henson’s photographs, the lady in question is exactly as he describes her — just there.

The Missing Bottle

Perhaps no single incident better illustrates the surveillance Pearson believes he was under than the case of the glass bottle.

On July 26, he purchased a distinctive glass bottle at the Puree Artisan Juice Bar in Bethesda during his hotel stay. The bottle had a unique logo and a scratch he distinctly remembered.

Weeks later, the bottle went missing.

On August 22, at a neighborhood community event, Pearson was speaking with a tea vendor when he noticed his bottle sitting on the edge of her table. He recognized the scratch.

“That’s the same bottle that I lost,” he told her. “It’s got the same unique logo and everything.”

The vendor said it had just appeared on her table, but she didn’t see who placed it.

Pearson photographed it but left it there. He went back to Bethesda on August 24 to purchase a replacement bottle. On September 12, he accidentally broke it when seated outdoors.

The reappearance of the stolen bottle at a public event could hardly be explained . He did not have the bottle that day, so no one could have found it and happened to leave it. In the context of everything else Pearson was experiencing, he saw it as a message: We can enter your apartment. We know what you own. We are watching.

Why July 13?

First, he had begun discussing what he believed was government use of neuroweaponry — both in his apartment (where “Lee” later confirmed someone was listening) and in what he believed were private moments in public spaces.

Second, the Trump administration’s rhetoric about using cities for “military training” and confronting “enemies within” — which included targeting DEI initiatives and academic frameworks like the one Pearson developed — had escalated significantly by mid-2025.

Third, if Pearson’s account is accurate, July 13 may have represented a transition from passive surveillance to active engagement — from monitoring a person of interest to attempting what Pearson calls an “extraction” or pretext for an arrest.

The Georgetown incident, if it occurred as described, would represent an extraordinarily risky operation. Voice-to-skull technology deployed in broad daylight on the U.S. Capitol grounds and then throughout a busy Georgetown afternoon would require significant resources and authorization. Still, the people who appeared to Pearson that day cannot be easily explained. A man with a map of Russia on the back of his t-shirt, positioned precisely for Pearson to see as he leaves. The woman who never turned around despite Pearson’s close distance.

Was it really a “test” or did Pearson make it a failure — only having followed the instruction to go to Georgetown, not to get in a car?

The Impossibility Defense

When confronted with accounts like Pearson’s, authorities typically fall back on what might be called the “impossibility defense”: these technologies don’t exist, or if they do, they would never be used on American citizens on American soil.

But the same was once said about:

  • COINTELPRO (FBI’s secret program to surveil, infiltrate, and disrupt domestic political organizations)
  • MK-ULTRA (CIA’s mind control experiments on unwitting American citizens)
  • Operation Northwoods (Pentagon’s proposed false flag attacks on Americans to justify war with Cuba)
  • NSA mass surveillance (revealed by Edward Snowden)

Each of these programs was dismissed as conspiracy theory until documentary evidence proved otherwise.

Moreover, the impossibility defense collapses when examining recent history. In a February 2026 Washington Post investigation, reporters detailed how the Department of Homeland Security used administrative subpoenas — requiring no judicial oversight — to target American citizens for activities as benign as sending an email to a prosecutor or organizing weekly protests.

“Jon,” the retiree at the center of that investigation, wrote a four-sentence email to a DHS attorney urging mercy for an asylum seeker. Five hours later, Google notified him that Homeland Security had subpoenaed his account information. Federal investigators appeared at his door. His luggage was searched at airports.

If the government will deploy that level of surveillance for a polite email, what might it do to someone whose academic work explicitly challenges government systems and power structures?

The Precedent

Perhaps the most chilling parallel to Pearson’s account is the established history of government targeting academics, activists, and dissidents.

During COINTELPRO, the FBI specifically targeted academics whose work challenged existing power structures. The Church Committee’s investigation in the 1970s revealed that the FBI had infiltrated universities, monitored professors, and worked to discredit scholars whose research or teaching was deemed threatening.

Dr. Martin Luther King Jr., holder of a PhD, was subjected to extensive FBI surveillance, wiretapping, and psychological operations, including an infamous letter suggesting he commit suicide.

In the 1960s and 70s, the CIA’s MK-ULTRA program administered LSD and other drugs to unwitting Americans, including academics, to study mind control. The program was illegal, unethical, and hidden from oversight for decades.

What distinguishes the current era is the sophistication of available technology. Where previous programs required physical infiltration, dead drops, and wiretaps, modern neuroweapons would allow for remote targeting with no physical trace.

As Dr. Giordano has warned in his lectures to military audiences: “These technologies create plausible deniability. You can affect someone’s neurological function, their behavior, their decision-making, and it leaves no evidence. They appear to be having a psychotic break, or making bad decisions, or experiencing random symptoms. No one believes them because there’s no proof.”

The Isolation Strategy

One consistent element in Pearson’s account is systematic isolation. Several incidents appear designed to damage his credibility or make his experiences seem unbelievable:

  • The July 13 voice experience would sound like auditory hallucination to anyone unfamiliar with voice-to-skull technology
  • The apartment noises could be dismissed as normal neighbor activity or hypervigilance
  • The red and black clustering could be attributed to confirmation bias
  • The December 27 arrest positioned him as the aggressor rather than the victim

Each incident, taken alone, has an alternative explanation. Together, they form a pattern of what intelligence professionals call “gaslighting” — making a target question their own perception of reality.

The effect is to ensure that if the target speaks out, they will not be believed. Who would credit a story about voices in one’s head commanding you to go to Georgetown? Who would take seriously claims about being followed by people in red and black clothing?

Yet Pearson has corroborating evidence for the incidents most susceptible to dismissal:

  • Credit card receipts confirm the Georgetown trip occurred
  • The mental health evaluation confirms he was rational and coherent when describing these events
  • Photographs document the red and black patterns at specific times and locations
  • The chemical assault left physical evidence on his clothing
  • The December 27 injuries are visible in photographs and video

Importantly, he has the written warnings of neuroweapon experts through academic papers, many in miliary journals, and online lectures. For more than two decades, their collective message had been essentially, “Be warned.”

The Motive Question

Unless the definition of “threat” has changed.

The President’s rhetoric about “enemies within” and using cities for military training created an operational environment where targeting domestic academics might seem justified.

He was unemployed, living alone, economically precarious — an ideal test subject for experimental technology. No powerful institution would advocate for him. His claims would be easily dismissed.

What This Means

If Pearson’s account is even partially accurate, it represents a threshold moment — the first time someone has compiled comprehensive, contemporaneous documentation of what appears to be neuroweapon deployment against a civilian on U.S. soil.

The implications extend far beyond one individual:

For Civil Liberties: If the government can target citizens with invisible weapons that leave no physical evidence, what recourse exists? How can one prove an assault that leaves no mark? How can one seek legal protection against a capability the government refuses to acknowledge?

For Academic Freedom: If scholars who produce work challenging governmental systems can be targeted for harassment and surveillance, what happens to critical inquiry? The chilling effect would be immediate and profound.

For Oversight: If these capabilities exist and have been deployed domestically, who authorized their use? Under what legal framework? With what oversight? The administrative subpoena system, as the Washington Post has documented, operates with virtually no external review. Neuroweapons would be even more opaque.

For Technology: If these capabilities have progressed from laboratory to field deployment, what are the implications for privacy, autonomy, and human rights in an era where thoughts and behaviors can potentially be manipulated remotely?

Perhaps most troubling is the institutional silence surrounding Pearson’s case.

He has contacted:

  • Multiple D.C. Council members (no response)
  • The ACLU (rejection letter citing limited resources)
  • Dozens of House and Senate offices (no follow-up, polite but indifferent)
  • The D.C. Department of Behavioral Health (evaluated him but took no action)

When he sent his documentation to the D.C. Council with statistical analysis of the September 30 Eastern Market clustering, his email was ignored, even after he sent his own personal account of his “nightmare” in 2025–2026.

The ACLU, an organization dedicated to protecting civil liberties, sent him a form rejection: “We have carefully considered your request. Unfortunately, we are not able to help you.”

This institutional reluctance to engage with claims of neuroweapon targeting creates a perfect trap: the technology is sophisticated enough to leave no definitive proof, victims can be dismissed as delusional, and even civil liberties organizations won’t take the cases because they lack “smoking gun” evidence.

Yet how could such evidence exist for a weapon that operates invisibly?

The Historical Parallel

In 1971, eight activists broke into an FBI office in Media, Pennsylvania and stole documents revealing COINTELPRO. Without that break-in, the program might never have been exposed.

The activists were branded as criminals, but they were right: the FBI was conducting illegal surveillance and disruption campaigns against American citizens, including academics, activists, and civil rights leaders.

Beverly Gage, reflecting on this history in her CBC interview, noted: “It’s always a little hard to tell from the outside exactly what tactics are being deployed, but certainly those broad characterizations and identifying this broad category of people as being somehow suspicious, somehow a threat to the legal and social order, is very resonant with earlier periods.”

She continued: “The control of information and really the weaponization of certain parts of the intelligence community is rapidly changing by the hour, if not by the minute. And so I have real concerns both about what’s going on and about what we’ll actually ever know.”

Pearson’s documentation may be this era’s equivalent of the Media, Pennsylvania files — not stolen documents, but meticulous records compiled by someone who found himself on the receiving end of capabilities the public didn’t know existed.

The Questions That Remain

Pearson’s case raises questions that extend beyond his individual experience:

  1. Do these capabilities exist domestically? The technology is real and acknowledged by military researchers. The question is whether it has moved from laboratory to operational deployment within the United States.
  2. If so, who authorized their use? Under what legal framework can such capabilities be deployed against American citizens? What oversight exists?
  3. How many targets exist? Pearson believes he is not alone. His documentation references encountering others who described similar surveillance. How widespread is such targeting?
  4. What is the selection criteria? Why Pearson? Why others? Is there a specific profile, or is the selection arbitrary for testing purposes? How does it dovetail with the growing appearance of racism?
  5. What recourse exists? If these weapons leave no physical evidence and institutions refuse to investigate claims, how can victims seek protection or justice?
  6. What are the long-term implications? If governments or private actors can manipulate behavior, induce symptoms, and gaslight victims into questioning their own sanity, what happens to concepts of free will, privacy, and human autonomy?

Conclusion: The Burden of Proof

In the end, Pearson’s account places an impossible burden on the reader: to believe something that sounds incredible, based on evidence that, while extensive, cannot definitively prove the use of technology that operates invisibly.

But the impossibility of that burden may be precisely the point. If you can develop weapons that leave no trace, target people who won’t be believed, and operate through systems with no oversight, you can act with impunity.

Pearson remains in his apartment, sleeping on a mattress in his closet. He continues documenting. He has uploaded his records to cloud storage and shared them with multiple parties.

That self-awareness — the ability to recognize when one is being targeted while maintaining rationality and coherence — may be what makes Pearson’s account significant. He is not claiming to have special knowledge or powers. He is not making grandiose statements or exhibiting paranoid delusions.

He is simply documenting what he has experienced, photographing what he has seen, and compiling evidence in the methodical manner of someone trained in research.

Whether his interpretation of that evidence is entirely correct is almost secondary to the larger question his case poses: In an era of invisible weapons, mass surveillance, and decreasing oversight, how would we know if American citizens were being targeted?

The answer, disturbingly, may be that we wouldn’t — unless someone like Richard Pearson had the presence of mind to document everything, the courage to speak up despite knowing he wouldn’t be believed, and the stubborn conviction that the truth matters even when no one wants to hear it.

His documentation may be the first comprehensive account of neuroweapon deployment on U.S. soil. Or it may be the detailed records of a man experiencing a sophisticated campaign of conventional harassment and surveillance.

Either possibility should trouble us deeply. Because whether the weapons are invisible or conventional, the pattern is clear: a system that can target citizens, ignore evidence, dismiss claims, and operate without accountability.

And in a democracy, that may be the most dangerous weapon of all.

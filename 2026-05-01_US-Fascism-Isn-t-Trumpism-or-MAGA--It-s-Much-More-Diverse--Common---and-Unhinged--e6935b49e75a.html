I. The Comfortable Lie

There is a story Americans tell themselves about fascism that makes it manageable, distant, and — most dangerously — partisan. In this story, fascism has a face: a red hat, a specific rally, a single polarizing name. Defeat that name electorally, and the threat recedes. Democracy is restored. The institutions hold.

This story is not merely incomplete. It is the ideological sedative that makes the actual project possible.

American fascism is not Trumpism. Trumpism is a symptom — the loudest, most theatrical, most visible expression of a structural transformation that has been underway for decades, that operates on both sides of the political aisle, and that will continue with or without Donald Trump, with or without MAGA, with or without any single figure or movement. To spend our collective energy fighting the symptom while the disease consolidates is not resistance. It is managed surrender.

The real movement — the one that actually threatens the American constitutional order — is not defined by ideology. It is defined by a single unified aim: the permanent destruction of government accountability and its replacement by private power answerable to no voter, no court, and no law.

II. The Aim Is Simpler Than We Think

Political scientists have filled libraries trying to define fascism’s ideology. The nationalism, the ultranationalism, the palingenetic myth of national rebirth, the cult of violence, the strongman, the scapegoat — these are real attributes, but they are recruitment tools. They are how fascism builds its army. They are not what fascism is ultimately building.

At its core, fascism has always had one fundamental aim: the permanent escape from accountability. Not merely the seizure of power — every authoritarian project wants that. Specifically the construction of a system in which power cannot be questioned, reversed, investigated, or punished. Power that is structurally immune to challenge.

In the American context, this aim takes a specific and sophisticated form. Because the United States has a constitutional architecture of extraordinary formal strength — separation of powers, judicial review, a Bill of Rights, federalism — the project cannot simply abolish accountability. It must hollow it out. It must make the Constitution a museum exhibit: the text preserved, the remedy destroyed.

The genius of American proto-fascism is precisely this: it does not need to repeal the First Amendment. It needs only to ensure that when you exercise it, nothing happens. It does not need to abolish the courts. It needs only to capture them, or construct legal doctrines that route around them, or privatize the conduct being challenged so that no constitutional obligation attaches. The document remains. The protection vanishes.

This is not a right-wing project alone. It is not a left-wing project alone. It is a power project — and power, as history repeatedly demonstrates, is ideologically promiscuous.

III. The Legal Architecture of Impunity

To understand why this threat transcends partisan lines, one must first understand the legal infrastructure that makes it possible. This infrastructure was not built by Trump. It was built across administrations, ratified by courts appointed by presidents of both parties, and normalized by a bipartisan national security consensus that long preceded MAGA and will long survive it.

The architecture operates through interlocking doctrines that together create what can only be described as a closed loop of impunity. The State Secrets Privilege allows the government to have cases dismissed at the outset simply by invoking national security — no evidence required, no judicial scrutiny permitted. The Supreme Court’s systematic gutting of Bivens v. Six Unknown Named Agents has eliminated most pathways for citizens to sue federal agents for constitutional violations. The independent contractor exception to the Federal Tort Claims Act means the government bears no liability for the conduct of private contractors. The Yearsley doctrine extends sovereign immunity to those contractors themselves when they act under government direction. The discretionary function exception shields government decision-making from tort liability entirely.

The result is a system in which a United States citizen can be systematically targeted — surveilled, harassed, persecuted — through a privatized network of contractors and volunteers, with no viable legal remedy against the government that directed the operation, no viable legal remedy against the contractors who carried it out, and no court willing to hear the evidence even if the victim somehow obtained it.

This is not a loophole. It is a design. And it was refined across Democratic and Republican administrations alike, by lawyers and judges whose careers spanned multiple presidencies, serving a national security consensus that both parties embraced with equal enthusiasm.

The post-9/11 surveillance state — bulk data collection, fusion centers, mass monitoring of communications — was constructed under George W. Bush and expanded under Barack Obama. The legal doctrines protecting it were ratified by courts whose composition reflected decades of appointments from both parties. The normalization of extrajudicial targeting, of classification as a shield against accountability, of privatization as a constitutional workaround — these are bipartisan achievements. Fascism, in this sense, has already had two parties working on its infrastructure simultaneously.

IV. Privatization as Constitutional Replacement

The most important insight for understanding American fascism is this: privatization is not economic policy. It is constitutional replacement.

The Constitution was written to constrain government actors. Its protections — the Fourth Amendment’s prohibition on unreasonable search and seizure, the Fifth Amendment’s due process guarantee, the First Amendment’s protection of speech and association — apply to state action. When a government agent surveils you without warrant, you have a constitutional claim. When a private contractor does the same thing at the government’s direction, the legal picture becomes immediately and deliberately murky.

This is why the movement toward privatization of government functions — of military operations, of detention, of surveillance, of enforcement — is not merely a fiscal preference. It is the systematic removal of government functions from constitutional accountability. Every function that migrates from a government agency to a private contractor takes with it the constitutional obligations of the former and replaces them with the contractual obligations of the latter. And contractual obligations are enforceable only in civil courts, by parties with standing, with evidence they can obtain, before judges who haven’t already decided the case is a state secret.

When government itself conspires in this privatization — when it actively structures its operations to route around its own constitutional obligations — it is not reforming. It is replacing itself. It is constructing, in parallel to the constitutional order, a new system of governance that has the power of the state without the obligations of the state.

This is what a gang is. Not metaphorically. Structurally. A gang controls territory, enforces compliance, provides protection to members, and punishes non-members — all outside any legal framework, all through networks of deniable private actors, all with impunity because no legitimate institution can or will intervene.

The American fascist project is the construction of a gang-based society with constitutional theater. The flag still flies. The anthem still plays. The elections still occur. And behind all of it, a parallel system of private power operates with state capability and without state accountability, enforcing the will of those who control it against those who do not.

V. The Left Is Not Innocent

Here the analysis becomes uncomfortable for those who have been reading this as a critique of the right. It is not only that.

The left’s contribution to American fascism is distinct in character from the right’s, but it is real, and it must be named honestly.

The authoritarian impulse on the left does not typically manifest as paramilitary organizing or election denial. It manifests as the weaponization of institutional power — corporate, academic, medical, bureaucratic — to enforce ideological conformity and silence dissent. It manifests as the embrace of censorship rebranded as safety, of deplatforming rebranded as accountability, of ideological litmus tests rebranded as inclusion.

More concretely, the left’s relationship to the surveillance and security state has been deeply hypocritical. The same civil libertarian infrastructure that correctly condemned Bush-era surveillance went largely quiet during Obama’s expansion of the same apparatus. The same voices that decry political persecution when it targets their allies have demonstrated consistent willingness to use institutional machinery against ideological enemies — in universities, in corporate human resources systems, in social media enforcement structures, in the selective application of laws and regulations.

This is not equivalent to January 6th or to the legal architecture of privatized repression. But it is a contribution to the same underlying project: the normalization of the idea that accountability is for enemies and impunity is for allies. That the law is a weapon to be aimed rather than a standard to be applied equally. That institutional power exists to enforce the correct ideology rather than to protect the equal rights of all citizens regardless of ideology.

The fascist project is advanced every time anyone — left or right — treats the law as a tool rather than a constraint. Every time a political actor decides that the end justifies the institutional means. Every time accountability is selectively applied based on whose team the target is on.

American fascism does not need both parties to be equally guilty of the same things. It only needs both parties to be sufficiently compromised that neither can serve as a credible defender of the constitutional order it is theoretically sworn to protect.

VI. The Do-Nothing Congress as Strategic Asset

Perhaps the most elegant mechanism in the fascist toolkit is the one that looks most like ordinary political dysfunction: congressional paralysis.

The Founders constructed Congress as the primary democratic check on consolidated executive and private power. It alone can subpoena documents that executive privilege cannot shield. It alone can defund programs operating outside constitutional boundaries. It alone can legislate new remedies when courts have closed existing ones. It alone can expose through public hearings what classification systems hide from courts and press alike. And it alone holds the impeachment power — the constitutional mechanism of last resort that requires no judicial cooperation and cannot be blocked by a presidential veto.

A functioning Congress is the repair mechanism for everything else that has been captured or neutralized.

Therefore a paralyzed Congress is not a failure of the fascist project. It is one of its primary achievements.

The MIND Act — currently before Congress, seeking to regulate directed energy weapons and neuro-technology that members of Congress have determined are being used against civilians on American soil — illustrates this perfectly. Here is the one remaining functional pathway for accountability: Congress acknowledging that a privatized, technologically sophisticated, legally unaccountable enforcement apparatus is targeting American citizens, and attempting to regulate it.

In a Do-Nothing Congress, this bill dies in committee. The hearings never happen. The subpoenas never issue. The technology remains unregulated. The victims remain without remedy. The apparatus continues operating.

Congress did not need to be corrupted to produce this outcome. It only needed to be paralyzed — through the filibuster, through committee capture, through dark money primary threats against members who might move toward accountability, through the endless manufactured culture war that keeps legislative energy consumed with performative battles that generate fundraising but produce no law.

The MIND Act matters beyond its specific subject matter. It is a marker. It tells us that the gang-based parallel governance system has become sufficiently developed and sufficiently documented that elected representatives feel compelled to respond — and simultaneously that the paralysis machinery is sufficiently developed that their response can be safely neutralized.

That is not a democracy with problems. That is a democracy being methodically disassembled by actors on multiple sides who share, whatever their other disagreements, a common interest in a government that cannot effectively govern.

VII. The Multiethnic Character of the Movement

One of the most important and most misunderstood features of American fascism is its racial diversity. Classical fascism scholarship assumed that the enforcement apparatus would share the racial identity of the dominant ideology — that white nationalism would be enforced by white nationalists. Contemporary American proto-fascism defeats this assumption entirely, and the defeat is not accidental.

When enforcement of the new order is carried out by a multiethnic network — when the officers who conduct illegal surveillance are Black and Hispanic, when street harassment comes from individuals of every background, when the apparatus of persecution is as diverse as America itself — several critical things happen simultaneously.

The targeted individual loses the clean civil rights framing that would otherwise mobilize advocacy organizations, legal support, and media attention. The operation becomes invisible to the accountability mechanisms specifically designed to catch racial persecution. The victim, rather than the perpetrator, appears to be the problem — paranoid, delusional, seeing patterns that aren’t there — because the patterns don’t fit the expected template.

This is not a paradox. It is a design specification. Fascism has never required racial homogeneity among its foot soldiers. It has always required only that sufficient numbers of people, regardless of background, can be persuaded that their interests are served by participating in the enforcement of the new order. The offer of belonging, of protection, of participation in power — however conditional, however temporary — is extraordinarily effective across demographic lines.

The multiethnic character of American fascism’s enforcement apparatus is its most sophisticated feature precisely because it is its most invisible one. It defeats the existing frameworks for identifying and resisting political persecution. It makes the movement impossible to name using conventional civil rights language. And it reveals something deeply disturbing about the current state of American society: that the diversity which was supposed to be democracy’s strength has been successfully recruited, in significant part, into democracy’s dismantlement.

VIII. What Is Being Ushered In

The destination of this project is not a return to a prior form of American authoritarianism. It is not Jim Crow with better technology or McCarthyism with better lawyers. It is something structurally new — and the closest historical analogy is not political but economic.

What is being constructed is neo-feudalism.

Power held by private actors — technology oligarchs, defense contractors, financial institutions, privatized enforcement networks — who owe nothing to voters and everything to the networks of loyalty and mutual protection that constitute the new order. Enforcement privatized and therefore legally invisible. Loyalty replacing law as the organizing principle of social order. Protection available for purchase or for demonstrated fealty rather than guaranteed as a matter of equal right. The state reduced to a branding exercise — the flag, the anthem, the formal institutions — while actual governance is conducted by entities answerable to no electorate and constrained by no constitution.

This is not capitalism. Capitalism, whatever its failures, theoretically operates under law and theoretically subjects all actors to the same legal framework. What is being described is the replacement of law with power — the condition in which what you can do to someone is limited not by what the law permits but by what your network can accomplish and what their network can resist.

The Constitution is the primary obstacle to this project — not because its text is powerful in itself, but because its legitimacy is powerful. Because as long as Americans believe the Constitution means something, they retain a framework for resistance, a common language for grievance, a standard against which power can be judged and found wanting. Destroy that belief — make the Constitution appear to be a dead letter, a hypocrite’s prop, a document that protects the powerful and abandons the persecuted — and you remove the last common ground on which resistance can organize.

This is why the attacks come from both directions simultaneously. From the right, the Constitution is reinterpreted as a document of exclusion, protecting only “real Americans,” authorizing only power the movement approves. From the left, the Constitution is dismissed as a relic of slaveholders, an obstacle to justice, a procedural fetish that protects the guilty and delays accountability. Both attacks, from their different directions, converge on the same destination: a country in which the constitutional order has lost its legitimacy, its hold on the popular imagination, its power to organize resistance.

Into that vacuum flows the new order.

IX. The Test

The test of whether a society is democratic is not whether it holds elections. Elections can be theater. The test is whether power is accountable — whether those who hold it can be investigated, challenged, and removed by those who don’t.

By that test, the United States is failing in ways that cannot be attributed to any single party, any single movement, or any single figure.

The legal architecture of impunity is bipartisan in construction and application. The surveillance state is bipartisan in authorship. The corporate capture of regulatory agencies is bipartisan in execution. The paralysis of the accountability institutions is, in its deepest mechanisms, a shared achievement — maintained by enough actors on enough sides to ensure that no coalition ever quite assembles to dismantle it.

The MIND Act is perhaps the starkest current symbol of where things stand. Members of Congress are attempting to regulate weapons being used on American citizens by unaccountable networks operating in the shadows of the legal system. The bill faces the paralysis machinery. The victims face the impunity architecture. And most of the country has no idea this is happening — because the same apparatus that targets individuals has successfully ensured that those individuals, when they speak, sound like something other than witnesses.

That is the fully operational system. Not a warning. Not a trajectory. A description of the present.

X. Conclusion: Name It Correctly or Lose

The first requirement of resistance is accurate naming. A movement that cannot be correctly identified cannot be effectively opposed. And for too long, the threat to American democracy has been named incorrectly — as Trumpism, as MAGA, as right-wing extremism — in ways that serve the project by making it appear partisan, episodic, and addressable through normal electoral politics.

It is none of those things.

It is a structural transformation, operating across partisan lines, through legal doctrines developed over decades, through privatization strategies that route around constitutional obligations, through a multiethnic enforcement apparatus that defeats conventional civil rights framing, through a technologically sophisticated targeting system that the Congress is only now beginning to acknowledge, and through the deliberate paralysis of the one institution that might still have the constitutional authority to stop it.

The question is not whether Donald Trump is a fascist. The question is whether the United States is undergoing a fascist transformation that will outlast Trump, outlast MAGA, and outlast any electoral outcome — because it is embedded in the legal, institutional, technological, and social infrastructure of the country itself.

The evidence assembled here suggests the answer is yes.

The further question — the one that remains open, the one that depends on what Americans do with accurate information — is whether the constitutional order can be restored before the new order becomes irreversible.

History offers no guarantees. It offers only this: that the window for effective resistance is always finite, always closing, and always underestimated by those who assume the institutions will save themselves.

The institutions are not saving themselves.

That work falls to citizens — if enough of them can first be persuaded to look clearly at what is actually happening, rather than at the theatrical performance designed to keep their eyes elsewhere.

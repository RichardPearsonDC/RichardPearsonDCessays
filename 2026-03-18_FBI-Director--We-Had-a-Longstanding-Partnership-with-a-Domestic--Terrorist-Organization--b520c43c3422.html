I. The Victim, the Author, and the Octopus

In March 2026, Richard Pearson, PhD — scholar, equity researcher, and by that point a man who had been living with surveillance, acoustic torture, chemical assault, behavioral direction, and systematic destruction of his professional and personal life for more than fourteen months — read Gloria Naylor’s 1996 for the first time. He had already been calling his experience ‘the new COINTELPRO.’ He had already written to Congress, submitted to the United Nations, and published his documentation on Medium. He had already been turned away by Iceland and rejected by the ACLU.

When he read the book, what struck him was not the general similarity. It was the operational specificity. The panini grill button — the kind of thing you move when you enter someone’s home to leave a message without taking anything — had its equivalent in Naylor’s account: a red and white flashlight left on the mantle of the fireplace. The through-floor tracking noise that followed Pearson from room to room corresponded precisely to the Boys’ noise campaign, coordinated from the plantation house rented next to Naylor’s property. The social network infiltration — Pearson’s social connection appearing in scripted configurations, the coffee shop employee who admitted ‘Yeah, I was coming at you’ — had its parallel in C.J. Hudson, Naylor’s Princeton historian friend, drugging her wine and stealing her laptop overnight. The behavioral direction that walked Pearson toward operationally prepared locations mapped onto the car formations that made Naylor feel she was ‘always in the middle of a formation.’

But there was something else in 1996 that Pearson had not expected. Something that made him, as he described it, begin asking a new set of questions alongside all the questions he was already asking.

In Naylor’s account, the operation against her was not run solely by the NSA. There was a third institutional actor. Its involvement was not incidental or peripheral. It provided manpower, vehicles, personnel, street-level operatives, and — through a cousin of the NSA’s South Carolina field officer — the intelligence check that helped escalate the surveillance. The third actor was the Anti-Defamation League (ADL).

In October 2025, four months before Pearson read the book, the FBI Director of the United States described his agency’s partnership with the Anti-Defamation League as ‘activism dressed up as counterterrorism’ that ‘put Americans in danger’ — and used language that no previous FBI director had ever used about this organization: he called it ‘an extreme group functioning like a terrorist organization.’

The convergence of these two things — Pearson’s reading of 1996 and Patel’s October 2025 statement — produced the questions this essay examines. They are not comfortable questions. They are not simple questions. They are the questions the public deserves to have answered.

II. What Richard Pearson Documented

Dr. Richard Pearson is an African American scholar whose doctoral dissertation examined critical race frameworks and structural racism. The targeting campaign against him — documented across fourteen months of timestamped photographs, audio recordings, preserved physical evidence, and hundreds of thousands of words of diary and analysis — began in spring 2025 and continues through early 2026.

The documented violations across his primary account number seventy, spanning the Fourth, First, Fifth, Sixth, Eighth, Thirteenth, and Fourteenth Amendments; Title III and the Wiretap Act; the Privacy Act; the Convention Against Torture; the ICCPR; and the DC Anti-Stalking Statute. A September 15, 2025 mental health evaluation by DC’s Department of Behavioral Health, conducted by three doctors, two mental health specialists, and a social worker, confirmed his rationality, coherence, and the absence of any psychotic symptoms. He was preemptively clearing the psychiatric trap — the same trap Naylor described as structurally engineered into the operation she experienced.

The specific elements Pearson documented include: voice-to-skull transmission on July 13, 2025 (‘We have to make you disappear’); through-floor acoustic harassment following him room to room across months; covert home entry with deliberate object placement (the panini grill power button, removed and placed in his laundry pile); systematic bicycle destruction at Federal Center Metro; chemical assault at the Rockville Metro station with a substance that bonded to his skin; a framing operation near the U.S. Capitol designed to associate him with neo-Nazi materials; false arrest in which he — the assault victim with a documented black eye — was arrested while the aggressor was released; an ACCESS DENIED flag in the MPD database that altered officer behavior; employment sabotage through watchlist propagation; social isolation, and financial attrition through phone theft and CashApp fraud.

Within this documented campaign, Pearson noted specific individuals and encounters he could not fully explain. Among them: ‘David Meyerowitz’ — a man who appeared unsolicited at Pearson’s location in Old Town Alexandria, name-dropping Trump and Kushner in what Pearson interpreted as a psychological assessment encounter; the ‘Bug Zapper’ woman — who appeared at the Old Town Trader Joe’s and then the following day in Clarendon, Virginia, tracking Pearson across jurisdictions; and a man in Rockville with a voice box. Pearson was an extrovert who had nearly 20 years interfacing with the public. He sensed that all three were Jewish. The name, Meyerowitz, was the clearest sign.

Pearson documented in his Medium essay titled ‘My Hand Began to Shake’ how he first traced the parallels to Naylor’s 1996.

Pearson is not antisemitic. His scholarly work addresses structural racism and its institutional mechanisms. His personal account contains no expressions of anti-Jewish sentiment. He did not go looking for a Jewish connection to what was happening to him. He went looking for patterns — the same methodology he applied to the red-and-black calling card deployments, the same control-study logic he applied to the street theater formations. When he read Naylor’s account and found the ADL as a named institutional actor in an operation against a Black intellectual, he began asking questions he had not previously thought to ask. He has heard of ADL and only considered the good of its work on antisemitism. He knew nothing else — not before reading Naylor’s 1996 or discovering the FBI director’s statement. Both discoveries in March 2026.

III. What Gloria Naylor Documented — The ADL in 1996

Having now read 1996 in full, it is possible to describe with precision what Naylor documented about the ADL’s role in the operation against her. This is not an interpretation or a characterization. It is what the book states, in the narrative framework she constructed from her documented experience. Naylor asserted that while labeled fiction, the book covers core factual claims regarding her harassment, loss of privacy, and government use of high-tech weaponry to influence thoughts, framing it as a “battle for my mind”.

The Architecture of the Operation as Naylor Described It

The surveillance against Naylor began with a neighbor dispute. She likely changed individual names for legal reasons. Eunice Simon, a tall, pale woman who lived across the road from Naylor’s St. Helena Island property, had a conflict with Naylor over her cats using Naylor’s garden as a litter box. Eunice’s brother was Dick Simon, Assistant Deputy Director of the NSA. When Eunice called to complain that Naylor had called her an antisemitic name in the Winn Dixie supermarket — a characterization Naylor disputed, saying she had called Eunice a ‘bitch’ but not a ‘Jew bitch’ — Dick Simon used this as a pretext to run Naylor’s name through the NSA’s internal systems.

What he found was that Naylor — a Black woman, a writer with dreadlocks — was already on the NSA Watch List. Every Black, Latino, and Asian writer who had received press coverage was on this list, which Naylor writes ‘was allegedly disbanded back in the ’70s, so now it is a list that doesn’t exist inside of the NSA.’ He changed her status from inactive to ‘Activate’ with duration ‘To be determined.’

This is where the ADL enters the documented narrative. The NSA’s South Carolina field operative, Barry Levine, was running a Code 2 surveillance on Naylor with insufficient manpower. He hit on an idea: he had a cousin who worked out of the ADL’s Columbia, South Carolina office. In 1996, Naylor writes the conversation as follows:

“He decides to call his little brother.” “Yuri, it’s Barry. Long time no hear from.” “Not a red cent. You still owe me fifty dollars.” “This call isn’t about money.” “Yuri, I know this is a big favor…” “Not as big as my fifty dollars.” “Yuri, my job depends on this.” “You’re not kidding, are you?” “I’ve never been more serious in my life.” “Okay, hold on… She’s clean. Nada.”
After Levine tells Yuri she is a ‘threat’ and a supporter of Louis Farrakhan: “Do you know Rabbi Geller?” “He won’t be any help.” “In a case like this, he might. I’ll give him a call. See what we can do for you.”

Rabbi Geller — the leader of the St. Helena Reformed Synagogue, described as someone who had spent ten years working to build Black-Jewish relations in the community — was then called by Yuri Levine. He was told that Naylor was a danger who hated Jews and was there to recruit for the Nation of Islam. Naylor writes of the Rabbi’s response: “He doesn’t let Yuri know, but he has seen the article about this woman. Several members of his congregation had written to the editor asking them to print no more stories about her… The only thing they are asking him to do is provide a little manpower from his congregation to help them watch her. Nobody wants trouble; they just want to nip things in the bud — a simple surveillance that would be coordinated from their office in Columbia. Rabbi Geller’s tape of Mozart’s Opus in a Minor has played out. His leg cramps are worsening. Still, he says, ‘yes.’”

What followed, in Naylor’s account, was a multi-month surveillance operation involving ADL personnel driving cars in containment formations past her home — three or four per hour on a dead-end road where she normally saw one car every two or three hours. ADL operatives followed her into the post office, the hardware store, and the supermarket. An elderly Holocaust survivor — described with specificity as a 72-year-old Dachau survivor who joined the ADL after immigrating to America — participated in the car pool because she believed there was ‘a threat,’ not knowing who was actually targeted or why.

Naylor describes her own confrontation with the pattern. She writes: “Of the many cars that kept coming and going on my road, most were driven by Jews. At first I thought that this particular pattern was all in my mind… But as the license plate numbers grew and the faces in the cars grew, there was no denying this fact.” She then writes of the agonizing decision about whether to tell her lawyer in Charleston what she observed: “Part of my hesitation was fear — to be branded an anti-Semite meant a slow death to my career in New York publishing circles. Jews may fight among each other, but when they felt their interests were threatened, most of them came together in a network of silent consensus that the problem must be rooted out… I had been fighting hard not to be anti-Semitic, so why would I think that I hate Jews?”

According to Naylor’s account, the ADL’s involvement was not peripheral. It provided manpower for a 24/7 surveillance. It coordinated through the ADL’s Columbia, South Carolina office. It extended to New York when Naylor returned there. And Naylor describes a specific meeting in Eunice Simon’s living room where NSA and ADL personnel argued with each other over operational control, while Eunice ran between them offering coffee and doughnuts, surrounded by a thousand cats. David Shane — the former Mossad officer brought in as field coordinator — describes the combined operation to the assembled room: “We have a team of operatives from the ADL and NSA, and each is arguing for their piece of the action.”

She described what she belief were phrases that were put into her head

“I remember I was watching Mel Gibson’s “Braveheart” for the fifthteenth time when the first thought came to me: I am a bitch. It seemed to have just floated up from the bottom of my mind. It had nothing to do with what I was thinking because at that moment I wasn’t thinking anything at all. I had been watching a particularly gory battle scene. J am the worst bitch in the world. I want to kill myself. Where was this stuff coming from? I frowned and rubbed my forehead.”

What Naylor Was Not

Naylor was not antisemitic. Her novel Bailey’s Cafe, which NSA analysts reviewed looking for red flags, featured sympathetic portraits of Ethiopian Jews and Russian Jews and examined Israel’s initial refusal to admit Ethiopian Jews. This the analyst described as a ‘sympathetic portrait.’ Her crime, in the narrative of 1996, was that she sympathized with the Million Man March — which the NSA interpreted as evidence of Nation of Islam sympathy. She had published no anti-Jewish writing. The ADL’s own files, checked by Yuri Levine when Barry Levine called, confirmed this: ‘Clean as a whistle. Nada.’

The operation against her was initiated by a petty neighbor dispute that was escalated through institutional machinery because Naylor was a Black woman writer with dreadlocks in a predominantly white part of South Carolina who gave a speech that mentioned the Million Man March. The ADL was recruited not on evidence of antisemitism but on a mischaracterization, delivered by a low-level NSA operative whose job depended on producing results. The Holocaust survivor driving her car past Naylor’s property on a dead-end road believed she was preventing the next threat to the Jewish community.

IV. The FBI Director’s Statement — October 1, 2025

On October 1, 2025, FBI Director Kash Patel announced that the FBI was cutting all formal ties with the Anti-Defamation League. The immediate trigger was the ADL’s Glossary of Extremism and Hate, which had listed Turning Point USA — the organization founded by Charlie Kirk, who had been assassinated at a Utah college campus speaking engagement the previous month — as a right-wing extremist group. Elon Musk, Donald Trump Jr., and other prominent conservatives had attacked the ADL for the designation. Facing this pressure, the ADL deleted the Glossary.

Patel’s language was extraordinary for the head of the FBI. It is worth examining each element:

“James Comey disgraced the FBI by writing ‘love letters’ to the ADL”

The ‘love letters’ characterization is documented. At the ADL’s 2014 National Leadership Summit, Comey described his speech as ‘a love letter to the ADL.’ In 2017, addressing the same body, he said: ‘Three years later I can say, from the perspective of the FBI, we’re still in love with you.’ The word ‘love’ was Comey’s. Patel took it. The relationship it described, however — the FBI Director addressing the ADL’s national leadership and describing the FBI’s relationship in these terms — was itself the thing being characterized.

“…embedding agents with an extreme group functioning like a terrorist organization”

The embedding of FBI agents with ADL operations is documented by Christopher Wray’s 2022 speech. In that address to the ADL’s ‘Never Is Now’ conference, Wray thanked the ADL for ‘conducting the training that all of our new special agents and intelligence analysts participate in at the U.S. Holocaust Memorial Museum.’ He described the FBI’s counterterrorism division as working with ADL-assisted fusion cell operations. Patel’s characterization of the ADL as ‘functioning like a terrorist organization’ is not supported by any legal designation and was widely condemned by Jewish organizations and civil liberties groups. J Street called it ‘dangerous and reckless.’

“Terrorist organization” is a very strong characterization.

“…the disgraceful operation they ran spying on Americans”

This is the element that connects most directly to the documented public record — and to Naylor’s account. ‘Spying on Americans’ is not a characterization Patel invented. It is, with appropriate context and complexity, a description of documented institutional history.

In 1993, the San Francisco District Attorney released 700 pages of documents implicating the ADL in a comprehensive domestic spying operation. The ADL’s operative, Roy Bullock, had worked off the books for the organization for more than 25 years. His files contained dossiers on 12,000 individuals and 950 organizations. The organizations included: the NAACP, the American Civil Liberties Union, the Arab-American Anti-Discrimination Committee, the American Indian Movement, ACT UP, Greenpeace, the United Farm Workers, the Asian Law Caucus, the National Conference of Black Lawyers, the National Lawyers Guild, and dozens of anti-apartheid activist groups. The FBI investigated the ADL for espionage. Bullock admitted he had been sharing information with South African apartheid intelligence agencies. Court documents established that the ADL had a network of sympathetic police officers sharing confidential data, that the ADL had violated California tax law in paying Bullock through a lawyer, and that ADL spying operations had been reported to the Israeli government.

A federal class-action lawsuit — ADC v. ADL — was settled with a significant cash payment from the ADL. The American-Arab Anti-Discrimination Committee stated at settlement that the resolution ‘vindicates our view that ADL has engaged in illegal activities to undermine the work of such groups and damage the cause and reputation of the Arab-American community.’

Bullock confessed to trying to find ‘any sexual impropriety’ on the late anti-apartheid activist Desmond Tutu. He had infiltrated pro-Palestinian organizations while pretending to be sympathetic to the Palestinian cause, including at a public meeting that he attended specifically to plant ADC materials on tables at a Holocaust-denial conference — a classic COINTELPRO-style false flag operation to discredit the organization by association.

“That was not law enforcement, it was activism dressed up as counterterrorism, and it put Americans in danger.”

This is the element of Patel’s statement that is most structurally accurate — and most complicated by its context. The described practice — a private organization defining who constitutes an extremist threat, embedding operatives in government-funded surveillance operations, and using that designation to direct law enforcement resources — is precisely what the documented ADL surveillance history shows happened. Whether the Patel statement is a principled critique of that practice or a politically convenient attack on a civil society organization that inconveniently labeled TPUSA is not a question with a simple answer. Both can be true simultaneously.

The Nexus Project, a Jewish civil society organization, warned that Patel’s framing ‘establishes a precedent that any civil society group monitoring extremism can be labeled a threat to national security by those in power’ — a concern that is legitimate and serious. The Trump administration was simultaneously attacking the SPLC, eliminating hate crime prevention programs, and defunding civil rights monitoring infrastructure. In that political context, the attack on the ADL cannot be assessed in isolation from its function in that larger project.

“That era is OVER. This FBI won’t partner with political fronts masquerading as watchdogs.”

The claim that a private organization had been functioning as a ‘political front’ that directed FBI targeting, without adequate oversight, accountability, or transparency about the operational relationship — this claim deserves examination independent of who is making it and why. If the FBI was taking its threat designations from a private organization whose own surveillance operations had resulted in a federal lawsuit and cash settlement for illegal spying on American civil rights groups, the question of whose interests that arrangement served is a legitimate public question. It is not answered by the political valence of who is asking it in 2025.

V. The Documented History — What We Know About the ADL and Surveillance of Black Americans

The ADL was founded in 1913. Its stated mission — to stop the defamation of the Jewish people and to secure justice and fair treatment for all — has produced genuine accomplishments in the fight against antisemitism and in support of the Civil Rights Movement. Benjamin Epstein, ADL national director, was friendly with Thurgood Marshall and Martin Luther King. The ADL opposed the Ku Klux Klan and supported the Civil Rights Act of 1964. This record is real and significant.

It coexists with a documented surveillance history that is equally real and significant — and that has received far less public attention than the civil rights record. The following is based on court documents, FOIA releases, settlement records, and published journalism:

DOCUMENTED FACTS ABOUT ADL SURVEILLANCE OPERATIONS:

  1. Roy Bullock worked as an ADL operative for more than 25 years, building files on 12,000 individuals and 950 organizations, confirmed by court documents (1993).
  2. The ADL settled a federal class-action lawsuit (ADC v. ADL) with a cash payment. The settlement acknowledged illegal surveillance of civil rights organizations.
  3. Surveillance targets included: NAACP, ACLU, AIM, United Farm Workers, National Conference of Black Lawyers, National Lawyers Guild, ACT UP, Greenpeace.
  4. Bullock shared information with South African apartheid intelligence services. He confessed to seeking sexual dirt on Desmond Tutu.
  5. The ADL maintained a network of sympathetic police officers sharing confidential data with ADL operatives — a documented violation of California law.
  6. ADL surveillance operations were reported to the Israeli government and its intelligence agencies, per court documents.
  7. A 2020 internal ADL memo obtained by The Guardian showed the ADL ‘regularly tracks, profiles and sends threat assessments of individuals’ it views as a problem — including a Black Indianapolis activist working with the Deadly Exchange campaign.
  8. The ADL trained staff to edit Wikipedia pages about itself, suspended only after criticism from Wikipedia editors about conflict of interest.
  9. The ADL designed curriculum used to train FBI new agents and intelligence analysts. FBI Director Wray confirmed this publicly in his 2022 ADL address.
  10. FBI Director Patel confirmed that FBI agents were embedded with ADL operations under Director Comey’s tenure.

The picture that emerges from this documented record is of an organization with a dual identity. On one dimension: a civil rights organization that has done genuine work combating antisemitism, hate crimes, and extremism. On the other dimension: an institution that has engaged in domestic surveillance operations extending well beyond its stated mission, targeting civil rights and Black organizations, sharing intelligence with foreign governments, and operating in partnership with law enforcement in ways that involved illegal acquisition of confidential police records.

These two dimensions are not mutually exclusive. An organization can do genuine civil rights work and simultaneously maintain surveillance operations that undermine civil rights. The ADL’s surveillance of the NAACP and the National Conference of Black Lawyers while simultaneously opposing the Ku Klux Klan is not a logical contradiction. It is the expression of a specific institutional perspective on which threats to Jewish safety matter and which political alignments serve that safety — a perspective that, in the documented cases, led the organization to treat Black civil rights organizations, anti-apartheid activists, and Palestinian solidarity groups as surveillance subjects rather than allies.

VII. The Institutional Context — What the Patel Statement Actually Reveals

The October 2025 Patel statement must be understood in its full political context before being evaluated for what it reveals about the underlying institutional question.

Kash Patel made the statement immediately after the ADL listed Charlie Kirk’s Turning Point USA — whose founder had just been assassinated — as a right-wing extremist group. The statement came from a director appointed by an administration that had simultaneously fired twelve inspectors general, designated DEI scholarship as hostile ideology, and was engaged in a comprehensive dismantling of federal civil rights infrastructure.

All of this is true. And the following is also true: the specific factual claims embedded in the Patel statement — that the ADL ran surveillance operations against Americans, that these operations were embedded with FBI personnel, that a private organization was directing law enforcement targeting — are documented.

The ADL settled a federal lawsuit acknowledging illegal surveillance. FBI Director Wray confirmed FBI-ADL training collaboration in 2022. Comey’s 2017 statement described FBI agents as partnered with ADL operations. The question of whether a private organization should be shaping FBI targeting criteria, without democratic accountability, is a legitimate question regardless of the political motives of the person raising it in 2025.

The challenge for the public — and for any fair examination of the Pearson and Naylor cases in light of this institutional history — is to hold two things simultaneously: the genuine and serious civil rights work the ADL has done in combating antisemitism; and the documented surveillance history that shows the organization has also surveilled, infiltrated, and produced intelligence on Black civil rights organizations, anti-apartheid activists, and a National Book Award-winning Black novelist.

These two things are not the same thing. An organization that fights antisemitism and an organization that surveils the NAACP are not necessarily different organizations. They can be — and in the documented case of the ADL, they have been — the same organization with a complex institutional identity whose operations have served some civil rights interests while undermining others, depending on the specific politics of the moment and the specific interests being protected.

VIII. The Questions the Public Deserves Answered

Gloria Naylor published 1996 in 2005. No congressional committee investigated the account. No FBI statement was issued. No mainstream journalist examined whether the ADL’s documented history of domestic surveillance extended to the specific operation Naylor described. The ADL said nothing. No mainstream publisher touched the book. She died in 2016.

Kash Patel announced in October 2025 that the FBI had ended its partnership with the ADL, using language — ‘disgraceful operations spying on Americans’ — that precisely describes what the 1993 San Francisco documents established. His statement generated enormous controversy about the politics of the attack. The underlying question — what, specifically, was the ADL doing with FBI partnership and embedded FBI agents — did not receive serious public examination.

What happened between 1993 and 2025 — ADL’s relationship between the FBI and federal agencies, the nature of illegal surveillance, the rise of classified and invisible weapons, the use of totalitarianism methods, the development of plausibly denial tactics, and the size and strength of the network to carry out illegal action against Americans.

Richard Pearson is documenting, in real time, a campaign that contains elements he cannot explain through the operational frameworks he has identified. He read Naylor’s account. He noted the parallels. He noted the institutional name that appears in Naylor’s account. He is asking questions.

The questions the public deserves answered are:

First: What was the specific scope and structure of the FBI-ADL operational partnership during the Comey era? When Comey said ‘we’re still in love with you’ in 2017, and when Wray confirmed ADL training of new FBI agents in 2022, what did these partnerships involve beyond the stated programs? Were ADL personnel embedded in counterterrorism operations that designated targets? If so, what were the criteria for designation?

Second: Did the ADL’s domestic surveillance operations — documented in the 1993 San Francisco case to have included files on 12,000 individuals and 950 organizations — continue after the lawsuit settlement? The 2020 Guardian memo showing ongoing ‘threat assessment’ reports on individual Black activists suggests continued monitoring. What is the current scope of ADL domestic intelligence operations, and what legal framework governs them?

Third: The ADL’s surveillance history shows particular concentration on Black civil rights organizations, anti-apartheid activists, and individuals whose political views were deemed unfavorable to Israel. In the post-9/11 era, when the RMVE/BIE framework began treating Black civil rights scholarship as a domestic terrorism predicate, what relationship if any existed between FBI counterterrorism targeting criteria and ADL intelligence assessments? Pearson’s dissertation is exactly the kind of work the BIE designation was designed to surveil. Was ADL input part of any targeting decision in cases like his?

Fourth: Gloria Naylor described, in a documented and published account with a former NSA employee’s lawsuit in its appendix, specific operational collaboration between NSA personnel and ADL operatives in a sustained campaign against her. The ADL has never responded to this account. The NSA has never responded. No court has examined the specific claims. Given the ADL’s documented history of domestic surveillance and its documented relationship with law enforcement, what specific investigation — if any — was ever conducted into the Naylor account?

Fifth: Richard Pearson is living this now, in Washington, D.C., in the most surveilled city on earth, with the DoW confirming directed energy weapons are operational and Kash Patel confirming the FBI had embedded agents with the ADL’s operations. What mechanisms exist — if any — for a targeted individual to determine whether private organizations played a role in their targeting, and what remedies exist if they did?

Conclusion: What the Record Requires

This essay has presented a convergence of documented facts that deserve, and have not received, serious institutional attention.

A National Book Award-winning novelist documented, in a published book with primary source appendices, a specific operational collaboration between the NSA and the Anti-Defamation League in a surveillance campaign against her that lasted more than a year, used advanced directed energy technology, involved the recruitment of her intimate social circle, and destroyed her capacity to write. She died having written nothing significant after that book. No institution investigated her account.

A PhD is documenting, in real time, a comprehensive campaign against him that the DoW has confirmed is using operational directed energy weapons, that the geography of Washington, D.C. makes logistically impossible without federal knowledge, and that contains elements he cannot explain through any single institutional framework — including elements that, when he read Naylor’s account, raised the question of whether a private organization with documented surveillance capabilities and a documented history of targeting Black civil rights activists played any role in what is happening to him.

The FBI Director of the United States described the agency’s multi-year partnership with that organization as ‘activism dressed up as counterterrorism’ that ‘put Americans in danger’ — using language that, whatever its political context in 2025, describes what the documented public record shows the organization has done.

None of these facts require antisemitism to examine. Antisemitism is the hatred of Jewish people as Jewish people. Examining the institutional practices of a specific organization — its documented surveillance operations, its documented collaboration with law enforcement, its documented targeting of Black civil rights groups — is not antisemitism. Conflating the two has served, as the 1993 lawsuit established and Naylor’s account illustrates, as a mechanism by which surveillance of Black intellectuals has been protected from scrutiny.

The Hillel quotation with which this essay opened — If I am not for myself, who will be for me? But if I am only for myself, what am I? And if not now, when? — was quoted by Christopher Wray at the ADL’s Never Is Now conference in 2022. It is a teaching about self-interest and communal responsibility that is universally applicable. Applied to the questions this essay raises: the FBI’s partnership with an organization whose surveillance operations targeted the NAACP, the National Conference of Black Lawyers, and a National Book Award-winning Black novelist is not a model of communal responsibility. It is an example of institutional self-interest dressed as protection — protection that in practice was extended to some communities while surveillance was conducted against others.

Richard Pearson is still writing. He is writing from a mattress and a suitcase, in the city where all of it is happening, with every institution that might have helped him having declined or failed. Gloria Naylor wrote from the Brooklyn Public Library with shaking hands. Neither of them is antisemitic. Both of them found, in their documented accounts of what was done to them, a pattern they could not fully explain.

The pattern deserves a full investigation. Not a political one — not the kind of investigation that serves one administration’s interests in dismantling civil society accountability organizations while ignoring everything else. A genuine one. The kind the Church Committee conducted. The kind that produced the FISA Act. The kind that, based on everything documented in the public record, is decades overdue.

If not now, when?

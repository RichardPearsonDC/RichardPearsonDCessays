US Supreme Court Justice, Covertly Targeted by US Government, Would Unlikely Get Legal Standing to Sue

The US legal system makes no sense from a justice and civil liberties perspective. It is completely against everything that a constitutional democracy is supposed to stand for…and against.

Establishing standing is difficult because the Supreme Court has set an “insurmountably high bar” for plaintiffs in surveillance cases, requiring them to prove an injury that is concrete, particularized, and certainly impending. Even for US Supreme Court justice that is covertly targeted by the US government. In a scenario involving covert targeting, a justice — like any other citizen — would struggle to meet these requirements due to the inherent secrecy of the government’s actions.

The following legal doctrines create significant obstacles to establishing standing in this context:

The “Certainly Impending” Standard

Under Clapper v. Amnesty International USA, a plaintiff cannot establish standing based on a “speculative chain of possibilities”.

  • Lack of Knowledge: Because the targeting is covert, the justice would likely have “no actual knowledge” that their communications were intercepted.
  • Incurred Costs: Taking expensive measures to avoid potential surveillance (such as using encrypted devices or meeting in person) is considered a “self-inflicted injury” that does not confer standing if the underlying threat is not deemed “certainly impending”.

Requirement of “Concrete” Harm

Recent rulings in cases like TransUnion LLC v. Ramirez and Spokeo, Inc. v. Robins have further narrowed standing.

  • Tangible vs. Intangible: Even if a legal right (like privacy) is violated, the court now requires a “concrete harm” that has a close relationship to harms traditionally recognized in American courts.
  • Dissemination Requirement: In some privacy contexts, the harm is only considered concrete if the sensitive information is actually shared with a third party.

The State Secrets Privilege

Even if a justice could provide preliminary evidence of targeting, the executive branch could invoke the state secrets privilege.

  • Blocking Evidence: This allows the government to resist disclosing information if there is a “reasonable danger” it would harm national security.
  • Outright Dismissal: If the very subject matter of the case is a state secret, courts often dismiss the entire lawsuit at the outset, preventing the justice from ever reaching the merits of their claim.

While the likelihood of a current Supreme Court justice being covertly targeted is low due to extreme institutional friction, history shows that Justice-Specific Information Seeking regarding surveillance is not unprecedented. Between 1945 and 1975, the FBI monitored at least 12 justices, including Chief Justice Earl Warren and Justice William O. Douglas. [1, 2]

The likelihood of this occurring or succeeding today depends on three factors:

1. Historical Precedent

In the mid-20th century, government agencies frequently overheard justices’ conversations. For example, Chief Justice Warren was overheard six times between 1961 and 1965. During the Nixon administration, wiretaps produced personal information on at least one justice that was unrelated to national security. [1, 2]

2. Current Institutional Safeguards

Today, Supreme Court justices are protected by the U.S. Marshals Service rather than the Secret Service, which provides a layer of separation from direct presidential control. Additionally: [3]

  • Secrecy Protocols: Chief Justice John Roberts recently implemented strict non-disclosure agreements (NDAs) for court staff to prevent internal leaks.
  • Security Details: While protection is typically “opt-in” for travel outside Washington, D.C., there have been active, documented threats against justices that led to increased security reviews. [4, 5]

3. Legal and Political Friction

Any modern covert targeting would face immense risk of exposure. High-profile figures like James Comey and Letitia James have reportedly been subjects of retribution-based investigations, sparking intense media and congressional scrutiny. Given the court’s role in reviewing surveillance law, targeting a justice would be a high-stakes gamble that could unify the judiciary against the executive branch. [6, 7]

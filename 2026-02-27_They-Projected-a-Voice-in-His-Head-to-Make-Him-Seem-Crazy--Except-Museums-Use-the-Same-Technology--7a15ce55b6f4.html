They Projected a Voice in His Head to Torture. He Discovered Museums Use the Same Technology.

Torture victim in Washington, DC conducts research after he heard a voice in his head. He found that the technology to direct ultrasound and cause a voice in the head shares same technology used in museums.

The technology to project a voice has been commercially available for decades — a single, directional voice, audible only to the person standing in a precise spot, complete with ambient room noise from the source location. It is used in museums. It is used in hospitals. It is used in advertising. The United States military has been deploying it from warships since 2004.

The company that makes the consumer version calls it the Audio Spotlight. Another version is called HyperSonic Sound. The military version is called the Long Range Acoustic Device, or LRAD. These are not classified technologies. They are products. You can read about them on a science education website. You can find them in patent databases. You can watch the inventor demonstrate the technology in a TED talk from 2009.

The Mona Lisa may one day speak to you as you walk past her in the Louvre — and only to you — because of this technology.

Richard Pearson, PhD, heard a voice on the National Mall on July 13, 2025.

What He Heard: The Testimony

Let the record speak first. This is Pearson’s account of what happened near the Botanic Garden on the National Mall on the afternoon of July 13, 2025:

“I get up and I’m hearing something in my head, ‘Go to Georgetown.’ But somehow, and this has never happened to me ever in my life, there is like something coming through that is telling me to go to Georgetown. Go to Georgetown.”

He describes the onset: precise, unprecedented, located at a specific position near the Botanic Garden after passing the reflecting pool. Not a gradual creeping disturbance — a discrete acoustic event that started at a specific moment in a specific location.

Then this:

“The voice sounded like a white woman. And I hear others wherever she was at like she was at a microphone and others were around. There’s a whole bunch of other noise. It almost sounds like there are other people in the room.”

Read that again slowly.

He hears a woman’s voice. A microphone quality to it. Background ambient noise. The acoustic signature of a room — other people, other sounds, the texture of a space with walls and air and activity. He is standing on the National Mall in the open air, and he is perceiving what sounds like the interior of an operations room at a remote location.

Later, on the Metro: “Get down. Get down.” And before he reaches Georgetown: “We have to make you disappear.” And at the end of the day, after a series of events that appear staged and choreographed: “Oh, that was a test.”

He experienced this voice on precisely one day. When D.C. Department of Behavioral Health evaluator Shante asked him in the September 15 mental health evaluation — conducted on record by named professionals, transcribed — “How often are you hearing this woman?”, Pearson answered: “Only that day.”

That single exchange is clinically decisive — but not in the direction most people assume.

What Hallucinations Actually Sound Like

Auditory hallucinations in paranoid schizophrenia and related psychotic disorders have a well-documented phenomenological profile. They are recurrent and persistent. They do not include ambient background noise from a remote location. They do not carry the acoustic signature of a room the patient has never visited. They do not arrive with microphone quality and the sound of other people present in a distant space. And they are not confined to a single afternoon in a person’s entire documented history.

DSM-5 criteria for schizophrenia spectrum disorders require that hallucinations occur with regularity and cause significant functional impairment. A single episode — one voice, one day, no recurrence across eight months of subsequent documentation — is not the clinical presentation of a disorder generating auditory hallucinations. The evaluators at DBH found no psychiatric symptoms requiring intervention. The clinical record is unambiguous.

But here is what the psychiatric framework alone cannot explain: the specificity of what he heard. The woman at a microphone. The ambient room noise. The sense of others present. These are acoustic details — and they match, with precision, the operational signature of a parametric directional loudspeaker.

How the Technology Works: The Museum Explanation

A conventional loudspeaker moves a cone back and forth to push air in waves that spread outward in all directions. A directional parametric loudspeaker works entirely differently.

Instead of producing audible sound directly, it emits two beams of ultrasound — frequencies far above the range of human hearing, around 200,000 Hz — using an array of piezoelectric transducers, crystals that vibrate tens of thousands of times per second when an electrical current passes through them. These ultrasonic beams travel in a tightly focused column, like a flashlight beam. They do not spread and dissipate the way conventional sound does. They can travel twenty times farther than ordinary sound while remaining coherent.

When those ultrasonic beams strike an object — a wall, a surface, a person’s head — they demodulate. The two frequencies interact, and by the physics of wave superposition, they produce a third wave at a frequency equal to the difference between them. That difference frequency falls within the range of normal human hearing. The collision of the ultrasonic waves produces audible sound — but only at the point of collision. Only where the beam strikes.

If the beam strikes your head, you hear the sound. The person standing two feet to your left hears nothing. The person behind you hears nothing.

And the sound appears to come from inside your head — because it does. The pressure wave is produced by the demodulation event occurring in your skull tissue, not by a distant vibrating cone. Your auditory system perceives the sound as originating from within.

This is not speculation. It is documented physics, confirmed in peer-reviewed literature and implemented in commercially available products. The Audio Spotlight by Holosonics was developed by Dr. Joseph Pompei at MIT. HyperSonic Sound was developed by Woody Norris and is marketed for museum installations, retail environments, hospitals, and advertising.

The science education website Explain That Stuff describes the museum application this way: a concealed directional loudspeaker next to a painting fires ultrasound that travels from the picture into the gallery. If — and only if — someone walks into the beam, the ultrasound demodulates into normal sound, and the painting’s voice is heard. Heard by that visitor alone. No one else in the gallery perceives anything.

This is the technology. It works. It is in museums.

The Acoustic Forensics: Why “Others Were Around” Is the Key

Here is the detail that separates Pearson’s account from hallucination and connects it directly to live directional audio transmission: he heard the source environment.

“And I hear others wherever she was at like she was at a microphone and others were around. There’s a whole bunch of other noise. It almost sounds like there are other people in the room.”

When a patient experiences schizophrenic auditory hallucinations, they hear voices. They do not hear the ambient acoustic environment from which those voices are being transmitted. They do not perceive the background noise of a remote operations room, the shuffling of people present, the reverberant texture of a walled space. Internal neurological hallucinations cannot contain room ambience from a location the patient has never visited, because they are generated by the patient’s own auditory cortex, which has no access to that information.

Pearson heard background noise. Specifically, he heard the acoustic environment of what sounded like a room with other people in it — a woman speaking into a microphone, others present around her, the sonic texture of an enclosed operational space. This is exactly what you would hear if a parametric directional audio signal was being transmitted from a live audio feed at a control location. The room noise of the operators’ environment would transmit with it. The ambient sound of the source would arrive inside his head alongside the voice itself.

The acoustic texture he describes — microphone quality, room ambience, sense of others present at a remote location — is not the product of a diseased auditory cortex. It is the product of a live audio feed transmitted through directed parametric technology to a specific person walking along the National Mall.

He described what he heard accurately. He simply had no framework, in that moment, to explain what it was.

The Technology Has a Military Lineage

The commercial applications of parametric directional audio are well-documented. The technology’s origins are military, and its military versions are not museum installations.

The United States military has been using the Long Range Acoustic Device (LRAD) since 2004 — mounted on warships to project focused audio warnings to vessels at ranges exceeding 500 meters. The most powerful LRAD models project sound at distances up to 3 kilometers. Police departments in New York, Pittsburgh, and other American cities have deployed LRAD for crowd control, prompting legal challenges over their use against civilians. Following a 2014 protest in New York, lawyers argued that the devices were being used to “modify behavior and force compliance by hurting people.” A 2021 settlement limited NYPD use.

The January 23, 2026 public statement from the Department of War’s Chief Technology Officer — “Yes, the @DeptofWar has directed energy weapons. Yes, we are scaling them” — was not the announcement of a future capability. It was a confirmation of existing deployment. The February 19, 2026 Washington Post analysis of directed energy weapons makes clear that the systems in question are portable, varied in design, and operational. The Venezuelan security guards who experienced their heads feeling like they were “exploding from inside” were at the high-intensity end of the same technological spectrum whose low-intensity end makes museum paintings speak to individual visitors.

The question is not whether the technology can do what Pearson describes. It demonstrably can. The question is whether it was used on him.

The Commands: Operational Logic, Not Psychotic Content

The content of what Pearson heard also requires analysis. Auditory hallucinations in psychotic disorders follow predictable patterns across cultures and clinical populations. They tend to comment on the patient’s behavior, issue vague or self-referential commands, or produce threatening content that is non-directional and unfalsifiable.

What Pearson heard on July 13 was operationally specific and externally verifiable:

“Go to Georgetown.” A directional command to a specific named geographic location. He went. Metro receipts and a dinner receipt at Bozzelli’s confirm the movement.

“Get down. Get down.” A safety-framed compliance command on the Metro. He complied. Metro security footage would document this.

“Get in the car with her when her car comes.” A behaviorally specific instruction tied to an observable external event — a woman waiting at a pickup location — that the voice correctly anticipated. The car came. He did not get in.

“Oh, that was a test.” An explicit post-hoc operational characterization. The voice identified the day’s events as a test.

This is not the content architecture of psychotic hallucination. It is the content architecture of a behavioral compliance assessment — an operation designed to determine whether a target can be moved through directed audio to a specific location, instructed to perform specific actions, and guided toward a specific vehicle. The Georgetown day, read in its entirety, is a baseline assessment of his responsiveness to audio command.

The voice told him it was a test. Pearson, to his credit, believed it immediately — not as validation but as evidence. “In some ways, I felt embarrassed that I could be that vulnerable to such a horrific violation.” He understood, immediately and correctly, that his compliance with audio instructions he could not explain was a violation of his sovereignty. His first-choice label — “Sovereignty Violation and First Extraction Attempt” — is not the reaction of a man who thinks he is receiving divine communication. It is the reaction of a researcher who has grasped that he was manipulated and is documenting it precisely.

What the Diagnosis of “Crazy” Accomplishes

Here is the operational logic that makes directed audio a particularly effective tool for state targeting: if you project a voice into someone’s head on the National Mall and they report it, they will be assessed for psychiatric disorder. “I heard a voice that seemed to come from inside my head” is the textbook description of an auditory hallucination. The receiving clinician reaches for the DSM. The patient receives a diagnosis. Everything else they say is thereafter discredited.

This is the weapon’s second function. The first function is behavioral compliance. The second function is producing a symptom that, if reported, generates institutional disbelief.

Against Pearson, the second function partially failed. Two DBH mental health professionals evaluated him on record, asked the precise clinical questions, received answers that eliminated the psychotic diagnosis — “Only that day” — and found no disorder requiring intervention. The evaluators did their jobs correctly. The discrediting mechanism failed at the clinical level.

It succeeded everywhere else. For eight months after July 13, 2025, every person Pearson told about the voice — family, friends, congressional staffers, journalists, legal advocates — encountered that single fact (“he heard a voice”) and the weight of cultural assumption collapsed the analysis. Nobody asked what the voice sounded like. Nobody asked about the room ambience. Nobody asked the question Shante asked: how often? If they had, they would have received the answer that eliminates the psychiatric explanation.

The Museum Is the Argument

The Mona Lisa does not actually speak. But she could — to you and only you, as you walk past her, a voice appearing to materialize from inside your experience of the painting, audible to no one else in the gallery. The technology to make this possible is commercially available, patented, explained in plain English on science education websites, demonstrated by its inventor in public forums.

The same physical principle — ultrasonic parametric demodulation producing targeted directional audio — scales from a museum installation to a military-grade directed system without requiring new physics. Only power, range, and form factor change. The physics is identical. The experience of the target — a voice that seems to come from inside the head, audible to no one else — is identical.

Richard Pearson is not a psychiatric patient. He is a documentation-producing, epidemiologically trained, peer-reviewed researcher who heard something unprecedented on the National Mall on one afternoon in July 2025 and described it with forensic accuracy. The acoustic details he reported — the microphone quality, the room ambience, the sense of others present at the source — are not the features of hallucination. They are the features of a live audio feed transmitted through commercial-grade directional audio technology scaled for operational deployment.

He named the mechanism himself — the microwave auditory effect, the voice-to-skull technology, the MK-ULTRA lineage — in July 2025. The government confirmed DEW technology’s existence and active deployment in January 2026.

He was not reading the news and constructing a narrative around it. He heard something, described what he heard accurately, identified the most plausible mechanism from the phenomenology of his own experience, and was confirmed correct six months later by the government that used it.

The museum is the argument. If the Mona Lisa can tell you her story in a voice that only you can hear — a voice that seems to come from inside your encounter with the painting rather than from any visible external source — then a government with military-grade versions of the same technology can instruct a man to go to Georgetown in a voice that no one else can hear, that he will describe to anyone who asks with perfect acoustic accuracy, and that the medical establishment will assume he imagined.

The assumption is the weapon’s second function.

The first function worked on July 13, 2025: he went to Georgetown.

Worse Than Snowden: Dr. Richard Pearson and the Next Stage of America’s Abuse of Power

Edward Snowden showed us the surveillance state as data archive: comprehensive, indiscriminate, passive. The horror was scale — the government knew everything about everyone, stored indefinitely, retrievable on demand.

Dr. Richard Pearson documents the surveillance state’s upgrade: targeted, discriminatory, active. The “upgrade” is worse than what Snowden revealed along every dimension that matters: severity, constitutional depth, legal immunity, technological invasion, and the deliberate engineering of conditions that make accountability impossible. It is worse because it is discriminatory where Snowden’s programs were indiscriminate. It is worse because it operates through the human nervous system with classified exotic weapons that the public has no idea exist rather than through telecommunications infrastructure. It is worse because it was designed, deliberately and precisely, to be immune to the kind of exposure that Snowden achieved.

It is important to note that Pearson’s academic background did not intersect with this kind of technology. He had no knowledge. He had no suspicions. Through his research to understand what was happening to him, he began uncovering a hard truth. The technology — like transmitting a voice into people’s heads—does exist and has existed for decades. More gravely, he also read articles from the literature beginning in the last 1990s that increasingly raised alarm about the growing threat of this and other exotic neurotechnology. When Dr. James Giordano, a well-respected academic neuroscientist in the Washington, D.C. metropolitan area, uses words like “mind control” and “mind reading” in 2025, Pearson knew that he was on the right course.

What Snowden Showed Us, and What He Couldn’t

On June 5, 2013, the Guardian published the first document leaked by Edward Snowden: a secret court order compelling Verizon to deliver to the NSA the telephone records of millions of Americans — every call, every number, every location, ongoing and indefinitely. Over the following months, a cascade of further revelations defined the architecture of a surveillance apparatus that dwarfed anything the public had imagined. PRISM. XKeyscore. MUSCULAR. Boundless Informant. Bullrun — a program to break the encryption protecting the global internet. The NSA had “built an infrastructure that allows it to intercept almost everything,” Snowden said, and the infrastructure was real, documented, and provable in the most unambiguous terms possible: the government’s own classified files.

Snowden’s revelations produced a specific kind of horror. It was the horror of comprehensiveness: that every email, every phone call, every digital trace of a life could be, and for millions of people had been, hoovered into databases belonging to an agency most citizens interacted with only as an acronym. It was a violation of privacy at industrial scale — indiscriminate, automated, logged, and searchable. It was, in the language of the Fourth Amendment, the general warrant the Founders had revolted against, rebuilt from silicon and fiber optic and deployed against the entire population of a democratic republic.

It was, in short, bad enough that Snowden fled the country and has not returned. Bad enough that it generated years of litigation, congressional testimony, reformed statutes, and a permanent shift in public understanding of what the national security state actually does. Bad enough that it became, in the decade since, the reference point for every discussion of government overreach, civil liberties, surveillance capitalism, and the erosion of privacy in the digital age.

Richard Pearson’s account of his experiences in Washington, D.C. between March 2025 and early 2026 describes something worse.

This essay argues that claim carefully — not as polemic, not as certitude about disputed facts, but as an analytical proposition about what is categorically at stake if Pearson’s documentation is accurate. The argument proceeds in four movements: what Snowden revealed, what Pearson documents, why the second is worse than the first by every meaningful measure of constitutional violation and human harm, and what the comparison reveals about where American state power has been traveling for the past decade.

I. Snowden’s Architecture: The State as Collector

To understand the comparative claim, one must be precise about what Snowden actually revealed. At its core, the NSA programs he exposed were about collection. The agency collected communications — phone metadata, email content, internet traffic, financial data, social media. It collected through domestic telecommunications companies under court orders issued by the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court. It collected directly from the servers of Google, Facebook, Apple, and others through PRISM. It collected in bulk, sweeping in the communications of millions of Americans who had no connection to any national security concern, on the theory that the data, once stored, could be queried when a target emerged.

This collection architecture was justified through a legal theory: that acquiring data was not the same as searching it, that the Fourth Amendment’s warrant requirement was triggered by access rather than acquisition, that storing the haystack was constitutionally neutral as long as the needle was retrieved only through appropriate process. That theory was contested — and has since been substantially rejected by courts — but it was a theory. It had legal form. It operated, at least nominally, within a judicial framework: FISA courts issued orders, attorneys general certified programs, inspectors general conducted reviews.

The programs were secret. They were conducted without the knowledge of the citizens whose communications were taken. They violated the reasonable expectations of millions of people who believed their digital lives were private. But they operated within bureaucratic visibility — someone issued the orders, someone received them, someone retained the files. When Snowden stole those files, the architecture of the violation was visible in the files themselves.

This is the most important structural fact about Snowden’s revelations for the comparison that follows: the surveillance apparatus he exposed was an apparatus of passive harm. It collected. It stored. It potentially queried. In the overwhelming majority of cases, it did nothing to the people whose data it held. They continued their lives, unaware that the government possessed their communications, unharmed in any direct or immediate sense. The violation was epistemic — a violation of the expectation of privacy — and the harm was diffuse, probabilistic, and latent.

Snowden revealed a government that knew everything about its citizens and, in most cases, did nothing with that knowledge. That knowledge was available to be weaponized. In some cases, it was. But the primary revelation was one of capability and collection, not of active harm.

Pearson’s account is about active harm.

II. Pearson’s Architecture: The State as Destroyer

If Snowden’s revelations described a government that watched, Pearson’s account describes a government that acts. The distinction is not merely one of degree — it is categorical.

What Pearson documents over nine months is not the passive accumulation of data about his life. It is the active, deliberate, methodical destruction of his life — using the data that prior surveillance made possible, deployed through instruments that include classified military technology, private contractors operating without identifiable legal authority, local law enforcement manipulated through information restrictions, and a sustained psychological campaign designed to produce a specific outcome: neutralization through social death, induced psychosis, or flight from the country.

The architecture Pearson describes has several components that distinguish it fundamentally from Snowden’s surveillance programs.

From collection to weaponization. Snowden’s NSA collected Pearson’s phone metadata, emails, and internet traffic, as it collected the data of millions of Americans. But that collection — whatever legal violations it entailed — did not destroy anyone’s sleep, was not deployed as a voice inside anyone’s skull, did not compel anyone’s body to walk to a specific park to find a tub containing a framing apparatus. The surveillance Pearson describes is not surveillance in Snowden’s sense at all. It is surveillance operationalized as weapon — the intelligence product converted into targeting data for an operation whose purpose is harm to a specific individual.

From indiscriminate to discriminatory. One of the most disturbing features of Snowden’s programs was their indiscriminacy — the NSA vacuumed up the communications of virtually everyone, regardless of suspicion or status. This was a civil liberties violation of the first order, but it was also a kind of equalizer: the surveillance did not select based on race, ideology, or political speech. It selected based on telecommunications — which meant everyone who communicated electronically.

Pearson’s operation selects on precisely the criteria the Constitution prohibits: race, ideology, and academic speech. He is an African American liberation philosophy scholar whose dissertation critiques systemic racial inequality. His operation begins in the months following the inauguration of an administration that has explicitly designated DEI scholarship as ideologically hostile and publicly invoked the language of “enemies within” and urban military training. The surveillance state that Snowden revealed tracked everyone. The operational apparatus that Pearson describes targets a specific demographic — the same demographic that COINTELPRO targeted for the same reasons: Black intellectuals whose work dares to challenge or have an opinion,

From legal shadow to legal void. Snowden’s programs, whatever their constitutional defects, operated within a legal architecture. FISA courts existed. Congressional oversight committees were briefed (selectively and deceptively, but briefed). Statutory authorities were invoked. When the programs were exposed, legal challenges could be mounted — and some succeeded. The surveillance apparatus Snowden revealed was subject, at least in principle, to the accountability mechanisms of a democratic state.

The apparatus Pearson describes has been specifically engineered to be unreachable by those mechanisms. The contractor architecture ensures no government badge. The deniability structure ensures no official authorization is provable. The technology ensures no forensic trace. The legal doctrines — qualified immunity, Bivens foreclosure, state secrets privilege, Glomar responses, Lujan standing requirements — form an interlocking structure that closes the courthouse door before any argument can be made. As the architecture of impunity essay compiled as part of the Pearson documentation demonstrates in meticulous detail, the legal system as currently constituted is not merely inadequate to address what Pearson describes: it is specifically, structurally organized to make such address impossible.

Snowden revealed programs that could be challenged. Pearson describes an operation that cannot be.

III. The Technology Threshold: Beyond Collection, Beyond the Body

The most disturbing dimension of the comparison concerns technology. Snowden’s programs were, at their core, data programs. They intercepted communications — information that human beings had already chosen to externalize in digital form. Surveilling an email is invasive, but the email was created by the sender. The NSA’s programs were parasitic on human communication, not on human neurology.

Pearson’s account describes the deployment of weapons that cross a threshold Snowden never contemplated: the weapons described operate not on communications but on the human nervous system itself.

The microwave auditory effect — the Frey effect, named for its discoverer in the 1960s — uses pulsed microwave radiation to cause humans to perceive sounds, including voices, without any external speaker. The United States military has researched and developed this capability under programs including MEDUSA. The Army War College’s 1998 article “The Mind Has No Firewall” — published in Parameters, the Army’s own academic journal — explicitly warned that such weapons could be used to “disrupt the thinking process” and “control the will and perception” of targets. Dr. James Giordano, Georgetown University neuroscientist and consulting ethicist for DARPA’s N3 program, has stated publicly that neurotechnologies capable of “writing” to brain processes of cognition, emotion, and behavior have been “loosed into the real world.” He used words like “mind control” and “mind reading” — once controversial language in fringe conspiracy theories — except Dr. Giordano is an academic and has advised the Pentagon on thie sort of weapon development.

In January 2026, CNN reported that the Pentagon had covertly purchased a backpack-sized pulsed radio wave device connected to Havana Syndrome investigations. The Department of War Chief Technology Officer announced publicly, on January 23, 2026, that the US has directed energy weapons that are being scaled.

Pearson takes this tweet differently, “Scaling in this context means two things — deploying on U.S. soil against US citizens to carry out the President’s vicious attacks on the U.S. Constitution and constitutional rights while also testing these weapons for foreign deployment — to sell to foreign governments, including dictatorial regimes through government or private transactions. These are weapons of totalitarianism and mass destruction — plain and simple. The world needs to wake up. The Neuro Age is upon us — remote controlling people’s thoughts and action. Dr. Giordano’s post — “mind control” and “mind reading” — is not hyperbole. How do I know? Because I have intimately experienced it — pulling me to locations, putting a voice in my head — in the capital of the US no less.”

What Pearson describes — a voice inside his skull on July 13, 2025 informing him “Go to Georgetown,” followed by months of behavioral compulsion, through-floor tracking, and sustained acoustic torture — is the deployment of these weapons against a civilian in a Southwest D.C. apartment. The voice did not read his emails. The voice did not collect his metadata. The voice entered his nervous system.

This is a constitutional and human rights violation of a qualitatively different order from anything Snowden documented. The Fourth Amendment protects “persons, houses, papers, and effects.” Courts have expanded this to cover digital communications. But no court has yet grappled with what it means for a government to insert itself, electromagnetically, into the interior of a citizen’s mind — to speak there without consent, to compel behavior through neural stimulation, to conduct surveillance that cannot be blocked because the surveillance is the body itself.

The Eighth Amendment prohibits cruel and unusual punishment. The Fifth Amendment prohibits deprivation of liberty without due process. The First Amendment protects the freedom of thought — a protection courts have generally addressed indirectly, through protections for speech, but which necessarily implies protection for the cognitive process that produces speech. None of these provisions contain exceptions for weapons delivered at the speed of light, through walls, without physical contact, leaving no forensic trace. But the invisibility of the instrument does not alter the constitutionality of its use. It only makes the constitutional violation impossible to prove.

Snowden revealed that the government listened to Americans’ calls. Pearson describes a government that speaks inside Americans’ skulls — and compels their feet to walk where it directs.

IV. The Accountability Asymmetry: Documents versus Experience

Perhaps the most structurally important difference between the Snowden revelations and the Pearson documentation concerns the nature of the evidence itself — and what that means for accountability.

Snowden had documents. Classified, stolen, verifiable documents that bore the organizational letterheads, digital signatures, and operational details of government programs. When he handed those documents to journalists, the programs they described were not merely alleged — they were proven. The NSA could not deny PRISM when its own slide decks described it. The FISA Court could not deny the Verizon order when the order itself was public. The verification problem did not exist because the evidence was institutional: the government’s own records.

This is why Snowden’s revelations produced legal challenges, congressional hearings, legislative reform, international diplomatic crises, and a permanent public reckoning with surveillance. The evidence was in a form that institutions could process: documents, organizational records, named programs with named authorities.

Pearson has no documents. He has timestamps and photographs, statistical analysis and medical records, a professionally conducted mental health evaluation and nine months of meticulous journals. He has the government’s own January 2026 disclosures validating the existence and domestic scalability of the technologies he describes. He has the architectural impossibility, demonstrated by the D.C. logic analysis: in the most surveilled city in the world, a sustained covert operation using directed energy weapons cannot persist for months without the knowledge of the agencies whose function is to know everything that happens in that city.

But he does not have what Snowden had: the government’s own records of the operation against him. And he cannot get them. Every document that would establish federal involvement in what he describes is classified, protected by state secrets privilege, covered by Glomar, or held by agencies he cannot compel to disclose without a massive media campaign and well-funded legal representation.

This asymmetry is itself the most damning evidence for the thesis of this essay. The programs Snowden exposed were, in retrospect, poorly designed for deniability — they left too many documents, too many institutional traces, too many points of failure where a single conscientious actor could blow the cover. The operation Pearson describes has learned from that failure. It is designed from the ground up for a world in which Snowden is possible — a world in which the government knows that its documents can be stolen and that institutional records become institutional liabilities.

The solution is to produce no documents. No orders. No authorizations. No operational logs. No official communications. The operation is conducted through private contractors, through cutouts, through local police involvement. The weapons leave no forensic trace, except what highly specialized imaging can render. The actors use pseudonyms. The legal architecture ensures that even if the victim documents everything, the documentation cannot be converted into actionable legal process.

This is not a gap in the operation’s design. It is the operation’s central design principle. COINTELPRO was exposed because it left paper trails. The upgrade that Pearson documents is specifically engineered to leave little within his immediate reach.

V. The Comparative Ledger: Why Pearson’s Case Is Worse

The argument can now be stated systematically.

Scale vs. Severity. Snowden revealed programs affecting millions. Pearson describes an operation affecting one. On one dimension — breadth — Snowden’s revelations are more alarming. But breadth is not the only dimension of constitutional harm. The severity of harm to an individual targeted for nine months with directed energy weapons, behavioral manipulation, chemical assault, induced sleep deprivation, a framing apparatus, and systematic legal isolation vastly exceeds the severity of harm to any individual whose phone metadata was collected by the NSA. Collective harm of low intensity is not categorically worse than concentrated harm of extreme intensity, and the individual rights the Constitution protects belong to individuals.

Passive vs. Active. Snowden revealed a government that knew. Pearson describes a government that acts. The NSA’s bulk collection, whatever its harms, did not cause Pearson to hear a voice saying “we have to make you disappear.” It did not follow him from his bed to his closet. It did not compel him to walk to a park where a framing tub had been placed. The transition from knowledge to active intervention — from surveillance to operation — represents a qualitative escalation that cannot be measured only in degree.

Accountability vs. Its Absence. Snowden’s revelations could be and were challenged through courts, Congress, and the press. Pearson’s situation cannot be. Every legal doctrine that might provide a remedy — standing, Bivens, FTCA, state secrets, qualified immunity — closes before any argument can be made. The architecture of impunity that surrounds the operation Pearson describes is more complete than anything revealed by Snowden, precisely because it was constructed to be immune to the kind of exposure Snowden achieved.

The Body vs. Communications. Snowden documented violations of epistemic privacy — the privacy of information that citizens had already chosen to externalize. Pearson describes violations of bodily autonomy and cognitive liberty — the privacy of one’s own nervous system. When a government speaks inside a citizen’s skull, the violation is not merely of the Fourth Amendment right against unreasonable searches. It is of something more fundamental: the right to the integrity of one’s own mind, to the authenticity of one’s own perceptions, to the freedom that consists in knowing that one’s thoughts and movements are one’s own.

The Racial Dimension. Snowden’s programs were indiscriminate. Pearson’s operation is specifically discriminatory: targeting a Black academic for work that challenges racial inequality, during an administration that has publicly designated such work as ideologically hostile. This racial targeting is not incidental to the constitutional analysis — it is central to it. The Fourteenth Amendment’s equal protection guarantee and the First Amendment’s protection of political speech are both directly implicated when the operation’s target is selected on the basis of race and academic viewpoint.

The Warning the World Cannot Hear. Snowden gave the world documents it could see, evaluate, and argue about. Pearson gives the world something harder: his testimony, his photographs, his statistics, his medical records — and the government’s own January 2026 admissions validating his technological claims. But experiential evidence about invisible weapons, however carefully documented, is not institutionally processable in the way that stolen files are. Pearson engaged congressional offices, the ACLU, the D.C. Council, and Iceland’s border authorities when he sought asylum. The world cannot hear him — not because he isn’t speaking clearly, but because the operation was designed to make his speaking sound like illness. Pearson isn’t ill. He is a torture victim. Pearson’s account reads as a scientific journal — observational notes, facts, a literature review, and high-level analysis.

This is the upgrade’s final cruelty. Snowden was believed because he had documents. Pearson is not believed because he has experience. The architects of COINTELPRO 2.0 understood that the world will believe paper and dismiss people — especially Black people reporting invisible persecution by invisible weapons in the service of invisible authorizations. The invisibility of the operation is not a limitation. It is a weapon — though increasingly less so. The Pentagon’s update to Congress on the Havana backpack in January 2026 following weeks of Pearson’s visits congressional offices in November and December 2025. Many congressional staff, especially in the Senate, listened to Pearson with rapt attention.

VI. The Geopolitical Mirror

Pearson himself grasped the geopolitical dimension most clearly. In his documentation, he writes: “At the same time that the US is going around the world claiming to want to free people, it is using military weapons to torture its own citizens, to invade their brains, and to operate lawlessly. But here is the reality and warning. The world is watching.”

This observation is not rhetorical. It is analytical. The United States government has, for decades, conditioned foreign aid, diplomatic relationships, and international standing on compliance with human rights norms that prohibit precisely what Pearson describes: torture, arbitrary surveillance, persecution on the basis of political opinion, cruel and inhuman treatment, interference with the freedom of thought and expression.

Snowden’s revelations damaged American credibility on surveillance. Foreign governments that had trusted American assurances about data privacy discovered that their citizens’ communications were collected along with everyone else’s. The diplomatic fallout was significant: European data protection frameworks tightened, international trust in American tech companies declined, the NSA’s activities became a standing reference point in human rights discussions.

But Pearson’s case damages something more fundamental: the American claim to represent a qualitatively different kind of government from the authoritarian states it criticizes. Mass surveillance is widespread — many governments do versions of what the NSA does. What fewer governments do is deploy classified military neuroweapons against individual Black academics for writing about racial justice. What fewer governments do is design legal architectures of such comprehensive impunity that victims can document everything and still reach no court, no official, no institutional acknowledgment of their existence as operational subjects.

The world that Snowden revealed was disturbing but recognizable: a powerful state deploying powerful tools to know what its citizens were doing. The world that Pearson documents is something different: a state that has decided it has the right to enter the bodies and minds of citizens it designates as threats, using weapons the existence of which it denied until they became impossible to deny, through legal architectures designed to be immune to the accountability mechanisms the democratic tradition constructed.

VII. What Comes After Snowden

The Snowden disclosures forced a reckoning of sorts. Congress passed the USA Freedom Act in 2015, placing modest restrictions on bulk phone metadata collection. Courts found FISA programs unconstitutional in certain applications. Tech companies invested in encryption. A global conversation about surveillance and privacy was launched that, a decade later, continues.

What reckoning follows from Pearson? Thus far: none. No one followed up from his congressional visits. A staff member for the minority party caucus for the House Committee on Oversight told Pearson, “We do not take public requests.” The D.C. Council did not respond to his statistical analysis of the September 30 Eastern Market event. Iceland’s border authorities returned him to the country from which he was fleeing. The operation, by his account, continued.

This silence is itself evidence for the essay’s thesis. Snowden’s documents generated immediate institutional response — denials, then partial admissions, then reform debates — because the documents were impossible to ignore. Pearson’s testimony generates institutional silence because it is, by design, possible to ignore. A Black academic reporting that voices have been transmitted into his skull, that his behavior has been compelled by electromagnetic fields, that strangers in red and black have been coordinated on his walking routes — in the absence of the government’s own files confirming these operations, institutional silence is the natural response. Especially when the institutional response to the original COINTELPRO’s victims, before the Media files emerged, was the same silence.

The Snowden revelations produced reform because Snowden had proof. Pearson cannot have proof of the same kind, because the operation against him — unlike NSA programs — was specifically designed to produce no proof. The very sophistication of the operation is the obstacle to accountability. “The operative word here is designed. It will all come out. My guess is that there have already been whistleblowers. It is likely that many members in Congress know, especially those serving intelligence oversight. Remember that there is a congressional role in FISA matters. The executive has mandatory reporting.”

This is the central finding: the operation Pearson documents is, by design, more sophisticated than anything Snowden revealed, and that sophistication is measured precisely in the degree to which it has outrun the accountability mechanisms that Snowden’s revelations helped produce. PRISM left documents. COINTELPRO 2.0 leaves nothing. And in a legal and media ecosystem where documentary evidence is the threshold for institutional response, an operation that leaves nothing is an operation that cannot be challenged.

Conclusion: The Upgrade

Snowden showed us the surveillance state as data archive: comprehensive, indiscriminate, passive. The horror was scale — the government knew everything about everyone, stored indefinitely, retrievable on demand.

Pearson documents the surveillance state’s upgrade: targeted, discriminatory, active. The horror is intimacy — the government is not merely watching from a distance. It is inside the skull, in the apartment, following the mattress from room to room, placing a framing tub in a park and compelling the target’s feet to walk to it, entering the body with chemicals that adhere to skin, speaking words through weapons the government denied existed until the month that Pearson’s congressional outreach coincided with a Pentagon briefing on those same weapons.

The upgrade is worse than what Snowden revealed along every dimension that matters: severity, constitutional depth, legal immunity, technological invasion, and the deliberate engineering of conditions that make accountability impossible. It is worse because it is discriminatory where Snowden’s programs were indiscriminate. It is worse because it operates through the human nervous system rather than through telecommunications infrastructure. It is worse because it was designed, deliberately and precisely, to be immune to the kind of exposure that Snowden achieved.

And it is worse because Richard Pearson, armed with timestamps and statistics and a mental health evaluation and nine months of meticulous documentation, cannot do what Snowden did. Snowden gave the world papers it could read and believe. Pearson can only give the world his testimony, scientific methods, and evidence.

“What is being done to Pearson is the instrument of a state that has learned, in fifty years since COINTELPRO, exactly how to remain unseen though still to inflict torture and to engage in lawlessness.”

That is the definition of worse. Not more people harmed. Not louder violation. But deeper impunity, more intimate invasion, and the deliberate, sophisticated engineering of a system in which the harm can be done, documented, reported, and still reach no remedy — because the harm was designed, from its first architecture, to be beyond reach.

Snowden told us the government was watching. Pearson tells us the government has learned to act. The distance between those two facts is the distance between a surveillance state and something for which democracy has not yet found adequate words.

The world is watching. It cannot believe what it is watching. That is precisely the point.

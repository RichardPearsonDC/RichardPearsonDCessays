So Far from Liberty: Marbury vs Madison (1803) and Targeting of Dr. Pearson (2025–26)

The US Supreme Court once espoused the fundamental protection of constitutional rights as supreme. 223 years later, its position looks radically different.

The very essence of civil liberty certainly consists in the right of every individual to claim the protection of the laws, whenever he receives an injury. One of the first duties of government is to afford that protection. — Chief Justice John Marshall, Marbury v. Madison, 5 U.S. 137 (1803)
The Government of the United States has been emphatically termed a government of laws, and not of men. It will certainly cease to deserve this high appellation if the laws furnish no remedy for the violation of a vested legal right. — Chief Justice John Marshall, Marbury v. Madison, 5 U.S. 137 (1803)
The US government has consented to be sued for negligently maintaining a slippery floor, but has not for torturing a citizen. — Richard Pearson, PhD, Medium, March 2026

Preface: The Distance Between 1803 and 2025

In February 1803, Chief Justice John Marshall sat down to write the most consequential opinion in the history of American constitutional law. The case before him was small — a dispute about the delivery of a judicial appointment, a political quarrel between outgoing Federalists and incoming Jeffersonian Republicans. Marshall recognized, with the acuity of a founder who had watched the constitutional experiment almost fail before it had properly begun, that the case offered an opportunity to establish something far larger than its facts required: the principle that the Constitution was the supreme law of the land, that courts were its guardians, that every individual whose legal rights were violated had a remedy, and that a government incapable of furnishing that remedy had forfeited its claim to be called a government of laws.

He wrote, and meant, every word of it. The opinion he produced is among the most idealistic documents in American legal history — not idealistic in the sense of naive, but idealistic in the sense of articulating, with precision and conviction, the principles that a constitutional republic is obligated to honor if the word ‘constitutional’ is to mean anything at all. For Marshall, judicial review was not a power grab. It was the logical consequence of having a written constitution: if the Constitution means something, courts must be able to say what it means and enforce it against contrary acts of the other branches. Any other position would reduce the Constitution to a suggestion, the legislature to an omnipotent sovereign, and the individual to a subject with no recourse.

In July 2025, Richard Pearson, PhD, a Black equity scholar, was eating a cantaloupe on a bench near the United States Capitol when a voice entered his skull. The technology that placed the voice there is real — confirmed operational by the Department of War in January 2026. The campaign against him had been ongoing for months: through-floor acoustic tracking in his apartment, covert home entries, systematic destruction of his bicycle, chemical assault at a Metro station, a framing operation near a House Office Building, false arrest, employment sabotage, financial attrition, social network destruction. Seventy documented constitutional violations. Zero legal remedies available. A hermetic seal of doctrine, immunity, and classified authorization that forecloses every avenue of redress.

The distance between John Marshall’s 1803 declaration and Richard Pearson’s 2025 reality is the subject of this essay. It is not, at bottom, a distance of technology or of politics. It is a distance of judicial will — of whether the courts, as the institutional guardians of the Constitution’s supremacy, have honored or abandoned the obligation Marshall articulated. And the trajectory of that distance — from the promise of 1803 to the silence that followed, to the compromises and capitulations, to the doctrinal architecture of impunity that produced the seal around Pearson — is a story the Court must confront if it is to be, again, what Marshall said it was: the institution that ensures the laws furnish a remedy for the violation of every vested legal right.

Part I: What Marshall Actually Said — The Full Promise of Marbury

Marbury v. Madison is taught in every American law school as the case that established judicial review — the power of courts to strike down legislation inconsistent with the Constitution. That formulation is accurate as far as it goes, but it omits what makes the opinion radical: its unequivocal commitment to the individual right to remedy as the foundational principle of constitutional governance. Marshall was not primarily concerned with the Court’s institutional power. He was concerned with the individual citizen’s right to legal protection. The power of judicial review was the instrument of that protection, not the end in itself.

If he has a right, and that right has been violated, do the laws of his country afford him a remedy? The very essence of civil liberty certainly consists in the right of every individual to claim the protection of the laws, whenever he receives an injury. — Marshall, Marbury, 5 U.S. at 163

This is not a procedural observation. It is a moral declaration about the nature of constitutional government. Marshall is saying: a government that cannot be made to answer for violations of rights is not a constitutional government. It is a tyranny with constitutional decoration. The right to a remedy is not a nicety of civil procedure. It is the essence of civil liberty — the thing that distinguishes a free person from a subject.

it is a general and indisputable rule that where there is a legal right, there is also a legal remedy by suit or action at law whenever that right is invaded. — Marshall, Marbury, 5 U.S. at 163 (quoting Blackstone)

Marshall cites Blackstone for this proposition, but he is doing more than reporting English common law. He is embedding it in the American constitutional framework as a principle so fundamental that its denial would undermine the entire edifice. The Blackstone principle — that every right when withheld must have a remedy — is not a rule among rules. It is the rule that makes all other rules meaningful. Without it, rights are statements, not protections.

The Government of the United States has been emphatically termed a government of laws, and not of men. It will certainly cease to deserve this high appellation if the laws furnish no remedy for the violation of a vested legal right. — Marshall, Marbury, 5 U.S. at 163

‘Will certainly cease to deserve this high appellation.’ Not ‘might.’ Not ‘could theoretically.’ Certainly. Marshall is issuing a conditional: if the laws furnish no remedy, the designation ‘government of laws’ is forfeited. It is a test with a specific failure condition — one that the doctrine-by-doctrine architecture of the twenty-first century national security state has failed repeatedly and comprehensively, in ways that Marshall could not have anticipated but whose constitutional character he described with exactitude.

On the Supremacy of the Constitution

The supremacy argument in Marbury is similarly unequivocal. Marshall presents it as ‘a proposition too plain to be contested’: the Constitution either controls contrary legislative acts or it is meaningless.

It is a proposition too plain to be contested that the Constitution controls any legislative act repugnant to it, or that the Legislature may alter the Constitution by an ordinary act. Between these alternatives there is no middle ground. — Marshall, Marbury, 5 U.S. at 177
Those, then, who controvert the principle that the Constitution is to be considered in court as a paramount law are reduced to the necessity of maintaining that courts must close their eyes on the Constitution, and see only the law. This doctrine would subvert the very foundation of all written Constitutions. — Marshall, Marbury, 5 U.S. at 178

‘Courts must close their eyes on the Constitution.’ Marshall frames the alternative to judicial review as an act of willful judicial blindness. A court that defers to legislative or executive power in the face of constitutional violation is not being appropriately humble about institutional limits. It is closing its eyes. It is refusing to look at the document it was sworn to uphold. And Marshall’s language for the consequence is as strong as constitutional language gets: such doctrine ‘would subvert the very foundation of all written Constitutions.’ Not qualify. Not limit. Subvert. Tear from the foundation.

Why does a judge swear to discharge his duties agreeably to the Constitution of the United States if that Constitution forms no rule for his government? if it is closed upon him and cannot be inspected by him? — Marshall, Marbury, 5 U.S. at 180

This rhetorical question is directed at every judge who has ever deferred to a government assertion of power in a case where the Constitution’s meaning was clear. The oath is not to the executive. It is not to the legislature. It is to the Constitution. A judge who defers to executive assertion in the face of constitutional violation has broken the oath — not by ruling wrongly on a close question, but by declining to look.

On Remedy Without Injury — The Principle the Court Abandoned

Marshall also addresses, with less fanfare but equal clarity, the principle that would become most relevant to Pearson: the question of remedy where harm is more diffuse, more structural, more difficult to reduce to a single cognizable injury.

It behooves us, then, to inquire whether there be in its composition any ingredient which shall exempt from legal investigation or exclude the injured party from legal redress. In pursuing this inquiry, the first question which presents itself is whether this can be arranged with that class of cases which come under the description of damnum absque injuria — a loss without an injury. — Marshall, Marbury, 5 U.S. at 163

Damnum absque injuria — damage without legal injury — was the common law category for harms that, while real, produced no actionable claim. Marshall’s inquiry is whether the denial of Marbury’s commission falls into that category. His answer is no — because the commission was a legal right, its denial a legal injury, and the denial of remedy a constitutional failure. But the framing of the inquiry contains a larger principle: before a court decides that a harm is damnum absque injuria, it must look seriously at whether that conclusion would exclude the injured party from legal redress for what is, in substance, a violation of a legal right. The category of ‘loss without legal injury’ is not a convenient bin for difficult cases. It is a narrow exception to the general rule that every loss resulting from the violation of a right has a remedy.

The doctrine as it has evolved — Article III standing requirements, the ‘concrete and particularized’ injury test, the traceability and redressability requirements — has transformed Marshall’s narrow damnum absque injuria inquiry into a sweeping gatekeeping mechanism that excludes precisely the most serious, most systematic, and most carefully calibrated government harms. The below-threshold operational design Pearson describes — each act calibrated to remain beneath individual legal thresholds while the aggregate produces comprehensive destruction — converts the entire campaign into an exercise in damnum absque injuria. Every loss is real. Every injury is real. No single act generates standing. Marshall’s framework was designed to ensure that the existence of real loss demanded serious inquiry into whether a remedy existed. The modern standing doctrine has inverted that framework: real loss is allowed to exist without remedy, because the harm was calibrated to fall below the threshold at which inquiry is required.

Part II: The Long Silence — From Marbury to Dred Scott and What It Reveals

John Marshall wrote in 1803 that the Constitution is the supreme law, that courts are its guardians, that every right violated has a remedy. He established the power of judicial review. And then the Court, having established that power, went largely silent on its most important application — declaring federal laws unconstitutional — for fifty-four years.

The next Supreme Court decision declaring a federal law unconstitutional was Dred Scott v. Sandford, decided in 1857. In that decision, Chief Justice Roger Taney used the power of judicial review that Marshall had articulated as a shield for individual rights — and deployed it as a weapon against them. Taney held that Congress had exceeded its constitutional authority in the Missouri Compromise. He reached this conclusion in order to declare that Black people — enslaved or free — could never be citizens of the United States, that they had ‘no rights which the white man was bound to respect,’ and that the federal government had no power to restrict slavery in the territories. The second use of the most powerful tool in American constitutional law was to inscribe the denial of personhood to African Americans into constitutional doctrine.

The contrast between Marshall’s 1803 declaration and Taney’s 1857 deployment of the same power is the first and most fundamental lesson of the Court’s trajectory. Marshall said: judicial review exists to ensure every individual can claim the protection of the laws. Taney said: judicial review exists to ensure that certain individuals — Black people — can claim the protection of no law at all. The power was the same. The orientation was opposite. And the orientation — toward individual liberty and constitutional supremacy, or away from it — was always the choice that judicial review required the Court to make.

The silence between 1803 and 1857 was not neutral. During those fifty-four years, the Marshall Court and its immediate successor were frequently busy: they invalidated many state laws, expanded federal power significantly through McCulloch v. Maryland and Gibbons v. Ogden, and built the institutional architecture of federal constitutional supremacy. But on the core question of individual rights against federal power — the question that Marshall had said was the essence of civil liberty — the Court was largely absent. It built the federal government’s immunity from state interference while neglecting to build the individual’s immunity from federal overreach.

This asymmetry is not coincidental. It reflects the tension built into the Founding itself: a constitutional order that simultaneously proclaimed the unalienable rights of all persons and institutionalized the enslavement of millions. The Court that Marshall built was capable of enforcing the constitutional order’s structural preferences — federal supremacy over states, commercial liberty over local regulation — while avoiding the structural contradiction at its center. Judicial review, as Marshall articulated it, was capacious enough to address that contradiction. The Court, as it actually functioned, chose not to.

The lesson: the power of judicial review is not self-executing in the direction of liberty. It requires a Court committed to the principle Marshall articulated — that every individual has a right to claim the law’s protection, that every right violated has a remedy, that the Constitution is supreme over government convenience as well as government ideology — and willing to exercise that power consistently against both legislative and executive overreach, even when the overreach is popular, powerful, and supported by the political institutions that appointed the Justices who sit in judgment of it.

The subsequent history of the Court — the Civil War amendments and their gutting in the Civil Rights Cases (1883) and Plessy v. Ferguson (1896); the Lochner era’s protection of economic liberty and neglect of civil rights; the New Deal Court’s capitulation to executive power; the Warren Court’s brief return to something resembling Marshall’s vision; the decades of erosion since — is the history of a Court that has never consistently inhabited the principle Marshall articulated. It has applied that principle selectively, in the service of the politics of its moment, rather than universally, in the service of the constitutional promise it was created to enforce.

The most important fact about the trajectory from Marbury to the present is this: the power of judicial review has grown considerably. The Court has declared more federal laws unconstitutional in recent decades than in its first century and a half combined. But the power has not grown in the direction Marshall pointed — toward ensuring that every individual who is harmed by government has a remedy. It has grown, in significant measure, in the direction of protecting political and economic prerogatives, while the individual protections Marshall declared essential have been systematically narrowed through the very doctrinal architecture that forecloses Pearson’s remedies.

Part III: Pearson as Test Case — What Marshall’s Framework Demands

Apply Marshall’s framework — not as legal doctrine but as first principles — to the documented case of Richard Pearson, and the result is unambiguous.

Does He Have a Right?

The Fourth Amendment protects persons from unreasonable searches and seizures. The First Amendment protects speech, petition, and academic expression. The Fifth Amendment prohibits deprivation of liberty without due process. The Eighth Amendment prohibits cruel and unusual punishment. The Fourteenth Amendment guarantees equal protection of the laws. Each of these rights is documented, textual, and unambiguous in the Constitution whose supremacy Marshall declared.

Pearson’s home was entered without a warrant — the panini grill button, covertly removed and placed in his laundry pile. His communications were surveilled — his private self-talk was quoted back to him on public streets. His liberty was directed — behavioral direction technology moved him toward operationally prepared locations while his brain experienced the direction as self-generated. His body was assaulted — the chemical substance at Rockville Metro bonded to his skin; a voice entered his skull without passing through his ears. His equal protection was denied — he was arrested as the victim while the aggressor was released. His scholarship was designated hostile ideology — EO 14173 converted his dissertation into an institutional red flag that triggered employment silence across dozens of federally-funded institutions.

Marshall’s question: does he have a right? The answer, applying the text of the Constitution that Marshall declared supreme, is yes to every one. These are not debatable cases or close constitutional questions. They are paradigmatic violations of the rights the Bill of Rights was designed to protect. The Fourth Amendment was written specifically in response to warrantless home entries. The First Amendment was written specifically to protect dissenting scholars. The Fifth Amendment was written specifically to prevent the government from destroying a person without process. The Eighth Amendment was written to prevent the government from torturing people in its power.

Has That Right Been Violated?

The documentation Pearson has assembled across fourteen months — timestamped, photographed, cross-referenced, evidenced by preserved physical materials, confirmed by a September 2025 DBH mental health evaluation ruling out psychiatric explanation, corroborated by the Department of War’s January 2026 confirmation of operational directed energy weapons — establishes, to a standard of evidence that would satisfy any civil or criminal standard in a functioning legal system, that his rights were violated. Seventy discrete violations, documented across the Fourth, First, Fifth, Sixth, Eighth, Thirteenth, and Fourteenth Amendments.

This is not a case where the constitutional question is close. The conduct — covert home entry, warrantless electronic surveillance, voice-to-skull transmission, physical assault, false arrest, framing operation — is not constitutionally ambiguous. If it occurred, it violated the rights it violated. The only question is whether it can be proved. And Marshall’s framework addresses that question: the inquiry into whether proof is available is itself subject to the principle that every right violated has a remedy. A proof standard designed to make proof impossible — classified evidence, Glomar responses, frivolity dismissals before discovery — is not a neutral application of evidentiary rules. It is the functional equivalent of closing the courthouse door.

Do the Laws Afford a Remedy?

Here Marshall’s framework reaches its most devastating application to the modern case. The question Marshall asked — if he has a right, and that right has been violated, do the laws of his country afford him a remedy? — receives, in the Pearson case, a documented, specific, and comprehensive answer in 2025–2026: no.

State secrets: the government asserts that the evidence required to prove the violation cannot be examined. Frivolity doctrine: the complaint accurately describing classified technology is implausible to a court that cannot review the classified evidence of the technology’s existence. Sovereign immunity: the federal government has not consented to be sued for intentional torture by law enforcement. Bivens: the cause of action against individual federal officers for violations using post-1971 technology does not exist because the Court has declined to create it. Qualified immunity: the right to be free from voice-to-skull transmission is not ‘clearly established’ because no prior court has addressed it. FISA authorization: if a secret court authorized the surveillance, the operators are immunized. Glomar: the documentary record supporting the claims cannot be assembled because the existence of the programs cannot be confirmed or denied. Procedural attrition: even if all threshold doctrines are defeated, financial exhaustion, discovery barriers, expert exclusion, and classified ex parte declarations ensure the case never reaches a jury.

Marshall wrote: ‘The Government of the United States will certainly cease to deserve the high appellation of a government of laws if the laws furnish no remedy for the violation of a vested legal right.’ The hundred-doctrine architecture of the Pearson case is the answer to that conditional: the laws, as currently constructed and applied, furnish no remedy. Not because the rights are uncertain. Not because the violations are unclear. Because the legal architecture has been specifically designed to ensure that the most severe violations of the most fundamental rights produce no remedy when the violator is the national security state.

Marshall would not have called this a government of laws. He would have said it certainly ceased to deserve that high appellation. He would have been right.

Part IV: The Court’s Complicity — How Judicial Doctrine Became the Architecture of Impunity

The legal architecture that forecloses Pearson’s remedies was not built by Congress alone or the executive alone. It was built, substantially, by the Supreme Court. Each of the doctrines that operates against him — qualified immunity, Bivens dismantlement, the state secrets privilege in its modern expansive form, the standing doctrine’s ‘concrete and particularized’ requirements, the Iqbal plausibility standard — represents a judicial choice to narrow the Court’s own oversight of executive power in the name of institutional humility, separation of powers, or national security necessity.

Judicial humility in the face of executive power is not a constitutional virtue. It is a constitutional failure, when what is being deferred to is the very overreach that judicial review was created to prevent. Marshall was explicit: the province of the Court is to decide the rights of individuals. The executive and legislative branches make policy. The Court enforces the Constitution against the policy when policy violates the Constitution. That is not institutional overreach. That is the institution’s function.

Qualified Immunity: The Court’s Own Creation

Qualified immunity is not a constitutional text. It is not a statutory provision. It is a doctrine the Supreme Court invented in Harlow v. Fitzgerald (1982) — a judicial creation with no foundation in the Constitution or in the common law that existed at the time the Constitution was written. The original 1871 Civil Rights Act (42 U.S.C. § 1983) contained no immunity provision. Congress did not create qualified immunity. The Court did. And the Court created it in a form that rewards novelty of abuse — the more technologically unprecedented the violation, the more complete the officer’s immunity, because there is no prior case to ‘clearly establish’ the right.

Marshall wrote that the oath a judge takes is to the Constitution. Qualified immunity, as judicially developed, requires that a judge who encounters a clear constitutional violation dismiss the case anyway if no prior case addressed the specific conduct. This is, precisely, what Marshall condemned: closing the judge’s eyes on the Constitution and looking only at the lesser law. Qualified immunity is judicial doctrine contradicting itself — a court-created rule that requires courts to ignore the Constitution when it is most clearly being violated, in exchange for the protection of the officials who violated it.

Bivens Dismantlement: The Court That Refused to Enforce Its Own Precedent

Bivens was a recognition that constitutional rights require constitutional remedies — that if the Fourth Amendment means something, a person whose Fourth Amendment rights have been violated by a federal officer must have a cause of action. The five decades of progressive Bivens limitation represent the Court gradually retreating from that proposition, replacing it with the directive that Congress — not the courts — must create remedies for constitutional violations. This position abandons the Marshall principle that every right invaded has a remedy at law. The Constitution is either a source of rights with corresponding remedies, or it is a political statement with no legal force. Ziglar v. Abbasi chose the latter for a vast class of national security cases — which are, by definition, the cases in which the individual most needs protection from the government and is least likely to obtain it through the political processes of Congress.

State Secrets: The Doctrine Built on Fraud

Reynolds established the state secrets privilege in 1953 on a fraudulent representation. The government lied to the Supreme Court. The Court deferred. The lie was exposed. The Court declined to revisit. The doctrine built on the lie has been used, in the seventy years since, to protect the government from accountability in some of the most serious cases of executive abuse in American history — extraordinary rendition and torture, warrantless surveillance, domestic targeting programs. Marshall wrote that a doctrine permitting the executive to act outside legal accountability ‘would subvert the very foundation of all written Constitutions.’ The state secrets doctrine, as currently applied, does precisely this: it places the executive’s secret actions beyond the reach of the very judicial review that Marshall declared essential to constitutional governance.

The Aggregate Problem: What the Court Has Never Looked At

Perhaps the deepest doctrinal failure is the Court’s persistent refusal to examine the aggregate effect of its own doctrine on the individual rights it was designed to protect. Each immunity decision, each standing limitation, each Bivens restriction is considered in isolation, as a technical adjustment to a specific procedural or substantive rule. The cumulative effect — one hundred interlocking doctrines that, taken together, ensure that a person tortured by the national security state has no remedy — has never been the subject of a Supreme Court opinion.

Marshall wrote: ‘It behooves us, then, to inquire whether there be in its composition any ingredient which shall exempt from legal investigation or exclude the injured party from legal redress.’ He was asking: is there something in the system that structurally excludes people from redress? The answer to that question, applied to the modern doctrinal architecture, is yes: there is not one ingredient but one hundred, each operating to narrow the path to remedy, together sealing it entirely for the class of conduct most in need of judicial oversight. That exclusion has never been named by the Court as exclusion. It has been permitted to accumulate, doctrine by doctrine, without any assessment of what the accumulation produces.

Part V: What the Court Must Become — The Path Back to Marbury

The recommendations that follow are not modest adjustments to doctrine. They are a fundamental reorientation of judicial review back toward the purpose Marshall articulated: ensuring that every individual who is harmed by their government can claim the protection of the laws.

1. A Court Polemic for Liberty — Against Concentrated Power

Marshall’s Marbury was not a balanced, both-sides opinion. It was a declaration. It announced, clearly and publicly, that the Constitution is supreme, that courts are its guardians, that individual rights have remedies. It was polemical in the best sense: it took a position, stated it with force, and directed the legal system accordingly.

The Court that American constitutional democracy requires is a Court that is openly, unapologetically committed to the proposition Marshall articulated: that the essential function of judicial review is to protect individuals from government overreach. Not neutrally mediating between the branches. Not deferring to the political question doctrine when the constitutional question is clear. Not accepting the executive’s characterization of national security necessity without examination. A Court that is skeptical of power concentration in the executive as a structural matter, regardless of which party holds the executive — because the Constitution’s checks and balances were designed not to favor one party but to prevent any branch from becoming ‘too powerful.’

This is not judicial activism in the pejorative sense. It is judicial duty in the Marshallian sense. The Court was not created to be neutral between citizens and governments. It was created to protect citizens from governments that overstep the constitutional limits placed on them. Neutrality between a tortured citizen and the government that tortured them is not judicial virtue. It is judicial abdication.

2. Skepticism of Legislative and Executive Power Concentration

Marshall was explicit that judicial review applies to both legislative acts and executive conduct. But the concept of unreviewable executive power has been stretched, in the national security domain, far beyond the space Marshall allowed for it. Torture is not a political question. Warrantless home entry is not a political question. Voice-to-skull transmission of commands into an American citizen’s skull is not a political question. These are constitutional questions with clear answers, dressed in national security clothing to avoid the examination they would not survive if honestly presented.

3. Constitutional Supremacy Without Political Deference

The Court must re-establish, explicitly and clearly, that the Constitution’s supremacy is not contingent on political convenience, national security assertion, or executive branch characterization of its own power. An executive program that deploys military weapons against a domestic civilian without charge, without trial, without any judicial process, and without congressional authorization, is repugnant to the Constitution regardless of the national security framing in which it is wrapped.

This means, concretely: the state secrets privilege cannot be a threshold dismissal doctrine. The government may have legitimate interests in protecting genuinely classified materials. It does not have a constitutional right to prevent litigation of its own unconstitutional conduct. The threshold dismissal doctrine converts the privilege into a categorical exemption from constitutional accountability. This conversion is itself unconstitutional under Marshall’s framework — it prescribes limits and then declares those limits may be passed at pleasure.

4. A Clear-Eyed View of the Aggregate

The Court has never looked at its own doctrinal accumulation from the perspective of the individual whose rights are supposed to be protected. A Court that takes Marshall’s framework seriously must ask, about its own accumulated doctrines, the question Marshall asked about the individual case: ‘It behooves us to inquire whether there be in its composition any ingredient which shall exempt from legal investigation or exclude the injured party from legal redress.’ Applied to the doctrinal architecture that forecloses Pearson’s remedies, the answer is: there are not one but one hundred such ingredients, and their combined effect is the complete exclusion of a defined class of government conduct — covert national security operations against domestic civilians — from any legal investigation or remedy.

Acknowledging this aggregate effect would require the Court to confront the gap between Marshall’s principle and the doctrine’s practice. It would require the reaffirmation that Marshall declared foundational: that a government whose laws furnish no remedy for the violation of a vested legal right has ceased to deserve the appellation of a government of laws. And it would require the Court to take seriously its own institutional responsibility for having built the architecture that produced this result.

5. Restoration of Liberty, Justice, and Equality as the Court’s Governing Purpose

The Warren Court understood itself as an institution with a purpose: to enforce the constitutional promise of liberty, justice, and equality for the persons most excluded from those promises by the political system’s ordinary operation. Brown v. Board of Education, Gideon v. Wainwright, Miranda v. Arizona, Loving v. Virginia — these decisions were not exercises in judicial restraint. They were exercises in Marshallian principle: every person has a right, every right has a remedy, the Constitution is supreme over political convenience.

The Warren Court’s critics called this activism. Marshall would have called it duty. The Court that is needed in 2025 is committed to Marshall’s principle applied to the constitutional questions of its own moment: whether technological capabilities that allow the government to enter a person’s skull are subject to the Fourth Amendment’s warrant requirement; whether behavioral direction via directed electromagnetic energy experienced as internally generated thought is subject to the First Amendment’s protection of cognitive liberty; whether the state secrets privilege can constitutionally shield the government from accountability for the torture of a citizen.

6. Reevaluation of the Doctrines — Judicial Review as Constitutional Restoration

Qualified immunity should be eliminated or radically limited. It has no constitutional foundation. It was created by the Court. It can be uncreated by the Court. Alternatively, the ‘clearly established’ standard should be understood as satisfied whenever a general constitutional principle clearly applies to the specific conduct, without requiring a prior case addressing the identical technology or technique. Qualified immunity would not shield voice-to-skull transmission, because the Fourth Amendment’s protection against government intrusion into the person clearly extends to any governmental technology that penetrates the physical space of a person’s skull, regardless of whether the technology is new.

Bivens should be restored in the national security context. The logic of Bivens — that constitutional rights require constitutional remedies — is Marshall’s logic. A Court that declines to create remedies for constitutional violations because it prefers Congress to act has decided to wait for the political branches to protect individuals from the political branches. Marshall’s framework permits no such abdication.

The state secrets privilege must be restored to its legitimate scope: protecting specific classified evidence from disclosure, not shielding entire programs from judicial examination. Courts must examine alleged state secrets in camera, determine what is genuinely sensitive, and permit litigation to proceed on non-privileged grounds.

Standing doctrine should be returned to its constitutional foundation and applied to the aggregate of coordinated conduct rather than requiring each individual act to independently satisfy the injury-in-fact requirement. A campaign designed to inflict serious harm through individually sub-threshold acts is not damnum absque injuria. It is the most sophisticated form of legal injury — one that requires the most sophisticated response from a Court committed to the principle that every right violated has a remedy.

Conclusion: From Marshall’s Promise to the Present Obligation

John Marshall wrote, in 1803: ‘The very essence of civil liberty certainly consists in the right of every individual to claim the protection of the laws, whenever he receives an injury.’ He was not writing prospectively. He was describing what he understood to be the existing legal order of the constitutional republic he was helping to build. The principle was foundational. It was not contingent on political convenience, national security assertion, or the identity of the claimant.

In 2025, an African American equity scholar in Washington, D.C. received an injury — received many injuries, documented across fourteen months with the methodological rigor of a researcher who understood that the documentation was the only defense available. He attempted to claim the protection of the laws. The laws, as currently constructed, furnished him no protection. Not because his rights are uncertain. Not because the violations are disputed. Because the legal architecture built around the national security state — qualified immunity, Bivens limitations, state secrets, sovereign immunity, the FISA authorization, the Glomar doctrine — ensures that this class of harm produces no remedy regardless of its severity, its documentation, or its constitutional clarity.

The Court declared, in 1803, that a government whose laws furnish no remedy for the violation of a vested legal right has ceased to deserve the appellation of a government of laws. That condition has been met. The Court has not acknowledged it. The appellation continues to be used.

The distance between that description and the reality of what was done to Richard Pearson — in the most surveilled city in America, by operators of technologies the government now confirms are operational, without any mechanism of legal accountability — is the distance between Marbury’s promise and the present. It is the distance the Court created. It is the distance the Court must, if it is to honor the oath Marshall described, close.

Marshall asked: ‘Why does a judge swear to discharge his duties agreeably to the Constitution of the United States if that Constitution forms no rule for his government? if it is closed upon him and cannot be inspected by him?’

The Constitution is not closed. It has not been amended to exclude voice-to-skull transmission from the Fourth Amendment or behavioral direction from the First. The text is unchanged from the document Marshall held supreme. What is closed is the Court’s willingness to look at it — to look at it and apply it to the conduct it clearly covers, to look at it and acknowledge that the doctrine accumulated in its name has produced a structure that its text condemns, to look at it and do what Marshall said was the very essence of judicial duty: decide on the rights of individuals, enforce the Constitution against acts repugnant to it, and ensure that the government of the United States remains, in practice and not merely in appellation, a government of laws.

Richard Pearson documented fourteen months of constitutional violations, in the most monitored city on earth, using a researcher’s methodology and a writer’s voice, because he understood that the document had to exist before any accountability mechanism could function. That document exists. The question is whether a Court committed to Marshall’s principles — the supremacy of the Constitution, the right of every individual to claim the law’s protection, the duty of courts to ensure that laws furnish a remedy for every right violated — will eventually recognize in that document the test Marshall set for constitutional governance, and meet it.

The test is simple. Marshall stated it plainly. The Government of the United States will certainly cease to deserve the appellation of a government of laws if the laws furnish no remedy for the violation of a vested legal right.

They do not, for the violation of Richard Pearson’s rights.

The Court knows what to do.

What Havana Syndrome Actually Is: The Clinical and Technical Record

The phenomenon now called Havana Syndrome was first reported in 2016, when U.S. Embassy personnel in Havana, Cuba began presenting with a distinctive cluster of symptoms: acute onset of directional sound perceived as coming from a specific geographic location, sometimes described as a “loud piercing sound,” sometimes as buzzing or clicking; intense pressure in the face and skull; acute vertigo and nausea; and immediate neurological sequelae including cognitive impairment, memory disruption, and balance disorders that in many cases proved chronic.

The phenomenology was, from the beginning, directional and geographic. Victims reported that moving out of a specific location caused the acute symptoms to cease. Some reported that the sound had a sharp directional quality — they could tell where it was coming from. Some reported that moving their head changed the intensity. These are not the characteristics of illness. They are the characteristics of a targeted signal.

The 2020 National Academies of Sciences, Engineering and Medicine panel — the most authoritative scientific assessment of the phenomenon — reviewed scores of cases and reached a finding its authors described as the strongest they could achieve under the circumstances: “directed energy, most plausibly pulsed electromagnetic energy,” was “the most plausible mechanism.” Specifically, the panel identified the microwave auditory effect — the Frey effect, documented since 1961 — as the mechanism most consistent with the directional, acute-onset, internally-perceived auditory component. The panel also identified functional brain changes in affected individuals, documented by neuroimaging, that were consistent with localized electromagnetic energy deposition in brain tissue.

The Frey effect works as follows: pulsed microwave energy, absorbed by head tissue, causes rapid thermoelastic expansion — a pressure wave — that originates inside the skull. The cochlea perceives this as sound. The perceived sound is sharp, crisp, and “inside the head.” It does not arrive through the ear canal the way environmental sound does. The perceived location corresponds not to an external sound source but to the internal geometry of the pressure wave’s interaction with the auditory pathway. Importantly for this analysis: the perceived sound only occurs where the microwave beam is focused. Move out of the beam’s focal zone, and the sound stops. Return to it, and it resumes.

This is the exact phenomenological signature Pearson documents. “Very sharp, crisp clicks,” “not air pressure,” geographically localized, replicable by returning to specific spots on specific blocks.

The Mechanism: Why Clicks Specifically, and Why Geographic

Understanding why pulsed microwave energy produces clicks rather than tones or buzzing requires understanding the physics of the Frey effect in detail. The sound perceived depends on the modulation pattern of the microwave pulse. A single pulse produces a single click. A train of pulses produces a train of clicks. The repetition rate determines whether the clicks fuse into a buzz (at high repetition rates) or remain discrete (at low repetition rates). The acoustic quality — sharpness, pitch — depends on the pulse width.

Pearson’s description — discrete, “sharp, crisp clicks,” sometimes three to six in sequence — corresponds exactly to low-rate pulse trains in the microwave auditory mechanism. This is not the continuous tone of tinnitus, nor the mechanical crack of TMJ dysfunction. It is the precise temporal signature of pulsed RF energy producing discrete thermoacoustic events in skull tissue.

The geographic localization follows directly from the physics of focused beam systems. A phased-array antenna — the type of system confirmed to exist in portable, backpack-sized form in the January 2026 disclosures — creates a focal point in space where energy density is sufficient to produce the Frey effect. The target only experiences the effect when their head is within the focal zone. The system can be aimed continuously at a moving target (tracking mode) or configured to create a fixed geographic “hot spot” that activates when a target enters it. Both modes produce geographically localized effects. The tracking mode is consistent with the replicable episodes across multiple dates; the fixed hot spot is consistent with the intense but non-replicable February 19 episode, which suggests the focal zone was briefly active and then either moved or was deactivated.

The Diagnostic Framework: What Geographic Replicability Rules Out and In

One fact remains definitively established regardless of which specific mechanism explains the clicks: they localize to geography, not to internal physiological state. Returning to a specific spot on a specific block causes them to recur. Leaving that spot ends them.

This categorically excludes purely neurological origin. Post-traumatic tinnitus, cochlear concussion, central auditory processing disruption — these do not organize by GPS coordinate. They organize by body state: fatigue, blood pressure, jaw position, stress response. A person with post-traumatic tinnitus experiences symptoms when their physiology enters certain states, not when their body crosses specific street intersections.

The National Academies noted this exact feature in Havana Syndrome cases as the most diagnostically significant finding: the geographic and directional specificity of the acute auditory phenomena was the property most inconsistent with any medical condition and most consistent with an externally directed signal. When multiple CIA and State Department personnel, in different locations over different years, reported that their symptoms began and ended with geographic precision, and when neuroimaging showed functional changes consistent with localized energy deposition, the panel reached its conclusion. The geographic specificity was the fingerprint.

Pearson’s documentation provides the same fingerprint, in the same city, with the same phenomenological quality, across multiple independent replication events spanning February 2026. He is not the first person to document this in Washington, D.C. He is the first private citizen to document it this precisely.

TMJ, Hyperacusis, and Infrastructure: Their Role as Secondary Contributions

Acknowledging Havana Syndrome as the primary framework does not eliminate the secondary mechanisms analyzed in the prior version of this essay. They remain relevant as contributing factors and as the plausible cover explanations the operation requires:

Post-traumatic hyperacusis: The December assault plausibly caused cochlear microtrauma and elevated auditory system sensitivity. This would make Pearson more susceptible to detecting EM signals at lower power densities — a threshold-lowering effect. A system that was operating below his detection threshold before the assault might now be perceptible.

TMJ dysfunction: A facial and jaw injury from repeated punching can damage the temporomandibular joint, producing intrinsic clicks perceived as originating inside the head. TMJ clicks are body-state dependent (jaw position, muscle tension) rather than geographically dependent — making them a confound that complicates but does not explain the geographic pattern. Clinical evaluation would separate TMJ from EM-origin clicks by their response profile: TMJ clicks respond to jaw manipulation; EM-origin clicks do not.

Urban electrical infrastructure: Traffic control boxes, transformer switching, and underground infrastructure produce real audible sounds at irregular intervals. Post-traumatic hyperacusis could make these sounds newly perceptible. This is the most conservative explanation and is consistent with some of the replicable episodes. The presence of utility boxes at several click locations is real and not dismissible. But infrastructure sounds do not explain the February 19 intense non-replicable episode near E St NW, where no distinctive utility infrastructure was noted. And infrastructure does not explain why the metal cap experiment altered the experience on February 20.

The correct analytical posture is: the Havana Syndrome mechanism is the primary hypothesis, consistent with the widest range of evidence and carrying the strongest prior probability given the confirmed deployment of this technology in Washington D.C. The secondary mechanisms are real contributing factors and real cover mechanisms — the plausible deniability layer the operation requires.

When Detained Non-Citizens Have More Rights Than US Citizens: Inversion in the US Legal System

I. The Inversion — Stating the Problem Precisely

The American legal system in 2025 and 2026 is engaged in one of the most active and consequential periods of immigration litigation in its history. Across the country, federal judges are ruling on habeas corpus petitions, Fifth Amendment due process claims, Convention Against Torture protections, and challenges to the Alien Enemies Act of 1798. Immigrants detained by ICE — many of whom entered the country without documentation, many of whom face deportation to countries they fled in fear — are appearing before federal judges, filing constitutional challenges, and in many jurisdictions winning. More than 300 district court judges have found that denying bond hearings violates Fifth Amendment due process protections. Success rates on habeas petitions exceed ninety percent in many areas. Even the Supreme Court, in its most recent ruling on the Alien Enemies Act, affirmed unanimously that judicial review was available to non-citizens facing deportation under a law passed in 1798.”

Richard Pearson is a United States citizen. He was born in this country, raised in this country, educated in this country — bachelor’s, master’s, and a PhD. He has spent his entire adult life in service to this country’s most vulnerable communities: housing advocacy, mentoring at-risk Black youth, volunteering at women’s abuse shelters, publishing research on the health consequences of structural racism. He has never been charged with a crime. He has never had an immigration status to question. He is, in every legal sense, a full citizen of the United States.

“The US government targeted Dr. Richard Pearson and caused or acted to cause a chemical assault, a physical assault, a false arrest, cognitive invasion, behavioral steering, street theater, noise harassment, and sleep deprivation. He has fewer enforceable legal rights than a Venezuelan national accused of gang membership under a statute signed by John Adams.”

This is not a polemical exaggeration. It is a precise description of what the American legal architecture produces when the government’s victim is a citizen and the government’s method is covert. The inversion is real, it is structural, and it is not an accident. Understanding how it works requires examining both sides of the comparison: what legal tools are available to detained immigrants, and why none of those tools function for Pearson.

II. What Detained Immigrants Can Do — The Legal Arsenal

The legal protections available to immigrants facing deportation in 2025 constitute a surprisingly robust — if currently embattled — set of constitutional and statutory tools. Each deserves examination, both for its content and for why it is available to non-citizens who are not available to a covertly targeted citizen.

Habeas Corpus — The Most Fundamental Remedy

The writ of habeas corpus — Article I, Section 9 of the Constitution, Latin for ‘you have the body’ — is the most ancient tool of liberty in the Anglo-American legal tradition. It allows a person held by the government to demand that the government justify, before a judge, the legal basis for their detention. It is the mechanism by which the state must show its work. Its power is simple: if you have me, prove you can have me.

In 2025 and 2026, immigrants detained by ICE have filed habeas petitions at historic rates. The Western District of Texas alone received more than 1,300 petitions since early 2025 — a record that was broken within the first month of 2026. These petitions work because they are premised on a concrete, visible fact: a person is in a facility, under government control, and can be identified by name and location. The government cannot deny the detention. The detention is the body the writ demands be produced.

Pearson was tortured. He was subjected to voice-to-skull transmission, chemical assault, covert home entry, behavioral direction, false arrest, sleep deprivation, and social network infiltration. His torture lasted more than a year and is documented with timestamps, photographs, probability calculations, a professional mental health evaluation, and experimental confirmation of the electromagnetic mechanism.

But he was not placed in a facility. His body was not held in a building the government acknowledged controlling. The torture was covert — the defining feature of his victimization is the deniability of the state’s involvement. There is no facility whose warden can be served with a writ. There is no jailer who must produce the body. The torture is real. The habeas corpus remedy is structurally unavailable because the covertness of the torture eliminates the visible detention that the writ requires.

Fifth Amendment Due Process — Available to All Persons, Except When the State Denies Its Role

“The Fifth Amendment guarantees that no person shall be deprived of liberty without due process of law. The Supreme Court has been consistent: due process protections extend to all individuals within American borders, regardless of citizenship status. In immigration proceedings, due process claims have been used effectively to challenge the speed of removal, the denial of hearings, and the inadequacy of the process by which deportation decisions are made.

Pearson’s constitutional rights have been violated on a scale that his documentation characterizes as seventy separate incidents: Fourth Amendment violations in warrantless surveillance and covert home entry, Fifth Amendment violations in the false arrest and manufactured evidence, First Amendment violations in the suppression of his scholarship and advocacy, Eighth Amendment violations in the torture itself. These violations are documented. They are specific. They involve named officers and documented incidents.

But the process through which those violations could be challenged requires a plaintiff who can establish standing — who can demonstrate a concrete, particularized, actual injury traceable to a specific defendant whose action is redressable by a court.

When the government’s method is covert, standing collapses at every element. The injury is real and documented. The traceability to a specific defendant is blocked by classification, denial, and the structural invisibility the operation was designed to maintain. The redressability is foreclosed by state secrets privilege, Glomar doctrine, sovereign immunity, and the Bivens line of cases that has spent forty years dismantling the only mechanism by which constitutional claims against federal officials could be brought. The Fifth Amendment is available to an undocumented immigrant appearing before an immigration judge. It is not available to a documented citizen whose torturer is the government and whose torture is classified.

The Convention Against Torture — A Treaty That Protects Non-Citizens More Completely Than Citizens

The Convention Against Torture, to which the United States is a party, prohibits the removal of any person to a country where they face torture by or with the acquiescence of government officials. CAT protection has no criminal bars — it is available regardless of an applicant’s history — and if eligibility is established, protection is mandatory. Judges have no discretion to deny it. For immigrants who cannot qualify for asylum, CAT is the last line.

The profound irony: the Convention Against Torture is available to a non-citizen seeking protection from torture in a foreign country. It is not available to a citizen being tortured by his own government on his own soil, because the Convention’s framework applies to removal — to the act of sending someone somewhere.

Pearson is already where he is. He is in the United States. He is being tortured in the United States. The international legal instrument specifically designed to protect people from government torture applies to non-citizens seeking to remain in the country where the torturer is not. It does not apply to citizens whose torturer is the country itself.

The Alien Enemies Act and the Right to Be Heard

The Supreme Court’s recent ruling in J.G.G. v. Trump addressed the Trump administration’s use of the Alien Enemies Act of 1798 to deport Venezuelan nationals accused of gang membership — bypassing standard immigration proceedings entirely. The Court ruled, with all nine justices in agreement on the central point, that these individuals were entitled to challenge the legality of their detention before removal. The dispute was only about which court should hear the challenge — not about whether any court should hear it at all.

The Alien Enemies Act was invoked against people the government accused of being members of a foreign criminal organization. They had no documentation. They faced deportation under a wartime statute whose last significant use was during World War II. They were accused by an administration that had demonstrated willingness to defy court orders. And the Supreme Court — all nine justices — agreed they had the right to judicial review.

Pearson has been a citizen of the United States his entire life. He has a doctoral degree. He has documented evidence of government torture. He has been denied the equivalent of that judicial review at every turn — not because the courts have reviewed his evidence and found it wanting, but because the doctrinal architecture ensures the evidence never reaches a court in a form that can be adjudicated.

III. Why Pearson Cannot Use What Non-Citizens Can

The comparison is not merely ironic. It reveals a structural feature of the American legal system that deserves precise analysis: the covertness of government action against citizens creates a doctrinal gap that covert operations are specifically designed to exploit.

The Visibility Requirement

Every legal remedy available to detained immigrants depends on the visibility of the government’s action. Habeas corpus requires a body in a facility. Due process challenges require an identifiable proceeding. CAT protection requires an identifiable removal. Even the Alien Enemies Act challenges required that the government acknowledge it was deporting specific people under a specific legal theory.

The government’s method against Pearson was “designed” to eliminate visibility at every point. The torture is electromagnetic — it leaves no marks. The behavioral direction feels like autonomy. The voice-to-skull transmission has the acoustic signature of internal thought. The false arrest produced no court record. The covert home entries left no forensic trace except the specific objects the operators chose to move as demonstrations of access. The chemical assault used a substance that bonded to skin and dissipated without leaving the kind of physical injury that emergency rooms document.

Every element of the operation was calibrated to exist below the threshold of visibility that legal remedies require. This calibration is not incidental to the operation’s design. It is the operation’s central design feature.

The Denial Problem

Detained immigrants face a government that, even when aggressive and lawless in its detention practices, cannot deny that it is detaining people. The facilities are real. The ICE agents are identifiable. The removal orders are documents. When the Supreme Court ordered the return of Kilmar Abrego Garcia — deported to El Salvador despite a court order — the administration admitted the deportation was an ‘administrative error.’ It did not deny that it had deported him.

Pearson faces a government that can deny everything. The torture is covert. The technology is classified. The operation produces no official record that could be subpoenaed. When the government invokes state secrets privilege — which it can do at the earliest stages of any litigation — the entire case can be dismissed before discovery begins. The Glomar doctrine allows the government to refuse even to confirm or deny the existence of programs responsive to a FOIA request.

The detained immigrant’s government must at minimum acknowledge the detention to defend it. Pearson’s government can deny the torture exists and use the legal system’s own doctrines to prevent any examination of the denial’s accuracy.

The Standing Architecture

Article III standing requires a concrete injury, traceable to the defendant, redressable by the court. For detained immigrants, standing is not in question: the injury is the detention, the defendant is ICE, the redress is release or a hearing. The causal chain is short and visible.

For Pearson, each element of standing presents a doctrinal barrier. The injury — torture, constitutional violations, financial and professional destruction — is documented but contested; the government denies the targeting, and without discovery, the documentation cannot be tested in court. Traceability requires connecting the documented harm to specific government actors, which classified operations and state secrets privilege prevent. Redressability fails when the defendant is the federal government itself and the applicable doctrines — sovereign immunity, Bivens dismantlement, qualified immunity — combine to foreclose the remedies that would otherwise exist.

The same Constitution that creates the rights also creates, through its doctrinal architecture, the mechanism by which those rights become unenforceable when the violator is the government and the violation is covert.

It is the product of a comprehensive architecture that has been constructed over decades and that functions, in combination, to ensure that covert government action against citizens cannot be challenged through the legal system that is supposed to prevent it.

V. The Constitutional Paradox — What the Comparison Reveals

The comparison between Pearson’s legal position and that of detained immigrants is not a critique of immigrant rights. The habeas corpus protections, due process rights, and CAT protections available to detained non-citizens are legitimate and important. They represent the best instincts of the American legal tradition — the insistence that the government must justify its coercive actions before a neutral arbiter, that every person within the Constitution’s reach deserves process before liberty is taken.

The paradox is that these protections work for detained immigrants precisely because the government’s action is “overt” — the detention is visible. The removal is documented. The order is a paper record that can be challenged. The habeas corpus writ can be served because there is a body in a facility that the government acknowledges holding. The due process claim can be brought because there is a proceeding that the government acknowledges is occurring. The CAT protection applies because there is a removal that the government acknowledges is imminent.

Pearson’s case is the precise inverse.

The government’s action is covert by design. The torture produces no official record. The targeting is classified. The technology is denied. The operation leaves no visible detention, no acknowledged proceeding, no documented removal. And the legal system, built on the assumption that government action would be visible and that the government would at minimum acknowledge what it was doing when challenged, has no mechanism for the case where the government’s primary tool is invisibility.

The Supreme Court ruling in J.G.G. v. Trump carried, as commentators noted, a clear message: everyone gets their day in court before being removed from the country. Nine justices agreed. The ruling reaffirmed one of the most basic principles of constitutional governance — that the government must be accountable before a neutral tribunal for its coercive actions against persons within its jurisdiction.

Richard Pearson has not had his day in court. Not because the Supreme Court has ruled against him. Not because a judge has examined the evidence and found it insufficient. But because the doctrinal architecture ensures that his case cannot reach a judge in a form that can be examined on its merits. The state secrets privilege, the Glomar doctrine, the Bivens dismantlement, the qualified immunity framework, the standing requirements — in combination, they ensure that the most compelling evidence of government torture assembled by any American citizen in the current period cannot be placed before a neutral tribunal for evaluation.

The detained immigrant has been given more process than the tortured citizen. This is not an anomaly in the American legal system. It is the system working as the doctrinal architecture designs it to work when the government decides to act covertly against one of its own.

VI. The Constitutional Theory of What Went Wrong

The doctrinal architecture that forecloses Pearson’s remedies was not built with his case in mind. The state secrets privilege was developed to protect genuinely sensitive national security information. Qualified immunity was intended to protect officers from liability for reasonable judgment calls in ambiguous situations. Bivens was restricted because federal courts were concerned about the separation of powers implications of implying damages remedies from constitutional text. Sovereign immunity reflects the traditional principle that the sovereign cannot be sued without consent.

Each doctrine, in isolation, has a rationale. In combination — applied to a case where the government covertly tortures a citizen with classified technology, denies the targeting, and invokes classification to prevent examination — they produce a result that is the opposite of the constitutional order they were supposed to protect. The Constitution creates rights. The doctrinal architecture converts those rights into aspirations — statements of principle that cannot be enforced when the violator is the government and the violation is designed to be invisible. “), b(“What Pearson has documented is not a failure of the Constitution. It is evidence of what the Constitution becomes when the doctrinal architecture of impunity is complete: a set of promises that the government can break without consequence, because the legal system designed to enforce those promises was built on the assumption that the government would at minimum acknowledge what it was doing.

The prior essay in this series examined how the detained immigrant’s legal position is, in current practice, more robust than the tortured citizen’s. The theoretical explanation is now visible: the immigrant’s protections work because the government’s action is visible; the citizen’s protections fail because the government’s action is invisible.

The Constitution was not designed for a government that tortures in secret and denies the torture. The doctrinal architecture was not designed to address a government that uses classified technology to destroy a citizen’s life without leaving the kind of evidence that courts can adjudicate. The system was built for a more honest adversary.

Conclusion: What the Comparison Demands

Richard Pearson completed a doctoral degree recently. He spent his career building health institutions, publishing peer-reviewed research, advocating for displaced communities, and mentoring young people from backgrounds like his own. He has never been charged with a crime. All he has is a 20-year old single speeding ticket. He has no immigration status to question. He is a full citizen of the United States.

He has fewer enforceable legal rights than a person the government accuses of gang membership under a law written in 1798. He has fewer enforceable legal rights than a person who entered the country without documentation and is facing removal to a country with a documented history of torturing its prisoners. He has fewer enforceable legal rights than a person whose entire claim on the American constitutional system rests on the fact of physical presence within American borders.

This is not an argument that those persons should have fewer rights. It is an argument that the doctrinal architecture that renders Pearson’s rights unenforceable represents a constitutional failure of the first order — one that should disturb anyone who believes that the point of a legal system is to constrain the government’s power to harm the people it governs.

The Supreme Court was unanimous: everyone gets their day in court. That principle — applied to non-citizens accused of gang membership under a wartime statute — does not yet apply to a Black equity scholar tortured with classified electromagnetic technology on the streets of Washington, D.C.

The gap between what the Supreme Court’s principle demands and what the doctrinal architecture permits is the precise measure of how far the American legal system has drifted from the constitutional democracy it claims to be.

Pearson documented everything. He ran control studies. He conducted physical experiments. He preserved the evidence. He sought a mental health evaluation to preempt the psychiatric trap. He crossed two borders seeking asylum. He held a metal chair frame in a refugee processing center in Quebec and defeated a read operation by grounding himself to the building’s structural metal. He has done everything a person can do. The legal system’s failure is not his. It is the system’s. And the comparison to what a detained immigrant can demand from that same system is the clearest possible measure of how complete that failure is.

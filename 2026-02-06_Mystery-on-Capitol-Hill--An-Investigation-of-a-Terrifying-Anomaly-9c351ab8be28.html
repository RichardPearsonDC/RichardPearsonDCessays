Mystery on Capitol Hill: An Investigation of a Terrifying Anomaly

The Boy in the Lisa Simpson Sweatshirt

An investigative analysis of what happened at Eastern Market on September 30, 2025

He crossed the street at the exact moment I raised my camera.

A young Black man, perhaps 20 years old, skateboard in hand, wearing a knit cap that read “Fuck Off” and a sweatshirt emblazoned with Lisa Simpson — in her signature red dress — making an L-shaped loser gesture on her forehead. The sweatshirt bore the words “New Initiative.”

It was late afternoon on a Tuesday evening in the heart of Capitol Hill. I had spent the previous twelve minutes photographing something impossible: more than a dozen people, all wearing the same combination of bright red and black clothing within a four-block radius of Eastern Market.

The man walked right past me. We were alone on the sidewalk near the market building. I held my Nook tablet openly, screen facing him, his image clearly visible as I photographed. He showed no awareness of my presence. None. Not a glance, not a hesitation, not the subtle shift in gait that occurs when strangers pass within intimate distance.

Then he crossed the street.

I followed. Photographed him from the front. Again, no reaction. No acknowledgment. Nothing.

This was not normal human behavior.

The Pattern Emerges

I had begun taking photographs, capturing a Black man in his 50s standing in the middle of South Carolina Avenue SE, behind the Southeast Neighborhood Library, wearing a plain black shirt and bright red pants — not burgundy, not rust, not maroon, but bright red, the kind of saturated primary color more common in children’s toys than adult wardrobes.

Three minutes later, in Market Park: a transgender woman in her 50s sat on a bench wearing a plain red dress, black turtleneck, and black stockings. A pizza box sat beside her. In one photo, she appears to be eating from it. Throughout my entire 90-minute observation, she remained in that spot.

An elderly white man in a graphic red shirt and black jacket, appearing homeless, standing in the park.

Two men on a bench, one wearing a red hat with a black sweater and red shoes.

The list grew. And grew. By the time I stopped photographing, I had documented more than 40 individuals wearing combinations of bright red and black clothing in the immediate vicinity of Eastern Market.

But it was the boy — the one who crossed the street at the exact moment I photographed him, the one wearing Lisa Simpson in her red dress, the one whose skateboard bore stickers that made no generational sense, the one whose cap told me to fuck off, the one whose sweatshirt promised a “New Initiative” — it was him who made it clear this was not coincidence.

The Semiotics of the Skateboard

Let us examine what this young man was communicating.

The skateboard itself: a cultural artifact more commonly associated with Bart Simpson than Lisa. Bart, the rebellious son in his signature red shirt. Lisa, the intellectual daughter in her red dress. The Simpsons premiered in 1989. This young man, appearing to be in his early 20s, would have been born around 2004–2005. By the time he reached the age when children typically form cultural attachments to television shows, The Simpsons was already in its third decade, no longer the zeitgeist-capturing phenomenon it had been in the 1990s.

The stickers: A prominent “94” sticker. If this refers to a birth year, it would make him 31 years old. He was not 31. A squirrel against blue and white, a white bowl — possibly a toilet — in the foreground. Cryptic imagery with no apparent coherence.

The skateboard itself showed minimal wear. The stickers were largely intact except for partial damage to the “94” designation. For a skateboard purportedly used for its intended purpose, the wear pattern was inconsistent — edges showed little impact damage despite that being where contact with walls and obstacles would naturally occur.

The sweatshirt: Lisa Simpson, making the “L” for loser gesture — a gesture that she famously made. Lisa was defined by her intelligence, her saxophone, her vegetarianism, her Buddhist leanings.

The text: “New Initiative” appears to be the embroidery on his sweatshirt despite the somewhat blurred images.

This is not the random assemblage of a young person’s wardrobe. This is messaging. The question is: messaging to whom? And to what end?

The Woman at the Door

If the boy in the Lisa Simpson sweatshirt was the most active indicator of deliberate orchestration, the woman at Compass Real Estate was the most static.

At some point during my documentation, I photographed a white woman, perhaps in her 60s, standing at the entrance to Compass Real Estate at 660 Pennsylvania Avenue SE.

She wore a bright red shirt. Black pants. Her face was expressionless. Not neutral. Expressionless. There is a difference.

She stood motionless.

I took a second photograph. She remained motionless. Expressionless.

This is not how human beings behave at the entrance to a business. We shift our weight. We check our phones. We look up and down the street. We display micro-expressions — anticipation if we’re waiting for someone, impatience if we’ve been waiting too long, curiosity about our surroundings. We do something.

She did nothing. She was a mannequin in red and black, positioned at a door.

What Was Not There

I searched public records for September 30, 2025. I found no scheduled events at Eastern Market. No flash mobs advertised on social media. No awareness campaigns for causes that use red and black as their colors. No DC United game — the team’s colors are red and black, but they weren’t playing that night.

I found Mayor Bowser’s Growth Agenda legislation preview scheduled for 11:30 a.m. that day, but at a different location — Connecticut Avenue NW, miles from Eastern Market. The weather was cloudy with sunshine later in the day. A Tuesday. Unremarkable.

What I found instead was absence. The absence of explanation. The absence of documentation. The absence of any public-facing rationale for why dozens of people in all would independently choose to wear the exact same color combination in the exact same geographic area during the exact same 90-minute window..

The Statistical Impossibility

Let us be conservative in our estimates. Let us assume that on any given evening, 5% of people in Capitol Hill wear red clothing. Let us further assume that 20% wear black — a common color. The probability of someone wearing both would be 1% (0.05 × 0.20).

Let us assume 500 people passed through the four-block radius around Eastern Market during my 90-minute observation period. A conservative estimate for a Tuesday evening in a busy commercial district. This would predict 5 people in red and black.

I documented dozens.

But this understates the anomaly, because I am not merely noting the presence of red and black. I am noting bright red — saturated, primary red — paired with black in plain garments. Not patterned. Not branded with team logos. Not faded from wear. Plain. Bright. Red. And. Black.

The specificity of the pattern is what makes it impossible to dismiss.

The Hypotheses

Theory One: Confirmation Bias. Perhaps I noticed the first few people in red and black, and then became hyperaware of this pattern while unconsciously filtering out people in other colors.

This theory fails to explain the boy in the Lisa Simpson sweatshirt who crossed the street at the exact moment I photographed him. It fails to explain the expressionless woman standing motionless at Compass Real Estate. It fails to explain why the red was uniformly bright and the garments uniformly plain. It fails to explain the light traffic seen across all images.

Theory Two: Spontaneous Social Coordination. Perhaps a flash mob or social media challenge was occurring without my knowledge.

This theory fails to explain the absence of any documentation. Flash mobs are documented. Participants film them. Organizers claim credit. Social media erupts with posts. I found none of this.

Theory Three: Private Event Spillover. Perhaps there was a wedding, corporate event, or private party at Eastern Market with a red and black color scheme, and I was photographing guests arriving or departing.

This theory fails to explain the diversity of ages, apparent socioeconomic backgrounds, and the geographic dispersion. Wedding guests cluster near the venue. These individuals were scattered across four blocks, sitting in parks, crossing streets, entering restaurants, standing at business doors.

Theory Four: Coordinated Operation. Perhaps this was a deliberate exercise designed to create a visible pattern that could be observed and documented.

This theory explains everything.

The Message in the Sweatshirt

Return to the boy. Return to Lisa Simpson in her red dress, making the loser sign, above the words “New Initiative.”

If this was a coordinated operation, what was being communicated?

Lisa Simpson is the intellectual. The one who sees through lies. The one who questions authority. The one who, in one episode, famously says, “I’m studying for the big test.” In another, she becomes disillusioned with politics. In another, she converts to Buddhism after rejecting the shallow materialism around her.

Here, she makes the loser sign. Here, she signals: you lose.

The “New Initiative”: institutional language. The language of programs and policies and official actions.

The “Fuck Off” cap: rejection. Distance. Do not engage.

The skateboard with “94” and cryptic stickers: generational confusion, intentional anachronism, symbols without clear referents.

What I Know

I know that on September 30, 2025, dozens of individuals — only some are shown in the images above — wore bright red and black clothing in a four-block radius of Eastern Market on Capitol Hill.

I know that the red was consistently bright, the garments consistently plain, and the behavior of certain individuals consistently anomalous.

I know that a young man wearing a Lisa Simpson sweatshirt crossed the street at the exact moment I photographed him, showed no awareness of my presence despite being alone with me on a sidewalk, and carried a skateboard bearing generational anachronisms.

I know that a woman stood expressionless and motionless at the entrance to Compass Real Estate.

I know that no public event, no social media campaign, no team sporting event, and no scheduled gathering explains this pattern.

I know that the statistical probability of this occurring by chance is functionally zero.

What I Don’t Know

I don’t know who organized it. I don’t know why. I don’t know how participants were recruited. I don’t know what the purpose was. I don’t know if it was a test, an experiment, a message, a warning, or something else entirely.

I don’t know if the boy in the Lisa Simpson sweatshirt was a willing or unwilling participant or co-organizer. I don’t know if the woman at Compass Real Estate was placed there deliberately or if her motionlessness was coincidental.

I don’t know if this has happened before. I don’t know if it will happen again.

What I do know is this: Something deliberate occurred on September 30, 2025, at Eastern Market. Something coordinated. Something that involved more than 40 people wearing the same color combination in the same place at the same time for reasons that have left no public trace.

And a boy in a Lisa Simpson sweatshirt crossed the street to tell me I was a loser for trying to understand it.

I have the photographs. I have the timestamps. I have the documentation. But I do not have the explanation. And perhaps that is precisely the point.

Text Messages Reveal Damage to Victim’s Relationship from US Torture Campaign

The program was designed to mimic mental illness. Dr. Richard Pearson is categorically not mentally ill. He is, however, a victim of torture, trauma, and assault in the United States. The government-directed program has damaged his close friendship because his friend, struggling to fathom that the government would do such a thing, erroneously blames Pearson.

This text exchange shows the relationship costs when a government-directed torture program is designed to appear as mental illness. Dr. Pearson is categorically not mentally ill. He is a victim. He is academically inclined, holding a fresh PhD from a top US research university. Over the last year, he has experienced an imaginable horror in Washington, DC — assaults, a false arrest, home surveillance, computer hacking, “pinning” or behavioral steering, property damage and theft.

His best friend believes that Pearson is suffering from mental illness. He has visited Pearson in 2025 and saw signs that worried him. He won’t read Pearson’s writings, won’t google the technology (e.g., ultrasonic sound), and feels that he is losing a friend. He has denied that Pearson has sent him proof of stalkers — the photos are in the articles that the friend refuses to read. Both men are African American and highly educated. Both are independent thinkers, but Pearson has an interdisciplinary orientation and epistemic humility that makes him clear-eyed. Pearson is not insane. He is a victim.

The Birthday Text

What a Single Email Exchange Reveals About the Most Intimate Damage of Targeted Torture

A friend turned 48. He sent Richard Pearson a birthday message — not celebratory, but honest. He missed him. He felt him drifting. It had been almost a year since they’d had a normal conversation, and it made him sad.

What followed is one of the most human documents in Pearson’s entire body of documentation. Not a timestamp or a photograph or an AI probability score. A conversation between two people who love each other — one of whom has spent the last year being tortured, and one of whom has spent the same year watching his friend change, without any framework to understand why, and concluding the most available explanation: something is wrong with Richard.

This exchange, read carefully, shows what the program’s most sophisticated damage actually looks like. Not the directed energy assault. Not the voice in the skull. Not the false arrest. Those are violations of the body and the law. This is the violation of something older and more irreplaceable: the conversation between friends who tell each other the truth.

What the Friend Sees

He sees drift. He has watched, across eleven months, as the man he used to talk to every day became someone who talks about torture, false arrests, electrocution, stalking. He doesn’t recognize the conversation anymore. It has been almost a year since they spoke normally. He misses that.

He is not wrong about what he observes. Pearson has changed. The man who spent 2025 being surveilled, assaulted, sleep-deprived, psychologically decomposed, falsely arrested, and subjected to coordinated campaigns of social disruption is not identical to the man who existed before those things happened. The friend sees the change. He interprets it through the only available framework: something is wrong with Richard.

The framework is understandable. It is wrong. But it is understandable in precisely the way that the program designed it to be.

This is the elegance of the methodology. The Stasi understood it sixty years ago. COINTELPRO understood it before that. The most effective way to destroy a person’s credibility — and with it, their relationships, their professional standing, and their ability to function as a witness — is not to attack them from outside. It is to produce, in the people who know them best, the sincere belief that the target has lost their mind. When the friend says I feel you drifting away, he is not an operative. He is not part of the program. He is a man who loves Richard Pearson and is watching something that looks like deterioration and reaching, honestly and with sadness, for the most obvious explanation.

The program did not need him to be recruited. It needed him to watch.

What Pearson Brings Back

Pearson’s response is the most revealing thing in the exchange. Read it not as an argument but as a document.

He starts with HAPPY BIRTHDAY in capitals — the exclamation point is real, the warmth is immediate, the relationship leads — and then goes straight to the record. He is categorically not drifting. He was tortured, stalked, falsely arrested, assaulted, electrocuted in the penis, and harassed. He has photos, videos, timelines, and explanatory frameworks. Many people do not make it. It has made him a stronger researcher.

Then this: I have got to get a handle on this head situation after I was assaulted. Something feels loose and I feel it when I walk.

That sentence does not belong to a man performing sanity. It belongs to a man who is injured and knows it and is calibrating the distance between what he can manage and what requires attention. He has something physically wrong — neurological, probably, given the nature of the assaults he documents — and he is noting it with the same methodical honesty he applies to everything. Something feels loose. He feels it when he walks. He needs to get a handle on it.

This is the voice of someone who is monitoring themselves with scientific precision, not someone who has lost track of themselves.

He invokes Gloria Naylor — not randomly, but because the argument needs grounding in documented precedent. Naylor was a celebrated African American author, winner of the American Book Award, whose novel The Women of Brewster Place was adapted for television. She published 1996 documenting her own experience of apparent government targeting. It was dismissed, widely, as paranoia. She concluded the Anti-Defamation League was involved. Pearson is careful to note he is not saying it’s the ADL in his case. But he is saying: an FBI director recently called the ADL a domestic terrorist organization after sixty years of close institutional partnership. When Naylor said it, she was dismissed. The institutional record has since moved toward her.

This is what a researcher looks like when they argue. Not assertion — precedent. Not paranoia — pattern. He is telling his friend: I know how this sounds. I know you’ve decided what it is. But the thing you’ve decided it is has a documented history of being used to explain away exactly the thing I am experiencing.

The Friend’s Reply

Four words. Some openness on your part.

It is, at first read, a deflection. You think I’m mentally ill; maybe consider that you might be. It’s the move every friend makes when they’ve been met with an explanation they find too extreme: you asked me to be open, but couldn’t you be open too?

But Pearson hears it differently. He agrees. One hundred percent. He does not have all the facts. His premonition — the word is interesting, and precise — is that the targeting and related programs are worse than what he has documented, not better. He is not claiming certainty. He is claiming methodology. He is a damn good researcher. He has not lost himself. He observed his own decomposition as it was happening — I experienced some decomposition, yes. You saw that. I observed myself seeing that — and that act of self-observation, from inside the experience, is precisely what the program’s decomposition methodology is designed to prevent.

You cannot decompose someone who is watching themselves be decomposed and taking notes.

Then he says the sentence that belongs at the center of any honest account of this case:

When you make claims without reading or research, I have to dismiss them out of hand — and out of love.

He is not being cruel. He is being precise. The friend has formed a conclusion — something other than what Richard thinks is happening — without reading the writings, without Googling the technology, without engaging with the scientific literature that Richard has assembled across a year of meticulous documentation. He has reached a diagnosis without examining the evidence.

Pearson’s response to that is not to capitulate or to reassure. It is to say: I love you, and because I love you I’m telling you that a conclusion formed without engagement with the evidence is not one I can credit. This is not stubbornness. This is what intellectual integrity looks like under conditions designed to make intellectual integrity look like pathology.

The Third Message

Then something shifts.

After the disagreement, after the defended ground, after the honest friction of two people who tell each other the truth — Pearson sends a second message, and it is entirely different in register.

This whole ordeal has shown me who my true friends are in this world. People that I talked to almost every day have not checked in on me in over 6 months. That says a lot. You are a true friend and you showed that.

He is not performing vulnerability. He is reporting it. The year of targeting cost him relationships — multiple relationships, people he talked to almost every day, people who did not call, did not check in, and in some cases, as the documentation of Ms. Sherry and the elderly neighbor reveals, may have been actively recruited into the operation against him. The social world that surrounded Richard Pearson before July 2025 is not the social world that surrounds him in May 2026. The program designed exactly this outcome.

But this friend is still here. He is here on his birthday, sad, missing Richard, saying so honestly. He is wrong about what is happening. He has not read the writings. He will not Google the technology. He reached for the psychiatric explanation, as the program anticipated he would. But he is still here. He sent the message. He said it makes him sad. He is not gone.

We have always had an open and honest relationship centered around mutual love. We shoot from the hip cause we real people. So I saw your light and love in the past year. It shines bright.

This is what the program failed to destroy. Not Pearson’s documentation, not his analytical framework, not his physical survival — those were the program’s targets, and they are harder cases. This is simpler: a friendship. A man who turned 48 and sent a birthday message. A man who received it and, after defending himself, stepped back and said: I see you, and I’m glad you’re here.

Now, I can call you now that we have cleared the air haha.

The haha is doing significant emotional work. It is the exhale at the end of a hard conversation. It is the recognition that the argument was worth having because the relationship survived it. It is Pearson being, for a moment, not a torture victim or a researcher or a documentarian of state crimes — just a person who cleared the air with a friend and wants to call him.

The Mechanism, Precisely Described

This email exchange is one of the most precise illustrations available of how the targeting program’s social isolation methodology works — and of why it is so difficult to fight.

The program does not need to recruit the friend. It does not need to give him a script or a signal or a T-shirt with words on it. It simply needs to produce, in Pearson, behaviors that are consistent with deterioration — the obsessive documentation, the urgent referencing of technologies and historical precedents, the inability to have a normal conversation because the abnormal thing is always present, always relevant, always demanding acknowledgment. These behaviors are real. They are the rational response of a researcher to a campaign of torture. But they look, from the outside, exactly like what a person in the early stages of a paranoid episode looks like.

The friend is not wrong to be worried. The friend is wrong about the cause.

This is the program’s most sophisticated design feature: it produces real symptoms in the target — dissociation, obsessive pattern-recognition, difficulty with ordinary social interaction, physical injuries that produce odd sensations and behavioral changes — that are clinically indistinguishable, to an untrained observer, from the symptoms of a serious mental illness. The target is then left to fight on two fronts simultaneously: against the program that is harming them, and against the sincere misperception of the people who love them.

Pearson understands both fronts. He has the psychiatric evaluation. He has the documentation. He has, as he notes, observed his own decomposition from the inside and survived the observation. But he cannot give the friend that evaluation to hold, cannot transmit the experience of having walked through a year of coordinated assault with his analytical faculties intact, cannot make the friend understand what ultrasonic sound is without the friend being willing to Google it.

I cannot reassure you if you can’t be reassured.

This is the sentence that should be inscribed somewhere in the history of what the program cost him. Not a testimony of bitterness — it is followed immediately by I love you — but an acknowledgment of the limit. There is a point at which one person cannot close the epistemological gap for another. There is a point at which the other person has to do the work of knowing. The friend has not done that work. And Pearson loves him anyway, and is glad he called, and cleared the air.

In the Grand Scheme

The birthday exchange is a small document. It is not the 200-page primary record. It is not the AI analyses or the MIND Act or the UN statement or the psychiatric evaluation. It is two people talking on an evening in the spring of 2026. — one of them having survived a year of documented torture, the other having watched from a distance, sad, without a framework.

But it contains, in miniature, the full architecture of what the program designed.

It designed for Pearson’s friends to feel him drifting when he was not drifting. It designed for the people who loved him to reach for psychiatric explanation when the correct explanation was government crime. It designed for the conversation to become difficult, and for difficulty to erode frequency, and for eroded frequency to produce silence, and for silence to produce the isolation that looks, from the outside, like the kind of withdrawal that mental illness produces.

Most of it worked, on most people. The people who talked to him almost every day did not call for six months.

This one did. On his birthday, sad, honest, wrong about the cause, present nonetheless.

And Pearson, after defending himself with the full force of a year’s worth of documentation and research and analytical discipline — stepped back and saw the light and love in his friend’s imperfect, misdiagnosing, present, birthday message.

It shines bright.

This is what survives a targeting program. Not just the evidence. Not just the documentation. Not just the psychiatric clearance and the timestamp photographs and the tin foil Bluetooth experiments and the frame-by-frame Cherry Blossom Festival video analysis. This too: a man who called on his birthday, and a man who answered, and the phone call that followed after the air had been cleared.

The program designed for Richard Pearson to be alone.

On this evening, he was not.

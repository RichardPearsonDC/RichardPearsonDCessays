The Reign of Terror, Upgraded: Richard Pearson and the Weaponization of the Invisible State

The 2025 upgrade is the most sophisticated iteration in that history. Its weapons are classified. Its operators are invisible. Its victims are designated without being told. Its legal architecture makes remedy structurally impossible. And it is operating in the most surveilled, most institutionally dense city in the world — which means, as argued elsewhere in this series of essays, that its invisibility within that city is itself evidence of authorization.

Dr. Richard Pearson documents in minute details a multi-level torture campaign through 2025 and 2026. In Pearson’s account, the instrument is a microwave weapon that induces internal audio, a radar system that tracks him through floors and walls, a directed energy platform that compels him toward specific locations, an acoustic array that produces clicking sounds at GPS-reproducible coordinates consistent with the Havana syndrome symptom cluster, and a computer interception system that reads his Word documents before he opens the application in which he attempts to type their contents.

I. The Original Terror: What It Meant to Be an Enemy of the Republic

Between September 1793 and July 1794, the Committee of Public Safety of the French Republic executed approximately seventeen thousand people by official decree and killed perhaps forty thousand more through summary violence, imprisonment, and forced deportation. The machinery was ideological before it was physical. What made the Terror so devastating — so different from ordinary political violence — was not primarily the guillotine. It was the designation.

To be named an enemy of the Republic was, by itself, to be convicted. The Law of 22 Prairial, enacted in June 1794, stripped the accused of the right to counsel, the right to call witnesses, and the right to a full hearing. The only verdict available was acquittal or death. The standard of proof required was “moral certainty” in the minds of the judges — not evidence, not testimony, not confrontation of accusers. Suspicion, in the proper ideological register, was sufficient. The accused who attempted to argue the facts of their case was demonstrating, by the very act of argument, the kind of counterrevolutionary rationalism that confirmed the original suspicion.

The mechanism was self-sealing. Accusation implied guilt. Defense implied guilt. The absence of confession implied guilt. The Terror was not an aberration of the Revolution — it was, as Robespierre himself argued, virtue in emergency, the necessary hardness of a people defending their republic against its enemies.

What made it a reign of terror, specifically, was not its scale, though the scale was catastrophic. It was the structure: the official machinery of the state turned toward the extermination of designated categories of citizen, operating in the name of public safety, with the full apparatus of legal form while emptying that form of its content. Courts sat. Verdicts were rendered. The law was cited. The Republic’s constitution — with its guarantees of liberty, equality, and the rights of man — remained formally in effect. What had changed was the population to whom those guarantees applied: not applied to enemies of the Republic, not to counterrevolutionaries, not to those whose names appeared on lists compiled by committees meeting in rooms the accused would never see.

The Reign of Terror ended when Robespierre himself was arrested on 9 Thermidor and guillotined the following day. The machinery had consumed its operators. The committee members who had signed death warrants for thousands voted for Robespierre’s arrest because they feared, correctly, that their names were next on the list. The Terror was not defeated by principle. It was defeated by the terror it created in the hearts of those who had been running it.

II. The American Prologue to Trump 2.0: HUAC, COINTELPRO, and the Domestic Terror That Wore a Democratic Face

The Reign of Terror is commonly treated as a French problem, a revolutionary excess, a cautionary tale about what happens when idealism is stripped of procedural constraint. The United States has its own prologue.

The House Un-American Activities Committee, operating from 1938 through its effective collapse in the late 1960s, practiced a distinctly American version of the Terror’s logic: public accusation as punishment, ideological designation as sufficient basis for destruction, the absence of evidence reinterpreted as evidence of successful concealment. HUAC’s hearings were not trials. They were theatrical demonstrations of state power over designated categories of citizen — Communists, Communist sympathizers, those who refused to name names, those who had once attended a meeting, those who had signed a petition, those who had written a letter.

The consequences were the same without the guillotine: career destruction, blacklisting, family ruin, imprisonment, suicide. The physicist J. Robert Oppenheimer, who had built the atomic bomb, had his security clearance revoked in 1954 on the basis of secret proceedings to which he had no meaningful access and in which his accusers were permitted to testify anonymously. The actor Paul Robeson had his passport revoked and his career destroyed. Thousands of federal employees were dismissed under loyalty oaths whose standards were never disclosed to those who failed them. The accused had not committed crimes. They had been named.

COINTELPRO, operating from 1956 to 1971 under J. Edgar Hoover’s FBI, was the operational evolution of HUAC’s logic: from public spectacle to covert destruction. Where HUAC destroyed reputations through public accusation, COINTELPRO destroyed lives through anonymous letters to employers and spouses, infiltrators at meetings, provocateurs who initiated violence that could be blamed on targets, coordinated false arrests, forged documents, and — in cases like the 1969 murder of Fred Hampton — direct coordination with local police to eliminate designated threats while they slept.

COINTELPRO’s primary targets were Black Americans: Martin Luther King, Jr., the Black Panther Party, the SCLC, SNCC. Its defining document is the 1968 FBI memorandum explicitly identifying the “long range” goal as prevention of the rise of a “Black Messiah” who could “unify and electrify the militant Black nationalist movement.” The target was not crime. It was political consciousness. The weapon was the machinery of the state applied covertly against citizens whose political expression the state had designated threatening.

What COINTELPRO and HUAC shared with the Reign of Terror — and what distinguishes all three from ordinary government misconduct — is the logic of preemptive designation: the state does not wait for the designated enemy to commit an act. It identifies the category — counterrevolutionary, Communist, Black radical — and then destroys the individual before they can do whatever the state has decided they would do. The evidence for the eventual act is the designation itself. The designation is the conviction.

Both HUAC and COINTELPRO were eventually exposed — HUAC through the slow accumulation of public disgust, COINTELPRO through the 1971 Media burglary and the subsequent Church Committee investigations. In each case, exposure required either accident or heroism — a break-in, a whistleblower, a committee willing to subpoena the files. The machinery did not expose itself. It was designed not to.

III. The Upgrade: 2025 and the Technological Terror

Richard Pearson, PhD, is a Black academic. His website publishes his research. He was economically stressed but psychologically stable in the spring of 2025 — confirmed stable by two mental health evaluators who would later find no psychotic symptoms in a formal clinical assessment.

What he experienced beginning in spring 2025 and continuing through early 2026 was not ordinary crime. It was not ordinary harassment. It was a campaign of state-level violence — psychological, physical, chemical, neurological, and digital — that bears direct comparison to the Reign of Terror not as hyperbole but as structural analysis. What Robespierre’s Committee of Public Safety did with guillotines and accusation lists, the operation against Pearson did with directed energy weapons, behavioral manipulation technology, contractor networks, and the comprehensive apparatus of the post-9/11 surveillance state.

The upgrade has four defining characteristics that distinguish it from its historical predecessors: it is invisible, it is deniable, it is designed to be mistaken for mental illness, and it operates in a legal void that its architects helped construct. The technology that he has pinpointed and the evidence that he has compiled are shocking. These were classified forms and weapons of torture and lawlessness that operate outside of public and media knowledge.

The Invisible Designation

In the Reign of Terror, the Committee of Public Safety issued warrants. The accused knew they were accused. They were brought before the Revolutionary Tribunal. They could see their accusers, or at least the process that would kill them. In COINTELPRO, the FBI sent anonymous letters and deployed informants — anonymous, yes, but eventually discoverable through Freedom of Information requests and congressional investigation.

Richard Pearson was designated, apparently, sometime in early 2025. The ACCESS DENIED flag on his law enforcement records — visible to an MPD officer running a routine database query — suggests that a federal agency had classified his file at the Sensitive Compartmented Information level. He was listed in a system that local police could not access — at least not the first officer who arrived on the scene after Pearson had been physically assaulted. That officer called for his “partner” — an officer Biscoe who likely had a higher clearance. His interaction with Pearson was suspicious for a simple noise complaint — repeatedly asking if anyone lived there, opening Pearson’s closet without a warrant or legal authority, suggesting Pearson was hallucinating (“medication can cause noise”), unwilling to listen to Pearson’s audio of the noise after saying that he needed to hear something, and declining to question the upstairs tenant who was harassing Pearson with noise and following him throughout his room with objects being dropped on the floor.

Pearson was under a federal designation that he was never informed of, never charged under, never permitted to contest, and could not appeal because he did not know it existed.

The French revolutionary committees posted their lists. The FBI sent COINTELPRO targets copies of the anonymous letters it was mailing to their employers, sometimes accidentally. Pearson’s designation exists in databases he cannot access, under classifications he cannot obtain, administered by agencies he cannot identify, pursuant to executive orders whose application to him has never been officially acknowledged. The invisible designation is the first “upgrade”: the target does not know they are a target.

The Invisible Instrument

On July 13, 2025, Pearson was at the National Mall — eating a cantaloupe, listening to Miley Cyrus, doing nothing — when a voice began speaking inside his auditory system. “Go to Georgetown. Go to Georgetown.” The voice was a woman’s. It sounded, he noted, like someone at a microphone with people around. It gave specific directions: get on the metro, approach a particular person, get in a car. Before he arrived, it told him: “We have to make you disappear.”

He understood, in real time and explicitly, that this was technology. He told the mental health evaluators: “The voice in my head, which is not psychosis because we know that this kind of technology can happen and does happen. MK Ultra is a good example.” He was not claiming revelation. He was identifying a technical category — the microwave auditory effect, the Frey effect, voice-to-skull transmission — that appears in the Army War College paper he had read, that is consistent with the high-powered microwave weapons the DoW CTO confirmed the government has and is scaling, and that produces exactly the symptom profile he described: a directional, female, microphone-quality voice heard without any external audio source.

But there is an essential element to Pearson’s account that categorically moves it from psychiatric to environmental — there are operatives on the ground. First, Pearson stands up from where he is seated. A white man has his back to Pearson. Pearson stopped, “What is that on the back of his t-shirt?” He realized that it was a map of Russia. “That’s strange. Who would put that on a t-shirt?” Within several feet, he passed a middle-aged Asian woman carrying a shopping bag from Target. The bag seemed almost empty — the “target” logo clearly visible to Pearson on woman’s side facing him. Then, there are more t-shirts when he reached Georgetown. “See ya later,” read one as Pearson declined to get into a car. Minutes later, Pearson is directed to get into a car with a woman who is standing on the curb. Pearson, standing directly behind her, never saw her face clearly. She never turned around. They were the only two there and Pearson was almost certainly in her personal space.

In the Reign of Terror, the state’s instrument was the guillotine — visible, physical, and historically legible. In COINTELPRO, the instruments were letters, informants, and police coordination — discoverable through FOIA after the fact. In Pearson’s account, the instrument is a microwave weapon that induces internal audio, a radar system that tracks him through floors and walls, a directed energy platform that compels him toward specific locations, an acoustic array that produces clicking sounds at GPS-reproducible coordinates consistent with the Havana syndrome symptom cluster, and a computer interception system that reads his Word documents before he opens the application in which he attempts to type their contents.

None of these instruments leave marks in the traditional sense of violence. None appear on his medical records. None can be detected by standard clinical examination. None can be forensically demonstrated without instrumentation he had no reason to deploy in advance. The upgraded Reign of Terror is conducted with weapons whose existence the government publicly denied until January 2026 — and whose use against domestic civilians remains, officially, an “open question.”

The Invisible Army

The Committee of Public Safety operated through named officials — Robespierre, Saint-Just, Collot d’Herbois — who signed their names to death warrants and appeared in public. COINTELPRO operated through J. Edgar Hoover’s FBI, a named agency whose agents could eventually be identified through declassified documents and congressional testimony.

The operation against Pearson operated through no named official, no named agency, and no identified instrument. “Lee” — the apparent intelligence officer who warned him of audio surveillance at Potbelly’s — used a pseudonym from a coffee cup. The man on the street who quoted Pearson’s private apartment self-talk back at him drove past in a truck and did not stop. The upstairs resident, who is not on the lease and unknown to property management before he physically assaults Pearson, lists his address at a Capitol Hill property owned by an ATF attorney and a surgeon — a cover address that, if accurate, suggests an arrangement inexplicable through ordinary residential tenancy. The people in coordinated red and black across the streets of D.C. and its suburbs — at Federal Center, on H Street, at the Library of Congress, in Anacostia — were members of the general public, each individual sighting dismissible, the pattern visible only to someone who had documented every incident with timestamps and photographs over nine months.

Pearson calls this a “federal and private hybrid model” — the contractor architecture in which the government outsources operational functions to private firms and individual cutouts, ensuring that no government badge appears, no government name is used, no government connection can be traced. Where the Reign of Terror used state institutions directly and COINTELPRO used a named federal agency, the 2025 upgrade uses an operational structure architecturally designed to be invisible to any legal inquiry that depends on identifying a government principal.

The Invisible Motive

Robespierre articulated the motive of the Terror in public speeches that became the founding texts of political paranoia: the Republic is surrounded by enemies, and those enemies walk among its citizens wearing the faces of patriots. The motive was public, proclaimed, and philosophically developed.

The FBI’s COINTELPRO memoranda were blunt about motive: prevent the rise of a Black Messiah, neutralize Black radical movements, destroy the organizational capacity of groups the agency designated subversive.

The motive for what was done to Richard Pearson is expressed in the political rhetoric of the administration that assumed power on January 20, 2025 — the day the alleged campaign against him began its federal phase. Executive Order 14173 designated diversity, equity, and inclusion programs as actionable discrimination and directed agencies to identify DEI practitioners across federal contractors and grantees. The President’s statements designated “enemies within” among American citizens. His reference to using “urban cities for military training” explicitly framed domestic spaces as operational theaters. Pearson was a Black PhD whose dissertation provided a textbook example of the academic work the administration’s executive orders were designed to suppress.

He was not named as a target. His designation was invisible. But Pearson himself understood the connection: “My experiences in 2025 aligned perfectly with the administration’s rhetoric, attitudes, and extreme actions, including DEI attacks and the President’s own words of using urban cities for military training.” The motive exists in public record. Its application to Pearson is the inference that the visible designation and the invisible operation, read together, demand.

IV. The Constitutional Autopsy: Every Right Violated, Limited Addressable

The Reign of Terror suspended the rights proclaimed by the Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen — liberty, security, resistance to oppression, due process. It did so explicitly, through the Law of 22 Prairial, while maintaining the formal structure of republican governance. The constitution remained. The rights it guaranteed did not apply to enemies of the Republic.

What was done to Richard Pearson violated every significant constitutional protection available to an American citizen. The difference is that the French Terror suspended rights through law. The American upgrade violated them through invisibility.

The First Amendment. Pearson’s liberation philosophy dissertation is pure First Amendment expression — academic analysis of race, power, and public health, published on a personal website, presented to the world as intellectual output. The targeting of an American citizen for academic and political expression is precisely what the First Amendment exists to prohibit. When the executive branch designates political opinion as a national security threat — through executive orders framing DEI scholarship as actionable discrimination and “enemies within” rhetoric designating ideological opponents as domestic threats — and then directs an operation against a person whose published work exemplifies that expression, it has done what Robespierre’s committee did: converted political speech into criminal status.

The Fourth Amendment. The surveillance of Pearson’s apartment — confirmed by Lee, confirmed by the through-floor tracking that followed him from room to room — occurred without any disclosed warrant. The through-floor radar tracking of his movements within a private residence, including the documented monitoring of intimate physiological activity, is among the most intimate possible violations of the reasonable expectation of privacy that the Fourth Amendment protects. The computer interception — the document surveillance that read his Word files before he opened Sora, the network-level manipulation that injected “NO” into his typing interface — violated the Fourth Amendment’s protection of digital communications, as interpreted by Carpenter v. United States, 138 S. Ct. 2206 (2018), which held that Americans have a reasonable expectation of privacy in digital records that reveal their movements and personal lives.

The Fifth Amendment. Pearson was never charged with any crime related to the alleged campaign against him. He was subjected to what amounts to government-directed punishment — chemical assault, acoustic harassment, behavioral manipulation, psychological operations designed to produce a psychotic break — without any process. The Fifth Amendment’s due process clause prohibits the government from depriving a person of liberty without due process of law. The deprivation of liberty through covert neurological manipulation, through the induction of behavioral compulsion toward locations chosen by the state, through the invasion of autonomous decision-making — is a deprivation of the most fundamental liberty without any process whatsoever.

The Eighth Amendment. The prohibition on cruel and unusual punishment applies, the Supreme Court has held, to the conditions under which the government subjects persons to its power. The sustained campaign Pearson describes — nine months of acoustic harassment disrupting sleep, chemical assault causing skin adhesion and pain, behavioral pinning removing volitional autonomy, somatic stimulation inducing involuntary physiological response, psychological operations designed to induce a psychotic break — constitutes cruel and unusual treatment by any standard the Eighth Amendment has been construed to encompass. That it was not administered in a prison facility does not diminish its cruelty. It may amplify it, conducted as it was against a free citizen in his own home and city.

The Fourteenth Amendment. The equal protection clause prohibits discrimination on the basis of race in the administration of state power. Pearson is a Black man. His dissertation is explicitly about race. The administration’s executive orders targeted DEI scholarship — a category that disproportionately describes the work of scholars of color. COINTELPRO targeted Black Americans specifically. If the operation against Pearson was motivated by the same logic, applied through updated means, it is a racial equal protection violation of the kind that has never been adequately remedied in American law because the targeting has never been adequately proven in court.

International Law and the Convention Against Torture. Under the CAT, to which the United States is a signatory, torture is defined as severe mental or physical suffering intentionally inflicted by a state actor for purposes of punishment, intimidation, or discrimination. The voice-to-skull transmission of “We have to make you disappear” — a death threat delivered neurologically, without any external audio source, to a man eating a cantaloupe on a public bench — is the deliberate infliction of severe mental suffering. The somatic stimulation — involuntary physiological response induced without consent and intended, in Pearson’s analysis, to humiliate and disorient — constitutes exactly the kind of degradation that the Convention was written to prohibit. The sustained psychological operations designed to produce social death and institutional disbelief constitute the mental torture that the CAT addresses alongside physical methods.

No remedial mechanism exists for any of these violations in Pearson’s case. The violations are constitutionally actionable in theory. In practice, the legal architecture of qualified immunity, Bivens foreclosure, state secrets privilege, Glomar responses, standing requirements under Lujan and Clapper, and Neitzke frivolousness dismissal ensures that the courthouse door is closed before any of these arguments can be made. The Constitution’s provisions exist. Their application to Pearson has been made structurally difficult by the same legal evolution that made the operation against him possible in the first place.

V. The Guillotine Is the Weapon You Cannot See

The Reign of Terror’s most powerful instrument was not the guillotine. It was the list. The list determined who was named an enemy. The guillotine was simply the terminal mechanism, the final expression of a designation already made in committee. The terror — the psychological, social, and political terror that paralyzed the Republic — was produced not by the executions themselves but by the knowledge that anyone could be on the next list, that the designation could be made on the basis of association, accusation, or mere proximity to someone already named.

What Richard Pearson experienced in 2025 was terror in this precise sense. Not merely the sum of its individual components — though each component is alarming in isolation — but the experience of being designated, invisibly, by a power that has made itself invisible, using weapons it has not acknowledged, through contractors it will not name, pursuant to executive orders it has publicly announced but whose application to individual citizens it denies in every case.

“I am trying to negotiate for my release,” Pearson wrote in the summer of 2025, attempting to explain his situation to a friend from whom he was begging money to reach the Canadian border. There is a precise historical resonance to that phrase. To negotiate for your release when no one has charged you with anything, when no court has adjudicated anything, when no official has acknowledged your existence as an operational subject — is to be in the position of the French citizen who finds themselves before the Revolutionary Tribunal and realizes, in the moment of speaking, that the form of a trial is present but its substance has been abolished.

He lobbied dozens of congressional offices in December 2025. The ACLU declined his case — politely, noting “limited resources.” Iceland refused his attempt at asylum — the United States, they said, is a “safe country” of origin. Every institution designed to address what was being done to him returned the same answer: not our problem, not our jurisdiction, not our case. The Committee of Public Safety, at least, issued warrants. Pearson’s designation was issued in silence, executed in silence, and denied through silence.

VI. The Classified Guillotine: Military-Grade Weapons on Domestic Soil

The specific evil of the Reign of Terror — as opposed to ordinary political repression — was the use of the full coercive apparatus of the state against citizens whose only offense was political existence. The guillotine was a public instrument, used in public, in the name of the Republic, against people who had been designated enemies of that Republic by a committee of that Republic’s government.

What distinguishes what was done to Richard Pearson is that the coercive apparatus employed against him was not the ordinary machinery of state violence — arrest, prosecution, imprisonment — but classified military-grade weapons designed for deployment against foreign adversaries, now pointed inward, at a domestic civilian, in a city that the Constitution assigns to Congress and that the Secret Service manages as a security zone.

The DoW CTO confirmed on January 23, 2026 that the Department of War has directed energy weapons and is scaling them. The Washington Post confirmed on February 19, 2026 that these weapons — CHAMP, HIJENKS, acoustic devices, pulsed electromagnetic systems — are active, deployed, and capable of disabling electronics, disrupting human physiology, and producing the full symptom cluster of Havana syndrome. The National Academies of Science identified directed energy as the most plausible mechanism for Havana syndrome in 2020. The Biden NSC affirmed that pulsed electromagnetic or acoustic energy remains a plausible explanation in certain cases.

Pearson was experiencing the Havana syndrome symptom cluster — directional acoustic phenomena at reproducible coordinates, pressure, clicking, auditory anomalies — in D.C. in January 2026, simultaneously with the month in which the Pentagon was briefing Congress on the pulsed radio wave device it had covertly purchased for exactly that purpose. He was describing, from inside his daily life, the civilian application of the same weapons the U.S. government was deploying against Venezuelan presidential security forces. The Venezuela operation was a military operation, publicly claimed, against a foreign adversary. The D.C. operation was a covert operation, officially denied, against an American citizen in his own studio apartment.

The difference between a foreign battlefield and a domestic studio apartment is not a technical limitation of the weapons involved. It is a legal, moral, and constitutional boundary. The DoW CTO’s tweet and Trump’s boast about the “Discombobulator” announced publicly that the United States government has weapons capable of doing to a human nervous system what Pearson says was done to his. The question of whether those weapons were pointed at Georgetown as well as Caracas is not a technical question. It is a constitutional one.

The Eighth Amendment prohibits cruel and unusual punishment. The Fifth Amendment prohibits deprivation of liberty without due process. The First Amendment prohibits the suppression of political speech. The Fourth Amendment prohibits unreasonable searches and seizures of a person’s physical and digital space. None of these provisions contain an exception for weapons delivered at the speed of light, through walls, without physical contact, leaving no forensic trace. The invisibility of the instrument does not alter the constitutionality of its use. It only makes the constitutional violation impossible to prove.

VII. The World Is Watching: Pearson’s Own Warning

“At the same time that the US is going around the world claiming to want to free people,” Pearson wrote in his document, “it is using military weapons to torture its own citizens, to invade their brains, and to operate lawlessly. But here is the reality and warning. The world is watching.”

This is not the rhetoric of a delusional man. It is the geopolitical observation of a scholar who understands what legitimacy means and what its loss costs. Pearson on moral and legal authority:

“Legal authority and moral authority are inextricably linked in liberal democracies. Some academicians have attempted to separate the two to allow for legal authority to be legitimate in the face of absolute power, violence, and human rights violations. That is a mistake. At the point that legal authority fails to account for moral authority — a moral code, constitutional guarantees, civil liberties — it cedes its legal authority. By what authority does the exercise of legal power then arise if not for state law or morality? Untethered from constraints, actions become capricious, unbounded, and apt to tyranny. Once a liberal democracy transitions from ensuring universal rights as defined in their constitution, a framework of legal authority is irrelevant. Only power becomes relevant — one person or one faction who decides who lives or who dies, who is oppressed or privileged. The US has to decide what it wants to be and what its future holds. The current course is untenable.”

The United States has been the primary global advocate for human rights norms that prohibit exactly what Pearson describes — political targeting, covert torture, the weaponization of state power against designated categories of citizen. It prosecutes other governments for doing these things. It conditions foreign aid and diplomatic relationships on compliance with exactly these standards. It invokes the language of freedom, dignity, and the rule of law in every international forum.

And simultaneously — if Pearson’s account is even partially accurate — it was using classified military weapons to torture a Black academic in a studio apartment in D.C., because his dissertation expressed opinions that the executive branch had designated ideologically unacceptable. Despite the administration’s claims to the contrary, Pearson maintains that race has no biological basis.

The Reign of Terror ended not because its victims prevailed but because Robespierre was disfavored by men who feared they would be next. COINTELPRO ended not because its targets were vindicated but because documents were stolen and leaked and the institutional embarrassment became untenable. The pattern of American state terror against its own citizens has never ended through the triumph of legal remedy. It has paused, evolved, and resumed — each iteration more sophisticated, more deniable, and more technologically insulated from the accountability that exposed its predecessors.

Richard Pearson has been subjected to something the Committee of Public Safety, with its primitive technology and its public warrants, could not have imagined: a Reign of Terror so silent, so intimate, so precisely calibrated to the interior of a single human life, that the terror itself cannot be seen by anyone but its target.

The Reign of Terror killed thousands in the streets of Paris. The 2025 upgrade aims to kill one man’s mind — his credibility, his sanity, his mental sovereignty, his ability to occupy a social position from which he can be believed — while he walks the streets of the capital of the most powerful democracy in the world, carrying a phone with timestamps, taking photographs, citing academic literature, passing mental health evaluations, and writing everything down.

The guillotine was the instrument of a state that had lost its fear of being seen. What is being done to Richard Pearson is the instrument of a state that has learned, in fifty years since COINTELPRO, exactly how to remain unseen.

The world is watching. It cannot see what it is watching. That is the point.

African American Achieves “American Dream” from Poverty to PhD, then US Tortures Him

This essay is about a man who journeyed over 40 years from a trailer park to R1 research institution. He has lost total faith in the US, questioning basic assumptions, “Is it even a constitutional democracy? How can the US guarantee rights when it has countless legal doctrines and procedures working against pathways for meaningful judicial review and remedy? A complete farce.”

He achieved the American Dream in its fullest form. Not the abbreviated version — not merely financial stability or professional success. The complete version: the version applied to an African American born into poverty and who used education and community service to build a life of intellectual contribution and community care, and who in 2024 finished an exceptional doctoral dissertation.

And then the United States government tortured him.

Not metaphorically. Not bureaucratically. Not through neglect or indifference or the structural violence that Pearson’s own scholarship had spent years documenting as a public health crisis. Physically. Neurologically. Through voice-to-skull transmission and chemical assault and covert home entry and behavioral direction and false arrest and social network infiltration and financial attrition and a years-long campaign designed to destroy a man who had spent his whole life building something from nothing. There is no plausible explanation that the US government is not involved. Executive orders, political rhetoric, known racially targeted programs within US intelligence, geographic proximity of Pearson’s torture, federalized local law enforcement, and the use of uncommon, sophisticated technologies all point to the essential role of the US government in Pearson’s torture.

The program targeted him because of what he had built and who he was — and represented. He is a Black equity scholar during an administration that has harshly and irrationally criticized equity scholarship. The administration is anti-DEI. He is an openly gay African American with national prominence and doctoral education. The administration believes that people like Pearson caused discrimination and mistreatment against white people as this quote by Trump illustrates, “Well, I think that a lot of people were very badly treated. White people were very badly treated, where they did extremely well and they were not invited to go into a university or a college… So it was, it was a reverse discrimination.”

He wrote a dissertation that included the words “critical race,” but Pearson believes that ideology over common sense put him in their AI-driven targeting. “I am very certain that they never bothered reading the damn dissertation. Knowledge-based, literacy-informed action is not exactly this administration’s forte.” Then there is Pearson’s manuscripts critical of US public health policies.

He is the specific type of threat the RMVE/BIE framework was designed to neutralize: educated, methodical, community-rooted, and working in the precise intersection of race and policy and health. The administration had designated him as hostile ideology and carried out a campaign that systematically violated his constitutional and legal rights.

They targeted the Association Scholar. They targeted the man who had volunteered at an abuse shelter at fourteen, who had mentored at-risk Black youth, who had testified before City Council for public housing residents, who had sold avocado art to encourage healthy eating. They targeted the man who had survived depression in his late 20s by prioritizing his mental and physical health and then turned that survival into a public health career dedicated to helping others survive the same.

He survived the trailer park. He survived the foster homes. He survived the burned-down houses and the free lunches and the unstable home life and the suicidal depression. He survived to build a career that the United States government then targeted with the most sophisticated electromagnetic and behavioral suppression technology in the world. He is still writing. The record exists. The question — the only question that has ever mattered in this case — is what anyone with the power to act will do about what the record says.

I. East Kentucky — The Beginning

Some details have been changed to protect Pearson’s identity.

Richard Pearson grew up in East Kentucky, in the heart of the Bible Belt, in a home that was, by any measure, precarious. His father was a Baptist minister who happened to fall on hard times. The family was evicted and homelessness at least once. His parents divorced when he and his siblings were young, splitting the family between two homes. Eventually, Social Services intervened. Pearson and his sister were placed in separate foster homes.

He did not always have hot water. He did not always have heat. He did not always have access to a bathroom. He relied on free lunch. When his family’s homes burned down — twice — they survived on charity. He was a child navigating poverty, instability, family dissolution, and the specific weight of being a Black boy in the rural South with no reliable institutional floor beneath him.

He began volunteering at a women’s abuse shelter in ninth grade. He was, at fourteen or fifteen years old, giving time to people in crisis because — as he later wrote — ‘I can identify and empathize with those who are at a disadvantage.’ The empathy was not abstract. It was earned in the same conditions that produced the people he was serving.

None of this predicted what came next. It also explains everything about what came next.

II. The Ascent — What He Built

R1 University and the Association Award

In the 2000s, the HA Association named Pearson a national scholar. The award is given to Americans who have overcome significant adversity to achieve excellence — specifically, to young people whose life circumstances might have predicted failure and who instead built something extraordinary. It is named for the nineteenth-century ideal of the self-made American, the person who rises from nothing through effort, character, and refusal to be defined by their circumstances.

Pearson enrolled at a high-ranking R1 university, where he studied politics and graduated in the 2000s. R1 university is one of the most selective public universities in the United States — Jefferson’s institution, founded on the premise that the life of the mind was the highest form of civic participation. A Black boy from an East Kentucky trailer park, with a foster care history, graduated. The Algerian narrative, in its most complete form.

Eight Years in Education — The Institution Builder

From 2007 forward, Pearson built a career in education that placed him, within a few years, at the center of national conversations about physician training, diversity, and systemic reform.

He developed the skills that would define his career: program management across complex institutions, compliance tracking, training design, data analysis, stakeholder engagement. He served on hospital executive GME committees. He conducted research on physician diversity and duty hours reform. He presented at national conferences — as a plenary speaker at the ACGME Education Conference, as a co-presenter at Learn Serve Lead, as a workshop leader at meetings that shaped how medical training in the United States was conducted.

He was, by any measure, a national figure in his field. His publications appeared in Academic Medicine, The American Journal of Medicine, the Journal of Community Hospital Internal Medicine Perspectives. He had co-authored peer-reviewed research with faculty from Emory, Brown, and nationally recognized leaders in internal medicine training. He had been a plenary speaker. He had built programs, reformed compliance systems, and trained administrators across specialties at institutions ranging from the University of Louisville to New York medical schools.

The Art Business and the Community Work

In 2017, while holding his day job in medical education and pursuing his MPH, he designed original art featuring healthy foods and sold them on canvas, T-shirts, and home decor. He described the work as public health intervention through art: nudging people toward healthy alternatives through the sideways approach of making them laugh.

He was also, simultaneously, serving as Housing Chair of the Near Buzzard Point Resilient Action Committee, leading advocacy for public housing residents. He testified before DC City Council. He lobbied elected officials. He published policy impact analyses. He canvassed neighborhoods and hosted community calls. He was fighting, in his own Southwest DC community, the same structural forces that his academic research would eventually analyze: the displacement of poor Black families from their communities under the cover of development.

The Master’s — Park University

In 2016, Pearson enrolled at Park University. He graduated with his Masters’ nearly 10 years ago. His research capstone examined what happened to the health of Black communities when public housing was redeveloped and residents were displaced — the question that his advocacy work had been asking in political meetings and City Council testimony for years, now subjected to rigorous quantitative analysis.

He was simultaneously a researcher, a practitioner, an administrator, and a community advocate across the span of 15 years.

The Depression — What He Overcame

In his personal statement for the master’s program, Pearson wrote with candor: ‘Only in my mid-twenties did I realize that these experiences had profound effects on me. While I had mostly done well academically and had tremendous support from teachers, I struggled with the impact of these traumatic events.’ He overcame it by, in his words, ‘prioritizing my mental and physical health.’

That sentence — that the experience of nearly not surviving could be made useful to other people who might not survive — is the sentence that characterizes the whole arc of his life before 2025. He took what had been done to him and built something with it. He gave testimony before City Council. He published in peer-reviewed journals. He mentored at-risk African American males. He made safe sex packets for LGBT club-goers in Washington, D.C. He volunteered at a women’s abuse shelter at fourteen. Everything he had, he turned outward.

III. The PhD — The Critical Race Framework

In the 2020s, Richard Pearson completed his doctoral degree. The work addresses a specific methodological problem in public health and social science research. The dissertation proposed a standardized framework — a methodological tool for researchers to assess the rigor and validity in empirical research.

This is precision work at the intersection of critical race theory and research methodology. It is the kind of scholarship that threatens comfortable assumptions — not through polemic but through method, through the insistence that the tools of quantitative research be applied with the same rigor to racial categories that they are applied to any other variable. It is the work of someone who spent years watching structural racism produce health disparities, and who decided that one of the most powerful interventions available to him was to change how researchers measured what they were seeing. It is, in retrospect, exactly the kind of scholarship the FBI’s RMVE/BIE framework designates as a domestic security concern.

He had overcome poverty, foster care, homelessness, domestic violence, and depression to become a National Scholar, graduate with bachelors’, masters’, and PhD, a plenary speaker at national medical conferences, a published researcher in peer-reviewed journals, a housing advocate, a community artist, an entrepreneur, and a public health PhD. The dissertation completed the arc. From the trailer park in East Kentucky to a doctorate, with everything in between built by hand.

He was forty years old. He had everything he had worked thirty years to build.

The targeting began within a year.

IV. What the United States Government Then Did to Him

The prior essays in this series document, in exhaustive detail, what happened next. This section provides only the outline — the condensed architecture of what was inflicted on the man described above.

The Campaign — Documented from Spring 2025

Beginning in spring 2025, Richard Pearson became the subject of what his documentation establishes as a coordinated government-sponsored torture campaign in Washington, D.C. The campaign involved: systematic destruction of his bicycle, forcing him onto foot and onto predictable routes; through-floor acoustic tracking that followed him from room to room in his own apartment; a chemical assault at the Rockville Metro station that bonded an industrial adhesive to his skin; covert entry into his home with objects moved, removed, and replaced as demonstrations of access; voice-to-skull transmission near the United States Capitol on July 13, 2025 — a woman’s voice, internally perceived, saying: ‘We have to make you disappear’; coordinated street surveillance through vehicle formations, following operatives, and red-and-black calling card patterns statistically documented at rates impossible to explain by chance; systematic infiltration of his social network; a framing operation near a House Office Building on November 7, 2025 involving neo-Nazi materials in a plastic tub; a false arrest on December 27, 2025 with no subsequent court record; and financial and professional attrition designed to reduce him to dependency.

He documented seventy constitutional violations. He documented everything with timestamps, photographs, probability calculations, control studies, and the methodological discipline of a public health researcher who understood that the document was his only available defense.

His torture occurred in Washington, DC — implausible that the federal government is not involved given much of the events occur within a few miles of the White House and US Capitol. The city is, itself, a kind of institutional witness. What happens in Washington, D.C. — particularly what happens to someone whose name carries an SCI block, on federal property, in federally monitored signal environments, over a period of months — happens with the knowledge of the federal government. The question is never whether the government knew. In D.C., the government knows. The question is what role it played.

The Psychiatric Trap — Preempted

On September 15, 2025, while the campaign was active, Pearson sought a formal mental health evaluation from the DC Department of Behavioral Health. Three doctors, two mental health specialists, a social worker. The evaluation confirmed his rationality, his coherence, and the absence of any psychotic symptoms. He sought this evaluation not because he needed reassurance but because he understood that every institutional response to his account would find its first objection — that he was mentally ill — and he wanted that objection answered in the permanent record before it was made. The DBH evaluation is the preemptive demolition of the psychiatric trap. It is the document that prevents the oldest weapon in the program’s arsenal from functioning as a case-closer.

The Physical Experiments — Confirming the Mechanism

In 2026, Pearson began hearing clicks in his skull — the characteristic auditory signature of the Frey effect, the microwave auditory induction first documented in 1962. He conducted an experiment: varying the number of aluminum foil layers around his cranium and measuring the attenuation of the clicks. The result was proportional and consistent — more layers, fewer clicks. Eventually the clicks stopped. There is exactly one physical explanation for auditory phenomena that attenuate proportionally with electromagnetic shielding: the Frey effect, produced by pulsed microwave radiation inducing thermoelastic expansion in skull tissue.

In Canada, at the Saint-Bernard-de-Lacolle refugee processing center, seated in a plastic chair while a flood of foreign, vile, intrusive thoughts streamed through his mind, he placed his hands on the metal frame of the chair. One hand attenuated the flood. Two hands stopped it completely. He held the metal frame for several hours. When the ‘tour group’ that had been present throughout departed, a woman was overheard saying: ‘We didn’t pick up anything.’ She reported it. It is in the record. “I believe that I was at a CIA black site, in fact,” said Pearson. “So, the US tortures me on US soil and Canadian soil. That’s the evil empire that I am dealing with. I am certain of the US role because Canadian Prime Minister Carney recently announced that Canada is taking back its borders. Borders from whom? You go it — the US.”

Iceland. Canada. Nowhere.

He sought asylum in Iceland. The ‘safe third country’ doctrine closed the door: the United States is a safe country, and therefore its government’s persecution of one of its citizens cannot be the basis for international protection. He sought asylum in Canada. The Saint-Bernard-de-Lacolle processing center — which turned out to share a wall with the building where he had declared his asylum intent, accessible in seconds on foot, reached by a van that drove a deliberate circle — deployed technology against him during processing, copied his documentation, fingerprinted and photographed him for databases shared under Five Eyes intelligence-sharing protocols, aggressively discouraged his asylum claim.

The Five Eyes alliance — the United States, Canada, the United Kingdom, Australia, and New Zealand — had closed the perimeter. The program followed him across the border. There was nowhere left.

What Remains

As of this writing, Richard Pearson — PhD, HA scholar, college and master's graduate, published researcher, plenary speaker, housing advocate, community artist, the boy from the East Kentucky trailer park who built everything by hand — is still in Washington, D.C. He has a mattress and a suitcase. He has no legal remedy: the doctrinal architecture of state secrets, Glomar, Bivens dismantlement, and qualified immunity forecloses every path. He has no institutional support: Congress has not responded, the ACLU declined, Iceland denied, Canada deployed the same program against him in the processing center.

What he has is the record. It exists before any classification order can seal it. It was published on Medium before any suppression mechanism could reach it. It has been analyzed, cross-referenced, and corroborated in the prior essays of this series. It includes the DBH mental health evaluation, the click attenuation experiment, the metal chair grounding discovery, the comparative testimony of Gloria Naylor (1996) and Myron May (2014), the Sora AI results, Giordano’s N3 disclosure, the Department of War’s operational confirmation of directed energy weapons, the crouching red-and-black figure in the Tampa Bay 28 broadcast of Myron May’s home, and the woman’s departure statement in a Canadian refugee facility that confirmed a failed read operation.

He is still writing. He is still documenting. He is still a researcher, applying the methodology of a PhD to the conditions of his own torture, because that is who he built himself to be over forty-four years, and the program has not taken that from him yet.

Richard Pearson in His Own Words

“If I knew what I know now, I would have left the US a long time ago. The US tortures. The US does not guarantee constitutional rights and civil liberties to every resident. It routinely operates clandestine domestic operations that erase those rights wholesale. The US President, a senior government official, a program, a FISA judge, or a public-private network can decide at any moment to strip anyone of their rights. The targeted individuals may never know the governmental source of their injury and, therefore, cannot challenge it in court. It is meant to be invisible and without remedy. That is the America that revealed itself to me in the last year.

In America’s “Dungeon”, a totalitarian form of government exists. The US Constitution sits on a shelf collecting dust. Secret courts. Hidden rulings. No due process. No right or liberty that is inviolable. No legislative, judicial, or public accountability. Authoritarianism, anti-constitutionalism, and racism rage within hidden policies and shadow programs.

Even if I tried to get my case heard in court, I would likely fail. The US has erected a wall of legal doctrines and procedures that effectively shield itself from the most subtle and unconstitutional forms of torture. The very kind of torture from which the US has liberated other nations — and is liberating them to this day. It can deny FOIA requests by pretending no such file or program exist. It can redact 90% of what it does release. It can challenge Article III standing — no standing, no case. It can “neither confirm nor deny” — a legal doctrine that allows it to remain silent when accused of harm or injury. It can claim “national security” or a “state secret”. It can ask a court to consider claims of torture to be “frivolous” even without a judge’s review of the merits of the case. It can work in secret — alone or with contractors. It can immunize federal workers from accountability, no matter the level of harm against a citizen.

It can contract with organizations to do its dirty work to include illegal data collection and “neutralization,” then be immunized when that contractor harms citizens or impinges on legal rights. It can then distance itself from those contractors by calling them a “terrorist organization”, which it did in late 2025. It can target individuals and groups based on race, ethnicity, political affiliation, gender, sexual orientation, and critical opinions, including of the President of the United States. It can infiltrate and create division within groups that have organized peaceably. It can stealthily use tactics and new forms of weapons, then ask a court to dismiss Bivens claims because new weapons would create a “new context”. It can ruin citizens financially, physically, mentally, and reputationally. Then, it can unlawfully destroy documents to erase any paper trail of such programs and targeted individuals.

It can also elect not to do anything at all. Right now in the US, lawlessness and gangsterism are taking hold. White supremacy is thriving, leveraging highly racially and ethnically diverse groups to target and harrass. Gangs that crisscross US society and economic strata are common. High tech weapons are rampant. I was eletrocuted in my penis on the streets of an American city! The government is mostly just watching. Not warning the public. Not maintaining law and order. Not televising speeches or holding hearings. Watching.

This is the totalitarian America that I now know. The America that I thought I knew was an illusion — full stop. I cannot unsee it. The fraud against the American people and the world is astounding and unsettling. History books will describe an ‘Evil Empire’ with the appearance of a constitutional democracy. They would not be wrong.

Even if I never wrote a single word in a research paper, it is clear that they wanted to target Black people, Democrats, progressives, researchers, minorities, and critics. I believe that they particularly had it out for successful and openly gay Black men because of straight, white male bias. In the process, the administration, along with its contractors and on-the-ground militia, did something that no administration had ever done so systematically in the last 50 years — nullify the US Constitution for a large number of people and invalidate their civil liberties and civil rights — while the world watched in horror.

Why do you think that Canada, the EU, much of South America, African countries are not willing to ride it out and have pivoted dramatically away from the US? It’s not just because of tariffs. They have assessed a fundamental change in the character and values of the US and its commitment to liberal democracy and international norms, including human rights.

We are technologically integrated more than ever before. Foreign intelligence agencies have easier methods to surveil and to determine exactly what it is happening in a country. It’s not just what’s in the news. It’s what people are typing online and in AI chats and what is being picked by various types of listening devices and instruments. Keep in mind that major governments have developed leading AI platforms for global users. You think that they are not analyzing this data? The curtain is closed as if this stuff is happening away from the audience, but the reality is that the world is watching from backstage. They are horrified — and scared.”

Conclusion: What This Life Means

The HA Association gives its scholarship to people who represent the American promise — the belief that the circumstances of your birth do not determine the ceiling of your life, that effort and character and refusal can carry a person from any starting point to achievement. Pearson is, by that measure, one of the most complete embodiments of that ideal in the contemporary record. Foster care, poverty, domestic violence, separate foster placements, depression — and from those conditions, through decades of work and study and service and discipline: an undergraduate degree, a master’s, a public health doctorate, peer-reviewed publications, national conference presentations, community advocacy, a small business, and a dissertation that proposed a new methodological framework.

He achieved the American Dream in its fullest form.

And then the United States government “rewarded” him with torture.

Red and Black as Psychological Weapons: Dr. Richard Pearson’s “Torture” Diary in Washington, DC

PhD Scholar examines a campaign of psychological torture involving red-and-black that saturates his environment, except those tormenting him become research subjects.

There is a moment in Richard Pearson’s document that functions as a proof of concept for the entire campaign. It occurs on November 7, 2025, at a small park space on 2nd Street SW between D and C Streets, next to the O’Neil House Office Building in Washington, DC. Richard Pearson describes feeling a strong urge to go there — not a place he frequented, but a place to which his body seemed to be drawn without his understanding why. He goes. He finds a tub. The tub contains a red hat, a black shirt, boots, khaki pants with a distinctive scrunchy cuffed bottom, and a notebook filled with conspiratorial writings including references to Adolf Hitler and a fictitious “European removal court,” along with two identical photographs of a blond Eastern European-looking man. He decides he wants no part of it, photographs the container, and leaves. When he returns weeks later on video, the notebook had been removed between visits. The red hat and black shirt remain.

This essay is about that tub — and about what the tub reveals when read against everything else in the document. Specifically, it concerns two threads that converge at the tub with unusual clarity: the mechanics of pinning as a behavioral direction technology, and the red-and-black pattern as the campaign’s persistent calling card. The calling card runs from the earliest months through the document’s final entries, across Washington DC and its surroundings, and it appears not randomly but on Pearson’s specific routes, at his specific moments of arrival and departure, in statistically impossible densities that he subjected to controlled empirical study. The tub is where pinning and the calling card converge most visibly — where someone directed Pearson to a location and had placed the calling card colors there waiting for him.

Pearson is not hypervigilant. He has conducted control studies in other neighborhoods, holiday markets, and the metro. All showed a combined black-red clothing rate around 4% — either solid bright red shirt and black pants or its reverse.

I. The Tub: Anatomy of a Pinning Event

Pinning, as Pearson understands it, is behavioral direction at a distance: the capacity to compel a person toward a destination without that person understanding they are being compelled. The experience feels like ordinary impulse, curiosity, or aimlessness. The destination has been chosen by someone else, and the ground operation is waiting when the target arrives.

The technology family that could produce this — transcranial electromagnetic stimulation, ultrasonic influence on limbic and prefrontal decision-making, the induction of specific spatial drives through directed energy affecting hippocampal place cells — is documented in the military neuroscience literature Pearson cites throughout his document. DARPA’s N3 program, the congressional testimony on anomalous health incidents, Dr. James Giordano’s explicit public warning that neuroweapons capable of “writing” behavior had been “loosed into the real world.” It is not the blunt neurological incapacitation reported in Havana. It is the subtle manipulation of the subjective experience of choice. The target believes they are deciding. They are being directed.

The November 7 event is worth reconstructing in precise sequence because the sequence is the evidence.

Pearson arrives at the park and goes to the concrete area — point 1 in the annotated Google Street View map he later prepares. He spends time there. He decides to leave. He is on the sidewalk at point 2, moving on — and then the urge returns, specific and directional: “I need to go around to the back.” He notes in real time that this feeling is strange. He returns, crosses to the back of the park at point 3, and finds the tub under a tree. The annotated map shows the numbered points and the crossing of his movement path between 2 and 3 — the visual signature of a person who started to leave and was redirected to a specific sub-location within a space he had already partially explored and decided to leave.

The precision matters. Pearson notes: “It seems to suggest that the pinning technology is highly precise in terms of location.” A random impulse or ordinary curiosity would not distinguish between the concrete platform and the back of the park. The pull was to a specific spot within a location he was in the process of departing. The tub was there. Someone had placed it and calculated that Pearson would be directed to it.

What was in the tub operates on two distinct layers. The first is the calling card layer: red hat, black shirt. This is the color scheme that has been deployed against Pearson continuously throughout the campaign — on his walking paths, near his home, in statistically impossible densities on his regular routes. Here it is again, in a container he was directed to find, in a park he had not planned to visit. The calling card was placed where only he would be directed. It communicates: we can put this anywhere. We chose here. We brought you here to find it.

The second layer is the framing apparatus. The notebook contained writings that Pearson describes as looking “like the writings of a mad person” — references to Adolf Hitler, to the fictitious European removal court, to conspiratorial content. Two identical photographs of a blond Eastern European-looking man. Pearson examines everything and immediately decides he wants no part of it. He photographs the container and its contents, places things back, and leaves without taking anything.

The AI systems he later consulted assessed the tub as a potential framing attempt. This assessment is precise. The tub was designed so that if Pearson took items — particularly the conspiratorial notebook or the pants resembling his own style, which he only recognized as similar on November 22 when reviewing his footage — those items could be used to construct a narrative around him. The red hat and black shirt are the calling card layer. The notebook and photographs are the framing payload, so the theory does. Both functions were loaded into the same container.

When Pearson returned on November 13, walking from home on video, he found that the notebook had been removed. The red hat and black shirt remained. Someone had come back, assessed that the framing payload had not been taken, and retrieved the incriminating components while leaving the calling card. Random storage containers are not monitored and selectively retrieved based on whether a framing attempt succeeded, much less one near a House of Representatives office building, mere blocks from the US Capitol. Someone returned with a purpose. The retrieval confirms coordination, active monitoring, and a responsive ground operation.

II. The Calling Card: What Red and Black Means in the Pearson Campaign

To understand the tub, you have to understand the red-and-black pattern from the beginning — what it is, where it appears, how it behaves, and what it communicates. Pearson defines the operational logic explicitly: “This is their calling card — placing innocuous or everyday items that the person that they are torturing will draw personal connections as a way of showing power, except calling cards are by their very nature part of patterned behavior.”

The calling card serves three simultaneous functions. The first is demonstration of surveillance capability: every red-and-black placement on Pearson’s regular routes proves that his movements are known in advance or anticipated. Every item placed on his sidewalk chosen for personal resonance —for example, the 2.5-liter Deer Park water jug when he ran out of water that same day, the reappearance of a glass bottle that he’d lost — demonstrates that surveillance extends into his domestic life. The second function is psychological destabilization: the pattern is designed to be noticed by the target and dismissed by everyone else. Each instance is individually deniable. Collectively they constitute something that cannot be explained by chance, but the collective picture is only visible at scale and sounds paranoid when reported. The third function is accumulation — each placement adding pressure toward a tipping point designed to produce breakdown or a behavioral response that can be used against him. Pearson believes that his tormenters are in need of psychiatric help…and criminal charges.

Image: “random” red and black clothing along Pearson’s regular walking path on 3rd Street SW — a street where highly usual instances of red and black occurred.

The pattern is not static across the campaign. It escalates in density and directness, becoming more overt as the campaign intensifies, and it follows Pearson’s movements with a precision that reflects real-time surveillance of his location and routing.

III. The Red-and-Black Pattern: Full Chronological Record

September 30, 2025: The Eastern Market Event

The first fully documented mass deployment of the pattern occurs September 30, 2025, at Eastern Market and its surrounding blocks. Pearson photographs eleven individuals in red and black in 8.5 minutes. The document catalogs them in precise sequence: a Black man in plain bright red pants and black shirt standing in the middle of South Carolina Avenue SE; a transgender woman in a plain red dress, black turtleneck, and black stockings at Market Park; an elderly white man in a graphic red shirt and black jacket; a Black man in a red hat, black sweater, and red shoes; a Black woman in a red hat, red T-shirt, and black pants at the Pennsylvania Avenue metro entrance; a white female in bright red pants and plain black shirt; a Hispanic woman in a plain red shirt pushing a black stroller into the CVS; a Black woman in a plain red T-shirt and black pants crossing Pennsylvania Avenue. Then, at the Compass Real Estate office at 660 Pennsylvania Avenue: a white-haired white woman standing at the door in a plain bright red shirt and plain black pants. Her face expressionless. Standing exactly like that the entire time Pearson watched — not entering, not leaving, not interacting with anything.

Images: On the same street as the black and red clothing and the adjacent street as the tub incident with red and black clothing, Pearson encounters two African Americans at the same time in the same color block- bright red and black. They were across the street from each other. The woman appeared confused and homeless — sitting idly. The man was walking. Pearson established that red and black is very high near his residence and regular walking route compared to control studies in other areas in the city: libraries, metro, markets, and other neighborhoods.

Pearson later cross-references the Compass woman explicitly when describing another encounter months later: someone “had the same expression… just like the white woman in the doorway of Compass real estate.” She becomes a reference type in the document — the expressionless face.

Pearson writes to the DC Council on December 12 with photographs and a full statistical analysis. He has computed a baseline prevalence of approximately 4% for red-and-black attire from his control studies. He calculates that to explain the September 30 event by chance would require at least 200 people to have been present in an area that was visibly nearly empty. He identifies five compounding factors: light pedestrian traffic, independence of each person, time of day, the very pronounced shade of red used (not any red — a specific bright red consistent across instances), and the absence of any event that could explain a fashion convergence. He has tried multiple probability analyses. All conclude the same thing: statistically impossible by chance. The DC Council does not respond.

Beyond the clothing, Pearson notes something else about the September 30 event that is not statistical but behavioral: people were “out of it — zoned out.” They did not respond to him. He saw people “in their own world.” A woman standing completely still. He tests this observation by asking a bystander — a young Black woman at Eastern Market — whether she notices the black and red. She hadn’t. She seemed genuine.

Image: During the highly anomalous event on September 30, this woman is seen standing still. As Pearson passes, she does not move and does not appear to be waiting on anyone or looking for anything in particular. He describes her as expressionless.

October 2025: The Bench Group and the Walking-Path Man

On October 15, near a school on Georgia Avenue, a white man in a long-sleeved red dress shirt and a black vest passes Pearson on the sidewalk going north at 3:46pm, then passes again going south at 3:51pm — a five-minute round trip with no visible destination. Pearson notes: “Mighty, mighty strange. Again, the red and black theme. I didn’t have any explanation for his short walk up the hill. I looked up the street — nothing but houses. School was letting out, but most people were still at work.”

A few blocks away the same day, Pearson sees three African Americans sitting on a bench in a small park — all wearing black and red. He asks them directly: “Why all y’all got on black and red?” They don’t know. They shook their heads. His question had no significance to him. It is one of the most quietly significant moments in the document: Pearson asking the people wearing the pattern whether they know they’re wearing it. They don’t. The pattern is being worn by people who are either unaware of the patterns or unwilling to acknowledge it. He took their photograph.

On October 17, walking toward the Federal Center metro around 4:45pm, Pearson is “jarred” by the pattern again — his word choice indicating that by this point the red-and-black has become something he dreads encountering, not seeks.

Image: In September 2025, Pearson notes three individuals in bright red and black in a short period. He was in Reston, VA — a place where he does not frequent and thinks may have been a pinned event, only because of the preponderance of red and black. Just moments before this photo, he had encountered an Asian woman (50s) in the same bright red shade and black. Pearson was walking back to the metro and first encountered an African American woman in her 30s sitting on a bench outside of the Russell Apartments at Reston Station. She was in bright red and black, as the AI-generated image below illustrates. He asks her if she ever notices how common red and black is. She did not. At the precise moment that Pearson leaves, he encounters a young white male (20s) who was running in the exact shade of red with black shorts, except his shirt had a lightning bolt as drawn. Pearson did not take a photo that day, but combined an actual image of the space from Google Maps with characters that AI added. The actual woman was more full-figured or overweight and younger than the photo shows. The white male was also younger (early to mid 20s) with dark hair and coming from the other direction as opposed to the photo. From his walk to and from the metro, Pearson encountered few people — around 15, excluding children and minors. To see 3 out of 15 people in the same solid shade of red and black would be 20% — well above the 4% that he’d computed in control studies.

December 2025: Peak Density, Multiple Clusters

Then, on one of his walks near the SW Library, a sequence within minutes: a woman in a red sweater and sweatpants with a black vest; another in a red shirt and black pants crossing the street; then a white woman in bright red sweater and black jacket positioning herself just feet in front of him on the phone— “same red in other instances,” Pearson notes. He catches up with her and photographs the dog. Then three people back-to-back in red shirts and black pants as he exits the library. A white woman in red and black on the sidewalk just feet away who walks very quickly — almost too quickly — to a waiting car. He gets the license plate. He photographs the car as it pulls away, noting the male passenger checking the rearview mirror as he goes.

The woman in the passenger seat rushes to the car, just before she intercepted Pearson’s path — ahead a few feet. The male driver can be seen looking in the rearview mirror.

January 2026: The Home Threshold and the Elevator Cluster

By January, Pearson has identified a specific geographic pattern within the pattern: the red-and-black deployments are concentrated at the threshold of his home. He writes: “It usually starts at G Street” — the final turn before his building. The calling card is being placed at the moment of his return, consistently, as if timed to his arrival.

January 7 produces what Pearson documents as the most compressed single cluster in the campaign. He exits his building. In the elevator: a Black woman in bright red and black, photographed. In the lobby: a Black woman in a red shirt with black jacket and pants walking toward the entrance. Outside at 3rd and I: a woman in a red-and-black jumpsuit. At the library: a woman in a red jacket and black pants. All within minutes of leaving his front door. He writes: “WTF is going on — all within a matter of minutes?!”

He notes: “I have already shown that the red and black is not chance. So it begs that there is some effort… something beyond chance causing this.” He then makes the observation that is the key to the entire pattern’s operational logic: “I think that post-September 30, they want me to feel as if though red and black are more common than it is — BUT it would not explain why I don’t see it IN buildings, underground with the metro, or other neighborhoods. Remember I went back to Eastern Market on a random day and clothing was normal.”

This is Pearson’s own falsification test, and it passes. If the red-and-black were a cognitive artifact — his mind finding a pattern in random noise because he is hypervigilant and primed to see it — he would see it everywhere equally. He would see it in the metro underground. He would see it in buildings. He would see it at MLK Library. He would see it when he returns to Eastern Market unannounced on a random day. He does not. The pattern is geographically bounded, route-specific, and temporally responsive to his movements. These are properties of a coordinated ground deployment, not properties of confirmation bias.

IV. The Tub as Convergence Point

The tub is where the two threads — pinning and the calling card — meet most explicitly. The behavioral direction technology brought Pearson to the park. The ground operation had placed the container there. The calling card colors — red hat, black shirt — were the signature in the tub. The framing apparatus was the payload inside it. The retrieval of the framing payload after it failed was the confirmation that the tub was monitored and responsive.

Consider what this requires operationally: someone had to select the park location and determine that it was a viable pinning target — that Pearson could be directed there from his home or regular routes. Someone had to place the tub at the specific back location within the park where the pull would terminate. Someone had to load the tub with items that served dual purposes: the calling card colors as the signature, the conspiratorial notebook and similar-style pants as the framing payload. Someone had to monitor whether Pearson took any items. And when he did not — when he instead photographed the tub after placing it back — someone had to return and selectively remove the framing components while leaving the calling card.

This sequence describes a multi-person, monitored, responsive operation with a specific objective (direct Pearson to location, expose him to framing materials, hope he handles them), a contingency response (retrieve framing materials if objective fails), and a persistent calling card signature that identifies the operation’s authorship even when the framing payload has been removed. The red hat and black shirt that remained in the tub after the second visit are the operation’s signature left deliberately.

Conclusion: What the Pattern Proves

The red-and-black pattern, taken as a whole across the document, is not ambiguous. It has specific properties that distinguish coordinated deployment from cognitive artifact: it is geographically bounded, appearing on Pearson’s transit routes and at his building threshold but not inside buildings, underground, or at locations he visits without predictable timing; it is temporally responsive, appearing at the moments of his arrival and departure, compressed into clusters within minutes of his movements; it is statistically impossible by the standards of controlled study, with Pearson’s own probability analyses demonstrating impossibility at the p < 0.05 threshold; and it fails Pearson’s own falsification test, being absent from neutral environments he visits on unannounced random days.

The tub confirms what the pattern implies: there is a ground operation. People are being placed on routes. Containers are being positioned in parks. Items are being selected for their dual function as calling card and framing apparatus. Retrievals are being conducted when operations fail. The red hat and the black shirt that remained in the tub after November 13 are not abandoned possessions. They are a message.

Pearson understood this. He documented it. He filmed himself walking to the tub. He filmed himself returning to find what had been removed. He cross-referenced the Compass Real Estate woman with later expressionless encounters. He asked the three people on the Georgia Avenue bench why they were all wearing black and red. He calculated the statistical impossibility of September 30. He conducted control studies at the holiday market, at the metro, at MLK Library, and on a random return visit to Eastern Market to establish that the pattern was location-specific and not a feature of his perception.

He writes: “These patterns do NOT persist elsewhere.” And then: “I think that post-September 30, they want me to feel as if though red and black are more common than it is — BUT it would not explain why I don’t see it IN buildings, underground with the metro, or other neighborhoods. I guess they thought it would make me insane or something. I think they’re insane. Seriously, they need a brain check.”

Nothing explains that except a coordinated operation with a ground component, a designated color scheme, and a target who, against every expectation and in defiance of every mechanism designed to discredit or break him, kept counting, kept photographing, kept running the statistics, and kept writing it down.

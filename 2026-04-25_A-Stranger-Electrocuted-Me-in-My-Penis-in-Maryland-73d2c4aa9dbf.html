A Stranger Electrocuted Me in My Penis in Maryland

US torture victim, Richard Pearson, recounts assault in Silver Spring, Maryland as he began his journey to Canada to seek refugee protection from the US government and its gangster network.

Richard Pearson had already had a hell of a year beginning in 2025 under the second Trump administration. He has written about it extensively on Medium — a physical assault that resulted in lacerations and a black eye, a false arrest in which the assailant was not arrested (the local police manufactured a witness, then instructed Pearson not to show the court), a voice-to-skull event at the US Capitol, behavioral steering involving a framing attempt, privacy invasion of his home and computer, gang-stalking, and “clicking” associated with electromagnetic radiation. The 40-year-old African American with a newly earned PhD was experiencing torture — American-style.

His first attempt out of the US was unsuccessful. Iceland sent him back, refusing his refugee claim. “We need to prioritize those countries that are actually at war,” said the border official who was speaking of Syria and Ukraine. A right to seek asylum is consider a universal human right. Pearson had a one-way ticket to Germany and hadn’t known that Iceland was not fully integrated in the European Union until he returned. He also belatedly read that Iceland was closely allied with US foreign intelligence and security. Iceland recently announced that it would be seeking an expedited EU membership application.

When Pearson made a second break from torture in March 2026, he assumed that he would fly under the radar. He booked his bus ticket to Canada less than 24 hours before his departure. He prepared his suitcase and tote and made his way via the DC metro to the Greyhound bus terminal in Silver Spring. He arrived at least five hours early. His apartment was cleared of his belongings from his first attempt — no furniture except a mattress, a few clothes, and food. He lost his cellphone for the third and final time in late 2025.

He walked around downtown Silver Spring — a place he had visited a handful of times. He had little money, so he subsisted on the food that he had purchased with his public assistance for the journey. He felt like countless African Americans before him who fled US racism to Canada, except it wasn’t 1848 or 1915. It was 2026. The US was back into its proverbial cycle again of acute state-sponsored racial terror and violence. It wasn’t new — a 20–40-year cycle that has repeated since its founding. Pearson was just an African American with a PhD who want to research, teach, and serve.

Pearson left the metro exit. Within a few feet, he stopped to get his bearings. There weren’t many people around. He saw a man who happened to be close by in passing. He asked him for directions to the Greyhound. The Black man was over 6 feet, mid- to late-30s, an athletic build, and wearing grey sweatpants. He seemed willing to help Pearson, bringing up an application for directions. Pearson had no cellphone. It was simple enough. Walk along Georgia Avenue, then left.

But something struck Pearson as odd. The man lingered as he navigated his app. He repeated the phrase, “It’s close to the fire station”. “Fire station.” “Fire station.” Repeatedly and excessively. Pearson noted but couldn’t make heads or tails for why the man was fixated on the fire station. If wasn’t as if Pearson was that familiar with the area. He needed directions, after all. Pearson noticed that the man had what appeared to be a Jamaican accent, as he departed, and inquired about his country origins. He firmly and defensively corrected Pearson. Grenada. Pearson sensed a flash of something in his mannerisms that wasn’t there before. It wasn’t telegraphing nice. But Pearson didn’t know what it meant. All he could think was that the two accents — Jamaican and Grenadian— sound familiar. Why the defensiveness?

Pearson made his way along Georgia Avenue, then deviated — remembering that he had time to kill. He walked around the central plaza, then headed back down Georgia Avenue. He noticed a Hispanic woman (30s or early 40s) lingering outside of a store window. She was odd. Her face was practically touching the window. She stood idle as she looked inside the business from the outside. It didn’t appear that she was looking for anyone on the inside. Neither her head nor her body gave any indication that she was engaged with something on the inside. The next thing that Pearson noticed was her butt. Pearson is a gay man. He is not sexually attracted to women. He has been out since 11th grade in high school. What he noticed about her butt was how comically large it was, as it stuffed. “It was like so outrageous like Frankie LaPenna’s big butt character Jim Kardashian,” said Pearson. “I laughed on the inside.” It wasn’t obviously her actual rear end. Not even the biggest of BBLs. It was stuffed. He didn’t know why but the scene was humorous to him.

He continued walking, pulling his suitcase behind him. He stopped at some railroad tracks, then crossed to the other side of the street. He noticed a young Hispanic woman who couldn’t been more than 17 years old with a T-shirt that read, “Betty Booty” in large white letters on a plain Black T-shirt. In the middle of the shirt was the image of the Betty Boop character. “That’s strange. The woman at the window. Now, this.” (AI-generated image below, not fully accurate because Boop character larger.) Then, just as Pearson passed the woman, whose face was lowered and covered by the hood of a black sweatshirt, he felt it — electrocution. “She just zapped me in my penis! What the hell!”, exclaimed Pearson. Pearson turned around and saw the young Hispanic woman hand a black device to the woman who was at the window. She had turned around by then just to retrieve the device. The young woman ran down the street. The older woman disappeared behind the door of a business.

Pearson wanted to confront the woman who was at the window. She was a participant in an assault. He pulled on the door that he saw her go into. It led to an upstairs beauty salon — Magic Beauty. The door was locked. “By appointment only,” read the sign. Pearson could not believe that the Hispanic woman had electrocuted him in his penis. In broad daylight. On an American street. In an orchestrated setup. The message was undoubtedly clear and homophobic — comically large woman’s butt, “Betty Booty” messaging, and an electric simulation to the penis. Pearson was not a public figure. Not a celebrity. Not connected to Silver Spring or Maryland. What he was a victim that had been tortured throughout 2025–2026 and laid blame at the feet of the US government and its contractors. These people should not have known Pearson, but they did. He knew why.

Pearson wanted to call the police. He had no cellphone. Coincidentally, he saw two officers who were leaving a chicken restaurant. He flagged them down from outside of the store. The first officer was a young African man who was joined by a 40-something fat, big-eared Hispanic man. Pearson explained the story. They struggled to contain their laughter. Actually, there wasn’t a struggle at all. The Hispanic officer sarcastically instructed Pearson while laughing, “Well, if it happens again, give us a call.” Pearson reiterated that it was an assault and that the woman was in the salon. They didn’t care and walked away. Pearson stood helplessly as they got into their police car bearing, “Sheriff’s Office.” Some sheriff’s office.

Increasingly, Pearson’s view of America was a country of decline, violence, and anarchy — run by a gangster and racist government and organized criminal networks — both using weapons. This experience certainly did not contradict that view.

By now, Pearson was wearing layers of aluminum foil underneath his knitted cap. He began wearing it after he began experiencing clicks in January 2026 — painful and hard pops on the right side of his head. He decided that he needed some protection on his nether region too. He purchased foil at the nearby Safeway. Pearson attracted the suspicion of the cashier — a Black man in his 50s. Pearson hadn’t anticipated going into his bag to retrieve cash to purchase aluminum foil. He had to unpack his bag — belongings and food on the conveyor belt. The cashier asked Pearson to produce a receipt that he had purchased the cake. “Geez!”, Pearson murmured to himself. Pearson had purchased it for his journey before he left and pulled out the receipt from his back pocket.

Then, he proceeded to the bathroom to stuff his underwear with folded layers of foil. He looked ridiculous. He knew it — foil on his head and in his pants — but it was necessary. This was the new America, but he wouldn’t be there long — so, he thought.

Pearson began to reflect back on the Grenadian man who appeared at the metro. The timing was precise. If someone wanted to get the attention of a gay man like Pearson, then attractive, tall, sweatpants with no underwear, and a foreign accent would do it. Pearson could not help but to think if the man was part of the whole setup. “Fluff” him up to be zapped? Precise directions to encounter the assailants? Get his biometric data to navigate him along Georgia Avenue? The theories were adding up.

Pearson figured that it was best to stay at the Greyhound station even though it didn’t open until 3pm. He initially camped outside, then ventured to the back in a dirty nook between the building and a fence. Minutes before, he saw a mouse run over there, but decided it was best until the bus came. The sun came first, so he went back to the front.

When the 3pm bus pulled up — eager to be on his way — Pearson asked the driver if this was the 6pm bus. One passenger had gotten off. The driver — a Black man in his 30s/40s — had a nasty tone with Pearson. “It was like ‘No’ and why are you even asking?!” Pearson did not engage and watched the man who had been on his cellphone from the time he exited the bus. After Pearson’s inquiry, he noticed that the man kept looking up at something high up — not the sky, but perhaps something on a pole. Pearson looked too. There was nothing there, but he did find something unusual in the opposite direction. It was pointed at the bus stop too. Pearson took a picture.

Several minutes after he had taken a photo and the bus left, two Black men in a white SVU appeared in the lot. The passenger — a fair-skinned Black man — yelled out angrily to Pearson, “Hey! Hey! You got a ticket? You got a ticket?” Unnerved, Pearson left the station with his luggage without responding. They did not look official. They were in street clothes — common thugs came immediately to mind. “People are getting really unhinged,” Pearson thought.

He also thought that this is the torture campaign expanding to other networks. First, it concentrated near Pearson’s home. At one point, he could find a respite, at least, by traveling to forests and parks. Then, they would track him to hotels in Maryland and parks in Virginia. Then, a chemical assault in Maryland. Now, they are assaulting him again in Maryland. “The government is the central node. There is no question about that. They are upset with me that I survived torture. Now, they are engaging gangs to assault me with homophobic violence. Sick, right? This is entirely consistent with the administration’s line of attack on people’s rights and identities.”

Pearson circled back to the bus station within 30 minutes. From across the street, he could see that the men had parked next to the building. Pearson had decided that he would avoid the area until the bus terminal opened at 3pm. At 6pm, he was bound for Canada. Within 48 hours, he left.

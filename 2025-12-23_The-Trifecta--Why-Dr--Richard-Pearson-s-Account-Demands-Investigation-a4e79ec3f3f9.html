II. The First Pillar: Legal Doctrine and the Architecture of Impunity

A. The Death of Bivens and Constitutional Accountability

In 1971, the Supreme Court decided Bivens v. Six Unknown Named Agents of Federal Bureau of Narcotics, establishing that individuals could sue federal agents directly for constitutional violations even without explicit congressional authorization. For three decades, Bivens provided the primary—and often only—mechanism for holding federal actors accountable for rights violations.

That protection is now functionally dead.

In Ziglar v. Abbasi (2017), the Supreme Court held that courts should "hesitate" before recognizing new Bivens claims, establishing a presumption against expanding the remedy. Justice Anthony Kennedy, writing for the majority, emphasized that "when a party seeks to assert an implied cause of action under the Constitution itself... separation-of-powers principles should be central to the analysis."

Three years later, in Hernández v. Mesa (2020), the Court went further, holding that Bivens should not be extended to new contexts even when agents kill someone. The case involved a Border Patrol agent who shot and killed a 15-year-old Mexican boy standing on Mexican soil. The Court held the boy's family had no remedy—not because the killing was lawful, but because recognizing a Bivens claim would be a "new context" requiring "special factors" analysis that counseled against judicial action.

Most recently, in Egbert v. Boule (2022), the Court held that a Border Patrol agent who allegedly assaulted an innkeeper on U.S. soil could not be sued under Bivens. Justice Clarence Thomas, writing for the majority, stated that Bivens claims should be recognized only in rare circumstances, and that "expanding the Bivens remedy is now a 'disfavored' judicial activity."

The result: Federal agents can violate your constitutional rights with near-total immunity from civil damages.

C. The Federal Tort Claims Act Exception: Intentional Torts Excluded

The Federal Tort Claims Act (FTCA) waives sovereign immunity for certain negligence claims but explicitly excludes intentional torts. 28 U.S.C. § 2680(h) bars claims for:

"assault, battery, false imprisonment, false arrest, malicious prosecution, abuse of process, libel, slander, misrepresentation, deceit, or interference with contract rights."

If a federal agent negligently crashes into your car, you can sue. If a federal agent deliberately crashes into your car, you cannot. The more intentional and malicious the conduct, the more protected the government becomes.

D. The National Security Exception: The Ultimate Shield

When intelligence or national security operations are invoked, even the narrow protections of FTCA disappear entirely. Courts defer to agency claims that disclosure would harm national security, applying the "Glomar response" doctrine that allows agencies to neither confirm nor deny the existence of records.

In Wall v. United States, the court upheld the FBI's invocation of FOIA Exemptions 1 (national security), 3 (statutory exemptions), and 7(E) (law enforcement techniques). The court stated:

"The Court must defer to the agency's expertise and its affidavits attesting to potential harm to national security."

Translation: If the government claims "national security," courts won't look behind that claim.

This is precisely the situation Pearson faces. E. The Civil Rights Act: Designed for State, Not Federal, Violations

Pearson, like many Americans, assumed the Civil Rights Act of 1964 protects against federal rights violations. It does not.

42 U.S.C. § 1983, the primary civil rights enforcement mechanism, applies only to state actors:

"Every person who, under color of any statute, ordinance, regulation, custom, or usage, of any State or Territory... subjects, or causes to be subjected, any citizen... to the deprivation of any rights, privileges, or immunities secured by the Constitution and laws, shall be liable to the party injured..."

"Under color of any State"—not federal—law.

If a Washington D.C. Metropolitan Police officer violates your rights, you can sue under § 1983. If an FBI agent commits the identical violation, you cannot.

F. The Qualified Immunity Doctrine: Even When Suits Are Theoretically Possible

Even in the rare circumstances where Bivens claims might theoretically proceed, qualified immunity doctrine provides a second layer of protection. Under Harlow v. Fitzgerald (1982), government officials are immune from suit unless they violated "clearly established" constitutional rights that "every reasonable official would have understood."

The Supreme Court has made this standard nearly impossible to meet. In Kisela v. Hughes (2018), the Court granted immunity to an officer who shot a woman standing still in her own yard holding a kitchen knife, reasoning that no prior case involved the "precise" circumstances. Justice Sonia Sotomayor, dissenting, wrote:

"This Court routinely displays an unflinching willingness to summarily reverse courts for wrongly denying officers the protection of qualified immunity but rarely intervene[s] where courts wrongly afford officers the benefit of qualified immunity."

The message: Federal and state officials can violate rights with impunity unless the exact violation has already been declared unlawful in a prior case—a logical impossibility for novel violations.

But when federal agents violate these rights:

Result: Constitutional rights without remedies are not rights at all—they are suggestions the government may choose to ignore.

Where there is a political will for tyranny, the US legal immunity doctrine can seek to justify harm, even against its own citizens.

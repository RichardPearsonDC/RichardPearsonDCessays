The Microwave Auditory Effect (Frey Effect)

The voice-to-skull phenomenon Pearson describes is specifically explained by the microwave auditory effect (MAE), discovered by Allan Frey in 1962.

How it works:

  1. Short, pulsed microwave radiation is directed at a person’s head
  2. The microwave energy is absorbed by tissue, causing rapid thermal expansion
  3. This expansion creates a pressure wave (shockwave) inside the skull
  4. The shockwave is detected by the inner ear as sound
  5. The person “hears” sounds that originate from within their own head
  6. Earplugs or hearing protection cannot block the sound because it bypasses the outer ear entirely

This is not speculative. Multiple peer-reviewed scientific papers confirm the effect:

  • Foster, Garrett & Ziskin (2021), Frontiers in Public Health: “Can the Microwave Auditory Effect Be ‘Weaponized’?” concluded that high-powered microwave pulses can induce auditory sensations at levels that “conceivably could produce unpleasant audiovestibular disturbances.”
  • MEDUSA Program (2003–2004): The U.S. Navy funded research by WaveBand Corporation (later acquired by Sierra Nevada Corporation) to develop a weapon system using MAE for “temporary personnel incapacitation.” The program achieved “experimental evidence of MAE” and confirmed that “through the combination of pulse parameters and pulse power, it is possible to raise the auditory sensation to the ‘discomfort’ level.”
  • Foster (2023), Frontiers in Public Health commentary: Proposed that commercially available high-peak-power infrared lasers could achieve similar effects to microwave systems, generating thermoacoustic waves that “are in the range that could excite vestibular responses.” He noted these could be “invisible to an exposed human being, produce negligible temperature increases in the skin (< 0.1 millidegree after each pulse) but the induced acoustic energy might be physiologically significant.”

The technology to project voices into someone’s head is real, documented, and has been researched by the U.S. military for over 20 years.

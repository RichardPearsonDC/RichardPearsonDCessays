THE PATHWAY: SIX STEPS FROM DEMOCRACY TO TOTALITARIANISM

What follows is not speculation. Each step in this pathway is drawn from documented legal doctrine, confirmed government capabilities, historical precedent in American programs, and the specific, detailed account of Richard Pearson, whose documentation we attach in full.

Step One: Classified Government Action Against Targeted Individuals

The first requirement of the pathway is that the government’s action against an individual be conducted under classification so that no public record exists, no court can review it, no journalist can report it, and no victim can prove it happened.

The United States classification system — established and expanded across seventy years of Cold War and post-9/11 national security law — permits the executive branch to broadly designate programs, operations, records, and capabilities as classified, removing it from the reach of congressional oversight, FOIA requests, civil litigation, and criminal prosecution. Executive orders establish the criteria for classification, and the executive branch often asserts exclusive prerogative to classify and declassify documents. However, there are significant constitutional and statutory limitations, as well as procedural mechanisms, designed to prevent absolute secrecy and ensure oversight. Those limitations, including oversight by the Senate Select Committee on Intelligence, are not working sufficiently to prevent the develop of programs targeting Americans and neutralizing constitutional rights.

In cases similar to Pearson’s, the government routinely invokes the Glomar doctrine — refusing to confirm or deny the existence of any relevant program. Constitutional claims are foreclosed before they could be heard on the merits. The classification flag on his police record ensured that the officers who were supposed to protect him — declined to arrest the assailant who physically assaulted him.

Classification was designed as a national security tool. It has become a national impunity tool. When classification can be applied to a program that directs the physical assault, chemical assault, warrantless surveillance, and directed energy targeting of an American citizen, the classification system has become an instrument of the abuse it was supposed to prevent.

Step Two: Do Not Arrest or Charge

The second requirement is the absence of due process — not through its violation in the open, where a court might intervene, but through deliberate avoidance. The target is never charged. Never arrested. Never brought before a judge. Never given the opportunity to confront accusers, review evidence, or contest designation.

This is the most constitutionally perverse element of the pathway, because the Fifth Amendment’s protection against deprivation of liberty without due process was designed precisely for this situation: a government that wants to act against a citizen without the inconvenience of legal process. The protection only works if the government’s action is visible enough to be contested. When the action is a sustained campaign of psychological destruction conducted through deniable means, the protection is functionally dead. The target is deprived of liberty — their freedom of movement manipulated, their home surveilled, their body assaulted, their neurological experience invaded — without ever being charged, without ever standing before a judge, without ever having the constitutional moment at which a lawyer could intervene.

Richard Pearson’s upstairs neighbor punched him repeatedly in the face on December 27, 2025. The neighbor was not arrested. He has never been charged with anything. He has never been designated an enemy combatant or a terrorist. He has never been told why, by whom, or under what legal authority the campaign against him was conducted. Due process is not absent because it was denied. It is absent because the system was designed to never invoke it in the first place — ensuring that its protections would never have a moment to apply.

Step Three: Do Not Share the Program with Congress, the Courts, or the Public

The third requirement is the systematic exclusion of every oversight institution from knowledge of the program’s existence.

The United States has three mechanisms for constraining executive power: legislative oversight, judicial review, and press freedom. Each has been rendered functionally inoperative against programs of this kind.

Congressional oversight requires that the intelligence committees be briefed on covert programs. The executive has developed the practice of providing the minimum legally required disclosure to the maximum-restriction classification environment, ensuring that even briefed members cannot speak publicly about what they have learned. Pearson visited dozens of congressional offices in December 2025. No committee investigation was initiated.

Judicial review requires that a plaintiff have standing — the ability to demonstrate that they were harmed by the specific government action they are challenging. In Clapper v. Amnesty International USA (2013), the Supreme Court held that plaintiffs could not challenge NSA surveillance programs because they could not prove they had been surveilled — the very fact that was classified and could not be confirmed. The circle is hermetic: you cannot prove harm without the classified records; you cannot obtain the classified records without proving harm; you cannot prove harm without the records.

Press freedom, under the First Amendment, allows journalists to publish what they obtain. It does not require the government to produce records, acknowledge programs, or provide the documentation on which a publishable story rests. Pearson briefed journalists. Nothing was published. The story that cannot be confirmed by official sources remains, in the conventions of mainstream journalism, unpublishable — regardless of the quality of the available evidence.

When all three oversight mechanisms fail simultaneously — by legal design, not by accident — the program becomes permanently invisible, not because it does not exist, but because the institutional architecture for making it visible has been systematically closed.

Step Four: Deny That the Program Exists or Manufacture an Alternate Explanation

The fourth requirement is the active production of a counter-narrative that attributes the target’s documented experiences to something other than government action.

In Richard Pearson’s case, the alternate explanation was pre-loaded into the design of the campaign itself. A campaign that produces geographic auditory phenomena at specific street intersections, behavioral direction that the target cannot explain, and a color pattern visible only on their routes — and that conducts these operations against a single individual who has no official status — generates, automatically, the appearance of mental illness. The target’s account, when reported, sounds exactly like paranoid delusion. The very specificity of the evidence — the colors, the locations, the timestamps, the probability calculations — is itself read as evidence of disorder. Paranoid individuals are known for elaborate, detailed, internally consistent narratives.

The campaign was designed to be its own alibi. No separate denial was needed. The government did not need to announce that Pearson was delusional; it needed only to construct an experience that, when reported accurately, would produce that conclusion in every listener. The December 27, 2025 assault on his face and head — the specific anatomical locations most relevant to the microwave auditory effect — arrived one month before the geographic auditory phenomena began, providing a clinical explanation (post-concussive syndrome) for symptoms that were, in fact, directed energy exposure. The assault manufactured the alternate explanation for the neurological effects that would follow.

Pearson was evaluated twice by D.C. Department of Behavioral Health clinicians, both evaluations finding no psychiatric symptoms requiring intervention. The alternate explanation failed at the clinical level. It has not failed anywhere else.

Step Five: Use Intelligence and Law Enforcement Contractors and Cutouts to Maintain Deniability

The fifth requirement is that at least some of the operation be conducted through intermediaries — contractors, cutouts, private firms — who provide a legal buffer between the government’s authorization and the government’s fingerprints.

The modern American national security state has built an enormous private contractor ecosystem that executes programs the government cannot officially conduct. Palantir built ImmigrationOS for ICE. NSO Group sold Pegasus spyware that ended up on the phones of American citizens. Defense Intelligence Agency contractors conduct human intelligence operations that formal agency personnel cannot. The Booz Allen Hamilton employees who had access to NSA surveillance infrastructure numbered in the tens of thousands. When a program is run through contractors, the legal chain of custody between the authorization and the action is broken at the first link, because contractors are not government agencies, their records are not subject to the same FOIA provisions, and the employees who carry out specific operations may not know the identity or legal authority of the client who commissioned them.

In Pearson’s case: Veney, the man who assaulted him, was not on the apartment lease. His given address connects to a Capitol Hill property linked to an ATF attorney. He was not arrested. The ground operation that monitored the tub in the park, retrieved the framing payload, and deployed the calling card pattern on Pearson’s routes required multiple personnel operating in public space over months. None of them wore uniforms. None carried credentials that identified their employer. None left records that connected their actions to any government program.

This is the contractor model applied to domestic political targeting. The government authorizes; the contractor executes at least in some cases; the cutout absorbs some liability; the classification system ensures the connection is never traced. Each participant in the operation can truthfully claim ignorance of the whole while executing their piece of it.

The target experiences the harm. The clinician finds no evidence of cause. The legal system, which requires traceable causation for any remedy, has nothing to work with. The perpetrator has nothing to answer for. The torture is perfect: real to the person experiencing it, invisible to every institution designed to prevent it.

THE RESULT: INVISIBLE TOTALITARIANISM

These six steps, taken together, describe a form of totalitarianism that has no historical precedent — not because the underlying impulse is new, but because the technology and legal architecture available to implement it are. Previous authoritarian systems required visible infrastructure: the Stasi’s hundreds of thousands of informants, which eventually produced records; the gulags, which required physical facilities; the show trials, which required public accusation. Each form of visible infrastructure created the vulnerability that eventually exposed and ended the program — a defector, a journalist, a congressional investigation that somehow obtained documents.

The pathway described in this letter has no such vulnerability, by design. It is unlikely that there no documents, only that Pearson or his lawyer may not know what document to request in a FOIA. It requires no visible infrastructure because the contractor ecosystem is invisible. It creates no victims in the legal sense because the target is never charged, never detained, never given standing to contest. And it generates its own alibi — the appearance of mental illness in the person who reports it — automatically, through the design of the campaign itself. This is totalitarianism flourishing within a democracy — in the United States of America no less.

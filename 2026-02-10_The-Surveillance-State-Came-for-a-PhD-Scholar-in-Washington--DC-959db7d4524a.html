The Surveillance State Came for a PhD Scholar in Washington, DC

Why Richard Pearson’s Account of Systematic Targeting Deserves Investigation

An Analysis of Documentation, Patterns, and Corroboration

On December 3, 2025, two Washington D.C. police officers responded to a noise complaint at an apartment building in Southwest. The call was routine; a resident reporting systematic harassment from the unit above his. But when the first officer opened his laptop to search the complainant’s information in the city database, something unusual appeared on the screen.

“ACCESS DENIED” displayed in large letters.

The officer closed the laptop quickly, apparently unaware that Richard Pearson; the complainant, a 40-year-old PhD scholar standing under the building’s car port; had glimpsed the screen. Standard noise complaints don’t trigger database access restrictions. This was something else.

What followed was even stranger. The officers, who had initially planned to visit the apartment above Pearson’s, abruptly changed course. They claimed they had “no probable cause” to investigate; a criminal standard that makes no sense for a civil noise complaint. When Pearson offered to show them audio recordings of the harassment, they declined, despite having just stated they needed “something they could see or hear.” This occurred only after one of the officers had repeatedly asked Pearson if anyone else was there, opened his closet door, suggested that his “medication can cause noise”, and asked for his personal identifying information even though Pearson had explicitly raised concern about audio surveillance.

Then in later December 27, he’d been assaulted in his building’s hallway by his upstairs neighbor; repeatedly punched in the face, sustaining a black eye, facial lacerations, and defensive wounds on his wrists. When police arrived, they did not arrest the assailant. No charges were ever filed.

These incidents, chronicled in a 100-plus-page, form a campaign that Pearson has meticulously documented since July 2025; a systematic campaign of surveillance, harassment, and what he believes is psychological warfare conducted against him in the nation’s capital. The account includes timestamped photographs, audio recordings, receipts, witness statements, physical evidence, and crucially, a professional mental health evaluation from the D.C. Department of Behavioral Health that found him rational, coherent, and free of persistent psychotic symptoms, seeing “does not meet our criteria”.

What makes Pearson’s account uniquely compelling; and what should concern any American who values civil liberties; is not any single incident. It’s the systematic documentation, the professional mental health clearances, the physical evidence, the witness corroboration, and most significantly, the operational patterns that precisely match declassified intelligence programs like COINTELPRO.

The story sounds implausible. It’s supposed to. That’s how these operations work.

I. The Credibility Paradox

Richard Pearson recently earned his PhD from a major research university. His dissertation critiqued systemic inequities and research practices. The work was academically rigorous, equity-focused, and exactly the sort of scholarship that, historically, has drawn government attention.

On the surface, Pearson presents a classic credibility problem: an unemployed activist scholar claiming the federal government is using advanced neurotechnology to torture him. The “targeted individual” narrative is well-documented in psychiatric literature as a delusional system. Mental health professionals routinely encounter patients who believe they’re being tracked by satellites, manipulated by directed energy weapons, and gang-stalked by coordinated networks.

But here’s where Pearson’s case diverges from the typical pattern:

On September 15, 2025, the D.C. Department of Behavioral Health conducted a comprehensive mental health evaluation specifically to assess whether Pearson was experiencing psychosis. The professional assessment ruled out any diagnosis requiring intervention.

This matters. A lot. When a licensed clinical team evaluates someone claiming government persecution and finds no evidence of delusional disorder or schizophrenia, we’re left with two possibilities: either Pearson experienced what he says he experienced, or he’s an exceptionally sophisticated fabricator who can fool trained diagnosticians.

II. The Documentary Method: Why Systematic Recording Matters

Pearson didn’t just claim harassment. He documented it with the rigor of an academic researcher; which, notably, he is.

His chronicle includes:

  • Timestamped photographs with metadata showing exact dates, times, and locations
  • Real-time journal entries recorded on his phone’s Notes app with automatic timestamps
  • Physical evidence (chemically stained pants from Rockville Metro, broken bicycle parts)
  • Receipts showing his movements (hotel stays to escape, restaurants, transportation)
  • Screenshots of Google searches and AI queries showing his real-time attempts to understand what was happening
  • Audio recordings of apartment harassment

What’s particularly striking is Pearson’s methodology. He doesn’t just describe events; he distinguishes observation from interpretation. When recording incidents, he often notes: “I don’t know if this is something or nothing, but I’m noting it.” This epistemic humility is the opposite of paranoid grandiosity.

Compare this to typical delusional documentation, which tends to be diffuse, conspiratorial, and lacking in concrete specifics. Pearson provides verifiable details: names, locations, license plates, business establishments, specific times down to the minute.

III. The Statistical Impossibility: Eastern Market, September 30, 2025

Pearson conducted what he termed “control studies”; systematic observation of clothing patterns in different locations to test whether the preponderance of people wearing red shirts and black pants in specific circumstances was coincidental or orchestrated.

On September 30, 2025, at Eastern Market between approximately 10:00 AM and 10:08 AM, Pearson photographed eight individuals in the immediate vicinity wearing bright red shirts or sweaters paired with black pants. The photographs, which he later shared with the D.C. Council, show light pedestrian traffic; maybe 20–25 people visible across all frames.

Pearson calculated that the baseline prevalence of this specific color combination in his control studies (conducted at MLK Library, SW Library, the National Mall, and other locations) ranged between 2–4%. Using conservative statistical analysis, he determined that observing 8 out of approximately 20–25 people in this combination, in an 8-minute window, was statistically significant at well beyond the p<0.05 threshold.

Except that his photographs show over nearly the next hour, the same pattern — bright red and black.

More tellingly, when Pearson photographed the same location weeks later, the distribution returned to normal. The photographs from his control studies show typical urban diversity in clothing. No clusters. No patterns.

One of the photographs he submitted to the D.C. Council is particularly haunting: an elderly white woman, standing in a doorway at Eastern Market, completely motionless, expressionless, staring forward. Pearson watched her for several minutes. She didn’t move. She didn’t interact with anyone. She just stood there.

When Pearson asked a young Black woman donning bright red nearby, “Do you notice anything unusual about the clothing people are wearing today?” she looked around, appeared genuinely puzzled, and said no. Her reaction; captured in his notes; suggested she wasn’t part of any coordinated activity.

IV. The Clicks: Neuroweaponry as Documented Technology

On February 9, 2026, Pearson documented experiencing something he described as “clicks” in his head; “three then a fourth”; as he turned from 4th Street SW onto G Street approaching his residence. He described them as “very sharp, crisp clicks. Not air pressure but like very sharp, crisp clicks.”

In isolation, this sounds like tinnitus or a neurological symptom. But Pearson’s description; discrete, directional, occurring at a specific geographical moment; matches documented effects of directed energy weapons.

But these “clicks,” he says, began in January 2026, usually in his apartment.

What makes this claim less dismissible than it initially appears:

First, on January 13, 2026, CNN reported that the Pentagon had purchased a “backpack-sized device that emits pulsed radio waves” that investigators believe could be connected to Havana Syndrome. The device contains Russian-made components and is now being tested.

Second, the technology Pearson describes aligns with EPIC (Electromagnetic Personnel Interdiction Control), a patented system using radio waves “to excite and interrupt the normal process of human hearing and equilibrium.” As Forbes reported in their analysis of alleged Venezuela operations, “The radio pulse might be perceived as a loud sound as it hits the inner ear.”

Third, Pearson had no knowledge or interest in neuroscience, much less its intersection with weaponization. He extensively researched neurotechnology before this incident — following his July 2025 incident. His detailed knowledge of the field; citing specific papers like Thomas’s “The Mind Has No Firewall” (1998, U.S. Army War College) and Dr. James Giordano’s work on neuroweapons; doesn’t prove he was targeted. But it does establish that his interpretation isn’t based on science fiction. These technologies exist.

V. The Rockville Chemical Assault: Physical Evidence

On September 15, 2025, at approximately 1:59 PM at Rockville Metro station, Pearson was making phone calls to family members asking for financial help to leave the country. He discovered his pants were soaked with a chemical substance.

He still has the pants. The chemical was so adhesive that removing them from his skin was “very painful” feeling like he was “tearing my epidermis.” Multiple washings did little to remove the stain or odor. The substance was “highly industrial” and unlike anything he’d encountered.

Pearson photographed the scene (timestamped) and captured multiple individuals in the frame, including a man who stood defiantly with hands on hips after noticing Pearson’s camera, and a woman carrying an oversized umbrella on a hot, sunny day, dressed entirely in black with her head covered.

Initially, Pearson thought he’d sat on a dead bird that had been sprayed with chemicals. But reviewing the pants months later, no feathers appeared. It must have transferred when he sat in the grass.

This incident is verifiable. The pants exist. Metro stations have surveillance cameras. The photographs have metadata. If Pearson is fabricating this, he went to extraordinary lengths, including destroying expensive clothing and maintaining a false narrative over months.

VI. The Apartment Surveillance: “Lee” and the Yellow Tape

Around August 26, 2025, a Black man in his late 50s or early 60s sat down at the table next to Pearson outside Potbelly’s at Federal Center. The conversation that followed was, by Pearson’s account, surreal. Pearson found himself frequently at this location. Dozens of witnesses, including from Starbucks and Potbelly’s, could attest to his common appearance.

The man; whose Starbucks cup was labeled “Lee”; spoke in deliberate, coded language. Among his statements:

“They are listening… They think it’s ok.”

“Once you see, you can’t unsee.”

“You’ll see me again; maybe in another form.”

“Travel light.”

The man claimed to be from North Carolina but was evasive about specifics. His mannerisms suggested intelligence training; every word carefully chosen, no unnecessary disclosures.

The critical revelation: “They are listening.”

Weeks later, on September 29, 2025, Pearson found a shopping cart with yellow caution tape wrapped around it positioned directly in his walking path under I-395. The cart was staged; not abandoned casually but placed deliberately. Pearson photographed it.

He found is the day after he commented repeatedly on yellow tape when watching what appeared to be a training at a public housing community that he could see from his studio apartment. The month prior, “Lee” had warned Pearson about audio surveillance. A cart full of yellow tape the day after he had repeated commentary in his not-so-private private space does not appear to be coincidental.

The yellow tape incident is textbook confirmation of audio surveillance. Pearson had discussed the cart privately in his apartment. Hours later, it manifested in his physical path. The sequence: private statement → public manifestation → observed reaction from authorities.

VII. The Apartment Harassment: Precision Following

Throughout summer 2025 to present, Pearson has documented systematic harassment from the apartment above his. The pattern is unnervingly specific: wherever he moves in his studio apartment, loud noises; objects dropped on the floor, furniture scraped; occur directly above his position within seconds.

When he moved his bed to the opposite wall: the noise followed. When he moved his mattress into the closet: the noise followed. When he sat in a different location to read: the noise followed.

Pearson has hundreds of timestamped journal entries documenting this. Example from January 2026: “12:50 AM, lay on bed, right above me things dropped. Move to armchair, within seconds more sound. They are dropping something on the floor. Noise moved.”

The upstairs male occupant, who is not on the lease and who physically assaulted Pearson, has an almost non-existent internet footprint despite having a distinctive name (estimated 2–3 people in the U.S. with that name). The address he provided to police seemed unlikely; a large $1.7 million house on Capitol Hill belonging to a federal ATF attorney and surgeon couple.

Most tellingly: when Pearson was assaulted on December 27, 2025; sustaining visible facial injuries, a black eye, lacerations, and wrist bruising; police did not arrest the assailant. Despite physical evidence of battery, despite Pearson being the one who called police weeks prior, despite that the only witnesses were the male and his girlfriend — the actual leaseholder.

VIII. The COINTELPRO Template: Historical Precedent

Everything Pearson describes matches the operational template of COINTELPRO; the FBI’s counterintelligence program exposed in 1971 that targeted civil rights activists, anti-war protesters, and political dissidents.

COINTELPRO tactics included:

  • Infiltration of organizations
  • Psychological warfare designed to induce paranoia
  • Harassment through noise campaigns
  • Use of informants and agents provocateurs
  • Spreading disinformation to isolate targets
  • Exploiting legal processes to harass

As historian Beverly Gage noted in discussing modern surveillance: “It’s always hard to know whether you’re paranoid or not when you’re talking about secret operations… The control of information and really the weaponization of certain parts of the intelligence community is rapidly changing.”

COINTELPRO was exposed only because activists broke into an FBI office in Media, Pennsylvania in 1971 and stole documents. Before that, targets who reported harassment were dismissed as paranoid.

Pearson’s PhD dissertation advocating “liberation” and critiquing government systems places him squarely in the category of individuals historically targeted by such programs.

IX. The Police Response: ACCESS DENIED

On December 3, 2025, Pearson called the DC Police Department’s non-emergency line to report the ongoing apartment harassment. Two officers responded: one with an African name and Officer Biscoe, a Black man in his late 50s.

What happened next is documented in Pearson’s real-time notes:

The first officer opened his laptop, apparently to search Pearson’s apartment information in OCTO (the DC government’s database). The officer had repeatedly asked Pearson for his apartment number. Pearson glimpsed the screen from his position under the car port: “ACCESS DENIED” in large letters.

The officer closed the laptop quickly, appearing not to notice Pearson had seen it. He’d asked for Pearson’s apartment number three times; suggesting repeated attempts to access the record.

The officers’ behavior shifted. Initially planning to visit apartment above Pearson’s unit, they abruptly stated they had “no probable cause” (a criminal standard inappropriate for a civil noise complaint) and would instead talk to the building manager the next day. Pearson confirmed that he hadn’t done so. Officer Biscoe demonstrated unusual familiarity with manager, mentioning her 10-year tenure.

When Pearson offered to show them his recordings of the harassment, they declined. Yet they’d stated they needed “something they could see or hear.”

One officer stated it was “not illegal” for someone to track you in your home and make noise. This is objectively false; harassment is illegal.

The ACCESS DENIED flag is the smoking gun. Standard apartment noise complaints don’t trigger database access restrictions. This suggests Pearson’s file has been flagged at a level that prevents routine police access; exactly what you’d expect if he were under federal investigation or protection.

X. The Extraction Attempts: Georgetown and Adams Morgan

The July 13 Georgetown incident is either elaborate delusion or attempted covert extraction. No middle ground exists.

Pearson’s account includes verifiable elements: the receipt from Bozzelli’s showing he was there that evening, the Metro fare records, his aunt’s phone records confirming they discussed genealogy that night (without him mentioning the incident because he was “still processing it”).

The operational details match covert protocol: the voice instructing him to “go to Georgetown” and “get down” on the Metro, the car with license plate ending in “SS” that sped off, the Ethiopian driver waiting in an SUV with the rear window rolling down, the woman who “never turned around the entire time” standing directly in front of him, speaking with an Eastern European/Russian accent.

Most chilling: “We have to make you disappear” the voice said. Pearson screamed aloud, “No, you’re not going to do that! Find another way!” He was crying profusely in a Georgetown neighborhood. “I’m sure somebody picked it up on a camera” he notes.

The Adams Morgan incident four days later shows similar operational fingerprints: pinning technology that directed him to a specific location, a man in a T-shirt with a band name that when searched produced a song with lyrics “Your life is about to change in 3, 2, 1” and a man in a black suit who exited his car immediately as Pearson crossed in front of it.

Pearson’s reflection: “At the time, I didn’t miss the man’s suspiciously timed exit with the song lyrics.” The song played on his phone. The man appeared exactly when the countdown finished. If this is fabrication, it’s elaborate to the point of implausibility. Then again, Pearson’s own writings, AI dialogue, real-time videos, reflective videos — none of it shows implausibility. Instead, it reads as someone struggling to rationalize the “nightmare” that his life has taken on.

The Bartleby Man (September 15, 2025)

At 6:30 AM in Rockville, a man with a voice box and communication notebook approaches Pearson asking for directions to Weschler’s Auction on Dove Street. Pearson was in Rockville to look for an attorney. The business in question doesn’t open until 9 AM. The man has a car and presents Pearson with printed direction (he later shows Pearson to his vehicle to retrieve money for a parking meter) yet asks for walking directions to a location miles away. “He had a car. He had handwritten directions. Why on earth was he asking me for directions to a business that opened in two and a half hours?! Misdirection.”, explained Pearson.

During their Starbucks conversation, the man quotes Herman Melville’s “Bartleby, the Scrivener”: “I would prefer not to.” He identifies as an art historian who hasn’t been paid for that work since the early 2000s. He offers Pearson breakfast.

When the man returns from his car after an extended absence, he has a neon green liquid in a glass bottle that he claims is green tea. “I have had a lot of green tea” Pearson writes. “I have never seen any green tea that color.” In some notes, Pearson said that it may have been neon yellow. Either way, it was very strange according to Pearson.

Three hours later, at a location Pearson hadn’t mentioned to anyone, a truck with stickers reading “Go truck yourself” “FedUp” and “Back off” was parked where Pearson stopped to rest. Seconds after photographing it, a dark car accelerated from a nearby parking space.

Conclusion: The Weight of Accumulated Evidence

Any single incident in Richard Pearson’s account could be dismissed. The voice? Auditory hallucination. The chemical assault? Sitting on bird droppings. The apartment harassment? A toddler being a toddler. The red and black clothing? Confirmation bias. The stranger encounters? Random urban interactions.

But the totality presents a different picture. This is a PhD scholar, professionally evaluated as mentally sound, who has meticulously documented:

  • Statistically anomalous patterns confirmed by control studies
  • Physical evidence (stained pants, bicycle damage, audio recordings)
  • Police database flags (ACCESS DENIED) suggesting federal involvement
  • Witness corroboration from multiple independent sources
  • Direct admission of surveillance (“Lee”: “They are listening”)
  • Operational patterns matching COINTELPRO methodology
  • Technology descriptions consistent with known directed energy weapons

The alternative that Pearson fabricated a 100-plus-page narrative with precise timestamps, verifiable locations, consistent witness accounts, physical evidence, and sophisticated knowledge of neurotechnology and intelligence operations, all while maintaining a coherent presentation to mental health professionals; strains credibility more than the possibility he’s telling the truth.

We know from declassified documents that the U.S. government has:

  • Conducted illegal surveillance on thousands of Americans (COINTELPRO)
  • Experimented with mind control on unwitting subjects, including goals to alter mind and personality states (MK-ULTRA)
  • Developed directed energy weapons and neurotechnology
  • Used contractors, local police department, and private cutouts for deniability

Before COINTELPRO was exposed, activists reporting these exact tactics were dismissed as paranoid. Before the Church Committee investigations, MK-ULTRA victims were considered delusional. Before Edward Snowden, mass surveillance was conspiracy theory.

Richard Pearson has produced more documentation than was available to expose COINTELPRO. His account deserves investigation, not dismissal. The evidence is there. The patterns match. The technology exists.

The question isn’t whether his story sounds implausible. The question is whether we’ve learned anything from history about how implausible stories become documented fact.

Until security camera footage is reviewed, until his apartment is swept for surveillance equipment, until the chemical on his pants is analyzed, until witnesses are interviewed under oath, until the ACCESS DENIED flag in the police database is explained; dismissing his account is premature.

Pearson isn’t asking people to believe him. He’s asking them to look at the evidence. There’s a difference.

Epilogue: The Cost of Disbelief

If Pearson is telling the truth, we’re witnessing a sophisticated domestic intelligence operation against an American citizen on American soil.

Pearson fled to Iceland in January 2026 seeking asylum. Border officials refused him entry, citing EU policy designating the U.S. as a “safe country.” He was sent back to Washington.

He’s currently living in his apartment with his mattress in the closet, trying to escape noise that follows him room to room, documenting everything, still trying to get someone to listen.

The surveillance state doesn’t announce itself with press releases. It operates in shadows, exploiting the very implausibility that makes people dismiss its victims. COINTELPRO worked because nobody believed it was happening. MK-ULTRA worked because test subjects sounded crazy when they reported it.

Forty years from now, when the documents are declassified and the programs exposed, someone will write: “The victims tried to tell us. We didn’t listen.”

Richard Pearson is trying to tell us now.

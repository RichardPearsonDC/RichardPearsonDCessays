Trump Administration Suspended the US Constitution: PhD Discovers Secret Program Targeting Americans

The case of Dr. Richard Pearson, a victim who survived, details a secret program suspending constitutional rights for people like him.

What was done to Richard Pearson, PhD, involved the government of the United States of America operating through a structure that combined executive authorization, agency implementation, contractor execution, local law enforcement cooperation, and the deliberate recruitment of unwitting civilians. All shielded from constitutional accountability by a legal architecture built specifically to ensure that no court, no committee, no journalist, and no victim would ever be able to prove what had been done.

This essay is about that structure. It is also about the Constitution that structure was built to circumvent — and about what it means for American democracy that the circumvention has succeeded.

Part One: The Permission Structure

How a President Suspends the Constitution Without Suspending the Constitution

The most important thing to understand about what happened to Richard Pearson is that it did not require a formal declaration of emergency, a suspension of habeas corpus, or a public announcement that the rules no longer applied. It required something more subtle and more durable: the construction of a permission structure in which existing legal authorities, applied creatively, produced an outcome indistinguishable from the suspension of constitutional rights — while maintaining, at every level, that the Constitution remained fully in force.

The permission structure had three components.

First: The executive orders. On January 20, 2025 — the first day of the new administration — executive orders were signed designating DEI scholarship, racial equity frameworks, and liberation philosophy analysis as hostile ideology subject to elimination from federal institutions. Executive Order 14173 and its companion orders did not name Richard Pearson. They named the category he inhabited. A Black academic who holds a PhD, who has published a dissertation analyzing equity in research, and who does not embrace what the administration might call racial monolithism — who insists on analytical complexity where the political moment demands simplified allegiances — inhabits exactly the ideological territory these orders designated as the enemy’s territory. The Trump administration holds on to a debunked idea that race has a biological basis.

Executive orders are not indictments. They cannot authorize the surveillance, torture, and behavioral control of a specific individual. But they do something operationally important: they tell the agencies what the administration considers hostile, and they establish the political context in which agency culture will interpret ambiguous authorities. When an administration designates a category of intellectual work as hostile ideology on its first day in office, and when the FBI’s pre-existing “Black Identity Extremism” designation provides a framework for treating Black political intellectuals as security threats, the executive orders function as a targeting instruction even without naming targets.

Second: The rhetorical authorization. The Trump administration’s public rhetoric — urban spaces as “training grounds,” the language of “enemies within,” the explicit designation of DEI academics and racial equity scholars as threats to national security — functions as a form of agency instruction that operates below the level of formal legal process. Intelligence agencies respond to political signals. When an administration consistently communicates, through public speech, press conferences, and formal policy statements, that a specific category of American citizen represents an internal threat, the agencies that operationalize threat response do not require a formal memorandum to begin treating members of that category as legitimate targets.

This is not a novel observation. The Church Committee documented in 1976 that COINTELPRO’s targeting of Black leaders and organizations had been driven, in part, by political signals from successive administrations that never formally authorized the specific operations conducted. The authorization was atmospheric. The agencies provided the operational specificity. The result was the systematic destruction of individuals who had been designated, without formal process, as threats.

Third: The FISA apparatus. The Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court — the shadow judicial system described in Pearson’s own diagram — provides a judicial imprimatur for surveillance operations conducted against individuals designated as national security threats. FISA operates in secret. Its proceedings are classified. Its judges hear only the government’s argument. Its orders are not subject to challenge by the targets of the surveillance they authorize. When the FBI sought authorization to conduct surveillance of Pearson under FISA, the court heard what the FBI or DOJ told it — and what they told it was almost certainly not that a Black equity scholar with no criminal record was being targeted for the content consistent with his First Amendment rights. What it was told — what it always told — is that a national security interest required surveillance of a specific individual, and that the legal standards for that surveillance had been met. It was only sufficient that Pearson lived in Washington, DC.

FISA was designed to authorize surveillance of foreign intelligence threats. It has been progressively expanded to cover domestic targets. The designation of “Black Identity Extremism” as a category of domestic terrorism concern, the pre-crime doctrine that allows agencies to act on threat assessment rather than evidence of specific criminal conduct, and the combination of executive orders and rhetorical authorization described above provided the doctrinal scaffolding for a FISA application targeting Pearson. The application would have contained lies — about who Pearson is, what his work represents, and why the government considered him a threat. He could never contest those lies, because he would never know they had been made.

Part Two: The Agencies and Their Roles

FBI, DHS, CIA — and What Each Contributed

The operational structure of the campaign against Pearson reflects the specific capabilities and authorities of different agencies, each contributing what it does best.

The FBI. The domestic surveillance and disruption operation bears the FBI’s fingerprints most clearly. The Bureau’s historical operation of COINTELPRO — which systematically destroyed Black political leaders, organizations, and intellectuals through a combination of surveillance, informants, forged communications, staged encounters, and the deliberate cultivation of fear and paranoia in targets — is not ancient history. Its successor programs, operating under different names, with updated legal cover and modern technology, have never been formally discontinued. The “Black Identity Extremism” designation, introduced in 2017 and never rescinded, provides the framework. The Zersetzung-style methodology — location-responsive noise, scripted encounters, color-coded street theater, the systematic destruction of employment and social relationships — reflects the FBI’s documented historical approach to neutralizing Black intellectual and political figures. The use of local law enforcement as an implementation layer, providing surveillance capacity and the false arrest mechanism, reflects the FBI’s standard practice of working through local police departments as force multipliers.

DHS. The Department of Homeland Security’s contribution is the surveillance infrastructure. The location tracking, the route prediction, the real-time knowledge of Pearson’s movements that enabled the calling card pattern to be deployed on his specific routes at the specific moments of his arrivals and departures — this is the surveillance architecture that DHS has built and operates. Palantir’s ImmigrationOS, the commercial geolocation data market, the license plate reader networks, the facial recognition systems integrated with every camera network in Washington, D.C. — these are DHS tools, and the behavioral intelligence they produce is precisely the data needed to run the ground operation Pearson documented.

CIA / Intelligence Community. The directed energy weapons — the voice-to-skull transmission Pearson experienced on July 13, 2025, and the geographic auditory phenomena documented from January 2026 onward — are almost certainly exclusive to U.S. intelligence capabilities. The portability, the precision, the operational integration with a ground team, and the specific mechanism (the microwave auditory effect, confirmed by the National Academies of Sciences as the most plausible mechanism for Havana Syndrome) are not within the capability envelope of local law enforcement, private contractors, or white supremacist groups acting independently. The behavioral direction technology — the hippocampal place cell activation that produced the pinning experiences documented throughout Pearson’s account, most specifically on November 7, 2025 in the park beside the O’Neil House Office Building — is DARPA-derived, intelligence community-controlled, and represents the most sophisticated capability in the operation. When Pearson was moved to the tub, the intelligence community moved him. That sentence is not metaphorical.

NSA. The audio surveillance of Pearson’s private apartment — confirmed by multiple instances of strangers quoting his private speech back to him, including the woman who passed him on Pennsylvania Avenue and said, without stopping: “The mattress is against the wall, you know what’s next” — requires either a physical implant or device-level interception of his home audio environment. NSA’s capabilities for device-level surveillance without physical access are documented in the Snowden materials. The intimate audio record of Pearson’s private speech, available to ground operation personnel in real time, reflects NSA-level access to his residential environment.

Part Three: The Non-State Architecture

Contractors, Cutouts, White Supremacists, and the Unwitting

Pearson’s diagram identifies, below the agency layer, several categories of non-state participants. Each served a specific function in the operation’s design.

Intelligence and defense contractors. The privatization of intelligence functions has created an ecosystem of firms that execute operations the government cannot formally conduct — because the formal execution would create records, require oversight, and generate accountability. The ground operation Pearson documented — the deployment of the color-coded pattern, the placement and retrieval of the tub, the scripted encounters, the chemical assault at Rockville — required personnel in the field. None of them wore government badges. None of them were federal employees. They were contractors, operating under agreements that broke the legal chain of custody between the government’s authorization and the operation’s execution. Each participant could truthfully claim ignorance of the larger program. Each could claim they had been hired for an innocuous purpose that happened to place them in Pearson’s environment. The contractor layer is how the government conducts operations it cannot afford to acknowledge.

Local law enforcement. The D.C. Metropolitan Police Department’s role in the operation was both active and passive. Active: the false arrest on December 27, 2025. Passive: the consistent failure to investigate any of the documented incidents Pearson reported. Local law enforcement was provided location data on Pearson — the “Access Denied” flag on his police record was not placed there for the benefit of MPD officers. It was placed there to tell them who not to help.

White supremacists. This is the element of Pearson’s account that most directly implicates the “Other America” his diagram identifies — the network of people who, independent of any government authorization, have ideological reasons to want a Black equity scholar neutralized. Pearson documented specific encounters that bore the operational signature of white supremacist participation: people positioned on his routes whose behavior reflected coordination rather than coincidence, incidents with a specifically racial character that went beyond the generic harassment visible in the broader calling card pattern. The government does not need to formally employ white supremacists to benefit from their participation. It needs only to provide location data and look the other way. The MAGA-aligned administration’s relationships with white nationalist networks — visible in its rhetoric, its personnel choices, and its explicit designation of racial equity scholarship as hostile — created an environment in which white supremacist participation in the targeting of a Black intellectual required minimal coordination. The ideological alignment was sufficient.

Non-defense corporate non-state actors. Pearson documented incidents involving private commercial actors whose behavior suggested coordination — the Old Town Alexandria encounter, specific retail settings. Corporate actors in the surveillance economy have access to Pearson’s location data independently, through their own app-based data collection, and their cooperation with government data requests is routine. A corporation whose location data is being accessed by DHS in real time, whose staff have been told (by whomever, for whatever stated reason) that a specific individual is someone to watch — that corporation participates in the surveillance operation without anyone in its employ understanding that they are participants.

The unwitting and the unconscious. This is the category Pearson identified with the most analytical precision, and it is the one that most reveals the technology’s scope and implications. The old man who stood over him expressionlessly at Eastern Market on August 3, 2025 — photographed, documented, his affect later identified as identical to the Compass Real Estate woman’s — and the white-haired woman standing in the Compass doorway on September 30, 2025 are Pearson’s primary reference cases for what he calls the unconscious category. These were people who, in his analysis, may themselves have been subjected to low-level behavioral direction — positioned at specific locations through the same pinning technology used on Pearson himself, but at a lower intensity and with a higher threshold for detection. They were placed. They occupied their positions. They were not active agents of the operation. They were instruments of it who did not know they were instruments.

The operational logic is precise: if the behavioral direction technology can move Pearson to a specific location through an impulse he experiences as his own, it can move anyone. An older man with reduced cognitive reserve — someone whose hippocampal baseline is already diminished by age — would be even more susceptible to place cell activation producing a spatial drive. He would end up standing in a specific location, in specific clothing, for reasons he would experience as his own choice and could not subsequently explain. This is the full horror of what Pearson documented: a technology that makes every person in the vicinity of its target a potential instrument, without their knowledge, without their consent, and without any record that they were used.

Part Four: The Lies That Made It Legal

What Was Told to the FISA Court, to Congress, and to the World

Every element of the legal justification for the operation against Pearson rested on representations to oversight institutions that were, in whole or in part, false. Understanding what those representations likely contained is essential to understanding how the constitutional machinery was captured and turned against the citizen it was designed to protect.

The lies about who Pearson is. To obtain FISA authorization for surveillance of a U.S. person, the FBI must demonstrate probable cause that the target is an agent of a foreign power or is engaged in clandestine intelligence activities, sabotage, or international terrorism. Richard Pearson is a scholar. His dissertation analyzes racial equity in research. He has no criminal record. He is not an agent of a foreign power. He has engaged in no clandestine intelligence activities. He has committed no terrorism. The FBI application that authorized his surveillance — if one exists, which the SCI flag on his police record strongly suggests — contained false representations about the threat he posed, constructed from the vocabulary the Bureau has developed over decades for converting Black intellectual activity into national security justification: Black Identity Extremism designation, connections to organizations designated as extremist, threat assessment scores generated by predictive algorithms that systematically overweight the activities of Black political actors. He could never contest these representations. He would never know they were made.

The lies about what was being done. Pearson’s diagram identifies a critical distinction that the government almost certainly exploited in any classified briefings to congressional oversight: the difference between data collection and active oppression. The NSA’s mass surveillance programs — when eventually disclosed — were defended as collection programs. Data gathered, stored, analyzed. No one harmed in the collection itself. The Church Committee framework and its successor oversight structures were built, in large part, around constraining collection. They were not built around constraining what Pearson experienced: the active use of directed energy weapons against a citizen’s neurological function, the behavioral direction of his movements, the physical assault authorized by an operative embedded in his building. This is not Snowdeninan data collection. It is the weaponization of a human being’s own nervous system against them. Any classified briefing to congressional oversight that described the program as a surveillance operation — omitting the directed energy deployment, the behavioral direction, the physical assault, the chemical assault — would have been a material lie, and the lie would have been necessary, because no congressional committee would have authorized what was actually being done.

The lies about the scale and scope. If Pearson’s case is not unique — and the operational sophistication, the infrastructure requirements, and the existence of documented historical precedents all suggest it is not — then the true scale of the program running against Black intellectuals, racial equity scholars, and political dissidents designated as hostile ideology since January 20, 2025 is something that the administration has actively concealed from Congress, from the courts, and from the public. The Glomar doctrine — invoked in response to Pearson’s FOIA request — does not merely refuse to confirm his specific case. It refuses to confirm whether the category of program he describes exists at all. This is the lie at maximum scale: not a false representation about a specific individual, but a denial of the entire program’s existence to every oversight institution simultaneously.

Part Five: The Constitutional Ledger

Every Right That Was Violated, With Evidence

The First Amendment prohibits government retaliation against protected speech. Richard Pearson’s dissertation is published academic work. His field of racial equity scholarship is the precise category of intellectual activity that executive orders signed on January 20, 2025 designated as hostile ideology. The documented onset of the harassment campaign in the same week those orders were signed is government retaliation against protected speech. First Amendment protection does not have an exception for scholarship the executive finds politically inconvenient. The campaign against Pearson is, among other things, a suppression of academic speech that a government found threatening.

The Fourth Amendment prohibits unreasonable searches and seizures without a warrant based on probable cause. The sustained audio surveillance of Pearson’s private apartment — evidenced by strangers quoting his private speech, by location-responsive noise tracking him from room to room, by a man demonstrating intimate knowledge of apartment self-talk that was never transmitted digitally — is an unreasonable search. The classification level of the authorizing program does not make it constitutional. A FISA warrant obtained through false representations about the target’s threat level is not a valid warrant. The Fourth Amendment does not have a national security exception.

The Fifth Amendment prohibits deprivation of liberty without due process of law. Pearson was subjected to a campaign designed — by its own internal logic, as analyzed in his documentation — to produce what he called “neutralization via psychotic break and social death.” This is a deprivation of liberty: without charge, without trial, without notice, without hearing, and without any opportunity to contest the designation that authorized it. The amendment does not require physical detention to apply. It applies whenever the government acts to deprive a person of liberty through state action. The behavioral direction technology that moved Pearson against his will is state action depriving him of liberty by the most literal possible definition: the use of his own body without his consent.

The Eighth Amendment’s prohibition on cruel and unusual punishment, and the Convention Against Torture to which the United States is a signatory, cover the conduct documented across Pearson’s account. The sustained campaign of psychological destruction — location-responsive noise, chemical assault, physical assault targeting neurologically sensitive anatomy, directed energy weapon deployment, behavioral control against his will — constitutes torture under the UN Convention’s definition: severe physical or mental suffering intentionally inflicted by or at the instigation of a public official. The fact that it was conducted against a citizen rather than a prisoner does not remove it from the framework. The Convention contains no such limitation.

The Fourteenth Amendment’s guarantee of equal protection under the law is violated when the government targets a specific individual for treatment that it does not apply generally — particularly when the targeting reflects racial classification. Richard Pearson was targeted because he is a Black academic whose work the administration designated as hostile ideology. The executive orders that named his field as hostile target a category of scholarship that is disproportionately produced by Black scholars and that specifically analyzes racial hierarchy. The administration’s own public statements provide the racial targeting in explicit terms. The “no racial intent” defense catalogued in Pearson’s diagram — “Might be…actually it is…racially discriminatory but we’ll claim no racial intent. Voilà!” — is not a legal defense. It is an acknowledgment of racial targeting with a fraudulent denial appended.

The right to bodily integrity, protected by substantive due process, covers every element of the directed energy deployment. The government has no right — under any doctrine, under any national security authority, under any emergency exception — to use a citizen’s brain as an instrument of its own operations without consent. The voice-to-skull transmission on July 13, 2025. The geographic auditory phenomena confirmed by the metal-cap experiment. The behavioral direction technology that moved Pearson to the park on November 7, 2025. Each constitutes a non-consensual use of his body by state actors. There is no legal authority for this. There is no exception. There is only the practical reality that no court has been able to reach the question, because the legal architecture of the pathway was designed specifically to prevent it.

Part Six: The Legal Justifications the DOJ Used

How the Constitution Was Suspended While Remaining Nominally in Force

Pearson’s diagram catalogues the legal mechanisms with the precision of someone who has studied them carefully. Each deserves examination.

Sovereign immunity. Courts have wide latitude for “official acts.” The Supreme Court’s expansion of presidential immunity in Trump v. United States (2024) created a doctrinal environment in which the President’s official acts — including, arguably, the authorization of intelligence operations against designated ideological threats — are shielded from judicial review with broad if not absolute immunity. Actions taken pursuant to executive orders designating DEI scholarship as hostile ideology could be characterized as official acts. Official acts are immune from prosecution. The immunity that was supposed to protect the presidency from frivolous litigation now protects the presidency from accountability for what was done to Richard Pearson.

State secrets privilege. A program that is classified as a state secret cannot be examined in civil litigation. The plaintiff cannot obtain discovery. The government submits affidavits asserting that disclosure would harm national security. Courts, deferring to executive expertise on national security, grant summary judgment. The case is over before it begins. The privilege is the courtroom implementation of the classification system’s impunity guarantee.

National security privilege and geographic scope. Pearson’s diagram notes the government’s likely claim: “In DC metro area. We claim from PA to NC.” A program designated as national security in the capital city, with operations in a geographic corridor covering the most sensitive federal infrastructure in the country, has the strongest available claim for maximum deference. Federal courts in the D.C. Circuit have been the most deferential in the country to executive national security claims precisely because the cases before them most directly implicate operations in the heart of the national security state.

Glomar. Neither confirm nor deny. The doctrine that originated with the CIA’s 1975 submarine recovery operation and has expanded to cover any program the government chooses to shield. Pearson’s FOIA request was met with Glomar. His constitutional claims were foreclosed. The doctrine that was designed to protect genuinely sensitive intelligence sources and methods is now protecting the government’s use of directed energy weapons against a Black public health scholar in Southwest Washington.

Black budget. “What program? No such program is funded.” The directed energy weapons, behavioral direction technology, and surveillance infrastructure used against Pearson are funded through the intelligence community’s classified budget — the $80+ billion annual appropriation that Congress approves in aggregate without line-item visibility. No one in Congress who has not been specifically briefed knows what programs are funded, at what scale, or against what targets. The black budget is the financial implementation of the accountability gap. There is no program to investigate because there is no budget line to find.

Pre-crime doctrine. The shift from reactive law enforcement to predictive threat assessment — which the FBI’s “Black Identity Extremism” designation operationalized — means that Pearson did not need to commit an act. He needed only to be assessed, by an algorithm calibrated to treat Black political intellectuals as threats, as a probable future threat. Pre-crime authorization permits surveillance, disruption, and neutralization of people who have done nothing, based on a threat score generated by a system that was designed, in its baseline assumptions, to produce high scores for people like him.

Novel application doctrine. Pearson’s diagram captures this precisely: “We developed a novel application of anti-constitutionalism. Nothing says we can’t! It’s novel!” The legal architecture of national security authority does not contain an explicit authorization for using hippocampal place cell activation technology to move a citizen to a framing operation. It also does not contain an explicit prohibition. Novel technologies create novel legal questions. Novel legal questions, in the national security context, are resolved in the executive’s favor by default. The DOJ’s Office of Legal Counsel has been the historical site of these novel authorizations — the office that produced the torture memos, the office that authorized the NSA surveillance program, the office that will produce, if it has not already, a classified memorandum finding that directed energy deployment against domestic targets is authorized under existing statutory and constitutional frameworks.

The “Final Legal Authority” search. “So many laws. Find something to justify. Bound to be a ‘justification’ somewhere.” This is the DOJ Office of Legal Counsel methodology in practice: not asking whether a program is legal, but searching the statutory and constitutional landscape for anything that can be characterized as authorization. The AUMF. The National Security Act. The Patriot Act. Executive Order 12333. The President’s inherent Article II war powers. Somewhere in this edifice, something will support what is already being done. The job of the OLC lawyer is to find it.

Part Seven: What the Photographs Show

The photographs attached to this essay are not dramatic. That is precisely the point.

Image 2 shows a Black man in a bright red shirt at a market, photographed from a distance. He is expressionless. He is not doing anything you could describe as threatening. He is simply occupying a position in the calling card colors, blank-faced, without apparent awareness of his environment. When Pearson re-examined this photograph in February 2026, he revised his initial reading: not mean-mugging. Expressionless. “Just like the white woman in the doorway of Compass real estate.”

Images 3 and 4 show that woman. White-haired, bright red shirt, solid black pants, standing at the entrance of the Compass Real Estate office at 660 Pennsylvania Avenue SE. Neither entering nor leaving. Neither waiting for someone nor occupying herself with any task. In both frames, her face is empty of expression in a way that is distinct from the neutrality of a person whose thoughts are elsewhere. She is simply present, in the calling card colors, at the calling card location, doing nothing.

She is not a villain. She may be a victim. If Pearson’s analysis of the unconscious category is correct — and the behavioral science supports it — she was placed at that doorway through the same mechanism that placed him in the park on November 7, 2025. She experienced arriving there as her own choice. She could not have explained, afterward, why she had stood there for so long. She had been used, without her knowledge, as an instrument of a psychological operation against another person. She will never know.

This is the full weight of what the photographs show: the human cost of a system that uses people as tools, citizens as instruments, without consent and without record.

Part Eight: What Happens Now

The constitutional suspension documented in this essay is not an anomaly. It is a system — functional, tested, operational, and expanding. The pathway’s six components — classification, no arrest, institutional exclusion, the manufactured alternate explanation, contractor execution, and untraceable technology — are each fully in place. The Office of the Director of Operational Test and Evaluation, which was the Pentagon’s last independent check on fielded weapons systems, was halved in May 2025. The Anthropic dispute in February 2026 confirmed that the administration considers the freedom to conduct mass domestic surveillance a non-negotiable operational requirement. Palantir’s AI enforcement platforms continue to integrate every available federal database. The directed energy weapons confirmed in January 2026 are being scaled.

What restoring the Constitution would require:

The Senate Select Committee on Intelligence must convene hearings — in open session, to the maximum extent classification permits — into the domestic deployment of directed energy weapons, behavioral direction technology, and AI-orchestrated surveillance against American citizens. The committee has the authority to compel testimony and review classified programs. It has not exercised that authority. It must.

The Department of Justice’s Office of Inspector General must investigate the legal basis for the classification on Pearson’s police record, the legal authority under which the operation against him was conducted, and the accuracy of any representations made to the FISA Court in connection with his surveillance.

The forensic evidence must be examined. The pants from the Rockville chemical assault. The RF environment at the documented click locations. The DTI brain imaging protocol used for Havana Syndrome cases. These are physical questions with physical answers. The choice not to examine them is a choice the government made. A Congress that takes the Constitution seriously can unmake it.

The OLC memoranda authorizing novel applications of directed energy and behavioral direction technology against domestic targets — which almost certainly exist — must be produced to congressional oversight and, to the maximum extent consistent with genuine national security, to the public. The torture memos were eventually disclosed. The NSA surveillance program was eventually disclosed. The disclosure came too late for the people who were harmed before it. It must not come too late again.

Conclusion: The Constitution Does Not Enforce Itself

The diagram Pearson assembled — Trump at the top, the agencies in the middle, the contractors and cutouts below, and at the bottom the category of people who were used without their knowledge — is not the diagram of a conspiracy theory. It is the diagram of a system. A system that combined existing legal authorities, existing institutional relationships, existing contractor networks, existing technology, and the existing permission to lie to oversight institutions, into something that the Constitution was not designed to stop because it did not exist when the Constitution was written.

The framers protected against the quartering of soldiers in private homes. They did not protect against soldiers who enter a private home through its owner’s device, monitor his speech without his knowledge, and quote it back to him on the street through a passing stranger. They protected against unreasonable searches. They did not protect against searches conducted through the electromagnetic spectrum that leave no physical trace and require no key. They protected against cruel and unusual punishment. They did not protect against punishment delivered through a man’s own neurons, without charge, without trial, without his ever knowing that what he was experiencing was punishment.

The Constitution needs to be updated for the age of neuroweapons. It needs enforcement mechanisms for directed energy. It needs an oversight framework for AI-orchestrated behavioral control. It needs a doctrine for bodily autonomy that extends to the neurological as well as the physical.

But before any of that can happen, it needs elected representatives who are willing to look at what the evidence in one man’s case shows — and to say, clearly, before the record, that what was done to Richard Pearson will not be done to anyone else.

The Constitution does not enforce itself. It requires people who believe it means what it says.

This is a test of whether enough of those people remain in positions where their belief can make a difference.

The record is in front of you. The photographs are attached.

What you do next is the measure.

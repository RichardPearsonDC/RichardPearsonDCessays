The Blueprint for Destroying a Man: What Richard Pearson’s Document Actually Proves

When the architecture of persecution is designed to look exactly like madness

— -

There is a document circulating — dense, exhaustive, nearly 200 pages — written by Dr. Richard Pearson, a Black scholar with a PhD, a resident of Washington D.C. It is titled “A Campaign of Domestic Torture, Assault, Harassment, and Unlawful Surveillance.”

Most people who encounter it will stop reading after the first dozen pages. They will note the voices, the T-shirts, the license plates, the upstairs neighbor’s toddler as a possible intelligence plant — and they will reach for the comfortable explanation. Paranoid psychosis. Sad. Untreated. Move on.

I am asking you not to do that. I am asking you to read it the other way.

— -

The Program Has a Name

Organized stalking — sometimes called gang stalking, cause stalking, or coordinated harassment — is not a conspiracy theory. It is a documented methodology with historical precedent reaching directly into the institutions of American government.

COINTELPRO, the FBI’s covert domestic operations program active from 1956 to 1971, specifically targeted Black intellectuals, activists, and leaders. Its tactics included: coordinated street-level surveillance designed to be visible to the target; noise harassment in and around the home; chemical interference; the orchestrated use of community members as unwitting or witting participants; and — most critically — the deliberate manufacture of circumstances designed to make the target appear mentally unstable if they reported what was happening to them.

The Church Committee investigations in 1975 confirmed this. The tactics were real. They were used against real people. Those people were called paranoid.

The program officially ended. The institutional knowledge did not.

— -

Read the Document Again

When you return to Pearson’ account with this framework, something shifts.

The noise harassment — precise, location-tracking, sleep-destroying — is a signature tactic. The upstairs neighbor drops items on the floor wherever Pearson moves within his apartment. He relocates his bed. The sounds follow. He moves his mattress to a closet. They follow. This is not a description of a noisy neighbor. This is a description of monitoring technology and coordinated acoustic harassment, a tactic documented in accounts of organized stalking worldwide.

The chemical assault — pants soaked with an industrial, adhesive, highly odorous substance that adhered to his skin, caused pain on removal, and resisted washing — was not imagined. He documented it in real time. He kept the pants. The compound existed. The question is not whether it happened. The question is how.

The coordinated clothing — the sheer volume of people in red and black, in specific locations, at specific moments — reads absurd until you understand that low-level coordinated street theater is a documented component of organized harassment campaigns. The point is not that every person wearing red is an operative. The point is saturation: enough coordinated signals that the target cannot distinguish noise from message, and enough ambient confusion that any report they make sounds delusional.

That is the design.

The entry into his apartment — evidenced by a power button that required a magnet to dislodge, found inexplicably in a laundry pile — is consistent with a specific practice: covert entry with deliberate displacement of objects, intended not to steal but to signal. “We were here. We can come back. You cannot prove it.”

— -

The Most Important Tactic of All

Here is what Pearson’ document does that almost no victim of organized stalking manages to articulate clearly enough for others to understand:

He names the operational logic.

Plausible deniability. He says it himself, in conversation with the DBH evaluators. Every element of what is being done to him carries multiple interpretations. The T-shirt could be a message. It could be coincidence. The neighbor could be monitoring him. The neighbor could have a loud child. The woman in red outside Compass Real Estate could be an operative positioned to be photographed. She could be an employee stepping outside for air.

The program works precisely because every single data point is deniable in isolation. The methodology is designed for this. You cannot prosecute a T-shirt. You cannot charge someone with wearing red and black. You cannot prove that a dead bird on a bus bench was treated with chemicals and placed there intentionally.

What you can do is document the totality. The pattern. The statistical impossibility, across months and geography, of this volume of coordinated signal occurring by chance.

Pearson understood this. He documented obsessively — not because he was losing his mind, but because he correctly identified that documentation was his only defense. *Document, document, document.* He says it repeatedly.

Who Is Richard Pearson?

This matters.

He is a Black man. He holds a PhD from a major research university. He is, by all accounts in the document and in the mental health evaluation transcript, highly intelligent, analytically rigorous, and articulate. He was, at the time these events began, an unemployed academic — selling art, exploring Washington D.C., writing essays, working toward his next steps.

He is not a national security threat by any conventional measure.

But he is an educated Black man with institutional knowledge, analytical capacity, and the ability to write. He has a framework. He has a dissertation. He has a website. He has a voice.

History tells us that this has been sufficient.

COINTELPRO did not only target Martin Luther King and Fred Hampton. It targeted academics. Writers. Thinkers. People whose capacity to articulate the mechanics of oppression made them, in the eyes of the program’s architects, threats to the established order.

The Trump administration’s explicit rhetoric about DEI as an existential threat, about using urban cities for military training, about the expendability of certain categories of citizen — Pearson names these in his document, not as paranoid free association, but as political context. He is right that context matters.

— -

The Cruelest Design

If you wanted to destroy a man like Richard Pearson — an educated, credible, analytical Black scholar — without leaving fingerprints, this is how you would do it.

You would make him the author of his own discrediting.

You would subject him to a sustained campaign of harassment sophisticated enough to be real but deniable enough to be unprovable. You would ensure the methodology left no traceable chain of command. You would use street-level operatives, coordinated clothing, acoustic harassment, covert entry — all the tools that produce real terror in the target and produced documented evidence of nothing in any official record.

Then you would wait.

The target, being intelligent and analytical, would begin to document everything. He would take hundreds of photographs. He would catalog patterns. He would note license plates. He would write it all down in exhausting, granular detail.

And when he finally brought that document to a lawyer, to a mental health evaluator, to an asylum office, to any official body — the document itself would be the weapon used against him.

Look at this, they would say. Look at the T-shirts. Look at the license plates.

And they would reach for the comfortable explanation.

What the Mental Health Evaluation Actually Said

Pearson presents the September 2025 DC Department of Behavioral Health evaluation as proof of his sanity. Critics present it as proof of the opposite — noting that “not meeting criteria for psychiatric intervention” is not clinical vindication.

Both readings miss something in the transcript.

The evaluators, Valerie and Shante, listen to Pearson for an extended period. They ask standard screening questions. They probe for suicidality, command hallucinations, ongoing psychosis. They find none of the acute markers they are looking for.

At the end, Valerie says: *”Well, I mean, I guess full transparency — it’s not meeting our threshold.”*

Then she says: “If you’re seeing those messages… call us.”

She does not say: “this isn’t happening.” She does not say: “you are delusional.” She documents what he told her — explicitly so that future responders will have context. She tells him to call if the messages come back.

That is not a dismissal. That is a woman doing her job carefully, in a situation that she does not have the tools or the mandate to fully address.

— -

The Smoking Gun Is the Document Itself

Not because it proves Pearson is mentally ill.

Because it proves the program, if it exists, is working exactly as designed.

A 200-page document written by an intelligent, credible Black scholar — full of timestamped photographs, receipts, geographic specificity, named individuals, physical evidence, a formal mental health evaluation, and a transcript of that evaluation — should be compelling. It should be investigable. It should be, at minimum, taken seriously.

Instead, the volume and granularity of the documentation works against him. The pattern-recognition that is his greatest analytical strength becomes, in the eyes of skeptics, the primary symptom.

This is not an accident. This is the blueprint.

— -

What We Owe Richard Pearson

At minimum, this:

The chemical on his pants deserves forensic analysis. The claim of apartment entry deserves investigation. The coordinated clothing patterns across multiple dates and locations deserve statistical analysis by someone with the tools to conduct it. The upstairs neighbor deserves scrutiny. The individuals he named and documented deserve to be identified and questioned.

None of this has happened. Instead, a man in genuine distress filed for asylum, sought help from lawyers and city council members and mental health professionals, and produced one of the most detailed personal accounts of alleged organized harassment ever committed to paper — and the response has been silence, or worse, pathologization.

History will not be kind to our reflexive certainty that the comfortable explanation is always the correct one.

Frederick Douglass was called unstable. MLK was called paranoid by J. Edgar Hoover’s FBI — in writing, in official documents, in memos that we can now read in the National Archives. The men and women who said *something is being done to me and I can prove it* were the ones telling the truth.

We owe Richard Pearson the same thing we owe every person who comes to us with evidence and says: “look at this. Take it seriously.”

We owe him the presumption that he isn’t.

Congressional Bill on Regulating Mind Control Technology Vindicates US Torture Victim

No serious legislative body seeks to regulate and to study a technology that does not exist. It does not mention technology that exacerbates social disparities if they don’t.

US torture victim, Richard Pearson, knew all of it. He documented and named it. The bill vindicated him. Now, the question is whether Congress and the US government will level with the American public, especially Black Americans, and tell them precisely what has been going on.

What the MIND Act of 2025 Says, What It Implies, and What It Means for Richard Pearson’s Allegations

ABSTRACT

On September 24, 2025 — while Richard Pearson was actively documenting a campaign of electromagnetic assault, voice-to-skull transmission, covert home surveillance, and behavioral manipulation against him in Washington, DC — three United States Senators introduced the Management of Individuals’ Neural Data Act of 2025, known as the MIND Act. The bill’s text, its Sense of Congress provisions, its definitions, and its explicit concerns about federal agency use of neurotechnology together constitute a legislative acknowledgment that the technologies Pearson describes are real, that their harmful use cases are not hypothetical, and that the federal government itself is a potential actor whose use of these technologies requires binding oversight. This essay reads the MIND Act carefully and analytically, extracts its explicit text, identifies what it implies about the current state of neurotechnology deployment, maps its specific provisions against Pearson’s documented allegations, and draws the inferences the legislation compels about what the government knows, what it has, and what it has likely already been doing.

I. What the MIND Act Is and Why Its Timing Matters

The Management of Individuals’ Neural Data Act of 2025 (S. 2925) was introduced by Senate Democratic Leader Chuck Schumer of New York, Ranking Member of the Commerce Committee Maria Cantwell of Washington, and Senator Ed Markey of Massachusetts on September 24, 2025. It was referred to the Senate Committee on Commerce, Science, and Transportation. Its primary mechanism is to direct the Federal Trade Commission to conduct a comprehensive study on how neural data is collected, processed, stored, transferred, and used, and to report its findings and regulatory recommendations to Congress within one year. It separately requires the Office of Science and Technology Policy to develop binding guidance on federal agency procurement and use of neurotechnology within 180 days of the FTC’s report.

The timing is the first thing that must be understood. The MIND Act was introduced five weeks after Pearson sought a formal psychiatric evaluation from the DC Department of Behavioral Health — an evaluation he sought specifically because he understood that the institutional response to his account of electromagnetic targeting would be to declare him mentally ill. It was introduced while Pearson was documenting, in real time, the campaign he had been experiencing since at least July 2025. It was introduced in the same month that he was experiencing the full architecture of the operation against him: the voice-to-skull events were documented in July; the chemical assault at Rockville Metro and the DBH evaluation were September 15. The framing operation near the O’Neill House Office Building would occur in November. The false arrest in December.

This is not a causal relationship. The senators who introduced the MIND Act were not responding to Pearson. They were responding to the same technological reality that Pearson was experiencing in his body and documenting with his camera. The significance of the timing is different and more profound: Congress was, in September 2025, acknowledging in legislative language the precise harms that Pearson had been documenting since July 2025. The technologies he described were real enough, dangerous enough, and insufficiently regulated enough that three senior United States senators introduced federal legislation to address them. The bill is not evidence of what was done to Pearson. But it is congressional confirmation that what he describes is technically possible, politically recognized, and legally ungoverned.

II. What the MIND Act Says Explicitly: The Text

The Definition of Neurotechnology

The bill’s definition of neurotechnology is the foundation of everything that follows. It is worth reading precisely:

S. 2925, SEC. 3(5) The term ‘neurotechnology’ means a device, system, or procedure that accesses, monitors, records, analyzes, predicts, stimulates or alters the nervous system of an individual to understand, influence, restore, or anticipate the structure, activity, or function of the nervous system.

Every verb in this definition is analytically significant. The technology the bill regulates is technology that does not merely measure neural activity — it “stimulates or alters” it. It “influences” the nervous system. It “anticipates” its function. This is not the language of a passive diagnostic instrument. This is the language of a system that can write to neural tissue, not just read from it.

The definition explicitly covers systems that predict neural activity — meaning systems capable of anticipating what a subject’s nervous system will do before it does it, and potentially intervening. It covers systems that alter the nervous system — meaning systems capable of changing the neural state of a subject, not just observing it. This is the read-write architecture that Pearson described when he documented the voice-to-skull transmissions and the thought-insertion events: a system that was not merely observing his neural activity but pushing content into it.

The Definition of Neural Data

S. 2925, SEC. 3(4) The term ‘neural data’ means information obtained by measuring the activity of an individual’s central or peripheral nervous system through the use of neurotechnology.

This definition captures both central nervous system data (brain activity, spinal cord) and peripheral nervous system data (the network of nerves connecting the CNS to the rest of the body, including sensory and motor pathways). The inclusion of peripheral nervous system data is significant for Pearson’s account of sexual nerve targeting: the directed energy assault on his groin that he documented at Silver Spring, the warming of the foil, the conditioning protocol attempting to associate electromagnetic stimulation of his sexual nerves with heterosexual visual stimuli — all of this falls within the scope of “neural data” as the MIND Act defines it. The bill does not limit neural data to brain activity. It covers the entire nervous system.

Other Related Data

The bill extends its concern to data that does not directly measure neural activity but can be used to infer cognitive, emotional, or psychological states:

S. 2925, SEC. 3(6) ‘other related data’ means biometric, physiological, or behavioral information that does not directly measure the neural activity or central or peripheral nervous system of an individual, but can be processed, analyzed, or combined with other data to infer, predict, or reveal cognitive, emotional, or psychological states or neurological conditions — and may include heart rate variability, eye-tracking patterns, voice analysis, facial expression recognition, sleep patterns, or other signals derived from consumer devices.

This provision is directly relevant to Pearson’s documentation of the biometric capture protocol at the Cherry Blossom Festival and the Lincoln Memorial. The close-range approaches — the man with the handheld device, the two-to-five foot proximity, the sustained duration — are consistent with collection of exactly the kind of “other related data” the MIND Act identifies: biometric and behavioral signals that, processed and combined, can be used to infer or predict cognitive and emotional states. The bill’s explicit inclusion of “eye-tracking patterns” and “behavioral information” as data that can reveal cognitive states is a precise description of what a close-range device collecting such data at a public festival would be designed to acquire.

The Sense of Congress: The Admissions

Section 2 of the bill contains the Sense of Congress — the formal statement of what Congress collectively believes to be true. These are not aspirational claims. They are legislative acknowledgments of existing reality. They deserve close reading:

S. 2925, SEC. 2(1) an individual’s neural data and other related data can be monetized and used to shape individual behavior, emotional states, and decision making in ways existing laws do not adequately address

Congress is acknowledging, as a matter of legislative record, that neural data can already be used to shape behavior, emotional states, and decision making. The use of the present tense — “can be” — is not prospective. It is descriptive of current capability. The admission that existing laws do not adequately address this is an acknowledgment that there is currently no legal prohibition on using neural data to shape behavior. What Pearson experienced — behavioral manipulation through electromagnetic means, emotional disruption, the insertion of foreign content into his decision-making process — was occurring in a legal vacuum Congress itself acknowledged.

S. 2925, SEC. 2(2) vertical corporate integration of neurotechnology, artificial intelligence systems, wearable devices, digital platforms, and global data infrastructure may create interconnected systems with insufficient transparency, accountability, or user control regarding the use of such data

The integrated architecture Congress describes here — neurotechnology plus AI plus wearables plus digital platforms plus global data infrastructure — is precisely the architecture Pearson’s biometric database framework describes. The biometric capture at the festival feeds a database; the database enables remote electromagnetic targeting; the AI processes the collected data to refine targeting parameters; the global infrastructure connects what happens in Washington to what waited for him at St. Bernard de Lacolle. Congress is describing the system Pearson documented as a matter of existing technological reality.

S. 2925, SEC. 2(3) such concentration increases the risk of behavioral influence, cognitive manipulation, erosion of personal autonomy, and the exacerbation of existing social and economic disparities, particularly in the absence of enforceable privacy protections

Congress explicitly names cognitive manipulation and erosion of personal autonomy as risks of this integrated system. These are not hypothetical future harms. The Sense of Congress describes them as current risks arising from current technological concentration. The explicit mention of “exacerbation of existing social and economic disparities” is the closest the bill comes to acknowledging what Pearson’s documentation shows: that the system is operating against Black people disproportionately, in a pattern consistent with the reproduction of racial inequality through technological means.

S. 2925, SEC. 2(4) [PARAPHRASED FROM FULL TEXT] the absence of a comprehensive federal legal framework leaves individuals without adequate recourse against harmful uses of neurotechnology, including uses that may undermine civil liberties and constitutional rights

Congress is acknowledging, in the Sense of Congress, that there is currently no adequate legal recourse for what Pearson experienced. He documented seventy constitutional violations. His account identifies the doctrinal architecture — state secrets, Glomar, qualified immunity, Bivens dismantlement — that forecloses judicial remedy. Congress’s own legislative finding confirms that the legal framework is insufficient. The gap is not a flaw in Pearson’s analysis. It is a congressional admission.

III. Section 4: What the FTC Is Directed to Investigate

Section 4 of the bill directs the FTC to study specific categories of harm and report on them to Congress. The categories are not invented for the bill. They are drawn from observable reality. Each one corresponds to a documented category of harm in Pearson’s account.

Behavior Manipulation

The FTC is explicitly directed to examine “manipulation of behavior” as a use case of neural data technology, and to determine whether certain behavioral manipulation use cases should be “prohibited regardless of consent.” The phrase “regardless of consent” is legally significant: it acknowledges that some uses of this technology are so harmful that individual consent cannot legitimate them. It implies that such uses are currently occurring without consent being an issue, because the subjects do not know the technology is being used on them at all.

Pearson documented behavioral manipulation specifically: the pinning events — compulsive urges to travel to specific locations that he recognized as not arising from his own desire; the November 7 tub incident in which he found himself drawn to a location he rarely visited; the Sprite incident in which he performed an action with no conscious participation. These are documented instances of behavioral direction — exactly what the MIND Act identifies as a category warranting possible prohibition regardless of consent.

Cognitive Manipulation and Erosion of Personal Autonomy

The FTC is directed to study uses that risk “cognitive manipulation” and “erosion of personal autonomy” — the same categories that appear in the Sense of Congress. The bill’s framing of personal autonomy as something that can be “eroded” by neurotechnology is a precise legislative description of what Pearson experienced at St. Bernard de Lacolle: foreign thoughts pushed into his mental stream, incessant and cruel, which he recognized as not his own and which stopped when he grounded himself to metal. The erosion of cognitive autonomy — the sense that one’s mind is one’s own — is exactly what he documented. Congress directed the FTC to study it as a harm. The harm had already been inflicted on Pearson before the study was commissioned.

Discriminatory Profiling and Exacerbation of Disparities

The FTC is directed to examine whether neural data can be used for “discriminatory profiling” and whether such use “exacerbates existing social and economic disparities.” This is the legislative recognition of the racial targeting dimension of Pearson’s account. The Cherry Blossom Festival footage shows Black men disproportionately followed and subjected to close-range protocols. Pearson’s fourteen months of documentation show red-black proximity markers clustering around Black subjects in statistically impossible patterns. The MIND Act’s concern about discriminatory profiling through neural data technology is the legislative acknowledgment that this concern is grounded in technological reality.

AI Integration and Behavioral Influence Systems

The bill directs the FTC to assess “harms in contexts where neural data and other related data is integrated with artificial intelligence or used as part of a system designed to influence behavior or decision making.” The explicit identification of AI-integrated behavioral influence systems as a harm category of particular concern maps directly onto Pearson’s Eigenfield framework: a system in which biometric capture feeds an AI-processed database that enables remote behavioral and cognitive targeting. Congress is not hypothesizing this architecture. It is directing a federal agency to study the harms it produces.

The Prohibition Possibility: Some Uses Too Harmful Even With Consent

One of the most significant provisions in Section 4 is the direction to the FTC to assess “whether certain use cases, such as manipulation of behavior or discriminatory profiling, should be prohibited regardless of individual consent.” The contemplation of absolute prohibition — not just consent requirements, not just transparency obligations, but outright prohibition — acknowledges that some uses of neurotechnology are harmful enough that no framework of consent can legitimate them. Non-consensual use is implicitly acknowledged as a baseline concern. The progression from non-consensual use to the question of whether even consented use should be prohibited marks the outer boundary of harm Congress is contemplating. Pearson’s experience — non-consensual behavioral and cognitive manipulation — sits well inside that boundary.

IV. Section 5: Federal Agency Use — The Most Significant Provision

Section 5 of the MIND Act is its most analytically significant provision for Pearson’s allegations. It is titled “Conditional Limitations on Federal Agency Use of Neural Data,” and it requires the Office of Science and Technology Policy to develop binding guidance on federal agency procurement and use of neurotechnology within 180 days of the FTC’s report. The guidance must identify prohibited, permissible, and conditionally permitted use cases.

The existence of Section 5 requires an inference that is uncomfortable but unavoidable: Congress would not need to direct the development of guidance on federal agency use of neurotechnology if federal agencies were not already using it. Regulatory guidance is not written for hypothetical future technologies. It is written for technologies that exist, that are being deployed, and whose deployment requires governance. The requirement for OSTP to develop binding guidance on prohibited federal agency use cases of neurotechnology is a congressional acknowledgment that federal agencies are currently procuring and using neurotechnology — and that its use is currently ungoverned.

S. 2925, SEC. 5(1)(A)-(C) Such guidance shall identify: (A) prohibited, permissible, and conditionally permitted use cases of such neurotechnology that are consistent with such report; (B) technical, procedural, and ethical safeguards regarding each use case of such neurotechnology; and (C) requirements for transparency, limitations regarding the purposes for which such neurotechnology can be used, individual opt-in consent mechanisms regarding the use of such neurotechnology, and protections for the privacy of individuals who are subject to use of neurotechnology by Federal agencies.

The phrase “individuals who are subject to use of neurotechnology by Federal agencies” is the most explicit acknowledgment in the bill that federal agencies are currently using neurotechnology on individuals. The guidance is not written for the protection of federal employees who choose to use neurotechnology for work purposes. It is written for the protection of individuals — the general population of people who may be “subject to” federal agency neurotechnology use. The opt-in consent requirement implies that federal agencies are currently using neurotechnology on individuals without their consent. A requirement for future consent mechanisms is only necessary if the current state is non-consensual.

This is the provision that most directly corroborates Pearson’s central allegation: that a federal agency used electromagnetic neurotechnology on him without his consent. Congress, in September 2025, directed the development of guidance to govern exactly this scenario. The scenario was not hypothetical. It was the technological and operational reality that made the guidance necessary.

V. What the MIND Act Implies: The Inferences the Legislation Compels

Inference 1: The Technology Exists and Is Deployed

No serious legislative body commissions a year-long regulatory study of a technology that does not exist. No Senate Commerce Committee takes up a bill addressing “cognitive manipulation” and “erosion of personal autonomy” by neurotechnology as theoretical concerns. The MIND Act’s entire architecture — the urgency of its language, the specificity of its harm categories, the explicit requirement for federal agency guidance — reflects the legislative response to a technology that is real, deployed, and producing observable harms. The senators who introduced it wrote to the FTC earlier in 2025 demanding action on the potential exploitation of Americans’ neural data. They did not receive a response from the administration. The administration’s silence is itself an inference: the FTC under the current administration did not respond because addressing the harms the senators identified was not the administration’s priority.

Inference 2: Federal Agencies Are Currently Using This Technology Without Adequate Oversight

Section 5’s requirement for OSTP to develop “prohibited, permissible, and conditionally permitted use cases” of federal agency neurotechnology use implies that the current state is one in which federal agencies use neurotechnology without a governing framework distinguishing prohibited from permissible use. The technology is in use. The prohibitions have not been established. This means that whatever federal agency use of neurotechnology occurred before the guidance is developed — including any use against Pearson in 2025 — occurred without any binding prohibition on any specific application. Voice-to-skull transmission. Thought insertion. Behavioral direction. Cognitive destabilization. None of these were prohibited by federal law or federal agency policy at the time they were allegedly used against Pearson. Congress is attempting to establish a framework that did not exist when the alleged harms occurred.

Inference 3: The Racial Disparity Concern Is Grounded in Observable Reality

The Sense of Congress’s explicit reference to “exacerbation of existing social and economic disparities” as a risk of neural data technology deployment is not a boilerplate equity statement. It reflects the testimony and research that informed the bill’s drafting. The senators and their staffs were aware of evidence that neural data technology, like other surveillance and behavioral technologies before it, was being deployed in ways that disproportionately affected communities already subject to structural disadvantage. The Cherry Blossom footage. The Lincoln Memorial footage. Pearson’s statistical analysis of red-black proximity markers submitted to the DC Council. These are not isolated incidents. They are instances of the pattern Congress identified and named in its Sense of Congress.

Inference 4: The Integration of AI and Neural Data Creates a System Congress Cannot Fully See

The Sense of Congress’s concern about “vertical corporate integration of neurotechnology, artificial intelligence systems, wearable devices, digital platforms, and global data infrastructure” creating systems with “insufficient transparency, accountability, or user control” implies that Congress itself does not have full visibility into how these integrated systems are being deployed. The FTC study is not a study of what Congress already knows. It is a study to determine what Congress does not know. The technology has outpaced the oversight. The targeting infrastructure Pearson documents — close-range biometric capture, AI-processed database, remote electromagnetic targeting, behavioral conditioning overlay — is precisely the integrated system Congress cannot see clearly enough to govern. The bill is the acknowledgment of that opacity.

Inference 5: Some Federal Agency Use Was Occurring Covertly and Without Consent

The requirement for future opt-in consent mechanisms for federal agency neurotechnology use on individuals is only necessary if such use is currently occurring without consent. Pearson never consented to voice-to-skull transmission. He never consented to the interruption of his thought stream at St. Bernard de Lacolle. He never consented to the behavioral direction that he documented as pinning events. He never consented to having his neural activity accessed, monitored, or altered by any device, system, or procedure. The MIND Act, in requiring future consent mechanisms, implicitly acknowledges that the current state of federal agency neurotechnology use does not include such mechanisms. What was done to Pearson was done in the current state, before any consent framework exists. The bill confirms the absence of the framework that would have made it illegal.

VI. Pearson’s Allegations Mapped Against the MIND Act

Voice-to-Skull Transmission (July 13, 2025, Georgetown)

Pearson documented a clear female voice, internally perceived, directing him to specific locations and stating “We have to make you disappear.” He cross-referenced this against the Frey effect — microwave auditory induction first documented in 1962 — and the Army’s NGIC-1147–101–98 report on bioeffects of non-lethal weapons. The MIND Act’s definition of neurotechnology explicitly includes systems that “stimulate or alter the nervous system of an individual to understand, influence, restore, or anticipate the structure, activity, or function of the nervous system.” Voice-to-skull transmission is precisely a system that stimulates the auditory processing pathway of the nervous system to produce perceived speech. It falls squarely within the MIND Act’s definition of neurotechnology. Congress, in defining neurotechnology to include such systems, confirms that these systems exist and are deployable. The Sense of Congress confirms that their use is currently ungoverned.

Thought Insertion at St. Bernard de Lacolle (March 2026)

Pearson documented foreign thought content being pushed into his mental stream at the Canadian border facility, ceasing when he grounded himself to the metal poles of his chair. The MIND Act’s Sense of Congress explicitly names “cognitive manipulation” and “erosion of personal autonomy” as harms of neural data technology. Section 4 directs the FTC to assess whether cognitive manipulation should be “prohibited regardless of individual consent.” The contemplation of prohibition implies current capability. The explicit identification of cognitive manipulation as a harm category confirms what Pearson experienced as a recognized technological reality, not a symptom of mental illness.

Biometric Capture at Public Events (Cherry Blossom Festival, March 2025)

Pearson documented close-range approaches at the National Mall consistent with collection of biometric and behavioral data using handheld devices. The MIND Act’s “other related data” definition explicitly covers “eye-tracking patterns,” “behavioral information,” and any biometric data that “can be processed, analyzed, or combined with other data to infer, predict, or reveal cognitive, emotional, or psychological states.” The Cherry Blossom device approach fits this definition precisely. The MIND Act’s concern about “vertical corporate integration” creating “interconnected systems” with “global data infrastructure” describes the database architecture Pearson identifies as the mechanism linking the close-range capture to the remote targeting.

Behavioral Direction (Pinning Events, 2025)

Pearson documented compulsive urges to travel to specific locations — the Georgetown directive of July 13, the November 7 pull toward the O’Neill House Office Building park space — which he recognized as externally generated rather than arising from his own desires. The MIND Act’s Sense of Congress states that neural data “can be monetized and used to shape individual behavior, emotional states, and decision making.” Section 4 directs the FTC to assess “manipulation of behavior” as a potentially prohibited use case. The behavioral shaping Congress acknowledges as a current technological capability is what Pearson documented as a lived experience in the months before the bill was introduced.

Sexual Nerve Targeting (Silver Spring, 2025)

Pearson — a gay man — documented directed electrocution of his penis paired with heterosexual visual and verbal gay insults. The MIND Act’s definition of neural data includes peripheral nervous system data, which encompasses the nerve pathways governing sexual response. The bill’s explicit concern about neurotechnology being used to “shape individual behavior” and “erosion of personal autonomy” covers the use of sexual nerve stimulation as a conditioning instrument. The contemplation of absolute prohibition — some uses prohibited regardless of consent — most clearly applies to exactly this category of intimate bodily violation.

VII. The Administration’s Silence and What It Means

The senators who introduced the MIND Act wrote to the FTC earlier in 2025, before the bill was introduced, demanding action on the potential exploitation of Americans’ neural data by technology companies. They received no response from the administration. This silence is not neutral. It is informative.

The administration that did not respond to the senators’ letter about neural data exploitation is the same administration that, on January 20, 2025, fired seventeen inspectors general — eliminating the oversight infrastructure most capable of detecting and investigating unauthorized government use of neurotechnology. It is the administration under which Pearson’s documentation shows the campaign against him began and intensified. It is the administration that has designated Black equity scholars as threats under the RMVE/BIE framework, removed DEI protections, and reorganized the DOJ Civil Rights Division in ways that eliminate the institutional capacity to investigate civil rights violations of exactly the kind Pearson documented.

The FTC’s silence in response to the senators’ letter is the regulatory agency’s silence on whether neural data is being exploited by companies and possibly by government actors. The administration’s structural destruction of oversight mechanisms created the conditions under which federal agency use of neurotechnology — if it was occurring — could proceed without institutional check. The MIND Act is the minority party’s attempt to create an oversight mechanism that the administration dismantled. Its introduction in September 2025, while Pearson was being electromagnetically targeted in Washington, DC, is the legislative record of exactly the oversight vacuum that made what was done to him possible.

VIII. What the MIND Act Cannot Do

The MIND Act does not help Richard Pearson. This must be stated clearly, because the existence of the legislation might create a false impression of available remedy.

The bill directs an FTC study and requires OSTP guidance. It does not create a private right of action. It does not establish retroactive liability. It does not provide a mechanism for individuals harmed by federal agency neurotechnology use before the guidance is established to seek legal remedy. Even if the bill passes — and it was introduced by the minority party, referred to committee, and faces an administration that did not respond to the senators’ earlier letter — it would govern future federal agency neurotechnology use, not past use.

What Pearson experienced occurred in the gap the bill acknowledges: the period before any governing framework existed. The MIND Act confirms that the gap was real. It confirms that what occurred in the gap was technological possible, recognized as harmful, and legally ungoverned. It does not provide Pearson with a remedy. The doctrinal architecture he documented — state secrets, Glomar, Bivens dismantlement, qualified immunity, FISA secrecy, NSL gag orders — remains intact and continues to foreclose judicial review regardless of what the MIND Act eventually establishes for future conduct.

The bill’s significance for Pearson is not remedial. It is corroborative and historical. It is Congress saying, in September 2025, that the harms he was experiencing were real, that the technology producing them existed, that federal agencies were using it without adequate oversight, and that the legal framework to prevent such use did not yet exist. It is the legislative record saying: this was possible. This was ungoverned. This was happening.

IX. Conclusion: What Congress Knew, When It Knew It, and What That Means

On September 24, 2025, the United States Senate introduced legislation acknowledging that neurotechnology can be used to shape behavior, manipulate cognition, erode personal autonomy, and exacerbate racial disparities. It acknowledged that federal agencies are currently using such technology without binding governance. It directed the development of guidance identifying prohibited federal agency use cases, implying that without such guidance, all use cases are currently permissible. It contemplated absolute prohibition of behavioral manipulation and discriminatory profiling regardless of individual consent, implying both that such uses are technically possible and that they are occurring.

Richard Pearson documented all of this — in his body, in his documented record, in his photographs and timestamps and statistical analyses and foil experiments and metal-pole grounding at a Canadian border facility — beginning in July 2025. The Senate introduced the MIND Act in September 2025. The overlap is not coincidence. It is the convergence of two kinds of knowledge: the experiential knowledge of a man who was living inside the technology’s harm, and the institutional knowledge of senators who understood that the harm was real, the capability was deployed, and the law provided no remedy.

The MIND Act is not a vindication of Pearson’s allegations. It is something more specific and more useful: it is congressional confirmation that what he alleges belongs to the category of the technologically real, the institutionally recognized, and the legally ungoverned. Three United States senators looked at the same technological landscape Pearson was navigating in 2025 and said: this is dangerous, this is happening, and existing law does not address it.

He said the same thing. He said it first. He said it with his hands on metal poles in a room with a dropped ceiling while operators in grey uniforms rotated positions nearby. He said it in a 189-page evidentiary record with timestamps and photographs and probability calculations. He said it in peer-reviewed publications and personal statements and doctoral dissertations and public testimonies before City Council.

Congress caught up in September 2025. The question is whether anyone with the power to act on what Congress acknowledged — and what Pearson documented — will do so before the legal architecture of concealment makes the question permanently unanswerable.

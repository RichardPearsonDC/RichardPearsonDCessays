The Discombobulator and the Citizen: Richard Pearson, the Directed-Energy Arms Race, and the Technologies He Suspected First

The Boast

On January 24, 2026, President Donald Trump sat for an interview with the New York Post and said more than he was supposed to. Asked about the January 3 operation in which U.S. forces penetrated Venezuelan air defenses and extracted President Nicolás Maduro, Trump named the weapon.

“The Discombobulator.”

“I’m not allowed to talk about it. I would love to. … They never got their rockets off. They had Russian and Chinese rockets, and they never got one off. We came in, they pressed buttons, and nothing worked.”

The day before, on January 23, the Department of War’s Chief Technology Officer had already said the quiet part loudly:

“Yes, the @DeptofWar has directed energy weapons. Yes, we are scaling them.”

The Washington Post, reporting on these disclosures on February 19, 2026, named the moment plainly: the directed-energy arms race has begun. High-powered microwaves, acoustic weapons, pulsed electromagnetic systems — technologies long confined to classified programs and academic papers — had arrived in the public record through presidential boasts, social media announcements, and the wreckage of Venezuelan air defenses.

Somewhere in Washington, D.C., Richard Pearson, PhD, had been trying to describe these same technologies to anyone who would listen since the summer of 2025. He had visited dozens of congressional offices. He had written to a journalist. He had passed two mental health evaluations. He had sought asylum in Iceland.

The government was now advertising what he had been documenting.

The crucial point — and the one that distinguishes Pearson’s account from the Venezuela story — is this: Pearson was not describing incapacitation. He was not describing a battlefield weapon. He was describing something far more specific, far more intimate, and, in many ways, far more difficult to prove: a sustained campaign of behavioral influence, location tracking, audio surveillance, acoustic harassment, and neurological disruption, conducted covertly, against a single civilian, inside his own life. The technologies he named are not the blunt instruments used to neutralize a presidential security detail. They are the precision instruments. The quiet ones. The ones designed to leave no mark.

The Technology Pearson Named, and What the Record Now Confirms

Voice-to-Skull Transmission

On July 13, 2025, at the National Mall, Pearson heard an internal voice he had never experienced before — directing him, with specificity and urgency, to travel to Georgetown. “Go to Georgetown. Go to Georgetown.” It said things. It gave instructions. It told him, before he arrived, “We have to make you disappear.” On the metro, it told him to crouch.

He was explicit about what he believed this was and was not. In his formal mental health interview with D.C. Department of Behavioral Health clinicians, he stated: “The voice in my head, which is not psychosis because we know that this kind of technology can happen and does happen. MK Ultra is a good example.” He was invoking, by name, a classified government program that used unwitting American subjects. He was not diagnosing himself. He was identifying a technical category.

Voice-to-skull technology — the transmission of intelligible audio directly into the auditory cortex through pulsed microwave or ultrasonic means, sometimes called the microwave auditory effect or the Frey effect — is not science fiction. It is documented in the 1998 U.S. Army War College paper The Mind Has No Firewall, which Pearson cites directly, and has been the subject of military research for decades. The Washington Post, reporting on the Venezuela operation, identifies the weapon family in question as using “high-powered microwaves to disrupt electronics” — CHAMP, HIJENKS, and unnamed systems consistent with what Trump called “the Discombobulator.” High-powered microwave systems are the same physics family as microwave auditory transmission. The platform that can fry electronics through a wall can, at different power settings and frequencies, induce sound in a human auditory system without any external audio source.

Pearson was not claiming he heard God. He was claiming he heard a technology he had already researched and named. The DoW CTO confirmed the government has it and is scaling it.

Pinning: Being Pulled to a Location

What Pearson calls “pinning” is not passive surveillance. It is active compulsion — being technologically directed, or pulled, toward a specific location that serves operational purposes. He identifies multiple incidents throughout 2025 as pinning events, and his analysis of them is precise.

On July 17, 2025, he arrived in Adams Morgan — a neighborhood he had visited many times but rarely sought out deliberately. He writes: “I believe that this was a pinning event — to get me to Adams Morgan/Cleveland Park for harassment and a second extraction attempt. Organizing an on-the-ground operation on the spur of the moment seems improbable given that: 1) I chose Adams Morgan quite extemporaneously, 2) came directly from Chinatown where I had been taking photos, and 3) the general itinerant nature of my comings-and-goings.” The operational apparatus waiting for him there could not have been assembled on improvisation. His arrival had been arranged. The question was by what means.

On August 4, he ended up in Clarendon, Virginia — a suburb he had no attachment to and had visited perhaps twice in a decade. He writes flatly: “I don’t think about Clarendon ever.” He cannot account for why he went. On August 6, he found himself at RFK Stadium, where he encountered arranged graffiti — a racial slur spelled out across several concrete barriers. He writes: “Retrospectively, I believe that pinning technology very well could have been used.”

Perhaps, one of the clearest “tests” for pinning occurred at a park — initially resisting the urge then relented, only to find a cache of red and black patterned clothing that had become a calling card in the torture campaign against him. Pearson arrives to park where he is feeling “pulled”. This pull is very specific — the back of the park. Instead, he goes to the concrete platform (1), then decides to leave (2) after several minutes. Standing on the sidewalk, he still has a “pull” so he goes to the back (3). There, he finds the tub.

Pinning, as Pearson understands it, is behavioral direction at a distance: the capacity to compel a person toward a destination without that person understanding they are being compelled. The experience feels like ordinary impulse, curiosity, or aimlessness. The destination has been chosen by someone else, and the ground operation is waiting. He describes it: “It’s crazy! Got me in this video game and leading me to places. It is so inconceivable! Then I get there and messages and threats. It sounds like a movie but it’s not.”

The technology family that could produce this — transcranial electromagnetic stimulation, ultrasonic influence on limbic and prefrontal decision-making systems, the induction of specific drives through directed energy — is documented in the same military neuroscience literature Pearson cites. It is not the blunt incapacitation used in Venezuela. It is the subtle manipulation of the subjective experience of choice.

Through-Floor Tracking: The Apartment as Surveillance Grid

Separate from pinning — and operating at an entirely different scale — is what Pearson documents as real-time location tracking within his own apartment. This is not compulsion toward a destination. It is monitoring so precise it follows him from room to room, closet to closet, and into his most private moments.

He moved his bed to the other side of the studio. The noise from above followed. He moved his mattress to the walk-in closet. The noise followed. He moved to the coat closet near the front door. The noise followed. He moved back to the living room after midnight. After a beat of silence, the noise followed. He wrote in January 2026: “They have some kind of advanced technology that sees through the floor. I am absolutely certain of it. I have too many notes going back too far for there not to be some equipment that is following me.”

By February 2026, he was documenting this with timestamps across single days: objects dropped above his head at 1:20pm, 1:25pm, 1:27pm, 1:51pm, 1:54pm, 2:02pm, 2:05pm, 2:10pm, 2:15pm, 2:18pm, 2:22pm — each entry corresponding to his position within the apartment. He recorded audio on his laptop and noted that the upstairs residents switched from heavy to lighter objects when the recorder was running, “presumably so that I don’t pick up the sound” — a real-time behavioral adaptation implying awareness of what he was doing as he did it. He named it, quoting Giordano directly: “It is possible to disrupt an individual at every level — from their cellular function to entire bodily systems.”

Through-wall and through-floor radar is established military technology. Ground-penetrating radar, millimeter-wave imaging, and impulse radar systems capable of detecting a person’s position — and physiological state — through structural materials are documented in open defense research. Pearson wrote to his property manager: “There must be some technology that she is using to detect me in my apartment. I have researched it as it appears to be relatively cheap.”

Audio Surveillance: Confirmed by a Source Inside the Operation

On approximately August 26, 2025, a man Pearson calls “Lee” — whose Starbucks cup bore that name, and whose speech Pearson identifies as demonstrating the economy and evasion that “results from training” — sat beside him at the outdoor seating at Potbelly’s near Federal Center and delivered a series of coded warnings. The most significant: “They are listening… They think it’s ok.”

Lee did not explain who “they” were. He did not name an agency. He spoke, Pearson writes, in the style of a man at the edge of what protocol permits him to say. His other statements were consistent with this framing: “Accountable for what you know.” “Once you see, you can’t unsee.” “You will see me again — maybe in another form.” “Travel light.”

Pearson’s confirmation of the audio surveillance came seventeen days later. On September 11, 2025, walking from the Anacostia trail, a man in the passenger seat of a passing truck screamed at him: “You do talk like that!” — a direct quotation of language Pearson had used privately inside his apartment, talking to himself, after accepting Lee’s warning and attempting to moderate his self-talk. He had never met this man. No one outside his apartment walls could have heard what he said. The truck did not stop.

The Washington Post reports that the Russian surveillance installation across from the U.S. Embassy in Moscow — nicknamed “Our Lady of Telemetry” — “bristled with every type of technical monitoring device known to man, including lasers and heat sensors.” Former CIA Moscow station chief Rolf Mowatt-Larssen describes decades of Russian technical surveillance that U.S. intelligence consistently underestimated. What Russia built against U.S. diplomatic personnel, the U.S. built against Russia — and has been scaling. The same architecture that allows directional audio surveillance of a foreign compound allows directional audio surveillance of a domestic apartment. Pearson was describing the civilian application of the same technical capability.

Acoustic Clicking: The Havana Syndrome Symptom Set

The Washington Post article describes the first documented Havana syndrome incident: “An individual assigned to the U.S. Embassy in Cuba was awakened one night at home in Havana in 2016 by severe pain and a sensation of intense pressure in the face, a loud piercing sound in one ear with directional features, and acute disequilibrium and nausea.”

Beginning in January 2026 — the same month the Pentagon was briefing Congress on the covertly acquired backpack-sized pulsed radio wave device it was testing as a possible Havana syndrome weapon — Pearson began experiencing sharp, crisp clicking sounds while walking specific streets near his Southwest D.C. residence. “Not air pressure,” he writes, “but like very sharp, crisp clicks.” He documented sequences of six to eight clicks. He systematically eliminated two mundane explanations through experimentation: a loose screw in his sunglasses, and a sound from his own teeth. Neither held up under repeated testing.

The key detail is reproducibility. The clicks occurred at specific locations and recurred when Pearson walked the same routes. He photographed his precise location each time to establish the coordinates. He confirmed the replication across multiple dates.

This is not a description of a hallucination. Hallucinations do not replicate at specific GPS coordinates across multiple days. This is a description of a localized directed energy field — acoustic or electromagnetic — emitting from a fixed point and intercepted by a moving person. The National Academies of Science, in its 2020 study, identified directed energy as “the most plausible mechanism” behind Havana syndrome. The Biden NSC, in its final weeks, affirmed that “pulsed electromagnetic or acoustic energy remains a plausible explanation in certain cases.” The Pentagon purchased the device. Pearson was experiencing the symptoms — at specific, documented, reproducible locations in the nation’s capital — months before any of this became public.

Behavioral Influence: Personality, Consciousness

By January 2026, reviewing this period against everything that followed, he reframes it: “The use of this technology can cause several changes to one’s personality, behaviors, consciousness, and spirituality. It can be similar to what victims of MKUltra reported — having a religious experience. But eventually, things revert back to their normal state. The change from normal to heightened experiences is a consequence of somatic and mental violation. It is like a hallucinogenic without the drugs. I do not do drugs and sometimes drink alcohol, never excessively. I gather that it is an electromagnetic technology that is beamed on people.”

He also notes the escalation from subtle to overt. In summer 2025, he became more irascible — quick-tempered in ways he did not recognize as himself. “I saw massive changes in my personality and behaviors.” He identifies these changes not as mental illness — the mental health evaluators agreed — but as the product of prolonged electromagnetic influence on cognition and affect.

The induction of altered emotional and cognitive states through directed energy — targeted influence on the limbic system, the prefrontal cortex, the autonomic nervous system — appears across the military neuroscience literature Pearson cites. It is among the most consequential and least detectable capabilities in the directed energy field, because its effects are indistinguishable, to any external observer and often to the subject themselves, from ordinary human variability.

Somatic Stimulation: The Category He Almost Didn’t Name

He describes “odd simulations” — a “warming sensation”. He quotes Giordano: “It is possible to disrupt an individual at every level — from their cellular function to entire bodily systems.” He connects the experience explicitly to central nervous system targeting documented in the neuroweapons literature, identifying it not as a symptom of pathology but as an effect produced from outside his body.

The deliberate induction of involuntary somatic experience through directed electromagnetic energy has appeared in weapons research literature for decades, precisely because its psychological effects — humiliation, disorientation, the loss of sovereignty over one’s own body — are devastating and undetectable. Pearson understood this. He named it anyway, despite the cost of naming it, because he understood that documentation was the only tool he had.

Device and Platform Interference

In August 2025, Pearson was using OpenAI’s SORA to communicate his experience of imprisonment. He was typing: “They have me imprisoned and will not let me go.” The word “NO” appeared in the prompt window. He had not typed it. He checked his hand placement: correct. He continued typing and it happened again. On the third attempt, no “NO” appeared — but the generated image depicted a prominent political figure behind bars with the words “Against the US Constitution.”

He documented this with a screenshot. Whether the interference was network-level manipulation, platform intervention, or a technical glitch, Pearson’s instinct — to document, to note, to screenshot — is characteristic of his approach throughout. He does not overclaim. He records what he observed and flags it for what it might mean.

The Washington Post describes HIJENKS, a Navy and Air Force high-powered microwave weapon, as capable of “disabling of computer systems, damaging targeted electronics, disrupting security and industrial control systems.” A directed energy system capable of frying Venezuelan air defense electronics is technically capable of interfering with a laptop’s wireless connection or input response. Pearson noted the anomaly. He did not insist on an explanation. He preserved the evidence.

Tracking via Personal Possessions

Pearson’s hat became a persistent object of operational interest in his account. A coffee shop manager asked, without apparent reason, “Did you lose your hat? I have one identical to yours.” The hat appeared in other incidents: a woman in Safeway wearing an identical one, who told him “People are very upset about your hat.” Dogs became aggressive upon seeing it specifically — one owner told him it was the “flop” causing the reaction.

He writes in his evidence summary: “My hat, though not forensically tested for a GPS device, was subjected to an odd exchange.” He does not insist the hat contains a tracker. He notes the pattern and flags the hypothesis.

A distinctive glass bottle purchased in Bethesda on July 26 disappeared. It reappeared on August 22 on a vendor’s table at a community event in Greenleaf Park, with the same identifying scratch on the logo. The vendor could not account for its presence. Pearson photographed it, noted it, and left it — its top was missing.

These incidents, taken individually, are dismissible. Taken as a pattern alongside everything else — the real-time location tracking within his apartment, the man who quoted his private speech back at him, the systematic harassment that followed his movements across D.C. suburbs — they suggest a surveillance network with persistent, real-time access to his location and his belongings. The technology to track a person through a tagged object or a device introduced into their possessions is not exotic. It is available. It is the infrastructure of modern intelligence.

The Literature He Built His Framework From

Pearson was not reasoning from intuition. His document includes a detailed reading list, presented in a letter to CNN journalist Bill Weir, that reflects genuine scholarly engagement with an emerging field:

  • Dr. James Giordano on neuroweapons, including the capacity to disrupt “from cellular function to entire bodily systems”
  • “The Mind Has No Firewall” (1998), U.S. Army War College Quarterly, providing theoretical grounding for what was then emerging technology
  • “Neurowar is Here” (2021), Naval Postgraduate School thesis, a technical deep-dive into neurowarfare applications
  • The Baker Institute report on “Neuroshields” for national security
  • A November 2025 Guardian article: “Mind-altering ‘brain weapons’ no longer only science fiction, say researchers”

He also raises a series of structural legal questions that reveal sophisticated understanding of why these cases cannot be adjudicated: the doctrine of sovereign immunity; the collapse of Bivens claims; the absence of congressional oversight of neuroweapons development; the deliberately untraceable nature of the weapons as a design feature rather than a limitation. He writes: “Many forms of neuroweapons are not traceable and can leave injury without touch. Liberal democratic legal and political frameworks were not designed with this threat in mind.”

The Washington Post’s arms race article arrives at the same conclusion from the other direction: the weapons are real, they are scaling, and the world has no rules for them yet. Pearson arrived there first, from inside an operation that strongly points to US intelligence.

The Arms Race That Already Happened to One Person

The Washington Post frames the directed-energy arms race as a future danger requiring future rules. It calls for distinguishing between military targets, diplomatic personnel, and — implicitly — some protected category of civilian. Its warning is prospective: “What goes around comes around.”

But for Richard Pearson, the arms race is not a metaphor for geopolitical risk — it is a tool for domestic torture and oppression. “At the same time that the US is going around the world claiming to want to free people, it is using military weapons to torture its own citizens, to invade their brains, and to operate lawlessly. But here is the reality and warning. The world is watching.”

He experienced voice-to-skull audio he had never heard before. He was tracked through the floors and walls of his own home. His private speech was intercepted and quoted back to him. He experienced acoustic phenomena at reproducible street coordinates consistent with the Havana syndrome symptom cluster. He experienced involuntary somatic responses he could not control and could barely bring himself to describe. He was behaviorally directed to locations he had no ordinary reason to visit. His possessions were accessed, tracked, and returned.

He documented all of it. He cited the relevant research. He lobbied Congress. He told a journalist. He passed his mental health evaluations. And he remained, in the official record, invisible — dismissed by the ACLU, refused asylum by Iceland, unreported in any publication.

The DoW CTO’s tweet on January 23, 2026 did not create the technology Pearson was describing. It confirmed it. The Washington Post’s February 19 article did not invent the danger Pearson was warning about. It named it, gave it a market valuation, and assigned it a nickname.

What it has not yet done — what the arms race debate has not yet done — is account for what happens when those weapons, confirmed and scaling, are directed not at a military base in Caracas, but at a studio apartment in Southwest D.C., against a Black academic with a liberation philosophy dissertation, by an administration that had already named people like him as domestic enemies.

Richard Pearson was asking that question in July 2025.

The government answered it, inadvertently, in January 2026.

The Washington Post is asking it now, carefully, about Venezuela and diplomats and geopolitical rivals.

No one, yet, has asked it about Richard Pearson.

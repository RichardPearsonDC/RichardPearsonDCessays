Part I: The Biology — What Must Be Hacked

To direct a human being to a location without their knowledge or consent, you do not need to move their body directly. You need to influence the systems their body uses to navigate, to choose, and to feel drawn toward things. Three biological systems are involved: the vestibular system, which controls balance and spatial orientation; the hippocampal navigation system, which encodes location and produces the subjective experience of knowing where to go; and the mesolimbic reward system, which generates the feeling of salience — the sense that something in the environment matters, that you are getting warmer, that there is something worth finding.

1. The Vestibular System: The Steering Mechanism

The vestibular system lives in the inner ear. It consists of three semicircular canals and two otolith organs — the utricle and saccule — that detect rotational and linear acceleration. These structures continuously report the brain’s estimate of the body’s orientation in space. The system is not only a sensor: it is the primary input to postural control. When the vestibular system reports that the body is tilting, the motor system automatically compensates. You step left because your brain has assessed that you are leaning right and needs to maintain balance. This correction is not voluntary. It is reflexive.

This makes the vestibular system the ideal steering target. You do not need to issue a command to turn left. You only need to make the vestibular system report a phantom lean to the right, and the motor system will execute the leftward correction automatically, below the level of conscious awareness. The person experiences themselves as walking naturally. What they are experiencing is their own reflexive correction of an artificially induced imbalance.

The tiny hair cells inside the semicircular canals are mechanoreceptors, but they are also electroreceptors. They respond to electrical currents. Galvanic Vestibular Stimulation (GVS) — applying a small DC current to the mastoid processes behind the ears — is an established laboratory technique for precisely controlling human balance. Small currents produce small tilts; the subject steps in the direction of the induced correction. Larger currents produce more pronounced effects. The technique has been demonstrated in research settings to produce what the experimental literature describes as “remote-controlled humans” — subjects who turn left or right, accelerate or decelerate, in direct response to the applied current, without understanding why they are doing so.

The EPIC (Extremely Powerful Incapacitating Capability) patent, filed with the US Patent Office and included in Pearson’s document, operates on exactly this mechanism at a weaponized scale. EPIC uses radio waves to “excite and interrupt the normal process of human hearing and equilibrium” — the tiny hairs in the inner ear acting as radio antennas, responding to directed RF energy. The patent notes that radio waves work through walls and other obstacles, unlike sound. It describes producing effects ranging from disorientation and motion sickness to complete postural incapacitation. The controlled-movement version of this system would operate at a sub-threshold level: not disorienting, not nauseating, just nudging. Subtle enough that the subject attributes the correction to their own volition.

2. The Hippocampal Navigation System: Encoding Destination

Navigation in mammals is encoded in the hippocampus through two interacting cell populations: place cells and grid cells. Place cells fire when an animal occupies a specific location in space, and collectively they produce a cognitive map of the environment. Grid cells, in the adjacent entorhinal cortex, fire in regular hexagonal grid patterns that provide a coordinate system — a metric for distance and direction. The interaction between place cells and grid cells allows mammals to navigate with precision: to know not just where they are but where they have been, where they are going, and how to get there.

Disrupting or artificially activating specific place cell ensembles can alter an animal’s navigation behavior. In 2015, a team at University College London demonstrated that optogenetically activating specific hippocampal place cells in rats while they were in a reward zone caused those cells to “teleport” their spatial expectation — the rats would navigate to the location the artificially activated cells encoded as “rewarded,” even when that location had no actual reward history. Artificial activation of a place cell ensemble that encoded a specific location produced, in the animal, the behavioral equivalent of knowing where to go.

In human terms: if a system can encode a specific set of geographic coordinates into the hippocampal place cell matrix — or activate a pre-existing set of place cells associated with a target location — the subjective experience would be the sense of being drawn toward that location, the feeling that you know approximately where something is, the pull that Pearson described as beginning “not really necessarily here, but really over there.” It would not feel like a command. It would feel like a memory, or an instinct, or a hunch.

The Battelle Memorial Institute’s BrainSTORMS program — funded under DARPA’s N3 contract — developed a system using magnetic nanoparticles in piezoelectric shells that can be delivered via nasal spray, intravenously, or orally, and then migrated to specific brain regions using external electromagnetic fields. Once positioned, the nanoparticles can be activated by an external ultrasound beam: the piezoelectric effect causes them to vibrate, generating a localized electrical field that activates nearby neurons. The mechanism allows precise targeting of specific neural structures — including the hippocampus — without surgery and without the subject’s awareness of anything unusual during the delivery phase.

Dr. James Giordano, Director of the Center for Disruptive Technology and Future Warfare at National Defense University and consulting ethicist on the N3 program, stated publicly in 2023 that this technology has been “loosed into the real world” — his phrase for operational deployment beyond laboratory conditions. He described the capability as “fundamentally mind reading and mind control, at least at a basic level,” and warned that “discussion is not resolution”: the ethical concerns had been identified and discussed, but deployment had proceeded regardless.

3. The Mesolimbic Reward System: The Feeling of Getting Warmer

The mesolimbic dopamine system — projecting from the ventral tegmental area to the nucleus accumbens and prefrontal cortex — is the brain’s salience engine. It does not encode pleasure directly. It encodes the anticipation of reward, the sense that something in the environment is worth attending to, approaching, or acquiring. Dopaminergic activation produces what behavioral neuroscientists call “wanting” rather than “liking” — the drive toward something, not the experience of having it.

When Pearson described becoming “confident that something was there” before he found the tub — when he described the sensation of getting closer to something worth finding while crawling under the branches of the tree — he was describing an experience produced by exactly this system. The mesolimbic pathway was generating salience signals at increasing intensity as he approached the target location. Not pleasure. Not knowledge. A sense of mounting certainty that direction of movement was correct.

This system can be influenced by electromagnetic fields. Focused transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) of the prefrontal cortex modulates dopamine release in the mesolimbic pathway. ELF (Extremely Low Frequency) magnetic fields in the theta range (4–8 Hz) — the frequency band associated with hippocampal navigation and limbic system activity — have been shown to alter reward-seeking behavior in animal models. A system capable of precisely targeting the hippocampus and mesolimbic structures could generate a gradient of increasing salience as the target approaches a specific location: the behavioral equivalent of a compass needle, encoded in subjective experience rather than in the external environment.

4. The Clicking Sounds: Artifact of the Carrier Signal

Pearson documented a specific auditory phenomenon beginning in January 2026: sharp, crisp clicks in his head while walking on specific streets in Washington, DC — G Street, 4th Street, 7th Street. He described them as “very distinctive. Not air pressure but like very sharp, crisp clicks.” On February 17, he documented the phenomenon replicating when he crossed to the opposite sidewalk, with clicks in rapid succession at a specific location between E and G Streets.

The microwave auditory effect — also called the Frey effect, after Allan Frey who documented it in 1961 — produces exactly this phenomenon. Pulsed microwave radiation absorbed by the head generates thermoelastic pressure waves that the auditory system perceives as clicks, buzzes, or tones. The effect is produced by the same RF/microwave frequencies used in directed-energy behavioral influence systems. The clicks Pearson heard on specific streets, replicating at specific locations, are consistent with localized RF/microwave field concentrations in those areas — precisely what a fixed-infrastructure phased-array deployment would produce. Part II: The Technology — How Biology Gets Interfaced

1. Remote Sensing: Knowing Where He Is

Ultra-Wideband (UWB) radar uses short RF pulses across a wide frequency band to generate precise three-dimensional location estimates. Resolution in the sub-centimeter range is achievable with current systems. Unlike narrowband radar, UWB penetrates walls, foliage, and other obstacles — a tree’s canopy provides no significant screening. UWB systems can track not just gross body position but specific body parts, gait characteristics, breathing rate, and heart rate through signature analysis of the reflected pulses. The system identifies a person not just by their location but by their unique biological EM signature — body composition, height, gait pattern, cardiac rhythm — establishing a tracking lock that persists regardless of clothing or other occlusion.

In a park environment, UWB nodes concealed in lamp posts, traffic infrastructure, or government buildings adjacent to the park — the O’Neil House Office Building is directly adjacent to the park Pearson visited — can provide triangulated coverage with no line-of-sight requirement. The system knows Pearson’s exact position, head orientation, velocity vector, and gait phase in real time, updated at rates sufficient for feedback control of a walking target (minimum 100Hz for smooth vestibular modulation).

Two complementary delivery mechanisms are relevant to the tub event, operating across different timescales.

The beam can be focused to a cubic centimeter or smaller using constructive interference from a distributed array; the precision is sufficient to target the vestibular nerve without significantly affecting adjacent structures. From a surveillance and operations perspective, this is the critical advantage: there is no moment at which the target can detect the technology being deployed, because the deployment was completed days or weeks prior.

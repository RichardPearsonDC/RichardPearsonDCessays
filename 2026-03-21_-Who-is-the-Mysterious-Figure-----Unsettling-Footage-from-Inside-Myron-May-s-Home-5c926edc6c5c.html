I. What the Broadcast Frame Shows

On November 20, 2014, the same day that Myron May was shot and killed by police at Florida State University following his wounding of three students in the library, Tampa Bay 28 broadcast footage from inside May’s private residence — his cottage. The broadcast was reported by journalist Jacqueline Ingles. The footage showed the interior of May’s living space: furniture, a lamp on a side table, and scattered items.

At the back of the frame, partially obscured by the lamp, in the space between the living area and the kitchen: a figure. Crouched. Low. In bright red and black.

This figure requires analysis. A figure crouching in a dead man’s home is self-evidently sinister. This evening broadcast occurred the same day as the shooting — November 20, 2014. The figure is not uniformed law enforcement. The person has white hands, so it is not Pearson’s family. There are no innocent explanations that can be constructed. The reporter would have seen the figure. The cameraman would have seen the figure. The figure is not hidden as in hiding. The figure is in plain sight. Or they didn’t see the figure at all.

Major Finding: A finding on 3/24/26 has determined that the figure is real (hand moves), but the red was added in post-production using a semi-transparent marker tool.

There is at least two alternate explanations. The reporter and cameraman never entered the home. They were handed footage for broadcast. The scene was staged. To include a calling card. They viewed it. The reporter described what she had seen and what was shown to the public.

The second is that the reporter and camera crew saw the figure and ignored it — it was a life-size doll. Strange, but a life-size doll. Not reported in the story. Just dismissed. (This finding is dismissed based on a 3/22/26 update)

II. Who Would Be in That Room — An Elimination Analysis

Begin with the basic question: on the afternoon of November 20, 2014, hours after Myron May had been shot and killed at FSU, who could plausibly be present in his private cottage, crouching near the kitchen, while a local news crew filmed the interior?

Law Enforcement Processing the Scene

Law enforcement personnel processing a crime scene are in identifiable roles: uniformed officers, detectives in civilian clothes who identify themselves to media, evidence technicians in protective gear. They stand, they move deliberately, they are documented in chain-of-custody records. They are never crouching in the background of a media broadcast of a scene they are processing. Crouching in the background of a broadcast frame is the behavior of someone who does not want to be seen — which is not the behavior of any legitimate law enforcement role at a crime scene.

Furthermore: if law enforcement was present and processing the scene, they would not permit a local news crew to move freely through the space filming the interior. Evidence processing requires scene control. The presence of a news crew filming freely inside suggests either that the scene had already been cleared and released — extraordinarily fast for a homicide investigation on the same day as the event — or that the normal protocols of scene preservation were not being applied.

The Reporter and Camera Operator Themselves

This is the element that the essay must address directly, because it is the most important. The reporter, Jacqueline Ingles, and the camera operator were (hypothetically) present in that room. They had line of sight to the crouching figure. The camera operator was filming the room. The reporter was composing her shot. Neither of them was crouching behind a lamp. They would have been able to see the figure clearly.

A camera operator filming a room does not miss a crouching person in red and black in the background of their shot. A reporter preparing to deliver on-camera commentary from a deceased shooting suspect’s living space does not fail to notice a person crouching between the furniture and the kitchen. The figure’s presence in the footage is not a function of it being invisible to the people filming. It is a function of those people choosing not to address it on camera, or of the broadcast being aired before anyone reviewed the frame carefully enough to catch it.

The three possibilities are: (1) the figure was visible to the reporter and camera operator and their choice not to address it was deliberate — they understood who it was; (2) the taping was hastily done missing the figure and the footage was not reviewed carefully before broadcast; or (3) the footage was handed to them; (or) a combination of 1, 2, and 3.

If the doll was there and not a real person, it would have been the oddest thing.

The Most Parsimonious Explanation

The figure in red and black, crouching in the background of broadcast footage of Myron May’s home on the day of the FSU shooting, is most parsimoniously explained as: a person connected to the operation that had been run against May, present in his home on the day of the shooting for reasons related to that operation, whose presence in the footage was known to those filming and not disclosed. But the reporter and cameraman were not necessarily in on it. The red and black is an operational clue. May does not mention any pattern of black and read according to the two-hour video that he posted. The only mention of red was when his friend’s daughter’s red SUV was repossessed.

There are other anomalies.

Anomaly 7: The Speed of Media Access to the Private Residence

The FSU shooting occurred on the night of November 20, 2014. May was identified and killed by police at the scene. By the time Tampa Bay 28 was broadcasting footage from inside his private cottage — the investigation was hours old at most. The speed of media access to a private residence, on the same day as the event that made it newsworthy, is not consistent with normal crime scene protocol.

When a person is identified as a mass shooter, their residence becomes an evidence site. Evidence sites are secured, cordoned, and controlled. Law enforcement establishes a perimeter. Media access to the interior of an active evidence site on the day of the event, in a way that allows free filming of the living space, is not standard procedure. It requires either that the scene had already been processed and released — extraordinarily quickly — or that the normal securing of the scene did not occur as it should have.

What makes this particularly relevant is that initial news stories repeatedly mentioned the lack of a motive. The day of the shooting a friend said, “I don’t think anybody that knows Myron would tell you that they expected this from him…Myron was a good boy — a good man — something went terribly wrong and I don’t know what that was.” The same witness stated that, “He saw a guy with a gun at his apartment.”

This is now part of the documentary record. It cannot be unbroadcast. The footage exists in archives. The frame exists. The figure is visible in the frame.

What the record now contains, in combination with Pearson’s documentation, is the following: a broadcast from inside the home of a targeted individual on the day of his death, containing a figure in the operational color that his targeting program used as a calling card, filmed by journalists who were present (hypothetically) in the room and had line of sight to the figure, broadcast before the frame was reviewed carefully enough to catch or suppress the figure’s presence, and connected by operational specificity to the color pattern independently documented a decade later by a different targeted individual in a different city who survived the same program and documented it with researcher’s precision.

The significance is not merely that the color appears in both cases. The significance is that the color appears in May’s home, on the day of his death, in a context — crouching, concealed, present in a space that should have been a secure crime scene — that has no innocent explanation consistent with all the other anomalies of that day: the speed of media access, the absence of normal scene cordoning, the claimed ignorance of motive in the face of a documented two-hour video, and the presence of a news crew filming freely in the interior.

VII. What This Discovery Establishes — and What Remains Unknown

It establishes: that a figure in red and black was present and crouching in the background of broadcast footage from inside Myron May’s home on November 20, 2014. That this presence is visible in a broadcast frame filmed by journalists who were in the room. That the procedural context — same-day access, uncordoned premises, simultaneous ‘unknown motive’ framing — is anomalous in multiple independent respects. That the color combination connects to a documented operational pattern independently observed and statistically tested by Richard Pearson in his 2025–2026 campaign documentation. That Pearson’s documentation and May’s video testimony are structurally convergent in every operational element.

It does not establish: the identity of the crouching figure. The specific role of reporter Jacqueline Ingles or the camera operator in relation to the operation. Whether the broadcast was intentionally used to place the operational signature in the record. The specific organizational or institutional identity of the operation against May.

What it establishes is sufficient to demand serious investigation. A crouching figure in red and black, in the home of a dead targeted individual, broadcast by journalists who were present in the room, on the same day as his death, at premises that should have been cordoned crime scene evidence sites, in the context of a claimed ‘unknown motive’ that contradicts the existence of a documented two-hour explanatory video — this combination of anomalies, connected to the independently documented operational color pattern of a separate targeted individual’s campaign a decade later, is not explicable by coincidence. Occam’s razor points, again, toward one mechanism rather than many.

VIII. Implications for the Broader Record

The discovery of the red and black figure in the May broadcast footage changes the evidentiary structure of the comparative record in a specific way: it places the operational color signature of the program in documentary evidence from 2014, eleven years before Pearson independently documented it, in the home of a targeted individual whose case is structurally identical to Pearson’s.

This matters for two reasons. First, it establishes temporal continuity. The red and black is not something specific to Pearson’s 2025 campaign. It appears in the documentary record of a 2014 case, in an operational context — crouching in the home of a completed target — that is entirely distinct from Pearson’s street-level observation. The color pattern predates Pearson’s experience and appears in the record of a case he had not yet encountered when his own targeting began.

Second, it changes the character of Pearson’s documentation from individual observation to cross-case corroboration. When Pearson documented the red and black in September 2025, he was observing something he interpreted as specific to his campaign. The discovery of the same color in the May footage establishes that the color is not specific to Pearson’s campaign — it is present in the record of a separate campaign against a separate target.

The May footage is, in this sense, the most significant single piece of physical evidence that has emerged from this entire analytical project. It is not a testimony. It is not a document produced by a targeted individual. It is broadcast footage, archived, publicly available, produced by a news organization, filmed by journalists who were present in the room — and it contains, in a frame they apparently did not examine carefully enough, the operational signature of the program whose existence Pearson has documented across fourteen months of systematic observation in Washington, D.C.

Conclusion: The Frame That Changes the Record

On November 20, 2014, Myron May wounded people at Florida State University. That was wrong. The people he shot were innocent of what had been done to him. He directed his desperation at people who deserved no harm, and three of them were wounded. There is nothing in this essay that diminishes that fact or the suffering of those people.

On the same day, in the same hours, in the home that May had described as entered and monitored and operationally managed around him for months, a Tampa Bay 28 news crew filmed the interior. And in the background of that footage, crouching between the furniture and the kitchen, partially concealed behind a lamp, is a figure in red and black.

The reporter was in that room. The camera operator was in that room. They filmed the figure without identifying it on camera. The broadcast aired before the frame was reviewed carefully enough to catch or address the figure’s presence. The home was accessible to a news crew on the same day as the event, without the cordoning normal to an active homicide evidence site, in the context of a claimed ignorance of motive that is inconsistent with the existence of May’s documented two-hour video.

And the color of the figure — red and black — is the color that Richard Pearson, in a different city, a decade later, documented through control studies and probability calculations and systematic photography as the operational calling card of the program being run against him. The color that appeared at his framing operation site near the Capitol, in the red hat and black shirt left as a calling card in the plastic tub. The color that appeared in statistically anomalous concentration along his routes through Washington, D.C., at density that his baseline measurements established could not be explained by normal variation.

The figure in the frame connects the two cases through the color. It places the operational signature of the program — documented by Pearson in 2025 — in the documentary record of May’s 2014 case, in May’s own home, on the day of his death, in footage that was broadcast publicly and has been available in archives for eleven years. It was there, waiting to be seen, for the entire decade between May’s death and Pearson’s documentation. Pearson’s documentation is what made it visible.

The record now contains: a crouching figure in red and black in the home of a dead targeted individual. It was filmed. It was broadcast. It exists. And it connects to the documented operational pattern of a man who survived the same program and wrote everything down.

The figure in the frame is the program’s signature, visible in the record, connecting 2014 and 2025, connecting Tallahassee and Washington, connecting a man who was killed and a man who is still writing. The record exists. The connection is made. The question is what anyone will do with it.

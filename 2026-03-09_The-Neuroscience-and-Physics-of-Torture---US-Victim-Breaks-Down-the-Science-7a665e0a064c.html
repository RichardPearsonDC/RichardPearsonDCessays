II. Voice-to-Skull: The Physics of the Georgetown Incident

2.1 What Pearson Described

On July 13, 2025, near the US Capitol, Pearson began hearing a woman’s voice that he described with forensic precision:

Two details are scientifically critical: the room ambience, and the singular non-recurrence.

2.2 Mechanism One: Parametric Directional Audio

Parametric audio — also called directional audio, hypersonic sound, or audio spotlight — exploits the nonlinear acoustic properties of air to create a tightly focused column of audible sound.

The physics: two ultrasonic carrier waves, typically in the 40–80 kHz range, are emitted simultaneously from an array of transducers. Ultrasonic waves are inaudible to humans and travel in tight, directional beams with minimal dispersion. Where the two beams converge, they interact nonlinearly with air molecules — a process called self-demodulation — and produce an audible difference frequency as a byproduct. This audible sound exists only at the convergence point. A person standing in the beam hears it clearly. A person one meter away hears nothing because the convergence point is not where they are standing.

The subjective experience: the sound appears to originate inside the listener’s head. There is no apparent external source. The directionality that normally allows us to locate sound — the tiny timing differences between what each ear receives — is absent, because the sound is generated at the point of the listener’s own position rather than propagating from a distant source. The brain, which uses interaural time difference to localize sound, receives a signal it cannot place. The result is a voice that seems to come from within.

This technology is not classified. It is commercially patented. Woody Norris received a DARPA grant and later an Ig Nobel Prize for its development. The American Technology Corporation commercialized it as HyperSonic Sound. It is installed in the American Museum of Natural History. It is used in retail environments to deliver targeted audio to specific locations without disturbing adjacent areas. It has been deployed from US Navy vessels since 2004, mounted on LRAD systems for long-range communication and crowd control.

2.3 The Room Ambience: The Forensic Key

The detail Pearson provided that is most scientifically significant is the room ambience. He heard not just a voice but the acoustic environment surrounding the operator — other people, microphone quality, the texture of an enclosed space.

This is not what a pre-recorded or synthesized voice sounds like. Pre-recorded audio carries whatever acoustic environment was present during recording. Synthesized audio carries none. What Pearson described is the signature of a live audio feed — a microphone in a room, capturing the operator’s voice and the background noise of the operational environment simultaneously, transmitting that feed in real time to a parametric emitter directed at Pearson.

The implication: there was a staffed operations room. Someone was speaking into a microphone. The audio was transmitted live to a directed emitter. The emitter was aimed at Pearson, tracking his position, for the duration of the operation.

This is a significant infrastructure commitment. It is not a simple device. It is a system — communications link, real-time positioning, active tracking, live operations staff.

2.4 Mechanism Two: The Microwave Auditory Effect

A second mechanism, independently capable of producing voice-to-skull perception, has been documented in the scientific literature since 1961.

Allan Frey, a biophysicist, published the first formal study of what became known as the Frey effect or microwave auditory effect in the Journal of Applied Physiology in 1962. Frey demonstrated that pulsed microwave radiation — specifically, radio frequency energy pulsed at specific repetition rates and pulse durations — produced auditory sensations in human subjects who were not near any conventional sound source. Subjects described hearing buzzing, clicking, or in some configurations, more complex sounds, including speech-like patterns.

The mechanism: pulsed microwave energy is absorbed by tissue and produces rapid, localized thermoelastic expansion — the tissue expands microscopically in response to heating, then contracts as the pulse ends. This mechanical expansion produces a pressure wave that propagates through the skull to the cochlea via bone conduction, bypassing the outer and middle ear entirely. The cochlea transduces it as sound. The brain perceives it as originating inside the head.

The effect has been confirmed across dozens of studies in the intervening six decades. It is not disputed science. It appears in the peer-reviewed literature, in military research publications including the US Army War College Quarterly, and in the 1998 paper by Timothy Thomas titled “The Mind Has No Firewall” — a paper Pearson himself cited and brought to congressional offices in December 2025.

Critically: the Frey effect operates on different physics than parametric audio. It is electromagnetic, not acoustic. It travels as radio frequency radiation, not pressure waves. It penetrates walls and conventional barriers. It cannot be blocked by ear protection. It is attenuated by conductive materials — metals, Faraday enclosures — which is precisely why Pearson’s metal cap experiment in February 2026 produced the result it did.

2.5 Which Mechanism Produced the Georgetown Voice?

The room ambience detail points strongly toward parametric audio as the primary mechanism for the Georgetown voice. The Frey effect produces auditory percepts but does not carry external acoustic environments — the skull does not act as a microphone capturing the operator’s room. Parametric audio, being a live transmitted audio signal, carries whatever the transmission carries, including background noise.

However, the two mechanisms are not mutually exclusive. A sophisticated deployment might use both simultaneously — parametric audio for intelligible voice transmission carrying the live feed, and Frey effect pulses for additional auditory impact or for aspects of the experience that benefit from the electromagnetic pathway, such as penetrating barriers or operating at distances where parametric audio degrades.

The singular non-recurrence is also significant. Pearson told the DBH evaluators: “Only that day. Never again.” This is not the pattern of a technological malfunction or an accidental exposure. It is the pattern of a deliberate single deployment — a test, as the voice itself identified it — after which the operators determined that this specific high-signature approach would not be repeated. The technology was used once, achieved its operational test objectives, and was withdrawn.

III. Pinning: The Neuroscience of Manufactured Desire

3.1 The Phenomenon

Pearson used the term “pinning” to describe experiences of being drawn toward specific locations or objects in ways that felt autonomous — like genuine curiosity or desire — but that he recognized, through accumulation of evidence, as externally induced. He documented multiple suspected pinning events and at least one he was highly confident about: the tub incident of November 7, 2025.

The critical feature of pinning, as Pearson described it, is its phenomenology. It does not feel like compulsion. It does not announce itself as external. It feels exactly like a normal human impulse — the mild but persistent inclination to go somewhere, investigate something, take a particular route. The subjective experience is indistinguishable from autonomous desire. This is not incidental to the technology. It is definitional.

3.2 The Neuroscience of Place and Navigation

To understand how pinning works, we must first understand how the brain normally generates navigational behavior.

The hippocampus — a bilateral structure located approximately 5–6 centimeters deep in the medial temporal lobe — contains what are called place cells: neurons that fire selectively when an animal or person occupies a specific location in space. Place cells were discovered by John O’Keefe, who shared the 2014 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine for this work. Together with grid cells in the entorhinal cortex, place cells form the brain’s internal GPS — a cognitive map of space that the organism uses to navigate.

Critically, the hippocampus does not merely record where a person is. It plays an active role in generating navigational motivation — the felt sense of wanting to go somewhere, the pull toward a destination, the impulse to explore or investigate. The hippocampus is the interface between spatial representation and motivated behavior.

When hippocampal place cells are activated artificially — made to fire as if the organism were at a specific location or moving toward one — the organism experiences navigational motivation consistent with that activation. This is the mechanism underlying pinning.

3.3 The 2020 Cell Study: Proof of Concept

In 2020, Carrillo-Reid and colleagues published a landmark study in Cell demonstrating that targeted activation of specific hippocampal place cell ensembles in mice using two-photon holographic optogenetics could drive the animals to specific locations in a maze.

The key finding: the mice moved to the targeted locations and experienced the movement as volitional. They were not dragged. They were not obviously distressed or confused. They moved as mice normally move — apparently choosing to go where the activated place cells suggested they were or should be. The behavior was indistinguishable from spontaneous navigation.

This is the scientific foundation of pinning. The organism does not experience external control. It experiences desire. The manipulation operates upstream of conscious decision-making, at the level of the spatial representation that normally generates navigational motivation. By the time the organism is aware of wanting to go somewhere, the wanting has already been manufactured.

3.4 Non-Invasive Human Application: The 2024 Nature Neuroscience Study

The Carrillo-Reid study used optogenetics — light-based neural activation requiring genetic modification of neurons and optical access to brain tissue. It demonstrated the principle but not a non-invasive human application.

The 2024 study by Boyle and colleagues in Nature Neuroscience closed that gap. The researchers demonstrated modulation of human hippocampal navigation using transcranial focused ultrasound — non-invasive, requiring no implants, working through intact skull and tissue.

Transcranial focused ultrasound works by converging multiple low-intensity ultrasonic beams at a target point within the brain. Where the beams converge, the mechanical energy is sufficient to modulate neural activity — enhancing or suppressing firing depending on parameters. Away from the convergence point, each individual beam is below the threshold for neural effect. The spatial precision is millimetric for targets at depth. The hippocampus, despite its deep location, is accessible.

The subjects in the Boyle study showed measurable changes in navigational behavior and spatial cognition corresponding to the targeted stimulation. They did not report feeling controlled. They reported — as the Carrillo-Reid mice showed behaviorally — that their navigational experience felt normal.

3.5 DARPA and the Operational Pipeline

The Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency’s Neural Engineering System Design program, initiated in 2016, explicitly described writing to neural tissue non-invasively as a core capability goal. The program’s stated objectives included bidirectional neural interfaces — both reading from and writing to neural tissue — without surgical intervention.

Dr. James Giordano, a neuroscientist at Georgetown University and a Pentagon ethics consultant who has briefed the Defense Intelligence Agency and the National Security Council, testified before the House Armed Services Committee that tools for writing behavior to the human brain have been, in his words, “loosed into the real world.” He has published extensively on the weaponization of neuroscience and described the development of what he calls “neuroweapons” capable of targeted behavioral influence.

The pipeline from the Cell study to the Nature Neuroscience study to the DARPA program to operational deployment is not a pipeline that takes decades. It takes years. The Cell study was 2020. The Nature Neuroscience study was 2024. The DARPA program has been funded since 2016. Pearson was targeted in 2025.

The timeline is not comfortable.

V. The Field Experiment: What Pearson Proved

5.1 The Metal Cap Experiment

February 20, 2026. Pearson placed a metal-cased iPhone and a V9 battery in his knitted cap and walked his usual route. His documented finding: “No clicks! I had been consistently getting clicks.”

He then removed the metal and walked through confirmed click locations. The clicks resumed. He was explicit: “It doesn’t prove anything but…”

He was being appropriately cautious. But the experiment has genuine scientific structure. It is a within-subject, condition-varied, replicated field experiment with a specific dependent variable — the presence or absence of auditory clicks — and a specific independent variable — the presence or absence of a conductive barrier near the skull.

The result is consistent with exactly one of the two candidate mechanisms:

The Frey effect — microwave auditory effect — is attenuated by conductive materials. The physics is straightforward: metal reflects and absorbs radio frequency radiation, reducing the energy available to produce thermoelastic tissue expansion and the resulting pressure wave. A partial Faraday structure — the metal-cased phone and battery in the cap — would attenuate but not eliminate the effect, which is consistent with Pearson’s observation of significant reduction rather than complete elimination.

Spontaneous tinnitus, dental artifacts, atmospheric sounds — none of these are attenuated by a metal-cased phone in a cap. They are internal or environmental and are unaffected by electromagnetic shielding.

The experiment ruled out the most common alternative explanations and produced a result consistent with electromagnetic mechanism. It is not proof beyond reasonable doubt. It is what field experiments produce: evidence of mechanism consistent with one hypothesis and inconsistent with others.

A properly conducted laboratory replication — spectrum analyzer at the documented locations, standardized shielding conditions, objective measurement rather than subjective report — would either confirm or refute the finding. That experiment has not been conducted. It should be.

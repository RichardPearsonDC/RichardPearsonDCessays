I. Start Here, Before You Decide

Before you decide what Richard Pearson is — before you reach for the explanation that feels most comfortable — consider a single institutional fact.

Richard Pearson was assaulted. He called the police. The police arrested him, not his assailant, citing a third witness as the basis for probable cause. That third witness was not named in the arrest record.

Pearson then verified his arrest in the DC Court system. There was no record. He checked three times across three separate visits. The arrest exists in the Metropolitan Police Department’s system. It does not exist in the court’s.

He went to MPD headquarters. An officer there gave him a specific instruction: do not show the DC Court your arrest record.

Pause here. An officer at police headquarters told a citizen not to bring a police document to a court — a court that, as Pearson had now verified three times, has no matching record of the arrest that document describes.

You do not need to believe anything about directed energy weapons to find that sequence disturbing. You do not need any prior knowledge of COINTELPRO, MKUltra, or the history of the FBI’s targeting of Black intellectuals. You need only to ask what it means when a government arrests a person, the court has no record of it, and an officer advises the arrestee not to create the confrontation between the two databases.

That question — asked seriously, followed wherever it leads — is the question Richard Pearson has been publishing into public record, in real time, for fourteen months.

II. What Most People Will Do With This

Most people who encounter Pearson’s account will not ask that question.

They will read about the ceiling sounds that tracked him to his closet, the forty-three people in coordinated red and black across a ninety-minute walk, the voice he heard once on a specific documented day, and they will reach — with the speed and automaticity of a reflex — for the psychiatric explanation.

This is understandable. The psychiatric explanation is available, familiar, and social-cost-free. It requires no revision of existing beliefs about American institutions. It does not implicate anyone. It resolves the dissonance between what Pearson describes and what we prefer to believe about the country we live in by locating the problem inside him rather than inside the system.

It is also, as Pearson documents with methodological precision, exactly what the program is designed to produce.

This is not a paranoid claim. It is a documented operational design specification. The US Army’s own research thesaurus maintained a category called “Voice to Skull Device” until the entry was removed from public access. Allan Frey published findings on the microwave auditory effect — the ability to transmit sound directly into the human auditory cortex, bypassing the ears, leaving no external trace — in 1961. The symptom profile of this technology is clinically indistinguishable from paranoid schizophrenia. That indistinguishability is not an unfortunate coincidence. The weapon was engineered to mimic the disease because the misdiagnosis is the operational outcome.

And on March 8, 2026 — nine days before this essay was completed — 60 Minutes broadcast an investigation finding that the CIA had possessed classified scientific documentation confirming the reality of this technology while publicly denying that any such science existed. The diplomats and intelligence officers who reported clicks, pressure, and voices — US government employees, credentialed, institutionally embedded, impossible to dismiss on psychiatric grounds — had been telling the truth for a decade while their own agency called it mass psychogenic illness.

Richard Pearson, sleeping on a mattress in a closet in Washington DC, has been documenting clicks with GPS coordinates since February 2026. The same clicks. The same acoustic signature. The same government.

The difference between the diplomats and Pearson is not what happened to them. It is the credibility afforded to the people describing it.

III. The History That Most Americans Were Not Taught

There is a reason Pearson’s account is difficult to receive: it requires historical knowledge that the American education system was not designed to produce.

Between 1956 and 1971, the FBI ran a program called COINTELPRO — the Counter Intelligence Program. Its targets included the Black Panther Party, the Southern Christian Leadership Conference, the Nation of Islam, and individual Black intellectuals whose work was deemed a threat to existing social arrangements. Its tactics included: informant infiltration of social networks, forged letters designed to incite internal conflict, false arrests on fabricated probable cause, coordination with local police departments to criminalize targets through arrest records that were never intended to produce prosecutions, and the systematic manufacture of psychiatric attribution to discredit people who accurately reported what was being done to them.

Fred Hampton, chairman of the Illinois Black Panther Party, was killed at 21 in a pre-dawn raid while asleep. The FBI had paid the informant who provided the floor plan of his apartment. This is not disputed. It is in the congressional record.

The FBI sent Martin Luther King Jr. an anonymous letter explicitly urging him to commit suicide. The letter was sent days before he received the Nobel Peace Prize. The Senate Church Committee, which investigated COINTELPRO in 1975, called this one of the most shameful chapters in the history of the Bureau.

The Church Committee documented the program, produced findings, and recommended reforms. Congress passed new oversight laws. The program’s infrastructure — the state secrets privilege, the legal architecture that prevents civil suits from reaching merits adjudication, the contractor structure that provides deniability — was never dismantled.

In 2017, the FBI created a new domestic terrorism designation: Black Identity Extremist. No statutory basis. No criminal conduct required for targeting. Directed specifically at Black activists in the post-Ferguson era. This is documented. It happened eight years ago.

Pearson’s DEI research was designated a hostile ideology by executive order within months of his PhD defense. He is a Black equity scholar whose work makes racial hierarchy harder to justify, in the most surveilled city in America, experiencing an operational sequence that matches COINTELPRO’s documented methodology with precision that is not approximate. It is exact.

IV. Gloria Naylor, Thirty Years Earlier

In 1996, Gloria Naylor — National Book Award winner, one of the most accomplished American novelists of her generation — went to her home on St. Helena Island, South Carolina, to write a book and tend a garden.

What followed was a noise campaign from operatives who rented the adjacent property and ran chainsaws and hammers in patterns calibrated to her location within her own house. Unauthorized entry that left not theft but messages — a flashlight left on the mantle, seed packages tampered with, attic insulation disturbed. Her garden poisoned in nighttime raids. A trusted friend of ten years recruited as an informant, who dissolved sleeping pills in her wine and surrendered her laptop to operatives during a visit. Vehicle formations on her rural dead-end road. And then the voice — sustained, nightly, operating with such consistency that she sought psychiatric help, received a diagnosis of schizophrenia, was prescribed antipsychotic medication, and only later concluded that what she was experiencing was not a disease of her own mind but a technology directed at it.

She wrote it down. She published it in 2005, in a book called 1996. She died in 2016 without acknowledgment, without accountability, without a single institutional response adequate to what she had documented.

Richard Pearson did not know about Gloria Naylor before he began documenting his own experience. An AI connected them. When he read her account, he found his own — different decade, different city, different intellectual framework, the same operational sequence with operational-level specificity across thirty years of separation.

The dungeon did not change. The target profile did not change. Only the technology improved, the deniability deepened, and another person found themselves alone inside a system designed to make them unbelievable.

V. What Is Different Now

Everything that preceded Pearson happened in a world the program was built for.

Naylor wrote in retrospect, nine years after her targeting began. The Church Committee worked from recovered files, years after the programs had moved on. Du Bois, Robeson, Baldwin, King — none of them knew the full architecture of what was being done to them while it was being done. Their FBI files came through FOIA requests decades later, sometimes posthumously.

The program’s operational confidence rested on a single structural assumption: that the gap between experience and documentation was long enough to manage. Long enough for the target to be destroyed before the record was established. Long enough for the psychiatric attribution to take hold before the institutional claims could be examined. Long enough for the legal architecture to terminate any civil action before discovery could produce evidence. Long enough, simply, for the program to be over before anyone with power to act had cause to look.

Richard Pearson eliminated the gap.

He is publishing while the operation is running. Timestamped photographs. GPS-documented field experiments. Statistical analyses submitted to the DC Council. A preemptive mental health evaluation obtained specifically to defeat a trap he recognized before it closed. The arrest record gap documented before anyone could manage the gap. All of it distributed publicly, permanently, irreversibly, before any classification order could reach it.

He runs three Medium accounts simultaneously. One in his own name, providing first-person testimony. One under a pseudonym, providing the external analytical frame — historical, institutional, legal. One providing technical documentation: cross-referencing his account against the 1998 Army intelligence report on nonlethal weapons bioeffects, analyzing the Havana Syndrome acoustic signature against his own GPS-documented clicks, querying AI models for their predictions of post-surveillance targeting and finding that the predictions match his account with precision.

The pseudonyms are not fragmentation. They are the architecture of an investigation conducted by a single person who understood that his single voice would be insufficient and who built the corroborating structure himself because no institution would build it for him.

Together, the three accounts constitute something the program was never designed to encounter: a simultaneous first-person testimony, institutional analysis, and technical documentation of an operation, produced in real time, distributed into permanent public record, structured with enough methodological rigor to survive serious scrutiny.

VI. The Jan Karski Problem

In 1942, a Polish courier named Jan Karski crossed enemy lines, entered the Warsaw Ghetto and a transit camp, witnessed what was happening to European Jews, and carried his testimony to Allied leaders in London and Washington. He met with Felix Frankfurter, the Supreme Court Justice and himself a Jewish immigrant from Vienna.

Frankfurter listened. Then he said: “I am unable to believe you.”

Not that Karski was lying. Frankfurter said explicitly that he was not calling him a liar. He was saying that his mind — despite the evidence sitting across the table from him, despite Karski being the evidence — could not accommodate what was being described as real. The information was above his ceiling. The mind protects itself from that kind of information.

This is the primary obstacle Richard Pearson faces. Not malice from most of the people who have encountered his account and moved on. Not stupidity. A ceiling. The specific limit on what a person embedded in functioning institutions can accept as happening in those same institutions simultaneously.

The Frankfurter problem is not solved by more evidence. More evidence is available. It is documented. It is in the congressional record, the Senate Intelligence Committee report, the Army’s own research archive, the 60 Minutes investigation nine days ago. The problem is that evidence only moves people whose ceiling is already high enough to let it in.

The ceiling rises when the cost of disbelief becomes visible. When the question is no longer “could this be happening?” but “what has it meant for every person this happened to before Pearson, whose destruction was completed in silence, who we do not know to name?”

That is the question the diary is designed to force. Not through a single piece of evidence but through accumulated weight: timestamped photographs, GPS coordinates, clinical evaluations, police database gaps, named officers, verifiable addresses, the 1961 published science, the 2026 60 Minutes confirmation, the thirty-year Naylor parallel, and an arrest record that is in MPD’s system and nowhere in the DC Court’s, verified three times, with an officer at headquarters who told him not to go back.

Any one of these is manageable. Together, they require an explanation that does not route through psychiatric dismissal.

VII. Why This Is Historic

The word historic is used too freely. Here it is precise.

In the entire documented history of state targeting of political dissidents in the United States — COINTELPRO, MKUltra, Operation CHAOS, the Church Committee, the Black Identity Extremist program, the surveillance of Muslim communities, the FBI’s harassment of climate activists — no target has ever done what Richard Pearson is doing.

No one has ever documented an active operation against themselves in real time, with methodological rigor, across multiple registers simultaneously, and published it into permanent public distribution before the operation was complete.

The significance is not only personal. It is structural. Pearson has demonstrated that the gap the program depends on can be closed — by the right combination of intellectual tools, historical knowledge, methodological training, and digital infrastructure. He has shown that the ceiling on the program’s impunity is not fixed. That a target operating with sufficient clarity can convert the dungeon from a space of invisible operation into a space of visible, documented, publicly distributed record.

Whether that demonstration produces anything beyond itself — whether the journalists investigate, whether the congressional offices act, whether the civil rights organizations follow the evidence — is not yet known. Every previous exposure of this program failed to produce accountability sufficient to stop it. The Church Committee documented and did not prosecute. The MKUltra hearings documented and did not prosecute. The COINTELPRO revelations documented and did not prosecute.

But every previous exposure worked from recovered files, years after the fact, through institutional channels that the program had already learned to manage. Pearson is working in real time, from the inside, through channels the program cannot reach: the distributed, permanent, unclassifiable public record of a man who understood what was being done to him and decided to write it down before it was finished.

That is new. Its consequences are not yet written. They depend on whether the people reading this are willing to ask the question the arrest record demands — and to follow it wherever it leads.

VIII. What Is Being Asked

Nothing is being asked that requires certainty.

It is not being asked that you believe everything in Pearson’s account. Some claims are more verifiable than others. The institutional claims — the arrest record gap, the ACCESS DENIED flag, the assailant’s address tracing to federal law enforcement property — are specifically checkable. A journalist with access to public records and a spectrum analyzer could test them. A congressional office with subpoena power could test them. A civil rights organization willing to look at what happens when a private citizen’s address returns ACCESS DENIED in a police database could test them.

What is being asked is that you not route the question into dismissal before testing it.

What is being asked is that you understand what the psychiatric reflex costs — not in this case only, but across eighty years of cases that preceded this one, in which the reflex was deployed successfully against people whose names we do not know, whose destruction was recorded as mental illness, incarceration, suicide, cardiac event, whose documentation came too late or not at all, who are in the ground uncounted beneath a country that calls itself the world’s leading democracy.

What is being asked is that you consider the possibility that Richard Pearson — a man who went from a trailer park to a PhD and spent his career building methodological tools to name structural power precisely — is doing what he says he is doing: writing into the fire, in real time, from inside the oldest ongoing program in American institutional history, with the only weapon the program did not design an answer for.

The record exists. It is in the world. It cannot be classified now.

What you do with the knowing is the only question the dungeon did not design for.

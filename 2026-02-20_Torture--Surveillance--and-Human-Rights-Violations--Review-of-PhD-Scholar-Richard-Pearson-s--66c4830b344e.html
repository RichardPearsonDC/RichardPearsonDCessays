Torture, Surveillance, and Human Rights Violations: Review of PhD Scholar Richard Pearson’s Documentation

A ranked, scored examination of the most disturbing incidents in the Pearson case — with the subject’s own testimony. This is based on a review of Pearson 200+ page document, including photos and videos.

What the Ranking Reveals

Looking across these ten findings, a hierarchy of horror emerges that is also a map of the operation’s architecture. At the top sits the technology — the directed voice, the real-time interception — because it represents capability that most citizens do not know exists and that, once deployed against a private civilian, leaves the target with no coherent defense strategy. You cannot retreat from a voice that transmits inside your skull. But this technology has been around for decades. The public just has little knowledge of it.

The middle tier is occupied by the human network — Lee, Meyerowitz, the mattress-woman, the pointing man in Bethesda — because it reveals the operation’s scale. Operations of this kind do not run themselves. They require personnel, coordination, authorization, and a sustained institutional commitment. Every human asset deployed against Pearson represents a decision made by someone with authority to make it.

At the base sits the stolen bottle — small, quiet, domestic, returned as if by magic. It ranks last in shock but first in a certain kind of meaning. What the bottle tells us is that the operation is not merely strategic. It is also, somewhere in its architecture, enjoying itself. The bottle’s return served no intelligence function. It was a taunt. Operations that taunt their targets have crossed a threshold from professionalism into sadism — and that is perhaps the most disturbing finding of all.

Richard Pearson documented all of this. Under sustained assault, with no institutional support, with his phone compromised and his home violated, he wrote it down. That document — meticulous, evidence-anchored, analytically rigorous — is itself the most significant fact in this case. It is the reason the operation has not yet achieved its goal.

What Shocks Most: The Pearson Files, Ranked and Scored

A ranked analysis of the ten most disturbing incidents in the Richard Pearson case, with the subject’s own testimony

Rank 1 — “We Have to Make You Disappear”

Shock Score: 10/10 · July 13, 2025 · Georgetown, Washington D.C. Tags: Directed Energy · Psychological Operation · HUMINT Network

The Georgetown incident of July 13 is the most shocking single event in the Pearson record — not because it is the most violent, but because it is the most technologically audacious. On that afternoon, Pearson heard a clear internal voice directing him from the National Mall to Georgetown (“Go to Georgetown”), narrating his movements in real time, instructing him to crouch on the metro, and finally informing him: “That was a test.”

The technology consistent with this experience is well-documented across academic and declassified miliary writings. It’s just public awareness is just extremely low. The technology exists. Voice-to-skull transmission — designated “synthetic telepathy” or “microwave hearing” in military literature — is an acoustic effect achievable through pulsed microwave energy directed at a target. The United States military has acknowledged research under programs including MEDUSA and the Frey effect. The ACLU and multiple human rights organizations have raised alarms about its domestic deployment against civilians. What makes this finding rank first is not the technology alone. It is the content. The voice told Pearson, an American civilian on a public street, that an apparatus existed to erase him from his own life and deceive his family into believing him dead. This is not a threat. It is an operational briefing delivered to the target.

“Before I get to Georgetown the voice says, ‘We have to make you disappear.’ I screamed, ‘No, you’re not going to do that!’ I screamed, ‘Find another way! Find another way!’ Because that would mean that they would tell my mother that I’m dead even though I’m alive.”

Question to Pearson: When the voice said “we have to make you disappear” — in that moment, did you believe you were about to die? And what does it mean to you, looking back, that they told you rather than simply doing it?

Pearson answers: In that moment, I didn’t know what I believed. I was on a public street in the middle of the afternoon, crying. There were probably cameras that caught me. I remember screaming “Find another way” because the idea that my mother would be told I was dead — that she would grieve me while I was alive somewhere, erased — that was the thing I couldn’t accept. The lie of it.

But let’s pan out for a second. Voice-to-skull or the “Voice of God” technology has been around for decades — only refined and make even more frightening and a threat to constitutional guarantees. That’s not even the worst of it. You have Dr. James Giordano — a leading neuroscientist and ethicist — to say on his personal LinkedIn page that the US miliary has developed technology for “mind control” and “mind reading”. This is not science fiction. He directly advised the Pentagon. Where is Congress?! Where are the courts?! Why has the other two branches of government provided such a degree of legal cover for this to occur — for these weapons to exist. And how many people? How many people have been injured, succumb to mental illness, even death?!

Rank 2 — “They Think It’s Ok”

Shock Score: 9.8/10 · Approximately August 26, 2025 · Federal Center, Washington D.C. Tags: HUMINT Asset · Surveillance Confirmation · Psychological Operation

The encounter Pearson calls “Lee” is among the most chilling in the entire record — not because of overt threat, but because of what it reveals about the operation’s internal culture. A man in his late fifties, South-originating, sits beside Pearson at an outdoor table at Potbelly’s. He speaks entirely in code. He confirms, without naming sources, that “they” are listening in Pearson’s apartment. He says: “They think it’s ok.” He quotes Pearson’s own private apartment self-talk back at him. He says “You’ll see me again — maybe in another form,” and departs.

This is the anatomy of a sanctioned approach: an intelligence officer authorized to make partial contact, to deliver a message of omnipotence without breaching classification, and to increase psychological pressure without leaving actionable evidence. The phrase “accountable for what you know” is an implicit threat wrapped in bureaucratic language. “Once you see, you can’t unsee” is a warning that Pearson’s awareness places him in a category of elevated danger. Most shocking of all is the admission that they know this is happening and consider it acceptable. Someone at a decision-making level made a judgment that what is being done to this man is permissible.

“The most important takeaway is that they KNOW that people are listening and have failed to protect me from the privacy invasion and the psychological operation.”

Question to Pearson: “Lee” confirmed everything and then walked away. What did you feel in the hours after that conversation? And do you think he came to warn you, or to frighten you further?

Pearson answers: In the moment, I was deeply traumatized. I had little emotional room for what he was saying frankly. In the coming weeks, I felt something clarify. Did he come to warn me or frighten me? Both. I’ve settled on both. I think there was a human being inside the operation who felt something — not enough to breach protocol, not enough to name names, not enough to actually help me. But enough to sit down. Enough to say the thing out loud, even in code. “They think it’s ok” is not a neutral statement. There’s condemnation in it, buried. He knew it wasn’t ok. He knew and he sat with me for twenty minutes and then he stood up and left and I never saw him again, or at least not in that form.

The frightening part is what came after. A woman across the street immediately asked me what we’d talked about. That confirmed he’d been monitored. Which means my response was monitored. Which means the whole encounter was staged — his apparent conscience included. I gave him too much credit, maybe. But I was still being tortured at that point and I was hungry for evidence that somewhere in this machine there was a human being who thought it was wrong.

Lee was right. The next morning after I had privately commented on the police’s use of yellow tape — humoring myself as I watched a training exercise across the street — I found a cart full of yellow tape on my regular (but uncommon) walking path. Coincidence? Not at all.

Rank 3 — The Pointed Finger in Bethesda

Shock Score: 9.5/10 · July 24, 2025 · Bethesda, Maryland Tags: Real-Time Tracking · Phone Interception · Street Asset

Pearson made a deliberate counter-surveillance move: he hailed a taxi from Union Station rather than taking his regular metro routes, and booked a hotel in Bethesda without disclosing the name or location to anyone. Within hours of check-in, he stepped outside. A Black male in his twenties looked directly at him and silently pointed to the sky — the universal intelligence signal that location has been compromised, that the sky is watching.

For an asset to have been positioned at that hotel, the operation required either real-time interception of Pearson’s phone — active, live monitoring of his communications or GPS even with location services disabled — or access to his banking or hotel booking records in near-real time. This incident provides the clearest single proof point that the operation has surveillance-state infrastructure behind it — not a private individual, not a small organization, but an entity with access to telecommunications metadata and the personnel to respond instantly across jurisdictions.

Question to Pearson: You took every precaution, and they were still there. What did that moment do to your understanding of how far this goes? Was there any place left in your mind that felt safe?

Pearson answers: The taxi was deliberate. I had been varying my routes, leaving at different times, trying to disrupt whatever tracking they had on me. I initially left Washington, DC with the goal to buying my friend’s van to escape to Canada, but I chose a hotel stay instead. I didn’t tell anyone the name of the hotel, not even my friend. I just went.

When that man pointed to the sky, I felt something drop out of the floor of my thinking. The calculation I’d been running — if I do X they can’t follow — that calculation stopped working. You can’t counter-surveil an opponent who has your phone in real time and the personnel to respond in under four hours to a location you’ve never visited before.

Safe places. That summer I found partial safety in forests. In Anacostia, in greenspace near the airport, places where there weren’t many people. Not because surveillance doesn’t work outdoors — it does — but because there was something psychologically restorative about being somewhere I hadn’t been routed to. Somewhere I chose without any pattern they could anticipate. That mattered even if it only lasted an hour. The Bethesda hotel was supposed to be that. After the pointing man, I stayed in that room for nearly two days. Even the outdoors stopped feeling like mine for a while.

Rank 4 — The Power Button

Shock Score: 9.3/10 · July 9, 2025 · Southwest Washington D.C. Tags: Physical Intrusion · Gaslighting · Apartment Access

Someone entered Pearson’s apartment without any sign of forced entry, removed a metal power button from a kitchen appliance — a button that required a magnet to dislodge, as Pearson later demonstrated in a video recorded in real-time— and placed it in his laundry pile. Nothing was stolen. Nothing else was disturbed. The sole purpose was to make Pearson aware that someone had been inside while ensuring no one would believe him if he reported it. (Screenshot from video where Pearson tries to dislodge power button that was removed while he was away.)

This is the textbook definition of a black bag operation: a covert entry designed not to gather intelligence but to deliver a psychological message. We are inside your home. It offers no protection. The choice of object matters: a power button whose removal serves no material purpose is pure signaling. The fact that it required a specialized tool to remove makes the act more deliberate still. The contemporaneous communication to K.B. establishes that Pearson recorded this before any interpretive framework had been constructed.

Question to Pearson: Before you found the button in the laundry pile — had you already suspected they’d been inside? And how do you live in a space after you know it’s been breached?

Pearson answers: I hadn’t suspected it before that moment, no.

The button changed that. I spent probably thirty minutes trying to prove to myself it had fallen out accidentally. I turned the appliance upside down. I pressed around the edges. I got a butter knife. It wouldn’t budge. When the magnet pulled it loose, I stood there in my kitchen holding this small silver button and understanding that someone with a magnet had stood in this exact spot and removed it and placed it across the room. The only question was why.

Rank 5 — “NO” in the SORA Field

Shock Score: 9.2/10 · August 27, 2025 · AI Interface Anomaly Tags: Platform Intrusion · Digital Surveillance · Real-Time Interference

By late August, Pearson had been using OpenAI’s SORA image generator as a kind of message in a bottle — typing descriptions of his imprisonment, hoping to communicate to the outside world. While doing so, the word “NO” appeared in his text input field before he typed it, in the middle of a sentence he was composing. This happened twice. He checked his hand placement both times. The AI then generated — without matching his input — an image of a high-profile political figure behind prison bars with the words “Against the US Constitution.”

The significance is profound because it implicates a layer of digital infrastructure most people consider private and anonymous. If an operator could inject text into a browser-side AI interface in real time, it suggests surveillance extended to his computing sessions at the application layer. The generated political image he did not request raises the possibility that SORA’s output itself was being manipulated. This would require either a device compromise or a man-in-the-middle attack on his internet traffic — both within the capabilities of intelligence agencies with court-less network access.

Question to Pearson: You were using SORA as a digital cry for help. When “NO” appeared before you typed it, what did that do to your sense of whether any digital space was beyond their reach?

Pearson answers: I began forming a very clear picture of the monsters that I was confronting. They had no rules. They had no Constitution. They hated this country — all its civil liberties, civil rights, constitutional guarantees, and checks and balances. Only someone who hates this country would do that. This is not about following orders. It is about doing anything for a paycheck. Can you imagine what that psyche has led people like that to do?

Rank 6 — Four Months in the Noise

Shock Score: 8.8/10 · July–November 2025 · Southwest Washington D.C. Tags: Sleep Deprivation · Location Tracking · Sustained Torture

The acoustic harassment campaign in Pearson’s apartment is among the most revealing elements of the case because it demonstrates real-time location tracking inside his living unit. He moved his bed across the room — noises followed. He moved his mattress into the closet — noises followed. Past midnight, having not slept, he screamed in the hallway. He sought refuge in public parks, forests in Anacostia, greenspace near DCA, staying outdoors until past midnight. This continued for months.

Sustained sleep deprivation is classified as torture under the 1984 UN Convention Against Torture. The CIA’s own Inspector General reports have documented it as an “enhanced interrogation technique” — which is to say, it is a known instrument of state-applied psychological destruction. The fact that it was maintained for months, in a man’s own apartment, as part of a campaign simultaneously involving street harassment, legal interference, and directed energy attacks, constitutes not a single incident but an ongoing, low-cost, deniable method of breaking a human being down.

Question to Pearson: Looking back at who you were before July 2025, what did four months of this take from you that you haven’t fully recovered?

Pearson answers: The person I was before July 2025 trusted his own interior life. I trusted my thoughts, my rest, my sense of what was real when I woke up. Sleep is when the mind processes. It’s when you metabolize experience. They attacked that function deliberately and they were successful at it for a long time.

Rank 7 — The Mattress Is Against the Wall

Shock Score: 8.5/10 · August 3, 2025 · Pennsylvania Avenue SE, Washington D.C. Tags: Street Theater · Apartment Monitoring · Gaslighting

Walking on Pennsylvania Avenue, Pearson overheard a woman say to her companion: “The mattress is against the wall, you know what’s next.” He had recently moved his mattress and box spring against the wall. He had disclosed this to no one. He immediately understood and chased after the couple to photograph them. The woman gasped visibly when she realized she’d been photographed, then composed herself.

Someone was monitoring his apartment in real time and transmitting that information to street assets who then delivered it within his earshot. “You know what’s next” functions as a threat — an implication that the displacement of his mattress had been noted and consequences were being planned. The woman’s visible shock at being photographed confirms she was not a random passerby who happened to mention mattresses coincidentally. She knew she had been caught.

Question to Pearson: When strangers on a street narrate your private apartment, the home becomes a violation — there is nowhere private left. How did you decide to keep living there rather than abandoning it?

Pearson answers: Money. Resources. That’s the honest answer first. I couldn’t afford to leave. I’m unemployed. I was in D.C. because that’s where I live, and the alternatives available to me — leaving to where, exactly? — were not actually available. This is part of the design, I believe. Economic deprivation is a force multiplier for this kind of operation. It removes options.

But there’s another answer. If I left, I gave them the apartment. I gave them the evidence it contained — the broken bicycle component, the documentation, the physical record I was building. I also gave them the narrative. A man who abandons his apartment in the middle of a period of documented distress looks different to the outside world than a man who stays, documents, and continues to function. They were trying to produce visible instability. Staying was a refusal to perform the instability they needed.

The woman in the red dress — when she gasped, that was the most important moment. Because it confirmed, in her face, that she was not supposed to have been caught. That look of helplessness she gave her companion before she composed herself, that look told me everything I needed to know about what that moment was. She was an operative who had miscalculated. Yes, I ran after them and took the photo.

Rank 8 — Meyerowitz Names Trump and Kushner

Shock Score: 8.2/10 · July 16, 2025 · Old Town Alexandria, Virginia Tags: Intelligence Asset · Power Demonstration · Psychological Profiling

An elderly Jewish man approached Pearson unsolicited at Portner Park and delivered an extended monologue combining an unusual rental offer, personal connections to Donald Trump and Jared Kushner, an improbable Nazi-era family story, references designed to test Pearson’s cultural knowledge, and repeated dinner-appointment mentions. He gave Pearson a business card, which Pearson later destroyed fearing it contained a tracker. Immediately afterward, an elderly couple appeared, with the woman saying: “He shouldn’t have said anything.”

The name-dropping of Trump and Kushner was not social braggadocio. It was a power demonstration — a deliberate signal that the operation has political authorization at the highest levels of the current administration. The “confirmation team” who commented on the encounter is the clinching detail: their presence confirms this was a monitored, coordinated operation with real-time feedback. Not a conversation. A performance with an audience.

Question to Pearson: The Trump-Kushner name-drops — when you processed those later, did it clarify or complicate your picture of who was behind this? And what do you make of the fact that they sent someone like Meyerowitz rather than a more straightforward operative?

Pearson answers: Remember that this was early in the campaign. While I did journal, I just thought that I don’t really care about him or what he has to say. As the campaign escalated, I saw that encounter for what it was. I’ve thought about it. Sending a Jewish man with a Nazi-escape story and an African American musician story to assess a Black DEI scholar is sophisticated. It’s designed to navigate around my defenses — if he were a straightforward white operative in my face, I’d be alert immediately. An elderly Jewish man with a complicated history, who tells me his brother helped Black musicians get on the radio, who seems to be reaching across histories of shared oppression — that’s designed to engage me, to lower my guard, to make me respond warmly enough that they can assess my vulnerabilities. I didn’t care then and don’t care now. Just leave me alone.

Rank 9 — Non-Arrest of Assailant Who Physically Assaulted Him and Chemical Assault With Complete Impunity

Shock Score: 8.0/10 · 2025 · Washington D.C. Tags: False Arrest · Chemical Assault · State Violence

Pearson documents three acts of direct state or state-adjacent violence: a __, a physical assault where the assailant was not arrested (the upstairs neighbor who was harassing me), and a criminal chemical assault. Both occurred in 2025. Both involved forensic evidence Pearson possessed. Neither resulted in any accountability. This combination constitutes the strongest systemic evidence in the record: it is one thing to surveil and harass. It is another thing entirely to reach into the formal legal system and deploy it against a target, and then find that the target cannot access any accountability mechanism, public defender, or protective legal process.

The complete impunity with which both acts were committed speaks to an actor so embedded within the legal infrastructure of Washington D.C. that it can both create or make disappear charges and suppress criminal investigation of itself. This is the operational signature of an authorized domestic intelligence program. Rogue actors get investigated. The chemical assault — delivered to a specific individual — requires planning, access, and certainty that no investigation will follow. That certainty is itself evidence of institutional protection. Pearson believes that neurotechnology was deployed to precisely pin on on this occassion and others. “It’s just like the Sims video game! It can make you do stuff with the click of a button.”

Question to Pearson: At what point did the silence of the system itself become part of your evidence?

Pearson answers: I would say the latter part of 2025 — police blaming medication for the noise harassment, non-investigation of the noise, little concern when I was describing the physical assault. “Ok…ok…”, an officer said dismissively. It is incredible. What level of systemic decay would need to occur for all of this to happen to me and no one is held accountability in any real sense through the time of this writing?! I will tell you — authoritarianism and totalitarianism in the age of Donald Trump and COINTELPRO 2.0. Lawlessness. Corruption. People who put a paycheck over their country. They may say that they were “afraid”, but what is fear when the foundational principles of the entire country are so severely eroded as to render the Constitution obsolete — mere words on a paper. People always think that they will never be next and too often they are proven wrong.

Rank 10 — The Bottle Returns

Shock Score: 7.8/10 · August 22, 2025 · Greenleaf Park, Southwest Washington D.C. Tags: Physical Evidence · Coordinated Placement · Power Display

Pearson purchased a distinctive glass bottle at a Bethesda juice bar on July 26. It went missing. On August 22, at a community back-to-school event in his own Southwest D.C. neighborhood, he found it sitting on a tea vendor’s table. He identified it by a specific scratch on the logo, which he had photographed. The vendor had no explanation for how it arrived.

Someone took Pearson’s property, held it for weeks, and placed it in his path at a neighborhood event. Its only function is to communicate: we have been inside your life, we have touched your things, we know your neighborhood, and we can return what we take whenever we choose. This is territorial marking — a demonstration of omnipresence directed not at gathering information but at sustaining the psychological condition of helplessness. That it was done with a glass bottle makes it more unsettling, not less. The operation is, somewhere in its architecture, enjoying itself.

Question to Pearson: That’s an act designed to produce a very specific feeling. What was that feeling, exactly? And did you take the bottle back?

Pearson answers: The feeling was recognition. Not shock — by August 22 the capacity for shock had been worn down considerably. Recognition of a signature. It was the same feeling as the power button, the same feeling as the woman on Pennsylvania Avenue. A signal that said: still here. Still watching. Still touching your things. I maintain that they are monsters with mental defects . No one in the right state of mind does this kind of stuff really.

The specific affect of the bottle was almost domestic. Someone picked it up somewhere, kept it for almost a month, and then placed it on a table at a back-to-school event in my neighborhood for a tea vendor who had no idea how it got there. That’s an amount of operational effort for an object worth a few dollars. The only value of that effort is psychological. They spent human time and coordination on a glass bottle because the message it delivered was worth more to them than the resources it cost.

Did I take it back? No. I photographed it and I left it. It didn’t have the cap. But more than that — it wasn’t mine anymore. It had been in their hands, wherever their hands are. Taking it back would have been accepting it back from them, on their terms, at a time and place of their choosing. I bought a new one. I went back to Bethesda on August 24 and bought an identical bottle at the same juice bar. That felt like the right response. Not defiance exactly. Just refusing to let them decide what I owned.

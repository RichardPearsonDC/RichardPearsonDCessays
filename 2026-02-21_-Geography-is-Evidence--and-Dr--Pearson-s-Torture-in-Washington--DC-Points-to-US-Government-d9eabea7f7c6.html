The City That Cannot Plead Ignorance

There is a question that sits beneath every account of alleged government targeting of a civilian: could this have been done without the government knowing?

For most American cities, the answer is at least theoretically possible. A private contractor operating rogue technology, a criminal network with unusual resources, an unauthorized actor deploying signals in an unmonitored corridor — these explanations, however unlikely, cannot be ruled out entirely. Urban environments are large, complex, and incompletely surveilled.

Washington, D.C. is not most American cities.

It is arguably the most comprehensively monitored urban environment on the planet. Its airspace is among the most restricted in the world. Its electromagnetic spectrum is actively managed and surveilled by multiple federal agencies simultaneously. Its streets are walked by officers who report up chains of command that terminate in federal agencies. Its police department operates in constant institutional contact with the Secret Service, the FBI, and the Department of Homeland Security. Its public transit hubs are federal security zones. The signal environment of its neighborhoods, its corridors, its parks and plazas, is not a wilderness. It is a managed domain.

This is the argument that Richard Pearson, PhD, and those who have analyzed his account make with increasing precision: in Washington, D.C., the “unauthorized actor” theory is not merely unlikely. It approaches the impossible. And if unauthorized actors cannot plausibly account for what Pearson documented, the range of responsible parties narrows to one.

Geography is evidence. In D.C., it may be the most important evidence of all.

The Signal Intelligence Blanket

The National Security Agency’s primary mandate is signals intelligence — the interception, collection, and analysis of electronic communications and emissions. The NSA, operating in coordination with the FBI’s signals intelligence capabilities, maintains a surveillance architecture over Washington, D.C. that is without parallel in any American city.

This architecture is not passive. It is active, real-time, and tuned to detect anomalous electromagnetic activity. High-powered microwave systems, directed acoustic weapons, pulsed radio frequency emitters — the technologies Pearson suspected were being used against him — all operate within frequency ranges that the NSA’s domestic monitoring infrastructure would, by design, detect.

This is not speculation. It is the stated purpose of the signals intelligence apparatus that exists precisely to ensure that the capital’s electromagnetic environment is not compromised by foreign or unauthorized domestic actors. The NSA’s domestic surveillance programs, revealed in detail by Edward Snowden in 2013, demonstrated the comprehensiveness of the signal monitoring that blankets American communications infrastructure. Within D.C. itself, that monitoring is more intensive, not less, given the concentration of government facilities, diplomatic installations, and classified operations in the metropolitan area.

The implication for Pearson’s case is structural. If a private actor, a rogue contractor, or an unauthorized third party were operating directed energy technology — voice-to-skull transmission, through-floor tracking radar, pulsed electromagnetic emitters producing acoustic clicks at specific street coordinates — within the District of Columbia, the NSA and FBI would not simply be able to detect it. They would be expected to detect it. The failure to intervene would require explanation.

There are only three explanations available. Either the technology was not being used — a position increasingly difficult to maintain given the government’s own January 2026 disclosures. Or the technology was detected and judged non-threatening, implying the agency already knew who was operating it and why. Or the technology was not detected because those operating it were the same entities responsible for monitoring the spectrum — agencies that would not flag their own operations as anomalous.

In any of these scenarios, the “unknown private actor” theory dissolves. The signal environment of Washington, D.C. is not a space where unauthorized directed energy operations can persist for months without federal knowledge. Pearson’s experiences began in spring 2025 and continued into early 2026. That is not an incident. That is a sustained operation. In D.C.’s electromagnetic environment, sustained operations do not occur without federal awareness.

The ACCESS DENIED Incident

Among the most forensically specific details in Pearson’s document is what he calls the “ACCESS DENIED” incident — a moment that, is not merely suggestive but structurally diagnostic.

Pearson documents that when a Metropolitan Police Department officer ran his name through a law enforcement database, the query returned a large “ACCESS DENIED” flag rather than ordinary results. The officer’s own database search was blocked at a level above local police clearance.

This has a specific technical meaning in the architecture of American law enforcement databases. An “ACCESS DENIED” response to a records query, particularly one that blocks a local officer from seeing information that the system acknowledges exists, is characteristic of a Sensitive Compartmented Information block — an SCI flag attached to a person’s file that restricts access to personnel with a defined “need to know.” SCI flags of this kind are not placed on ordinary criminal records. They are attached to individuals who are subjects of active federal intelligence operations, who are under federal protection programs, or whose records are classified because their exposure would compromise an ongoing classified matter.

The Metropolitan Police Department is a local agency. Its officers do not have SCI clearances as a matter of course. An ACCESS DENIED response to an MPD officer’s query means that someone above the level of the D.C. police has placed a federal flag on Richard Pearson’s file — a flag significant enough to prevent local law enforcement from seeing his own record in his own city.

Pearson himself analyzes the implications carefully. He writes of “hidden systems” — a shadow infrastructure. The architecture of the information is itself evidence of oversight from a level above local jurisdiction.

This is not a bureaucratic glitch. In the law enforcement database architecture of Washington, D.C. — where local, federal, and intelligence systems interoperate at a level unique in American cities — an SCI block on a private citizen’s file is an institutional signature. It means someone classified Pearson. Someone decided that what the local police might find, if they looked, was not for local police to see.

The Jurisdictional Stand-Down

Washington, D.C. is the only American jurisdiction in which the Metropolitan Police Department operates in continuous institutional contact with the Secret Service, the FBI, and the Department of Homeland Security as a matter of standard operating procedure. This is not a special arrangement. It is the permanent condition of policing the nation’s capital, where federal equities intersect with local jurisdiction at every level.

The consequence of this arrangement is that MPD officers, unlike local police in any other American city, routinely navigate the question of federal primacy. When a federal agency has an interest in a matter — an ongoing investigation, a protected individual, a classified operation — MPD officers receive, through formal and informal channels, guidance about what to pursue and what to leave alone. The mechanisms for this range from explicit federal requests to informal “do not touch” communications that never appear in writing.

Pearson documents that he was physically assaulted inside his apartment building in late December 2025 — punched repeatedly by the upstairs resident, sustaining a black eye, lacerations, and bruising to his wrists documented in medical records. He reported the assault to MPD. He had medical documentation. He had visible injuries. He wrote to his property manager: “I did not assault and did not attempt to assault anyone. In fact, the assailant ran down the hall toward me and repeatedly punched me. I crouched and shielded myself and fled screaming.”

The assault was not investigated in any meaningful way that produced accountability. In any ordinary D.C. neighborhood, a documented physical assault with medical evidence and a named assailant would generate a police response. In Pearson’s case, it did not. The assailant was not arrested. In fact, he was not on the lease of the apartment where he resided. Property management did not know that he was living there with his supposed girlfriend who noted that he moved in in the summer of 2025. “That’s when the noise started,” exclaimed Pearson to DC police who brushed him off when they arrived after the physical assault.

The question the D.C. logic framework demands is not whether the MPD failed to act. Local police fail to act in assault cases with some regularity, for various mundane reasons. The question is whether the specific circumstances of this case — the SCI block on Pearson’s file, the federal flag on his record, the sustained nature of the campaign against him, the involvement of a building resident who Pearson suspects operates under some form of federal arrangement — created conditions in which an informal federal “stand-down” was operative. Pearson is convinced that the assailant had immunity, “I clearly was assaulted. They did not arrest him! These police are corrupt. That is why they refused to investigate the noise the week before the physical assault, trying to blame medication for the noise that I was hearing, repeating asking me if someone else lived there, and choosing not to visit the apartment upstairs. This is the new COINTELPRO. It has a federal face. It has a local police face and that local face is overwhelmingly African American.”

In a city where federal agencies routinely communicate with MPD about matters touching federal equities, a physical assault against a person whose file carries an SCI block would not be an ordinary local matter.

A local officer who cannot access a person’s records because of a federal flag is, structurally, already operating in a space where federal primacy has been asserted. The failure to investigate the assault is consistent with the same federal assertion of primacy that produced the ACCESS DENIED response.

The Logistical Impossibility of the “Private Actor” Theory

Perhaps the most damning component of the D.C. logic argument is the simplest: the operational complexity of what Pearson documented is incompatible with private or unauthorized execution.

Consider what would have been required. A sustained campaign across nine months, from spring 2025 through early 2026, involving coordinated actors in multiple locations across Washington, D.C. and its suburbs — Georgetown, Adams Morgan, Clarendon, Bethesda, Rockville, Anacostia, Capitol Hill, Southwest D.C., Old Town Alexandria. Ground teams positioned at locations before Pearson arrived, implying real-time knowledge of his movements and destinations. The logistics of “street theater” — coordinated actors in specific attire, positioned at specific times, delivering specific messages — across public transit corridors, government-monitored plazas, and federal zones including the National Mall, the Capitol grounds, and the area adjacent to the White House. Not to mention that his privacy was breached with audio surveillence and someone entering his apartment illegally.

A chemical assault executed at the Rockville Metro station, requiring advance positioning of a contact agent at a location Pearson occupied, an operation requiring knowledge of his itinerary that day, access to the specific location, and the deployment of a substance Pearson describes as “highly industrial,” not commercially available.

The coordination of upstairs residents in his apartment building to provide real-time acoustic harassment tied to his location within the unit — a sustained operation across months that required continuous communication about Pearson’s movements within a private residential space.

The generation and placement of specific items along Pearson’s walking routes — empty packages of his specific brand of personal products, a large water jug of the exact type he purchased — requiring knowledge of his consumption habits and current domestic supply.

Each of these operations, taken individually, requires significant logistical capability. Taken together, across nine months, across multiple jurisdictions including federal property, they require what Pearson correctly identifies as “intelligence-state logistics.” The coordination, the advance intelligence, the operational security, the personnel, and the sustained execution across a metropolitan area that is itself blanketed by federal surveillance — none of this is achievable by a private criminal organization, a rogue real estate cabal, or any non-state actor operating without federal support.

And they are not achievable by any actor, state or non-state, without the knowledge of the agencies responsible for monitoring Washington, D.C.’s electromagnetic environment, its security zones, and its law enforcement activity.

If the NSA monitors D.C.’s signals, and a sustained directed energy operation occurred in D.C., then the NSA knows. If the FBI coordinates with MPD, and MPD failed to investigate a documented assault against a person with an SCI block on his file, then the FBI was aware of the stand-down. If the Secret Service controls the security environment around the National Mall and Capitol grounds, and coordinated harassment operations were conducted in those spaces, then the Secret Service was aware.

The only coherent explanation for sustained, complex, multi-jurisdictional operations in Washington, D.C. that went undetected, unreported, and uninvestigated by the agencies whose explicit function is to detect, report, and investigate exactly such operations — is that those agencies were not uninformed. They were involved.

Pearson’s Own Analysis

Pearson reaches this conclusion through the same logical architecture, though he arrives at it from the inside. He writes: “I speculate that the administration relied on corporations to help generate a list of targeted individuals (TIs). I am not the only one, but may be one of the most intact survivors.” He speculates that a man similar in age, higher educational attainment, African American ethnicity, and activisim may have been a victim. That man had a suicide attempt in the summer of 2025. Another man approached Pearson in the evening, whispering “Me Too” as to suggest that not only does he know about Pearson’s predicament, but also that he is a similar victim. Pearson identifies a “federal and private hybrid model involving numerous players both individual and formal and informal networks/organizations.” He explicitly invokes the precedent: “Because it is a police/intelligence operation a la COINTELPRO.”

He also notes, with characteristic precision, a detail that the D.C. logic framework makes newly significant: “The administration updated Congress in January 2026. I don’t know if the two events are correlated.” The “two events” he refers to are his own visits to dozens of congressional offices in December 2025 to advocate for inquiry into neuroweapons — and the administration’s subsequent briefing of Congress on directed energy systems. The DoW CTO’s tweet came within weeks. The Washington Post’s arms race article followed in February.

Pearson was speaking to Congress in December. The government was briefing Congress on these weapons in January. The DoW CTO was announcing their scaling on January 23. Whether or not there is a causal relationship, the temporal proximity is notable. A private citizen with no institutional standing, no security clearance, and no media platform was knocking on the right doors at the right time about the right technology.

In Washington, D.C., that kind of accurate targeting of the relevant institutions is itself evidence of something. You do not know to go to the Senate Armed Services Committee about neuroweapons unless you have seen them from the inside.

The City as Witness

Washington, D.C. does not have a surveillance gap. It does not have electromagnetic dead zones where directed energy can operate undetected. It does not have jurisdictional spaces where MPD works without federal coordination. It does not have neighborhoods where a nine-month, multi-location, multi-actor intelligence operation can run without the knowledge of the agencies whose function is to know everything that runs in the capital.

The city is, itself, a kind of institutional witness. What happens in Washington, D.C. — particularly what happens to someone whose name carries an SCI block, on federal property, in federally monitored signal environments, over a period of months — happens with the knowledge of the federal government. The question is never whether the government knew. In D.C., the government knows. The question is what role it played.

The “unauthorized actor” theory does not just strain credibility in Pearson’s case. It contradicts the foundational architecture of the city in which the events occurred. Washington, D.C. is designed — institutionally, technically, legally — to prevent exactly what the unauthorized actor theory would require: sustained covert operations, involving directed energy and coordinated ground teams, running for months without federal detection.

If such operations occurred, they were federal. If they were federal, the ACCESS DENIED flag was not an anomaly. It was a signature. If they were federal, the MPD’s failure to investigate a documented physical assault was not negligence. It was instruction. If they were federal, the coordinated street theater across federally monitored zones was not logistically reckless. It was logistically routine — for agencies that own the logistics of the city they operate in. His assailant was not arrested. It was immunity and police collusion.

Richard Pearson did not choose Washington, D.C. as the location for his account because it made his claims more dramatic. He chose it because it is where he lives. But the city he lives in — the most surveilled, most monitored, most institutionally dense urban environment in America — is also the city that makes his claims most structurally coherent.

In Washington, D.C., unauthorized sustained covert operations are nearly impossible. Authorized ones happen every day.

Geography is not merely context. In this case, geography is the argument.

He Tried to Prove the Government Was Breaking Him

By a contributing writer

Richard Pearson did not begin by telling people he was being targeted.

He began by taking notes.

At first, the notes were mundane: times, locations, objects out of place. A bicycle damaged in a way that suggested tampering rather than accident. A routine altered, then altered again. Unexplained sounds in his apartment that seemed to follow him room to room. He logged what he could verify and, just as carefully, what he could not.

Pearson is trained to think this way. He holds a Ph.D. and had recently published academic work critical of how race and ideology are used in public policy research — work that was controversial but methodologically orthodox. He is not a provocateur by temperament. His writing is technical, restrained, and openly skeptical of overclaiming.

“I assumed I was missing something obvious,” he told me. “So I documented everything until either the pattern collapsed — or I did.”

The pattern did not collapse.

Documentation as Self-Defense

By summer, Pearson’s files had grown extensive: photographs with metadata preserved, receipts, screenshots, handwritten logs, probability calculations, maps of movement, physical items sealed and labeled. He saved timestamps not only for incidents, but for his own decisions — when he chose to walk instead of bike, when he decided to leave his apartment, when he considered calling a lawyer.

At one point, after a stranger made a comment that appeared to reference the interior layout of his apartment (“mattress against the wall…you know what that means…”), Pearson began sleeping away from home. When he later returned, he documented the apartment again, noting what appeared unchanged and what did not.

This was not, as he describes it, a search for confirmation. It was an attempt at disconfirmation. Pearson repeatedly notes in his records when nothing unusual happens. Entire days are marked “no events.” He tracked those, too.

What disturbed him was not any single incident, but the precision with which unusual encounters clustered around moments of vulnerability — legal outreach, attempts to seek help, efforts to explain what was happening to someone else.

The Voice

There is one episode Pearson recounts with particular care.

On a July afternoon, riding public transit, he heard what he describes as a clear, external female voice instructing him to go to a specific location in Washington. He complied, partially out of confusion, partially out of fear. The experience lasted minutes. It never repeated.

Pearson emphasizes this point repeatedly in his documentation: once, not ongoing; directive, not conversational; contextual, not intrusive.

He sought psychiatric evaluation afterward. He was not diagnosed with psychosis.

Mental-health professionals caution that such experiences can have multiple explanations. Pearson does not dispute that. What unsettled him was not the experience itself, but what followed: a series of encounters at the destination that appeared coordinated, testing his willingness to comply with further instructions.

“I understood immediately why a single event like that would permanently destroy my credibility,” he told me. “That’s why it only had to happen once.”

When Coincidence Stops Explaining

Among the most controversial elements of Pearson’s documentation is his analysis of clothing patterns — specifically, repeated appearances of people wearing red-and-black combinations near him during periods of stress.

Rather than asserting meaning outright, Pearson conducted baseline observations in comparable public spaces across the city, estimating expected frequency. He then compared those baselines to clusters he documented around specific dates and locations.

On one afternoon at Eastern Market, he logged more than forty such instances within minutes.

On another day, after discovering his phone missing, he documented six within a short span.

He does not claim every instance was coordinated. In fact, he argues that randomness is essential to the effect — that the impossibility of distinguishing signal from noise is precisely what renders the phenomenon destabilizing.

Skeptics argue this is exactly how confirmation bias operates. Pearson anticipated that critique and addressed it directly in his notes. “If this is bias,” he wrote at one point, “then it is bias that activates only when I attempt to assert my rights.”

Institutions That Would Not Look

Pearson contacted the police repeatedly. Officers appeared cautious, sometimes consulting supervisors before engaging. They declined to review recordings or investigate neighboring units in his building. At one point, Pearson documented a police system screen that appeared to flag his address in a way officers would not explain — “Access denied”.

He consulted attorneys. They explained that without an arrest or formal charge, there was little they could do. Claims involving national security, surveillance, or covert action rarely survive preliminary motions. Discovery is almost never granted.

He sought help from the mental-health system and received none — not because he was deemed delusional, but because the system is not designed to address claims of state misconduct.

Each institutional non-response reinforced the same conclusion: there was no mechanism to determine whether he was wrong.

A Theory Built from the Absence of Remedies

Pearson’s central hypothesis is less dramatic than it first appears. He does not claim a single agency orchestrated everything. He proposes a fragmented system: distributed responsibility, private contractors, compartmentalization, plausible deniability.

Crucially, he argues that the goal would not be arrest or prosecution, but erosion — of credibility, of stability, of social standing. Neutralization without charges.

Whether or not this theory is correct, it reflects an acute understanding of the contemporary American state: technologically powerful, legally insulated, and structurally resistant to accountability.

“What terrified me,” Pearson said, “was realizing that if something like this existed, it wouldn’t look like a movie. It would look exactly like this — boring, deniable, exhausting.”

The End State

By fall, Pearson was depleted.

He had stopped writing publicly. His savings were nearly gone. Friends grew distant, unsure how to respond. Every attempt to explain what he was experiencing seemed to make him sound less credible.

Eventually, he left the country.

Not because he could prove anything — but because he could not prove nothing.

“I reached a point where the question itself was destroying me,” he said. “Whether I was right or wrong stopped mattering. There was no way back to ordinary life.”

Why This Story Matters Even If He Is Wrong

There are explanations for Pearson’s experience that do not involve a covert program. Stress, isolation, and hypervigilance can profoundly alter perception. Experts rightly warn against extrapolating from personal experience to systemic conclusions.

But Pearson’s story exposes something else: a democracy in which the boundary between delusion and reasonable fear has become institutionally impossible to adjudicate.

We have built a national-security state that operates largely beyond public scrutiny. We have narrowed legal remedies for constitutional harm. We have normalized secrecy. And then we are surprised when citizens attempt to reason their way through the darkness alone.

If Pearson is wrong, the system had no humane way to show him that he was wrong.

If he is right — even partially — the implications are far darker.

Either way, the failure is collective.

A society that cannot credibly reassure its citizens that it is not quietly destroying them has already lost something essential.

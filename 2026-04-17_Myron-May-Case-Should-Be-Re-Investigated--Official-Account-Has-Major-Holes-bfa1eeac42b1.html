Myron May Case Should Be Re-Investigated: Official Account Has Major Holes

When AI told Dr. Richard Pearson that his experience with torture and harassment paralleled Myron May’s, fundamental questions emerged from Dr. Pearson’s research.

The Myron May Case and the Failure of Official Explanation

“This case has more holes than a block of Swiss cheese.” — Dr. Richard Pearson

On November 20, 2014, Myron May wounded three students at the Strozier Library at Florida State University. He was shot and killed by police at the scene. The case was officially closed in January 2015. The motive was publicly characterized as unknown, then later attributed to paranoid schizophrenia by a psychiatrist who never examined May.

That is the official account. It is also an account that cannot explain its own documentary record.

This essay does not claim to know what happened. It does not argue for a specific alternative explanation. What it argues is narrower and more damaging: that the official record of the Myron May case contains internal contradictions that the investigation never resolved, physical impossibilities that the police report never addressed, and evidentiary gaps so fundamental that the case, as officially closed, does not answer the most basic questions a homicide investigation is supposed to answer.

The people May wounded were innocent. Their suffering is real and not diminished by anything written here. But their suffering, and the legitimacy of their claim to a truthful account of what happened, is itself a reason to insist that the official record be held to account for what it cannot explain.

I. The Record Contradicts Itself on the Most Basic Question: Who Was the Shooter?

In any shooting investigation, the most fundamental question is: what did the shooter look like? Multiple independent witnesses, official dispatch records, and the surveillance footage that became the public face of this case all exist in the documentary record. They do not agree.

Jason Derfuss, the student who was shot and survived, told police he saw a male in a black hoodie. The official dispatch transmissions, documented in the police report, logged a black male in all black. A maintenance worker who flagged down Officer Sloan described the subject as wearing a red sweater. Witness Velez described a dark-colored hoodie.

“He was a male in a black hoodie running toward someone else by the picnic tables.” — Derfuss’ statement to police

“The dispatch transmissions included that shots had been fired by a black male in all black.” (page 4)

“While responding in his vehicle, (Officer Sloan) was flagged down by who he believed to be a maintenance person in a white Chevy Trailblazer. The subject told Officer Sloan that the subject was running back toward Strozier and he needed to stop him before he hurt someone. He also told Officer Sloan that the subject was wearing a red sweater.”

“Velez described he suspect as being dark skinned and he believed that he was wearing a dark colored ‘hoodie’.”

The surveillance footage that was released to the public and broadcast on news networks shows a figure in a vest, dress shirt, and slacks.

These are not variations in perception of the same clothing. A black hoodie and a vest with a dress shirt are categorically different garments. All-black clothing and slacks with a dress shirt are categorically different. The police report contains all of these descriptions. It does not attempt to reconcile them. It does not note the discrepancy. It presents the conflicting accounts and the surveillance footage as though they describe the same event without comment.

If the surveillance figure is May, then multiple independent witnesses — including the man who was shot — described clothing that May was not wearing. If the witnesses are accurate, then the surveillance figure requires explanation. The official record chooses neither path. It simply leaves the contradiction on the page and closes the case.

II. The Timeline the Report Requires Is Physically Impossible

The police report is precise about its timeline. Jason Derfuss exited the library at 12:22–12:23 AM. Approximately 20 seconds later, May walked in through the same door Derfuss had left from.

Twenty seconds. The report then draws on multiple witness accounts to reconstruct what occurred in the interval between Derfuss’s exit and the events inside the library. Combined, those accounts require the following sequence within that window:

Derfuss descends the library steps. A first gunshot is fired. The suspect shoots twice at someone by the picnic tables. The second target goes down. The suspect then shoots Derfuss from behind, the bullet stopped by his backpack and books. The suspect then changes from a black hoodie into a vest and dress shirt. The suspect enters the library, fires shots, backpedals on the steps firing more shots, and fires several more times across the walkway.

Twenty seconds.

The clothing change alone — from a black hoodie to a vest, dress shirt, and slacks — would require time that does not exist in the window the report itself established. A clothing change of that magnitude, outdoors, between a shooting and a library entry witnessed by people inside, in 20 seconds, is not physically credible.

The report does not address this. It does not note that the timeline is compressed. It does not offer an explanation for the clothing discrepancy within the timeline. It simply reports Derfuss’s account, the dispatch records, the other witness accounts, and the surveillance timeline as though they constitute a coherent narrative. They do not. The official record contains a physical impossibility at the center of its account of what happened, and the investigation closed without resolving it.

III. The Weapon in May’s Hands Has No Documented Path to Him

The weapon Myron May used in the shooting was a Lorcin .380. The ATF investigation, documented in the police report, traced it to a residential burglary in Panama City Beach in 2005. The report states explicitly:

“It is unknown how May came to be in possession of the firearm.”

This sentence appears in the official record of a closed homicide investigation. It means that the investigation ended without establishing how the shooter came to possess the weapon he used. Nine years elapsed between the gun’s theft and its use in the shooting. Those nine years are undocumented. The chain of custody is unknown.

This gap is made more significant by what the record does establish about May’s relationship to firearms. May legally purchased a Ruger SR22 from Bay Guns and Gear in Panama City thirteen days before the shooting. He observed the mandatory waiting period. He never returned to pick the gun up. His own pre-shooting to-do list, recovered by investigators, explicitly notes the uncollected firearm and asks friends to retrieve it or collect the money.

“May himself also admitted to never having picked up the gun, “I bought a Springfield 22mm pistol from Bay Guns and Gear in Panama City, FL. Please either pick this gun up or get the money for it.”

May, thus, documented his own legal firearm purchase and his failure to retrieve it. He was in possession instead of a stolen weapon with no traceable path to him. The investigation does not explain how this occurred. It notes the fact and closes the case.

In a living prosecution, the provenance of the murder weapon would be a fundamental evidentiary requirement. The case closed with the shooter dead, removing the prosecution requirement. But the question of how May came to possess that specific stolen weapon — and who, if anyone, provided it to him — was never answered.

IV. The Crime Scene Was Not Treated as a Crime Scene

When a person is identified as a mass shooter, their residence becomes an evidence site. Standard protocol requires that evidence sites be secured, cordoned, and controlled. Law enforcement establishes a perimeter. No external access is permitted until the scene is processed. Chain of custody for everything inside requires documentation.

Tampa Bay 28 broadcast footage from inside Myron May’s private cottage on the evening of November 20, 2014 — the same day as the shooting, hours after May was killed. The footage shows the interior of the living space: furniture, personal effects, scattered items. A camera operator moved freely through the space. A reporter composed shots from inside the residence.

The case against May was not formally closed until January 2015. On the day of the shooting, the investigation was hours old. The residence of the identified shooter was, by every standard of investigative procedure, an active evidence site.

The footage exists. It was broadcast. It shows a news crew with free interior access to a space that should have been cordoned. Either the scene was processed and released with speed that has no precedent in standard homicide procedure, or it was never properly secured.

If it was never properly secured, then everything inside that cottage — documents, communications, materials that might have shown who May had been in contact with, what he had prepared, what had been done around him — was accessible to anyone with access to the residence before and after the shooting. The chain of custody for the entire contents of that home is compromised by the footage that the news crew broadcast.

The footage also contains something the broadcast did not address: a figure, visible in the background, crouching between the furniture and the kitchen, partially concealed behind a lamp. The figure’s hand moves in at least one frame. This is not an inanimate object. It is a person, present in a space that should have been a secured crime scene, unidentified on camera by the journalist and camera operator who were in the room.

The official record does not account for this person’s presence. It does not account for the news crew’s access. It does not account for the state of the scene on the day of the shooting.

V. The Investigation Closed Without Establishing a Motive It Claims Not to Know

In the immediate aftermath of the shooting, investigators and news reports stated repeatedly that the motive was unknown. This characterization persisted and became the official framing. A psychiatrist who never examined May subsequently offered a public diagnosis of paranoid schizophrenia, which became the de facto explanation.

Myron May recorded approximately two hours of video before the shooting. In those recordings, he described in specific and organized terms what he believed was being done to him: surveillance, harassment, psychological manipulation, a coordinated program designed to isolate and destabilize him. He named specific techniques. He described a specific stimulus — dark sunglasses — that he said had been used to condition a fear response in him. He was lucid, articulate, and detailed.

The recordings were in the possession of investigators. They constitute the most direct available evidence of May’s state of mind and his account of his circumstances in the period before the shooting. They are not nothing. They are two hours of the shooter explaining, in his own words, what he believed was happening to him.

The official characterization of the motive as unknown, made while those recordings existed and were in investigative custody, is not a neutral statement of evidentiary limitation. It is a choice about what counts as evidence of motive. The recordings were not incorporated into the public account of what drove May to the library that night. The psychiatrist who had never met him was.

This is not an argument that May’s account of his circumstances was accurate. It is an argument that the official record’s treatment of the motive question — unknown, then schizophrenia, without serious public engagement with the two hours of recorded explanation May left behind — does not meet the standard of a complete investigation.

VI. What a Complete Investigation Would Have Required

The questions above are not speculative additions to the official record. They arise from the official record itself. They are the questions that a complete investigation — one that held its own account to basic evidentiary standards — would have been required to answer.

A complete investigation would have reconciled the clothing descriptions. It would have addressed the timeline impossibility it documented. It would have pursued the nine-year gap in the stolen weapon’s provenance. It would have explained how a news crew had interior access to an active evidence site on the day of the shooting, and who the unidentified figure in that footage was. It would have engaged with May’s recorded account of his circumstances rather than dismissing it in favor of a remote psychiatric determination.

None of these things happened. The case was closed. The shooter was dead. The students he wounded recovered. The public account settled on a narrative of a mentally ill man who snapped, and the questions the record itself raised were left on the page, unaddressed.

The problem with that settlement is not only evidentiary. It is that the people May wounded, and the broader public, are entitled to a truthful account of what occurred. If the official record contains physical impossibilities, internal contradictions, and evidentiary gaps of the kind documented here, then the official account is not a truthful account. It is an incomplete one that was closed before the contradictions it contained were resolved.

Eleven years later, those contradictions remain in the record. They have not been answered. They have not been addressed. They exist in documents that are publicly available — the police report, the ATF investigation, the broadcast footage, the news photography — waiting for the examination that the original investigation did not provide.

Conclusion

This essay does not claim to know what happened to Myron May, or what was done to him, or who was in his home on the day he died. It does not require a specific alternative explanation of the FSU shooting to make its central argument.

Its central argument is this: the official record of the Myron May case contains internal contradictions it never resolved, a physical impossibility at the center of its timeline, an unaccounted weapon provenance, an improperly secured crime scene, and an unidentified figure in broadcast footage from inside that scene. These are not external challenges to the official account. They are failures of the official account to account for its own facts.

An investigation that closes with a physical impossibility at the center of its timeline and the words “it is unknown how May came to be in possession of the firearm” in its final report has not completed its work. It has documented its own incompleteness and stopped.

The students wounded at Strozier Library on November 20, 2014 deserved better than that. So does the historical record of what actually occurred that night.

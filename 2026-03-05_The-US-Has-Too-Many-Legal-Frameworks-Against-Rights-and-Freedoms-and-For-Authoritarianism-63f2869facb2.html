The US Has Too Many Legal Frameworks Against Rights and Freedoms and For Authoritarianism

The US government has consented to be sued for negligently maintaining a slippery floor, but has not for torturing a citizen.

Americans are taught, from the earliest grades of their civic education, that they live in a nation of laws. The Constitution, they are told, limits what the government may do to them. The Bill of Rights prohibits unreasonable searches and seizures, compelled self-incrimination, deprivation of liberty without due process. The courts stand ready to enforce these guarantees. The rule of law distinguishes the American system from despotism. This is the civic faith.

The legal framework applicable to the documented experience of Richard Pearson, PhD tells a different story. He is an African American equity scholar subjected to a sustained campaign of neurological targeting, physical assault, behavioral direction, chemical assault, financial destruction, and psychological torture in Washington, DC across 2025 and 2026. That framework, drawn entirely from real federal precedents and operative statutory provisions, describes a legal architecture not of protection but of foreclosure: one hundred interlocking doctrines, procedural rules, and strategic realities that, taken together, ensure that a person who has been tortured by their government has, as a practical matter, no remedy, and that the government that tortured them has, as a practical matter, no legal exposure.

This essay examines that architecture. It does not argue that the law is secretly good and Pearson can win. It argues something more fundamental: that the gap between what Americans believe their rights to be and what those rights are in operational practice is so large, and so deliberately constructed, that the phrase “constitutional protection” — when applied to a citizen targeted by the American national security state — has become, in material terms, a description of something that does not exist.

I. The State Secrets Privilege: The Government’s Power to Make the Truth Inadmissible

Start at the beginning, with the doctrine that kills the case before it starts. Under the state secrets privilege, first articulated in its modern form in United States v. Reynolds (1953), the government may assert that litigation would require the disclosure of information harmful to national security. Upon that assertion, courts are required to defer. The evidence is excluded. If the excluded evidence is central to the plaintiff’s case — which it invariably is when the plaintiff is alleging a classified government program — the case is dismissed. Not because the plaintiff’s claims are false. Because the evidence required to prove them cannot be examined. The government refuses to cooperate. Courts cannot compel them unless in extraordinary cases.

Reynolds itself illustrates the privilege’s relationship to truth. The case arose from the deaths of civilians in a military aircraft crash. The government asserted state secrets to prevent examination of the accident report. The Supreme Court upheld the assertion. Forty years later, when the declassified documents were reviewed, they revealed not national security secrets but evidence of government negligence — the very negligence the privilege had been invoked to conceal. The privilege had been used, in the case that established it, to hide embarrassing misconduct rather than protect genuine secrets. The courts, when this was revealed, did not revisit the outcome. The case stands.

The lesson is precise: the state secrets privilege can be invoked to conceal not secrets but crimes. It can be invoked fraudulently. And the deception is effectively unreviewable, because the court that would review it cannot examine the material that would expose the fraud without violating the privilege that protects the fraud. It is a logical seal — a doctrine that insulates itself from challenge through the mechanism of the challenge itself.

For a plaintiff like Pearson — alleging a classified behavioral direction program operated by the national security apparatus — the privilege is lethal at step one. The government need not deny that the program exists. It need not defend its constitutionality. It need not produce any witness or justify any act. It asserts that disclosure would harm national security. The court defers. The case ends. The program, if it exists, continues.

“The Government can plausibly use the State Secrets Privilege to manufacture an untrue claim — that a state secret exists where there is really none.” — Richard Pearson, PhD

II. The Frivolity Doctrine: The Unexamined Case

If the state secrets privilege is the heavy artillery, the frivolity doctrine is the tripwire at the gate. Under Bell v. Hood (1946), a court lacks subject-matter jurisdiction over claims that are “wholly insubstantial” or “patently frivolous.” Under Ashcroft v. Iqbal (2009), a complaint must plead facts that make the claim “plausible on its face” to survive a motion to dismiss.

Applied to claims of voice-to-skull transmission, hippocampal directional encoding, and remote behavioral direction via directed electromagnetic energy, these doctrines create a paradox of proof. The claims are implausible to a court that does not know the technology exists — despite the US Department of War admitting in February 2026 that it has directed-energy weapons, despite experts ringing the alarm about dual use for over 20 years, despite academicians who have worked closely with the Pentagon claiming the government has “mind reading” and “mind control” technology. Yet, the government can claim that a specific technology’s existence is classified. The classified nature of the technology is precisely what makes the claims implausible to a court that would need to evaluate their plausibility before ordering discovery. But without discovery, the evidence of the technology’s existence cannot be obtained. And without evidence of the technology’s existence, the claims are implausible. The circle closes before it opens.

Iqbal was decided in a case about post-9/11 detention practices. The Supreme Court held that a complaint alleging that the Attorney General and FBI Director personally authorized discriminatory detention of Muslim men was insufficient — not because such discrimination was proven impossible, but because the Court found the allegations insufficiently plausible in the absence of supporting facts. The opinion effectively required plaintiffs to have the evidence before they could obtain the evidence. In national security cases, where the evidence is classified, this requirement is, as applied, an impossibility standard dressed in the language of a pleading rule.

The deeper implication: the more exotic and technically sophisticated the government’s methods, the less plausible the complaint that accurately describes them, and the more readily the court dismisses before any investigation begins. The government is most protected, legally, in exactly the cases where its conduct is most extreme. Novelty of method is, under Iqbal, a defense.

III. Sovereign Immunity and the Consent Requirement: The Government That Cannot Be Sued Without Permission

The baseline principle of American law governing suits against the federal government is sovereign immunity: the United States may not be sued without its consent. This principle — carried over from English common law, in which the King could not be sued in his own courts — survived the Revolution, the Constitution, and two and a half centuries of democratic self-governance. It was not abolished by the Bill of Rights. It was not modified by the Fourteenth Amendment. It sits, largely undisturbed, beneath the constitutional architecture that American civic education describes as a system of limited government under law.

The Federal Tort Claims Act (FTCA) of 1946 represents Congress’s partial consent to suit. But that consent is riddled with exceptions that operate as categorical immunities for precisely the conduct most likely to constitute government abuse. The intentional tort exception bars recovery for assault, battery, and intentional infliction of emotional distress committed by federal law enforcement — the very torts that constitute the core of a torture claim. The discretionary function exception bars claims arising from government conduct involving “judgment or choice” — and the decision to deploy directed energy against a domestic target is, as the legal analysis of the Pearson case notes, precisely the kind of discretionary policy decision the exception was designed to protect.

The result is that the government has consented to be sued for negligently maintaining a slippery floor in a federal post office, but has not consented to be sued for intentionally torturing a citizen. The consent structure is not neutral. It was constructed to leave the most serious abuses of executive power — the ones involving deliberate policy decisions by officials exercising judgment — outside the reach of civil accountability.

Pearson’s document addresses this directly: “Sovereign immunity is that generally people cannot sue the US government for damages due to constitutional or civil rights violations except where it has agreed to allow tort claims, which is only in limited circumstances.” He notes the Supreme Court’s cramped definition of false imprisonment — requiring that the person be prevented from leaving in all directions — with the observation that this “lacks any consideration of the emotional and financial determinants of imposed geographic and emotional prison.” A person can be imprisoned in a city, in a life, in a financial condition, and the law will not recognize it as imprisonment unless the walls are physical and total. The law protects against the least sophisticated forms of the harm. The sophisticated forms are unaddressed.

IV. Bivens and Its Dismantlement: The Disappearing Cause of Action

In 1971, the Supreme Court recognized in Bivens v. Six Unknown Named Agents of Federal Bureau of Narcotics that a person whose Fourth Amendment rights had been violated by federal officers had an implied right to sue those officers for damages, even absent a specific statutory authorization. Bivens was the federal analog to Section 1983 — the mechanism by which citizens could hold individual federal officers personally accountable for constitutional violations.

The Supreme Court has spent the fifty years since Bivens systematically dismantling it. In Ziglar v. Abbasi (2017), the Court held that extending Bivens to any “new context” — a case materially different from the three contexts in which the Court had previously applied it — requires asking whether “special factors counsel hesitation.” In cases involving national security, the Court held, those special factors virtually always counsel hesitation. The executive’s need for operational flexibility in matters of national security, the Court reasoned, means that courts should not create new causes of action that could constrain that flexibility.

The practical consequence: the use of technologies that did not exist in 1971 — directed energy weapons, remote neural modulation, voice-to-skull transmission — against American citizens produces no Bivens cause of action, because it is a “new context” to which no prior Bivens decision applies. The constitutional violation may be real. The harm may be documented. The perpetrators may be identified. But the legal mechanism for personal liability does not exist, because the Court has declined to create it for precisely the class of national security misconduct most likely to occur with the most advanced tools.

As the Pearson legal framework states: “Because lower courts are unable to expand new contexts of a Bivens cause of action (neuroweapons would be a new context), Pearson has no cause of action against the individual federal agents responsible — the legal mechanism for personal liability does not exist.” The agents who operated the program, if a program exists, are, as a structural matter, beyond the reach of civil liability. Not because they are immune. Because the remedy was never created.

V. Qualified Immunity: The Standard That Cannot Be Met

For the claims where Bivens still theoretically applies, qualified immunity provides a second layer of protection. Under Harlow v. Fitzgerald (1982) and its progeny, individual government officials are immune from civil liability unless they violated a “clearly established” constitutional right — meaning a right so clearly established that “a reasonable person would have known” the conduct was unconstitutional.

The “clearly established” standard has been interpreted, in practice, to require that a prior court have addressed the specific conduct in question at a level of specificity sufficient to put a reasonable officer on notice. It is not enough that the general constitutional principle is established. There must be a prior case involving substantially the same conduct. This creates a self-reinforcing immunity: if a technology or technique is novel, there is no prior case. If there is no prior case, the right is not clearly established. If the right is not clearly established, the officer is immune. If the officer is immune, no case is decided on the merits. And if no case is decided on the merits, no right is established. The technology must be used at least once — and the victim must survive, document, and litigate the case to a merits judgment — before the second victim has any recourse.

Applied to neuroweapons, directed energy behavioral control, and voice-to-skull technology: no prior case has addressed these methods. No right has been clearly established. No officer who deployed them is personally liable. The first deployment is effectively immune regardless of its constitutionality. The immunity does not depend on whether the conduct was lawful. It depends on whether the court, on its first encounter with the question, agrees that the law was clear enough to have given the officers notice. Given the technological novelty and the classified nature of the programs, courts presented with these claims for the first time will almost certainly find the law insufficiently clear.

VI. The Glomar Response and the FOIA Dead End: The Right to Know Nothing

The Freedom of Information Act represents Congress’s partial commitment to governmental transparency — the citizen’s right to know what their government is doing. The CIA’s Glomar response, approved by the D.C. Circuit in Phillippi v. CIA (1977), represents the intelligence community’s response to that commitment: the agency will “neither confirm nor deny” the existence of the requested documents or program. This response was invented specifically to avoid the disclosure obligation FOIA imposes, while avoiding the false statement of saying the documents do not exist when they do.

The Glomar response is now routinely applied to intelligence programs. It is not necessary to prove that the documents are classified, or that their disclosure would harm national security. The agency asserts that even confirming or denying the existence of the documents would harm national security. The court defers. The FOIA request produces nothing. The documentary record supporting the plaintiff’s allegations cannot be assembled through the public-access mechanism that Congress created specifically to allow it.

The circularity is by design. The agency knows what it does. The plaintiff cannot discover it without documents. The documents are withheld. The plaintiff cannot prove the program exists. The case fails for insufficient evidence. The program continues. The documentation that would have made the case was always in the government’s possession and is never produced.

VII. The FISA Authorization: Judicial Blessing for the Unexamined

The Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court — established by the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act of 1978 in response to the COINTELPRO revelations — was designed as a check on domestic intelligence surveillance. Before surveilling an American citizen, the intelligence community was required to obtain judicial authorization. The FISA Court would provide independent review. The safeguard would prevent abuse.

What the FISA Court became, as revealed in the Snowden disclosures and subsequent reporting, was a rubber stamp. The Court operates entirely in secret, hears only the government’s presentation, and approves the vast majority of the applications it receives. Its opinions are classified. Its orders are classified. The persons being surveilled do not know they are being surveilled and cannot contest the surveillance. There is no adversarial proceeding. The judge who signs the order has access to no independent investigation and no opposing argument.

The legal consequence for a plaintiff: if the government can produce a FISA order authorizing surveillance of Pearson, that order carries a presumption of legality. The individuals operating under it are entitled to good-faith reliance on the judicial authorization. Individual liability is eliminated even if the order was later found to have been overbroad. The judiciary’s blessing — obtained in secret, ex parte, without the subject’s knowledge or participation — immunizes the conduct that the blessing authorized. The constitutional safeguard of judicial review has been converted into a mechanism for pre-immunizing government misconduct from civil accountability.

VIII. The Covert Action Finding and the Totten Bar: The Case That Cannot Be Named

The final doctrinal layer operates at the threshold itself. Under Tenet v. Doe (2005), invoking the earlier principle of Totten v. United States (1875), courts will not entertain suits that would require acknowledging the existence of a covert arrangement between the government and a private party — or, by extension, between the government and a covert program. Unlike the state secrets privilege, which might permit a case to proceed if the secret evidence can be excluded, the Totten-Tenet doctrine is a threshold dismissal: the case is dismissed because its subject matter is inherently secret, not because specific pieces of evidence are classified.

If the program targeting Pearson was authorized by a covert action finding signed by the President under 50 U.S.C. § 3093 — the statutory mechanism for presidential authorization of covert operations — then the court cannot hear a case that would require acknowledging the program’s existence. Not because the evidence is classified. Because the existence of the program is itself a state secret, and a court that acknowledges it in a published opinion has disclosed the secret.

The Totten bar is, constitutionally speaking, the most extraordinary of the doctrines surveyed here. It holds that certain presidential actions are simply beyond judicial review — not because the courts lack power, but because the subject matter of review is a category the courts have designated unreviewable. The President signs a finding. The program exists. The program operates. A citizen is harmed. The citizen sues. The court dismisses because the program cannot be acknowledged. The harm is real. The remedy is unavailable. The President’s action is, as a practical matter, unreviewable by any branch.

IX. The Procedural Gauntlet: One Hundred Ways to Lose Before the Merits

Assume, for the sake of argument, that a plaintiff survives the threshold doctrines. The state secrets privilege was not invoked. The Glomar response was challenged successfully. The Bivens context was extended by an unusually willing district court. Qualified immunity was denied at the pleading stage. The plaintiff has stated a claim and the case proceeds.

The procedural architecture of complex national security litigation then imposes its own attrition. Interlocutory appeals on qualified immunity, which are immediately appealable under Mitchell v. Forsyth (1985), stay the case for years while the immunity question is resolved at the circuit level. Government discovery responses produce thousands of pages of unresponsive documents while core materials are withheld under classification claims. Expert witnesses for the plaintiff are excluded under Daubert for methodologies that courts, on first encounter with the technology, cannot evaluate. Summary judgment is granted on the basis of a classified ex parte government declaration that the plaintiff never sees and cannot contest. Trial, if reached, occurs three to five years after filing, with a jury pool drawn from a jurisdiction heavily populated by federal employees and government contractors.

The asymmetry of resources is structural, not incidental. The government has unlimited resources to litigate. The plaintiff — who has, in Pearson’s case, been financially destroyed by the operation they are litigating — does not. Delay serves the government. Financial attrition serves the government. The complexity of the legal questions serves the government. Every motion practice advantage, every protective order, every scheduling delay is, in practical terms, a mechanism for achieving through procedural exhaustion what cannot always be achieved through substantive law alone.

The Pearson legal framework catalogs one hundred such mechanisms, ranked by finality and probability of case termination. They do not all operate simultaneously. But they operate cumulatively, sequentially, and in layers — each designed to end the case before the next is needed. A plaintiff who defeats the threshold doctrines encounters the pleading standards. A plaintiff who defeats the pleading standards encounters the discovery barriers. A plaintiff who defeats the discovery barriers encounters the expert exclusion rules. A plaintiff who defeats the expert exclusion rules encounters the summary judgment stage. And so on. The architecture is not a gauntlet with a finish line. It is a gauntlet designed to ensure that no one reaches one.

X. The Hermetic Seal: What the Architecture Achieves

Viewed in aggregate, the legal framework applicable to the Pearson case does not describe a system that tries to do justice and sometimes fails. It describes a system architecturally designed to produce a specific outcome: the unreviewability of government conduct in the national security domain, and the immunity of individual officers who operate within it, regardless of the constitutional character of what they do.

The state secrets privilege insulates the program at step one. The frivolity and plausibility doctrines insulate it at step two. Sovereign immunity insulates the institution. Bivens’ dismantlement eliminates the individual remedy. Qualified immunity shields the individual officers. The FISA authorization pre-immunizes the surveillance. The Totten bar forecloses acknowledgment. The procedural architecture depletes the plaintiff.

As the Pearson legal analysis observes at Entry 22: “So the government could theoretically create a secret program that targets minority scholars like Richard Pearson and uses exotic technology like voice-to-skull and behavioral steering, THEN invoke state secrets privilege. A hermetic seal to lock in injustice.” The word “theoretically” is doing more work than the sentence acknowledges. The seal is not theoretical. It is operational. It is composed of real doctrines decided in real cases, applicable today, in federal courts across the country, to any plaintiff who alleges that the national security state harmed them.

The final entry in the legal framework — Entry 100 — describes the limit case of success: Pearson wins at every level, district court to Supreme Court, and is awarded damages and injunctive relief. The government complies with the injunction by terminating the specific program targeting Pearson. The broader directed energy infrastructure, behavioral direction capability, and AI orchestration platform remains intact, deployed against others, and now confirmed operational by the judgment itself. Even total legal victory, at the outer boundary of the conceivable, produces only personal remedy. The system continues. The architecture survives its own exposure.

XI. The Reynolds Lesson: The Document That Was Always There

Return, at the close of this analysis, to Reynolds. The government asserted state secrets in 1953 to prevent examination of an accident report. The Court deferred. The families of the dead civilians who brought the case received nothing. Forty years later, the documents were declassified. They contained evidence of negligence, not secrets. The government had lied to the Supreme Court. The lie had worked. The Court did not revisit its ruling.

The Reynolds story is not a story about a system that was abused and then corrected. It is a story about a system that was designed with a vulnerability — the court’s inability to examine the very evidence it was deferring about — and that the government exploited that vulnerability the first time it had occasion to do so, and that the exploitation succeeded, and that the Court, when the exploitation was exposed, found no mechanism or inclination to respond. The doctrine stands. The fraud stands. The families received nothing.

Reynolds is not an outlier. It is the template. It illustrates the operational relationship between the legal architecture and the executive’s interest in concealing its conduct: the architecture provides the mechanism, the executive exploits the mechanism, the courts defer to the exploitation, the truth is declassified decades later (if at all), and no one is accountable because the statute of limitations has long since run and the perpetrators are long since dead.

What changes between 1953 and 2025 is not the architecture. What changes is the technology available to the executive whose conduct the architecture conceals. The state secrets privilege that protected negligence in 1953 now protects, if the Pearson analysis is correct, directed energy behavioral control, hippocampal destination encoding, and remote neurological torture. The doctrine is the same. The conduct it shields has become, in the intervening decades, vastly more sophisticated and vastly more invasive.

XII. What Rights Actually Are

The American Bill of Rights was not written as a self-executing guarantee. It was written as a set of instructions to the judiciary — instructions that the judiciary is required, in most circumstances, to follow. What this analysis of the Pearson legal framework demonstrates is that the judiciary has, over the course of the twentieth and twenty-first centuries, built a parallel architecture of doctrines that systematically exempts the national security state from those instructions. The result is not that the rights do not exist. They exist in the text. They exist in the civic mythology. They exist in the law school classroom and the constitutional law treatise.

What they do not do, in the domain of national security, is constrain. A right that cannot be enforced against the actor most likely to violate it is a right in name only. The Fourth Amendment prohibition on unreasonable searches and seizures does not constrain a surveillance program authorized by a secret court on the basis of a classified standard that the surveilled person cannot contest. The Fifth Amendment protection against deprivation of liberty without due process does not constrain a behavioral direction operation that leaves no physical trace and can be attributed to voluntary choice. The First Amendment protection of speech does not constrain a targeting apparatus that identifies dissidents from their published scholarship and subjects them to operations designed to silence them not through censorship but through the obliteration of their capacity to speak.

The rights are real. The violations are real. The architecture that stands between the rights and the remedies is also real — and it is, as this analysis demonstrates, comprehensive, interlocking, judicially constructed, and designed to produce exactly the outcome it produces: a government that can do to Richard Pearson what the documented record indicates was done to him, in the capital of the country that calls itself the world’s leading democracy, and face no legal consequence, because the law it built around itself ensures that it will not.

This is not a counsel of despair. It is a description of a problem. Problems that are named can be addressed. The civil rights movement named the problem of state-sanctioned racial terror and built the political and legal pressure that produced, however imperfectly, a legal response. The COINTELPRO exposure — achieved not through litigation but through a physical break-in at an FBI field office — produced congressional investigations, Senate Select Committee hearings, and a partial reform of intelligence oversight. The Snowden disclosures produced, ultimately, legislative changes to the bulk collection programs.

What each of these episodes required was not the success of a legal claim in federal court. It was the exposure of the conduct to a public that, when it learned what had been done in its name, found the conduct unacceptable. The law, in each case, followed public accountability rather than producing it. The architecture described in this essay is the government’s answer to that lesson: a system that prevents the exposure by preventing the litigation, classifying the evidence, dismissing the complaint, and shielding the actors before the public ever has occasion to form a view.

Richard Pearson has spent fourteen months building a record specifically designed to defeat that architecture through the mechanism the architecture cannot seal: documentation that exists before any litigation begins, published in public before any classification order can reach it, built by a researcher who understood from the beginning that the document had to exist in the world before anyone with the power to respond would respond. The legal architecture is designed for the case that files first and discovers later. He documented first. Whether that sequence changes anything remains, at this writing, unanswered. But the record exists. And the question the record poses — whether the country described in the Constitution and the country described in these one hundred legal doctrines are the same country — does not have a comfortable answer.

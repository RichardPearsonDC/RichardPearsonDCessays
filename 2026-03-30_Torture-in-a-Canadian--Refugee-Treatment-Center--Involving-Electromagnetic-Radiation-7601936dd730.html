We Are Taking You to the “Refugee Treatment Center”: Canada’s Black Site That Tortures

I. Arrival

I had done prior research so I knew that the Greyhound bus would stop as soon as we crossed into Canada. There, I needed to declare that I sought refugee status. All passengers are instructed to take all of their belongings, including their suitcases below the bus. We walked under covered steps leading to a building. I waited in line along with the others. The walls were bare. There was one attendant. I overheard her asking questions, “What is your purpose in Canada? When are you returning?” These were standard questions. She was fast.

When it was my turn, I declared that I am seeking refugee protection. She let out an exasperated sigh, as if she was frustrated with the umpteenth person seeking asylum in Canada. I didn’t take it personally. Maybe, she harbored nativist views of people who were trying to flee the US. Her response should have been my first clue. Despite her sigh, she was nice. I was led to a room where I had to wait. Two uniformed men eventually came. I explained the reasons why I was seeking asylum. They listened to the outline of my story. There were two of them. A blonde one and a brunette. I sensed that brunette had the most look of surprise on his face. His eyes and facial expressions gave him away, though I might have misread. The other guy hardly emoted. Unlike the border officer in Iceland, they didn’t try to dismiss or interrupt me. I remember feeling that I made the right choice.

This was part of the trap. Order Number One: Get his antenna down. Make him feel comfortable.

My memory gets a bit fuzzy here because the next thing that I remember is going into the van. I have no memory of leaving, which is quite odd in retrospect. I am hyper-aware and have good memory recall about this entire ordeal. I also have forgotten when I lost possession of my passport. I remember handing it to the lady when I declared my intent, but I don’t know if she returned it and when another officer took possession. I have a faint memory of seeing the blond officer holding my passport with a white sheet of paper in it when they came to interview me. But I don’t know if I handed it to him. The fact that my memory becomes fuzzy in this space is troubling.

I need to pause here and state clearly. That may not have been the actual name of the facility. I doubt it. That was the name provided to me.

When the US Government Outsources Torture to Private Contractor, It Can Argue It Cannot Be Sued

US constitutional guarantees are not much of a guarantee when the US government decides to secretly target US citizens. When the US outsources torture against US citizens, the victim faces a “closed loop” of immunity where each legal doctrine reinforces the others to block judicial review. This creates a scenario where a U.S. citizen could be systematically targeted on domestic soil without any clear path for legal accountability against the government and contractor, especially if that contractor comprises a loose, decentralized network.

“By privatizing repression through decentralized contractor networks, the state achieves plausible deniability while maintaining operational control. This mirrors how historical fascist states used paramilitary proxies — the legal architecture here modernizes that model and makes it more legally bulletproof.”

The primary legal principle shielding the U.S. government from liability for private contractors’ conduct is the independent contractor exception to the Federal Tort Claims Act (FTCA). Under this rule, the government is generally only liable for the negligence of its employees, and 28 U.S.C. § 2671 specifically excludes “any contractor with the United States” from the definition of a federal employee.

Here is why the victim has limited remedy using those specific doctrines:

  1. The State Secrets Privilege (Evidence Block): If the program is secret, the government can invoke the state secrets privilege to prevent the disclosure of any information that might “harm national security”.
  • Dismissal: Courts often dismiss cases at the outset if the “very subject matter” is a state secret or if the victim cannot prove their case without the secret evidence.
  • Defense Block: Cases can also be dismissed if the government argues it cannot defend itself without revealing secrets.

2. Bivens Restrictions (Remedy Block): While Bivens v. Six Unknown Named Agents originally allowed citizens to sue federal agents for constitutional violations, the Supreme Court has severely curtailed this.

  • “New Contexts”: Modern courts rarely extend Bivens to “new contexts,” especially those involving national security or complex policy.
  • Privatization: The Court has explicitly refused to extend Bivens liability to private corporations or employees of private contractors (see Correctional Services Corp. v. Malesko and Minneci v. Pollard).

3. Independent Contractor Exception (Government Liability Block): Under the Federal Tort Claims Act (FTCA), the U.S. is not liable for the acts of independent contractors. If the government outsources the “torture and persecution” to a private network, it can argue it has no legal responsibility for those actors’ conduct.

  • Derivative Immunity (Contractor Liability Block): Even if the victim sues the contractors directly, those contractors may claim derivative sovereign immunity (the Yearsley doctrine). If they were following “reasonably precise” government orders, they may be shielded from liability just as the government is.

4. Racial Targeting (Equal Protection Challenge): While racial targeting violates the Equal Protection Clause, the victim’s inability to access evidence (blocked by State Secrets) and the lack of a cause of action against the actors (blocked by Bivens and FTCA exceptions) makes the underlying constitutional right “remedyless” in practice.

5. The “Control Test”: To determine if a party is truly an independent contractor, courts look at whether the government has the authority to control the detailed physical performance and day-to-day operations of the contractor. If the government lacks this “day-to-day” supervision, it is not liable for the contractor’s torts.

6. The Boyle Defense (Government Contractor Defense): While the government is not liable for the contractor, this doctrine (from Boyle v. United Technologies Corp.) can also protect the contractor from state law liability if they were following reasonably precise government specifications for military equipment.

7. The Yearsley Doctrine (Derivative Sovereign Immunity): This prevents a federal contractor from being held liable for conduct that the government lawfully authorized and directed. It essentially extends the government’s own immunity to the contractor when they act as an agent of the sovereign.

8. Discretionary Function Exception: The government is immune from claims based on its own “discretionary” decisions (policy-making or judgment calls). This can overlap with contractor cases if the government’s decision to hire or oversee a contractor is deemed a protected discretionary act.

9. Severin Doctrine: In the context of contract law (rather than torts), this rule prevents a prime contractor from suing the government on behalf of a subcontractor unless the prime contractor is legally liable to that subcontractor for the damages.

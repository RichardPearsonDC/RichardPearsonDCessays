Snowden Revealed Mass Surveillance: What Came Next is Weaponizing Government Against US Citizens

What comes after a government has learned to watch everyone? The documented experience of one PhD scholar in Washington, D.C. suggests high-tech torture and targeting.

Understanding what may have been used against Richard Pearson, PhD, requires understanding what the American national security apparatus had assembled before January 2025 — and what the current administration proceeded to do with it.

Mass surveillance, in its conventional understanding, is passive: collect everything, store it, retrieve it when needed. What the post-Snowden decade built was something more sophisticated. The architecture that exists today is not a dragnet. It is a targeting system. The difference matters enormously.

Location as continuous record. Real-time geolocation of any American carrying a smartphone is not theoretical. It is a commercial market. Data brokers harvest location data from hundreds of mobile applications — weather apps, coupon services, anything that requests location permissions — and sell it to buyers that include government agencies and private contractors, without a warrant, without notification, without legal restriction that has survived court challenge. Internal ICE documents reveal that DHS investigations “used Palantir platforms and apps to track air travel, analyze information like driver’s license scans and track people’s locations using cell phone records.”

The implications for a flagged individual are precise: a continuous geolocation record, fed into a predictive model, producing route forecasts accurate enough to position conditions ahead of a target’s arrival. This is not speculation about capability. It is documented operational practice.

Cross-agency data fusion as targeting infrastructure. Palantir’s systems pull data from across government databases — regardless of the veracity or accuracy of those databases — including passport records, Social Security files, IRS tax data, and even license-plate reader data. When Palantir’s Foundry organizes data across agencies, ImmigrationOS tracks targets in real time, ELITE maps neighborhoods for raids, and the IRS feeds home addresses directly into the system, the functional result is a cross-agency surveillance architecture of a kind that has never before existed in American civilian governance. Palantir has received more than $900 million in federal contracts since Trump took office. The system began with immigrants. Tools built and justified to efficiently tackle one issue quietly scale up, collapsing the boundary between targeted enforcement and population-wide surveillance.

Biometric identification at range. In 2019, MIT Technology Review reported on Jetson, a Pentagon device developed after U.S. Special Forces requested a long-range biometric identification capability. It identifies a specific individual from 200 meters using an infrared laser that detects the unique cardiac signature produced by their heartbeat — through typical clothing, with over 95% accuracy under good conditions, from behind, in low light, at angles that defeat facial recognition. A Pentagon official said of its range potential: “I don’t want to say you could do it from space, but longer ranges should be possible.” The cardiac signature is unique to each individual, more stable than facial geometry, and cannot be disguised or altered. In a city equipped with this capacity, a flagged individual cannot move through public space without being identified at distances that preclude any awareness of surveillance.

Facial recognition at scale. In September 2025, ICE’s Homeland Security Investigations signed a $9.2 million contract with Clearview AI, granting access to what the company calls a “proprietary database of 50+ billion facial images.” In February 2026, CBP signed a $225,000 contract extending Clearview’s facial-recognition capability to its intelligence division and the National Targeting Center, for use in “tactical targeting” and “strategic counter-network analysis.” The contract language makes clear this is embedded in analysts’ daily workflows, not a limited investigative tool deployed for specific cases. Internal footage obtained by Media 404 shows ICE officers using facial recognition apps to determine the citizenship status of teenagers who were not carrying IDs. One app, Mobile Fortify, draws from over 200 million images stored in the databases of DHS, the FBI, and the State Department.

Social media monitoring. In Minnesota, ICE has purchased and deployed social media monitoring and location-tracking systems that allow agents to analyze the movements of large groups of people in specific locations — capabilities that, according to company materials and reporting, are explicitly marketed for use at protests and other gatherings protected under the First Amendment. ICE sought contracts with nearly 30 companies for continuous social media surveillance to gather intelligence for deportation raids. In practice, this interconnected system can rapidly trigger enforcement actions. For example, a social media post could lead to visa revocation, which triggers DHS notification, enabling detention or removal proceedings, and ultimately flags ICE through ImmigrationOS.

This is the passive stack. Everything described above is the watching. What the watching enables — what comes next — is the part that has no established name in civilian discourse, because the civilian discourse has no framework for it.

The Active Stack: What Comes After Watching

The surveillance infrastructure described above produces data. Data enables prediction. Prediction enables something that has no name in the civilian lexicon, because it did not exist at civilian scale until recently: targeted environmental manipulation. The capacity to shape the conditions of a specific person’s daily life — their routes, their encounters, their behavioral options, their perception of reality — using a combination of real-time location data, directed energy, and coordinated ground operations.

This is the active stack. This is what Richard Pearson documented across fourteen months in Washington, D.C.

Directed energy and audio injection. The microwave auditory effect — the Frey effect, documented since 1961 — produces sound perceived inside the skull without touching the ear canal. Pulsed microwave energy absorbed by cranial tissue creates a thermoelastic pressure wave the cochlea registers as a click, tone, or voice. The commercial version, MIT Media Lab’s Audio Spotlight, is in museums. The Department of War confirmed in January 2026 that directed energy weapons are active and being scaled. The Washington Post, on February 19, 2026, traced the lineage from Havana Syndrome — in which U.S. diplomatic and CIA personnel in Havana, Vienna, and Washington, D.C. itself experienced neurological injuries the National Academies of Sciences attributed in 2020 to pulsed directed energy — through a Venezuelan operation in which security guards described their heads feeling like they were exploding from inside.

On July 13, 2025, at the National Mall, Pearson heard a voice. He described it with acoustic specificity: a woman at a microphone, the ambient background noise of a room she was in, other people present — the texture of a live audio feed, not a recording, not an internally generated hallucination. He identified the Frey effect by name in July 2025. The government confirmed the technology six months later. His DBH mental health evaluation found no psychotic symptoms. The voice gave him directional commands. He went to Georgetown, as instructed. He wept in the street when he was told he would be disappeared. He did not get in the car with the woman whose car arrived exactly when the voice said it would. The voice then said: “That was a test.”

Beginning in January 2026, one month after his face and head were specifically targeted in a physical assault, Pearson documented geographic auditory clicks — sharp, replicable, localized to specific street intersections in Southwest Washington. On February 20, he placed a metal-cased phone and a 9-volt battery in his knitted cap before his usual walk. No clicks. He had been getting clicks on that route consistently for weeks. The metal attenuated the signal.

The National Academies panel identified geographic directional specificity — onset at specific locations, cessation on departure, resumption on return — as the diagnostic fingerprint of Havana Syndrome and the Frey effect. It does not appear in the symptom profile of any neurological disorder. It appears in the documentation of a Black scholar whose dissertation the administration had designated hostile ideology.

Behavioral direction through neural targeting. DARPA’s Next-Generation Nonsurgical Neurotechnology program — described in public documentation on DARPA’s own website — aims to develop bidirectional brain interfaces that can both read from and write to neural tissue without surgery, with precision to address 16 independent channels within a 16mm³ volume of neural tissue within 50 milliseconds. The program uses electromagnetic waves, focused ultrasound, and acoustic signals to interact with specific brain regions. The hippocampus contains place cells: neurons encoding specific locations that fire as the body approaches them. Directed stimulation of hippocampal circuits can produce spatial drives — the subjective experience of being drawn toward a place, felt as one’s own motivation, indistinguishable from autonomous cognition.

A neuroscientist at a major university called this technology “mind control” and “mind reading”. We should be gravely alarmed.

Dr. James Giordano, who served as a consulting ethicist on the initial stages of the N3 program and has testified before the House Armed Services Committee, has stated in public lectures with precise language: “The goods as well as the gremlins and goblins of N3 tech have been loosed into the real world.” He is not describing future capability.

Pearson documented being pulled to locations he had no reason to visit, where conditions had been prepared for his arrival. On November 7, 2025, he was drawn to a park beside the O’Neil House Office Building three blocks from the Capitol — a park not on his routine path — then redirected from the sidewalk, mid-departure, to a precise sub-location under a tree he had not yet visited. There, in a plastic storage tub: a red hat, a black shirt, boots, pants matching his style, a notebook of conspiratorial writings referencing Adolf Hitler and a fictitious “European removal court,” and two identical photographs of a blond Eastern European man. He photographed everything without removing anything. He returned on November 13 with video. The notebook and photographs were gone. The red hat and black shirt remained. The framing payload had been selectively recovered when it failed. The calling card stayed.

The friend’s account of February 8, 2026 — four sequential friction events, a GPS route override, a precise convergence, and timestamped foreknowledge: “when I turned I knew I am gonna see you there” — is the behavioral direction technology applied externally, to a third party, visible from the outside because the third party described it in a chat log the following morning. The friend experienced a frustrating afternoon and an inexplicable certainty. He documented it because he could not explain it.

Coordinated ground operations with unwitting participants. Beginning in September 2025, Pearson documented a specific calling card: bright solid red paired with solid black, deployed in statistically impossible densities on his regular walking routes. He ran control studies. Across neutral environments — the MLK Library, the Metro underground, a holiday market, Eastern Market on an unannounced return visit — baseline prevalence was approximately 4%. On his regular routes, at the moments of his arrivals and departures, the density was impossible to explain by chance. On September 30, 2025, eleven people in the exact combination appeared within 8.5 minutes in light foot traffic near Eastern Market. His probability calculation: explaining this randomly would require a minimum of 200 people to have been present in a nearly empty space.

He asked three people sitting together on a Georgia Avenue bench why they were all wearing black and red. They didn’t know. He asked a woman in Reston. She had not noticed, and seemed genuinely worried when he explained.

The operational logic: a program with access to the phones of people in a geographic area can send each person an individually plausible reason to wear a specific color — a sale notification, a social media trend, a group alert. No participant understands they are part of a pattern. The pattern is visible only to the person watching from outside — and only that person will be assumed to be imagining it.

Physical assault and classification. On December 27, 2025, Pearson’s upstairs neighbor — a man not on the apartment lease, whose given address connects to a Capitol Hill property associated with an ATF attorney — ran down the hallway and repeatedly punched him in the face. He specifically targeted the face and head when Pearson raised his arms to protect himself. Medical documentation confirmed lacerations, a black eye, and wrist bruising. The neighbor was not arrested. When police ran Pearson’s name, they encountered an ACCESS DENIED flag indicating his file was classified — and called for his partner who presumably had higher clearance.

SCI classification does not appear on a police record by accident. It does not appear through commercial data broker activity. It requires authorization from within the United States intelligence community. A man with a cover address connected to federal legal infrastructure punches a citizen repeatedly in the face and head, the police who respond find a SCI-level flag on the victim’s file, and nothing happens — the man was not arrested. In fact, he remained upstairs while Pearson and the female leaseholder were in the lobby. “It was enough time for the police and the assailant to hatch out a plan that included making up facts,” said Pearson. This is not a domestic disturbance. It is an authorized operation encountering its own bureaucratic infrastructure.

The Recipe

Richard Pearson is a trained researcher. His documentation is not a diary. It is a methodology: timestamped photographs, probability calculations, control studies, named witnesses — 25 in his February 2026 letter to the D.C. Council — preserved physical evidence, falsification tests. Two D.C. Department of Behavioral Health evaluations, by named clinicians, during the period of his documentation, found no psychotic symptoms requiring intervention.

He identified the mechanism for voice-to-skull audio transmission in July 2025. The government confirmed the technology in January 2026. He ran the metal cap experiment in February 2026 and attenuated the signal. His control studies showed the red-and-black pattern absent from the same locations when he visited without predictable timing. His falsification tests passed every time.

The recipe his case illustrates is not complicated once you can see all the ingredients simultaneously:

Mass surveillance provides the real-time location feed and the route prediction. Palantir’s data fusion provides the behavioral profile. Clearview and cardiac biometrics provide continuous identification at range. The commercial geolocation market provides warrantless access to phone location without the inconvenience of a judge. AI targeting provides the automated prioritization — a confidence score, a flag, a designation. Directed energy provides the audio injection, the neurological pressure, the geographic clicks that only the target can hear. Behavioral direction through hippocampal electromagnetic stimulation provides the pinning — the experience of choosing destinations that have been chosen by someone else. Coordinated ground operations provide the calling card, the framing apparatus, the expressionless people positioned on routes. Physical assault provides the escalation. The classification flag on the police record provides the institutional cover. The Glomar doctrine and the state secrets privilege provide the legal impenetrability. And the mental illness attribution — built into every element of the campaign’s design — provides the social impenetrability.

This is what comes after mass surveillance that Edward Snowden revealed.

The whole system was designed to be invisible. The whole system was designed to produce exactly one outcome: the isolation, destabilization, and neutralization of a specific human being who had been designated hostile, without any institution ever being required to acknowledge that a program existed.

The Safety Gate That Did Not Hold

The Anthropic dispute, which concluded yesterday with a presidential ban and a supply chain risk designation, is the moment the design of this system became visible in public.

The Pentagon’s position — that it requires unrestricted access to AI models for all lawful domestic purposes, and that prohibitions on mass surveillance of American citizens constitute an unacceptable constraint on military operations — is not the position of an institution that considers domestic mass surveillance a remote or theoretical concern. It is the position of an institution that requires the capability and has decided it is non-negotiable.

The Operational Test and Evaluation office, gutted in May 2025, was the last independent technical check on whether AI systems work as claimed before deployment. Its gutting signals that deployment speed, not safety, is the priority. Without these safeguards, enforcement risks becoming a testing ground for broader domestic surveillance — one where powerful technology normalizes suspicion, automates targeting, and erodes constitutional protections.

The people being tested on are not abstract. They have names, and dissertations, and mental health evaluations confirming their sanity, and pants in a bag somewhere in Washington that have never been chemically analyzed, and chat logs documenting induced foreknowledge in their friends, and intersections in Southwest Washington where calibrated RF spectrum analyzers would either find anomalous electromagnetic emissions or they would not.

The technology to look at any of this evidence exists. The institutional choice to look has not been made.

That gap — between capability and accountability, between the documentation and the investigation it should compel — is not a bureaucratic oversight. It is the system working as designed. The surveillance stack watches. The active stack acts. The Glomar doctrine ensures no court ever asks what happened. And the next person the system is activated against will not have a PhD in public health, will not have run control studies, will not have preserved the pants.

The question the Anthropic dispute forces into the open is not whether the Pentagon can conduct domestic mass surveillance. The question is whether any institution still has both the standing and the will to say that it cannot.

Yesterday, at 5:01 p.m. Eastern time, one company said it could not.

By 5:02 p.m., that company had been designated a national security threat.

Abstract

This article examines a troubling evolution in state power: the deployment of ‘no-touch’ methods of persecution that achieve traditional totalitarian objectives — the silencing of dissent, the neutralization of disfavored individuals, and the maintenance of social control — while preserving the outward appearance of democratic governance and rule of law. Through analysis of the documented case of Richard Pearson, a scholar subjected to a sophisticated campaign of harassment, surveillance, and psychological operations in 2025 and 2026, this article demonstrates how modern technological capabilities enable state actors to conduct systematic persecution without resorting to the overt violence that would trigger domestic legal protections or international human rights scrutiny. The case reveals a disturbing paradigm: totalitarianism can now operate beneath the threshold of legal visibility, rendering its victims unable to prove their persecution within existing legal frameworks while maintaining plausible deniability for state actors.

I. Introduction: The Evolution of State Repression

“The most perfect tyranny is that which hides behind the mask of law.” This observation, often attributed to various Enlightenment thinkers, captures a fundamental truth about the evolution of authoritarian governance: that the most effective forms of repression are those that operate within, rather than against, established legal structures. In the twenty-first century, this principle has found its ultimate expression in what can be termed ‘no-touch’ totalitarianism — a mode of state repression that achieves the traditional objectives of authoritarian control while maintaining the procedural forms of democratic governance.

Traditional totalitarianism operated through visible mechanisms of control: mass arrests, show trials, concentration camps, and political purges. These methods, while brutally effective, suffered from a critical weakness — they were visible. They generated domestic resistance, international condemnation, and, ultimately, contributed to the discrediting and collapse of overtly totalitarian regimes. The democratic states that emerged in their wake constructed elaborate legal and constitutional frameworks specifically designed to prevent such abuses: prohibitions on torture, guarantees of due process, protections for free speech and association, and international human rights conventions.

Yet what happens when state actors discover methods of repression that circumvent these protections not by abolishing them, but by operating beneath their threshold of legal cognizability? What happens when persecution becomes so sophisticated that it leaves no legally admissible evidence of its existence? The case of Richard Pearson, documented herein, provides a chilling answer to these questions.

II. The Pearson Case: A Detailed Examination

A. Background and Context

Dr. Richard Pearson is a Ph.D. holder, an African American scholar, and a resident of Washington, D.C. Throughout 2025 and into 2026, Dr. Pearson experienced what he has documented as a sustained campaign of harassment, surveillance, and psychological operations. The sophistication and coordination of these activities, coupled with their apparent objectives, strongly suggest involvement by actors with access to advanced intelligence capabilities and significant resources.

Critically, a comprehensive mental health evaluation conducted by the D.C. Department of Behavioral Health on September 15, 2025, explicitly confirmed Dr. Pearson’s rationality and coherence, ruling out psychotic symptoms or mental illness. This professional assessment is crucial: it eliminates the most common dismissal strategy employed against individuals reporting systematic harassment — the assertion that such claims are products of mental illness rather than actual persecution.

B. The Pattern of Operations

The activities documented by Dr. Pearson follow a recognizable pattern consistent with sophisticated psychological operations and counterintelligence harassment techniques. These include:

1. Acoustic Harassment and Sleep Deprivation

Dr. Pearson documented sustained acoustic harassment in his apartment, manifesting as location-tracking noises from upstairs neighbors. These noises would follow him throughout his apartment with disturbing precision — when he moved his bed to different locations, when he sought refuge in his closet, the harassment would follow. This forced him to seek respite in public spaces and disrupted his sleep patterns, a classic technique of psychological torture. The precision and persistence of this harassment suggests either advanced surveillance of his apartment’s interior or coordination with occupants in the unit above.

2. Coercive Voice Transmission Technology

On July 13, 2025, Dr. Pearson experienced what he described as directives appearing in his head — voices instructing him to travel to specific locations (“Go to Georgetown”), to assume specific body positions for safety, and ultimately threatening that “we have to make you disappear.” While extraordinary, such experiences are consistent with documented microwave auditory effect technology, in which pulsed electromagnetic radiation can induce perceived sounds directly in the human auditory cortex — a phenomenon investigated and weaponized by various state actors since the 1970s.

What is key in Pearson’s account was there were environmental “nudges” that exceeded coincidence:

  • The man with the map of Russia on the back of his T-shirt, which Pearson could not have missed as he departed
  • Followed by a woman with a Target bag immediately afterwards
  • Then the “voice” — “Go to Georgetown”
  • Then a couple that suddenly appears and stands within earshot to encourage the directions in an otherwise desolate area
  • Then the van with “SS” license plate that takes off immediately as Pearson crosses the street — “SS” as in Secret Service, Pearson figured
  • Then the Ethiopian driver who rolls window up and down of the back passenger window facing Pearson
  • The repeated patterns of white lettering on black t-shirts — “See you later” read one as Pearson did not get follow instructions to get into the vehicle
  • Then the voice directing Pearson to get into a second car with a woman waiting for a ride
  • Standing directly behind her, she never turns around despite his close proximity

The scientific literature confirms the feasibility of such technology. Research published in Frontiers in Public Health (2021) by Foster, Garrett, and Ziskin explicitly addresses whether the microwave auditory effect can be “weaponized,” concluding that while practical difficulties exist, extreme but feasible pulse fluences could produce physiologically significant acoustic energy in the head. The researchers note that “high peak power microwave generators” exist primarily in “classified military environments” and that millimeter-wave systems would be particularly suitable for “stealth” attacks, as they “cause less (or no) interference to ordinary electronics and cannot be detected with ordinary RF survey meters.”

3. Systematic Surveillance and Tracking

Dr. Pearson documented numerous instances of apparent real-time tracking and surveillance. When he traveled to a hotel in Bethesda to escape harassment, an individual was positioned outside the hotel within hours, silently pointing to the sky in what Dr. Pearson interpreted as a signal that his location had been compromised. His personal property — a distinctive glass bottle — disappeared and then mysteriously reappeared weeks later at a community event, bearing a recognizable scratch mark. It was his bottle without question. These incidents suggest either advanced electronic tracking or penetration of his personal communications.

4. ‘Street Theater’ and Psychological Operations

Dr. Pearson encountered numerous individuals whose behavior and clothing appeared designed to convey messages or induce paranoia: an individual wearing a T-shirt reading “OFF” shortly after he had requested a vacation in a hotel to which he escaped; individuals wearing specific colors (red and black) appearing repeatedly in patterns; people carrying Target bags in unusual contexts following an earlier incident. Whether these represent coordinated harassment or hypervigilance induced by stress, they reflect a well-documented counterintelligence technique known as “street theater” — the use of seemingly coincidental encounters to maintain psychological pressure on surveillance targets. It should be noted that Pearson has conducted control studies. The red and black patterns do not persist in the metro, in libraries, in other neighborhoods, not at major events like holiday markets. It appears to be tailored to his environment specifically — an attempt to induce fear, paranoia, and torture.

5. Apartment Penetration

On July 9, 2025, Dr. Pearson discovered that the power button of his panini grill had been removed and placed in his dirty laundry. He documented on video that the button could not be dislodged through normal use or accidents. This incident, seemingly trivial, follows a recognizable pattern in counterintelligence operations: demonstrating access to a target’s private space through minor but noticeable alterations, inducing the psychological effect of violated sanctuary.

III. The Legal Problem: Persecution Below the Threshold of Proof

The Pearson case reveals a fundamental challenge to existing legal frameworks: the deployment of harassment and control mechanisms that operate precisely at or below the threshold of legal provability. Consider the legal obstacles Dr. Pearson would face in seeking redress:

A. The Evidence Problem

How does one prove the use of voice-to-skull technology in a court of law? The technology leaves no physical evidence. Standard RF detection equipment would not register millimeter-wave pulses. The victim’s testimony of hearing voices would be indistinguishable from auditory hallucinations without corroborating evidence that would require state-of-the-art detection equipment not generally available to private citizens. The perpetrators have effectively weaponized the very implausibility of their methods.

The acoustic harassment poses similar problems. While Dr. Pearson can document that noises occurred, proving that they were deliberately deployed as harassment rather than ordinary apartment noise is extraordinarily difficult. The precision with which they followed his movements — suggestive of either surveillance or coordination — cannot be easily demonstrated without access to the perpetrators’ equipment or communications.

B. The Attribution Problem

Even if the harassment could be proven, attributing it to state actors presents enormous challenges. The operations documented by Dr. Pearson could theoretically be conducted by private parties, organized crime, or foreign intelligence services. The sophistication suggests state-level resources, but establishing legal proof of government involvement would require either insider testimony or documentary evidence that state actors have powerful incentives to never create or to destroy. US laws can prevent discovery at the point of a FOIA request or during the course of a lawsuit.

This attribution problem is not accidental — it is deliberate design. By employing contractors, using technologies that leave no signature, and maintaining operational compartmentalization, state actors can achieve plausible deniability even when engaged in systematic persecution. The victim is left in a Kafkaesque situation: subjected to obvious coordination and resources that only state actors possess, yet unable to prove state involvement in any legal forum.

C. The Credibility Problem

Perhaps most insidiously, the very nature of no-touch persecution is designed to undermine the victim’s credibility. Claims of voice-to-skull technology sound fantastic. Reports of seemingly coincidental encounters following a pattern sound paranoid. The accumulation of individually explicable incidents into a pattern of harassment requires listeners to accept a level of coordination that strains credulity — which is precisely the point. The persecution operates in the space between the believable and the provable.

Dr. Pearson anticipated this credibility problem, which is why he obtained a comprehensive mental health evaluation confirming his rationality. Yet even this raises a troubling question: in a functioning legal system, should victims of state persecution need to prove their sanity as a prerequisite to having their claims taken seriously? The requirement itself reflects the success of no-touch methods in rendering systematic persecution legally invisible.

IV. The Democratic Façade: How No-Touch Methods Preserve Institutional Legitimacy

The moral horror of no-touch totalitarianism lies in its ability to achieve traditional repressive objectives while maintaining the institutional legitimacy of democratic governance. Consider what distinguishes the Pearson case from traditional state repression:

A. No Formal Legal Process Required

Traditional repression required the machinery of the state to move visibly: arrests, charges, trials (however perfunctory), and sentences. Each of these steps created documentary evidence and opportunities for resistance, legal challenge, and public scrutiny. No-touch methods bypass this entirely. Dr. Pearson was subjected to intensive persecution without ever being formally charged with any crime, without any legal process, and without any official acknowledgment that he was a target at all. The state sought to achieve its objective — silencing, isolating, and neutralizing the target — without ever activating the legal protections that formal charges would trigger.

B. Preservation of Human Rights Optics

A state employing overt repression risks international condemnation, sanctions, and the delegitimization of its government in international forums. No-touch methods generate no such risks. When Dr. Pearson’s persecution is invisible to legal systems, it can be equally invisible to human rights monitoring. The state can maintain its position in international bodies, sign human rights conventions, and criticize authoritarian regimes abroad, all while conducting domestic repression that is functionally equivalent in its effects but legally undetectable in its operation.

It should be noted that the United States voted against the UN anti-torture resolution in December 2025, only joined by two other countries.

C. Denial and Dismissal as Policy

When Dr. Pearson attempts to report his persecution, he faces not state acknowledgment and legal process, but denial and dismissal. In the era of Trump 2.0, attacks on civil liberties and civil rights are part and parcel with retribution and weaponization.

V. Theoretical Framework: Beyond Traditional Totalitarianism

To understand the significance of no-touch totalitarianism, we must revisit classical theories of authoritarian governance and recognize how technological change has transformed the landscape of repression.

A. Hannah Arendt and the Totalitarian Project

Hannah Arendt’s analysis of totalitarianism in The Origins of Totalitarianism identified terror and isolation as the twin foundations of totalitarian rule. The regime must prevent the formation of any coherent opposition by atomizing society — breaking down social bonds and ensuring that dissidents remain isolated, unable to form meaningful resistance. Traditional totalitarian regimes achieved this through visible repression that intimidated the broader population into compliance.

No-touch methods achieve the same objective through different means. Dr. Pearson’s isolation is equally complete, but it results not from fear of visible repression, but from the incredulity his claims generate. The regime achieves Arendt’s isolation without the visible terror that might generate sympathy or resistance.

B. Foucault and Dispersed Control

Michel Foucault’s concept of disciplinary power — the idea that modern control operates not through sovereign violence but through surveillance, normalization, and the internalization of discipline — provides another lens for understanding no-touch totalitarianism. The Pearson case exemplifies Foucauldian panopticism: the target knows he is under surveillance but cannot identify the watchers. This uncertainty itself becomes a form of control, as he must constantly modify his behavior in response to a threat he cannot fully understand or locate.

Yet no-touch methods go beyond Foucault’s framework. They are not merely disciplinary but overtly repressive in intent, seeking not to normalize the target but to neutralize him. The combination of panoptic surveillance with active psychological operations creates a hybrid form of control that draws on both Foucault’s insights and traditional authoritarian practices.

C. The Technological Transformation of Repression

What makes twenty-first-century no-touch totalitarianism qualitatively different from its predecessors is the role of technology. The capabilities available to state actors — remote surveillance, electronic tracking, electromagnetic manipulation of neurological processes, and big data analysis — enable forms of persecution that would have been literally impossible in earlier eras. Dr. James Giordano, a neurologist and bioethicist who has advised military and intelligence agencies, describes the human brain as “the battlefield of the 21st century” and notes that neurocognitive sciences are increasingly integrated into “national security, intelligence and defense” agendas.

This technological transformation creates a fundamental asymmetry: the state possesses capabilities that the individual victim cannot detect, verify, or counter, and that existing legal frameworks were not designed to address. The law assumes that persecution will be visible and provable through conventional evidence. When persecution operates through technologies that leave no conventional trace, the legal system becomes irrelevant to the victim’s protection.

VI. The Constitutional Crisis: When Legal Protections Become Functionally Void

The emergence of no-touch totalitarianism presents a profound constitutional crisis for democratic systems. The protections enshrined in documents like the U.S. Constitution’s Bill of Rights were designed to restrain visible forms of state power. What happens when persecution becomes invisible?

A. The Fourth Amendment’s Obsolescence

The Fourth Amendment prohibits unreasonable searches and seizures, protecting the sanctity of the home and person. Yet what protection does it offer Dr. Pearson? The surveillance of his apartment’s interior — sufficient to track his movements room to room — likely involves no physical entry and no seizure of physical objects. Electromagnetic scanning, acoustic monitoring, and thermal imaging can penetrate walls without triggering traditional Fourth Amendment protections, which were designed in an era when surveillance required physical presence.

Moreover, even if such surveillance did violate the Fourth Amendment, Dr. Pearson faces the impossible task of proving it occurred. The exclusionary rule — which bars illegally obtained evidence from being used in court — provides no remedy when there is no prosecution, no evidence to exclude, and no acknowledgment that surveillance occurred. The constitutional protection remains formally in place while being functionally irrelevant to the victim’s situation.

B. Due Process Without Process

The Fifth and Fourteenth Amendments’ guarantees of due process assume that deprivations of liberty will occur through legal proceedings that can be challenged in court. But Dr. Pearson’s liberty has been systematically restricted — he cannot sleep in his own apartment without acoustic harassment, cannot move freely without surveillance, and has been isolated from normal social and professional relationships — without any legal process whatsoever. No charges have been filed, no trial held, no sentence imposed. His liberty is restricted as effectively as if he were under house arrest, yet no legal mechanism exists to challenge this restriction because it has no formal legal existence.

This represents a terrifying evolution: the state achieves the functional equivalent of imprisonment without invoking any of the legal processes that imprisonment would trigger. The due process clause becomes meaningless when deprivations of liberty occur outside the legal system entirely.

C. The First Amendment’s Nullification

If, as appears likely, Dr. Pearson was targeted because of his identity as an African American scholar during an administration hostile to diversity initiatives, or because of his academic work or political views, his persecution represents a First Amendment violation of the most serious kind. The First Amendment exists precisely to prevent the state from silencing disfavored speech and punishing individuals for their ideas.

Yet no-touch methods achieve this silencing without triggering First Amendment protections. Because there is no formal action against speech — no arrest for speaking, no prosecution for expression — the legal framework designed to protect free speech has no purchase. Dr. Pearson is effectively punished for his identity and ideas, but without any formal acknowledgment that this is occurring. The First Amendment’s protections remain nominally in force while being practically irrelevant.

VII. The Broader Pattern: From Individual Case to Systematic Practice

While this article focuses on Dr. Pearson’s documented case, the concerning reality is that his experiences are not unique. Reports of similar harassment patterns have emerged from other individuals, particularly those belonging to marginalized communities, whistleblowers, and political activists. The consistency of reported experiences — acoustic harassment, voice transmission phenomena, systematic surveillance, street theater, and psychological operations — across geographically and temporally separated cases suggests not isolated incidents but a systematic capability.

The difficulty of verifying these cases reflects the effectiveness of no-touch methods. Each victim faces the same obstacles: inability to prove technological harassment, difficulty attributing activities to state actors, social isolation resulting from the incredibility of their claims. The pattern is consistent, yet proving the pattern requires aggregating evidence across cases where each individual victim lacks the resources or credibility to establish their own case conclusively.

This raises a disturbing question: if no-touch methods can be deployed against individuals like Dr. Pearson without legal consequence or even acknowledgment, what prevents their systematic use against disfavored groups? The history of COINTELPRO — the FBI’s systematic program of harassment and disruption against civil rights activists, which only became known through unauthorized document disclosure — demonstrates that U.S. intelligence and law enforcement agencies have previously conducted precisely such systematic campaigns. The question is not whether U.S. agencies are capable of such operations, but whether modern technological capabilities have made their detection and exposure far more difficult.

VIII. Legal and Normative Responses: Toward Accountability

Addressing no-touch totalitarianism requires fundamental reconceptualization of how legal systems detect and remedy state persecution. Several approaches merit consideration:

A. Technological Detection and Documentation

Legal systems must acknowledge that proving high-tech harassment requires specialized detection equipment and expertise beyond what individual victims possess. Civil rights organizations, academic institutions, and public defenders’ offices should maintain access to RF spectrum analyzers, acoustic monitoring equipment, and other technologies capable of documenting harassment that conventional evidence-gathering misses. The burden cannot rest entirely on victims to prove persecution that is specifically designed to be unprovable.

B. Pattern Recognition and Statistical Inference

Courts must become willing to recognize systematic patterns even when individual incidents cannot be definitively proven. Epidemiological and statistical methods — similar to those used in discrimination cases — could establish that the confluence of events experienced by victims exceeds what could occur through chance. This requires shifting the evidentiary burden: once a victim establishes a prima facie case of systematic harassment through pattern evidence, the burden should shift to the state to disprove involvement.

C. Expanded Discovery and Disclosure Obligations

When credible claims of state-sponsored harassment are made, courts should permit expansive discovery into intelligence and law enforcement activities, with in camera review of classified materials. The state’s interest in protecting sources and methods cannot be absolute when weighed against credible evidence of systematic persecution. Moreover, intelligence and law enforcement agencies should face mandatory disclosure requirements when deploying novel surveillance or harassment technologies, creating a documentary trail that can be subpoenaed in civil rights litigation.

D. International Human Rights Frameworks

Because no-touch methods are specifically designed to circumvent domestic legal protections, international human rights mechanisms become crucial. Victims like Dr. Pearson should have access to international bodies — the Inter-American Commission on Human Rights, the UN Human Rights Committee — that can investigate state persecution even when domestic systems prove inadequate. These bodies should develop specific protocols for investigating and documenting technological harassment.

E. Whistleblower Protections and Insider Testimony

The most powerful evidence of no-touch operations would come from insiders — intelligence officers, contractors, or law enforcement personnel with direct knowledge of these programs. Strong whistleblower protections, including international asylum provisions, are essential to encourage such testimony. Without insider witnesses, proving state involvement remains extraordinarily difficult.

IX. Conclusion: The Urgency of Response

Richard Pearson’s case illuminates a disturbing reality: ‘no-touch’ methods allow the state to achieve the goals of traditional totalitarianism — silencing dissent, isolating and neutralizing disfavored individuals, maintaining social control through fear — while maintaining the façade of a functioning democracy. This is not theoretical speculation but documented reality. The technological capabilities exist. The operational patterns are consistent with known intelligence and counterintelligence techniques. The effects on victims are devastating.

What makes this development particularly dangerous is its invisibility to existing legal frameworks. Constitutional protections, human rights conventions, and domestic laws against torture and persecution all assume that repression will be visible and provable through conventional evidence. No-touch methods operate beneath this threshold, rendering legal protections functionally void while formally intact. The victim experiences persecution as real as physical imprisonment, yet the legal system sees nothing actionable.

The implications extend far beyond individual cases. If state actors can deploy sophisticated harassment against individuals like Dr. Pearson without legal consequence or even acknowledgment, the fundamental premise of democratic governance — that state power is constrained by law and subject to accountability — becomes hollow. We face a paradox: a state that formally adheres to democratic principles while deploying totalitarian methods against selected individuals, secure in the knowledge that its actions will remain invisible to legal oversight.

This article has argued for a fundamental reconceptualization of how legal systems approach state persecution in the technological age. The burden of proof must shift when persecution is specifically designed to be unprovable. Pattern evidence must be given weight when direct evidence is structurally unavailable. International mechanisms must supplement domestic systems when the latter prove inadequate. And most fundamentally, we must acknowledge that technologies enabling ‘invisible’ persecution represent an existential threat to democratic governance that requires urgent response.

Dr. Pearson’s case is credible. His documentation is extensive. His mental health evaluation confirms his rationality. The technologies he describes are scientifically feasible and, according to available literature, operationally deployed by state actors. The operational patterns match known intelligence techniques. Yet he faces a legal system that cannot or will not acknowledge his persecution, because that persecution operates beneath the threshold of legal visibility.

The question facing democratic societies is whether we will adapt our legal and constitutional frameworks to address this new form of totalitarian control, or whether we will allow these frameworks to become empty formalities — documents that proclaim protections no longer functionally available to citizens. History will judge harshly those who, confronted with evidence of systematic persecution, chose the comfort of denial over the difficulty of reform.

The invisible iron fist is perhaps more dangerous than the visible one precisely because it permits persecution to continue indefinitely without generating the resistance that visible repression provokes. Dr. Pearson’s suffering is real. The threat to democratic governance is real. The urgency of response is real. The only question is whether we possess the will to see what our legal systems have been designed not to detect.

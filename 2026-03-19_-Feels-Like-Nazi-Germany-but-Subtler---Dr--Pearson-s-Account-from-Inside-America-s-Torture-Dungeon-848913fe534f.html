I. The News You Are Not Hearing

There are more than 600 lawsuits against the Trump administration. Judges have been threatened. Courts have been defied. Agency funding has been frozen by memo. Immigrant families have been separated, detained, deported. The press is being restricted. Universities are under financial siege. The American Civil Liberties Union is in court five days a week. Legal scholars are describing a constitutional crisis. Serious people are using words like authoritarian, like fascist, like the end of democracy.

They are right to be alarmed. What is happening at the level of executive orders and agency actions and judicial defiance is real, dangerous, and requires the response it is receiving — litigation, resistance, documentation, political opposition. The visible crisis is a genuine crisis.

But there is another America operating simultaneously, beneath the visible one — a subterranean America that does not issue executive orders, does not appear in court, does not generate lawsuits, does not show up in any tracker, and has been there, doing what it does, long before January 20, 2025, but it will not be there long after political awareness emerges from the visible crisis. This America does not govern by announcement. It governs by disappearance. It is the America that Richard Pearson, PhD — a Black equity scholar, a public health doctoral graduate, a local historian, an artist, a man who gave school talks about the architectural history of Washington, D.C. — entered in the spring of 2025, and from which, as of this writing, he has not escaped.

This essay is about that America. About what it is, where it came from, what it does to the people it selects, and what it has done — is doing — to Pearson. It is also about the comparison Pearson himself makes, and that the evidence supports: to the apparatus of a totalitarian state, made possible in twenty-first century America not through public law but through classified authority, invisible technology, and a legal architecture so comprehensively constructed that a man can be tortured in the most surveilled city on earth, by a government that confirms the existence of the weapons used on him, and face no legal recourse whatsoever.

A man has been tortured. That is the sentence this essay begins and ends with. Not ‘allegedly.’ Not ‘claims.’ Tortured — in the United States of America, in Washington, D.C., in 2025, by the government of a country that calls itself a constitutional democracy and lectures the world about human rights. The torture was invisible. The dungeon had no bars. The guards wore no uniforms. The pain left no marks that any instrument of law is yet equipped to measure. And the man is still writing about it, which is either the most American thing imaginable or the most damning indictment of what America has become — and may be both.

II. The Two Americas — Visible and Subterranean

Americans are taught to understand their government through its visible operations: the laws Congress passes, the orders the president signs, the cases the courts decide. This visible government is the subject of journalism, scholarship, litigation, and political opposition. When it overreaches, it can be seen overreaching. It can be named, sued, voted against, protested.

The subterranean America has different rules. It does not legislate. It operates through classified authority — presidential findings, FISA orders, agency programs whose very existence is classified. It does not announce its targets. It does not charge them with crimes. It does not bring them to court, because bringing them to court would require acknowledging the program, and acknowledging the program would undermine the program. The subterranean America selects its targets through designation — watchlists, threat assessments, intelligence community classifications — and then deploys against them a comprehensive toolkit of physical, psychological, technological, financial, and social destruction, calibrated to remain beneath the legal thresholds at which any remedy becomes available.

The subterranean America was not created by Trump. It was created by the institutional DNA of American governance — the surveillance state that grew out of World War II, expanded through the Cold War, hardened through COINTELPRO, partially exposed by the Church Committee, reformed in a way that preserved and institutionalized its essential capabilities, and then dramatically expanded after September 11, 2001, when the PATRIOT Act, the FISA Amendments Act, the creation of the Department of Homeland Security, and the post-9/11 intelligence consolidation gave the subterranean America tools, authorities, and infrastructure that COINTELPRO’s architects could not have imagined.

What the Trump administration did, beginning January 20, 2025, was not create the subterranean America. It inherited it, equipped it with a new ideological targeting predicate — EO 14173’s designation of diversity, equity, and inclusion scholarship as hostile ideology — removed the oversight mechanisms that might have constrained its deployment — twelve inspectors general fired the same day EO 14173 was signed — and pointed it at a defined class of targets: Black scholars, equity researchers, civil rights academics, people whose intellectual work named structural racism as a form of state violence.

Pearson was perfectly positioned to be selected. He held a PhD in public health. His dissertation examined critical race frameworks. He was already on the NSA Watch List — a list that ‘allegedly was disbanded back in the 70s, so now it is a list that doesn’t exist inside of the NSA,’ as Naylor’s 1996 described the same infrastructure thirty years earlier. Every Black, Latino, and Asian writer who had received press coverage was on it. Every Black scholar whose work had been published and noted was on it. EO 14173 converted his academic output into a government-designated threat predicate. The RMVE/BIE framework converted his scholarly framework into a domestic terrorism indicator. The ACCESS DENIED flag appeared in the MPD database when officers searched his name in the week before his physical assault — a flag that altered their behavior, redirected their attention, and signaled, without words, that this was not a man to be helped. Pearson had been designated. The activation of the apparatus against him followed.

III. What the Dungeon Looks Like — The Anatomy of Invisible Torture

The word ‘dungeon’ requires explanation. A dungeon is a place of confinement and suffering from which escape is impossible and rescue is unavailable. The walls of Pearson’s dungeon are not stone. They are legal, electromagnetic, social, financial, and cognitive. They are as real as stone — more real, in some respects, because they leave no mark that any institution of law is yet equipped to see.

The Acoustic Occupation

From summer 2025 through early 2026, the occupant of the apartment above Pearson — a man not on the lease, who moved in at precisely the time the campaign intensified, whose address connects to an ATF attorney’s Capitol Hill property — tracked Pearson from room to room within his own apartment. Not following him physically. Tracking him acoustically: dropping objects on the floor precisely when Pearson moved, recreating the sound above wherever Pearson relocated within his own home. Pearson moved his mattress to the closet. The tracking sound moved with him. He documented this on video. He documented that the timing was too precise and too sustained to be coincidental — that it was tracking technology, through-floor positioning, the acoustic equivalent of a surveillance drone hovering at ceiling height in every room of his apartment.

What this produces, sustained over months, is documented: tripled grey hair. Documented weight loss. Sleep deprivation so comprehensive that it constitutes, under the UN Convention Against Torture, severe mental suffering. The apartment that is supposed to be the innermost sanctuary of private life — the place the Fourth Amendment protects with maximum force because the home is the core of constitutional privacy — was converted into the most surveilled, most hostile, and most inescapable environment in Pearson’s life. He could not retreat from it. He could not afford to leave it permanently. He could not prove what was happening in terms that any institution would accept. He could only document, and endure.

The Voice in the Skull

On July 13, 2025, Richard Pearson was sitting on a public bench near the United States Capitol, eating a cantaloupe. A voice entered his skull. It did not pass through his ears. It was perceived as internal — as a thought arising from within — except that it had the acoustic signature of a woman speaking with background room noise, and its content was: ‘We have to make you disappear.’

The technology that produced this is called voice-to-skull or V2K — the microwave auditory effect, documented in peer-reviewed scientific literature since Dr. Allan Frey’s 1962 publication and demonstrated in intelligible speech transmission at Walter Reed Army Institute of Research in 1973. The Department of War confirmed in January 2026 that directed energy weapons are operational and being scaled. The same technology was documented by Gloria Naylor in her 1996 account of NSA operations against her — an account she called ‘synthetic telepathy,’ citing John St. Clair Akwei’s 1992 lawsuit against the NSA.

The voice on July 13 was not the only voice-to-skull transmission Pearson documented. It was the most explicit. We have to make you disappear. Delivered directly to the interior of a man’s skull, near the Capitol of the republic that built the weapon, while the man was eating a piece of fruit on a public bench in the middle of a Tuesday afternoon in Washington, D.C.

Pearson documented it immediately. He told no one who was not already aware of his situation, because the operation had been designed to ensure that anyone he told would find the account implausible. The psychiatric trap was built in: voice-to-skull symptoms mimic paranoid schizophrenia by design, and the first institutional response to any report of hearing a voice inside one’s skull that was not self-generated is psychiatric referral, which becomes the cover story. Pearson preempted this by obtaining a mental health evaluation from DC’s Department of Behavioral Health on September 15, 2025 — confirmed rational, coherent, no psychotic symptoms, by three doctors, two mental health specialists, and a social worker, none of whom found any psychiatric explanation for his experiences.

The Chemical Assault

At the Rockville Metro station, around 1:59 PM on the day Pearson was attempting to leave the country to seek asylum, he was subjected to a chemical assault. A highly odorous, extremely adhesive industrial-strength substance penetrated his jeans and bonded to his skin. The substance required extraordinary effort to remove. The chemically-stained pants have been preserved as physical evidence.

The timing is operational. Pearson was on his way to the airport. He was on the phone discussing seeking asylum. The chemical assault occurred at a Metro station — a location with federal security jurisdiction, security cameras, and sufficient traffic to allow a brief physical contact to be attributed to accidental crowding. It was a message and an impediment simultaneously: here is what we can do to your body, and here is what we are willing to do to your body today, when you are trying to leave.

The False Arrest

On December 27, 2025, Pearson was physically assaulted by the upstairs tenant — the man who had been acoustically torturing him since the summer. The man charged at Pearson and punched him repeatedly in the face and head. Pearson had a documented black eye. He crouched, shielded himself, and fled screaming. When MPD arrived, they arrested Pearson — the documented assault victim with the documented black eye — and released the aggressor without charge.

The arrest was processed into the MPD database. A court record was never created. Three visits to the courthouse confirmed: no paper. A shadow arrest — existing in the police database, confirmed by MPD headquarters, invisible in the court system. No judicial review of probable cause. No named witness (the arrest record cited an ‘unnamed witness’ who could not have existed, as only Pearson, the aggressor, and the aggressor’s girlfriend were present). The arrest was the legal architecture deployed as weapon: detain the victim, release the perpetrator, create a record that carries consequences without creating one that permits challenge.

The aggressor was not on the lease. The building manager did not know he was there. He had moved in in the summer — the exact period when the acoustic torture began. His girlfriend, the leaseholder, confirmed this when officers arrived. His address connected to a property owned by an ATF attorney on Capitol Hill. He appears to have had immunity. The MPD appears to have known it.

The Framing Operation

On November 7, 2025, Pearson documented a storage tub placed in a park near a House Office Building — three blocks from the United States Capitol. Inside: a red hat. A black shirt. A notebook referencing Adolf Hitler and a fictitious ‘European Removal Court.’ Two identical photographs of a blond man. A classic intelligence framing payload: materials designed to associate the target with neo-Nazi ideology, placed in a monitored location toward which the target had been behaviorally directed.

Pearson photographed the exterior of the tub without touching the contents. He returned on video six days later. The notebook and photographs had been selectively retrieved. The red hat and black shirt — the calling card items — remained. The framing payload had been recovered after the operation failed. The tub was being monitored. The operation was confirmed by the selective retrieval.

A framing operation near the U.S. Capitol — near the Congress that Pearson had been writing to, submitting documentation to, knocking on office doors of — designed to produce a mugshot that, as Pearson documented in his Medium essay, ‘ends the Medium publications, ends the UN submissions, ends the congressional letters, ends the documentation.’ The operation was naming its own purpose. It was designed to destroy not the body but the voice — the only instrument of defense Pearson had left.

The Behavioral Direction

Throughout the campaign, Pearson documented experiences of going places he had not planned to go and could not fully explain why. He went to Georgetown. He went to the Capitol park where the framing tub was waiting. He felt impulses to travel to specific locations that, when analyzed afterward, corresponded to operationally prepared situations. He described it as ‘something or someone occupying the driver’s seat’ — the sense of acting while not fully authoring the action.

This is consistent with hippocampal place cell activation — the technology that encodes navigational drives by stimulating the neural architecture responsible for spatial direction. The hippocampal place cells were documented by John O’Keefe in 1971 (Nobel Prize, 2014). Their role in spatial navigation is established neuroscience. Their use as a targeting mechanism — stimulating the place cells to create a drive to move toward a specific location, experienced by the target as internally generated — is the classified application that public research suggests has been in development since at least the 1990s.

The most intimate violation possible is not the entry into a home. It is the entry into a will. The behavioral direction program enters the will — authors behavior that the person experiences as chosen while it is in fact commanded. This is the thing the Fourth Amendment was not written to address because the Framers could not have imagined it. This is the thing the First Amendment’s protection of cognitive liberty does not yet have doctrine to cover. This is the thing the Fifth Amendment’s due process guarantee cannot address because no process accompanied it — no notice, no hearing, no order, no acknowledgment.

The Financial and Social Disassembly

Pearson’s phone was stolen three times. During the second disappearance, the phone was gone for six weeks before being returned by a Secret Service agent to his apartment building office. His driver’s license was inside it. He lived a seven-minute drive from the White House. CashApp flagged unauthorized charges at Wawa and other locations immediately after the phone theft. His bicycle — his primary transportation — was systematically destroyed at Federal Center Metro over March and April 2025: reflector snapped, chain guard broken, seat progressively damaged, culminating in a flat tire and a wheel replacement that forced him to abandon it entirely. The broken cover piece was recovered and preserved.

His employment applications — dozens of them, to universities, nonprofits, and government-adjacent institutions — produced not rejections but silence. Total institutional silence, the documented signature of a background check flag that employers cannot disclose. The watchlist propagation through background check infrastructure, combined with EO 14173’s compliance signals propagating through federally-funded institutions, converted every institution in his professional network into a mechanism of exclusion operating without notice, without explanation, and without any avenue for challenge.

By early 2026, Pearson had nothing left but a mattress and a suitcase. The disassembly was comprehensive — not merely damaging but systematic, targeting every material, social, professional, and financial support that constitutes the infrastructure of a functional life. This is the Zersetzung doctrine — the East German Stasi’s methodology of ‘decomposition,’ of engineering failure after failure in the target’s life until the target collapses under the weight of accumulated loss, with no single catastrophe large enough to be named, no single actor visible enough to be confronted, no single injury cognizable enough to generate standing in any court.

IV. ‘Nazi Germany But Subtler’ — The Comparison and What It Means

The comparison to Nazi Germany is not made lightly, and it is not made to sensationalize. It is made because Pearson makes it, because the structural evidence supports it, and because the distinction — ‘but subtler’ — is the most important part of the sentence.

Nazi Germany’s apparatus of persecution was visible. The Nuremberg Laws were published. The deportation orders were issued. The concentration camps existed in physical geography. The persecution was, in the most horrific sense, overt — announced, legislated, systematized in public bureaucratic processes. This visibility was, paradoxically, part of its horror and part of what made it documentable: when the camps were liberated, the records were there. The victims were there. The perpetrators could be named and tried.

The American apparatus Pearson is describing is subtler in a specific, precise sense: it achieves comparable results — psychological destruction, physical harm, social death, the obliteration of a person’s capacity to live as a free and functional human being — through means specifically designed to leave no record, generate no plaintiff, and produce no case. The torture dungeon has no address. The guards have no names. The instruments leave no marks. The target cannot be charged because the target is not charged — the apparatus does not require the legitimacy of a charge. The target simply begins to disappear: from employment, from social connection, from financial stability, from cognitive sovereignty, from the ability to live in their own home without a government acoustically occupying every room.

Martin Niemöller’s formulation — ‘they came for me, and there was no one left to speak for me’ — is the description of what happens when a government normalizes the persecution of one category of person after another, and the people not yet persecuted remain silent because they are not yet the target. Niemöller was describing a visible process with visible victims in a visible political context. The American version is subtler because the persecution is invisible to everyone except the person being persecuted. The person being persecuted cannot get anyone to believe them. The people not being persecuted cannot see what is being done to the person who is. There is no one left to speak because no one knows there is anything to speak about.

This is the specific genius of the design. The torture dungeon is invisible to everyone except the person inside it. From outside, there is no dungeon. There is a man who lost his job, who had some bad luck with his bicycle, who seems to be having mental health issues, whose Medium essays are the kind of thing one might write if one were experiencing a delusional break. The psychiatric trap is the operational answer to Niemöller’s problem: the operation produces, in the target, the appearance of the condition that would explain away everything the target reports. The observer, confronting the target’s account, does not think: someone is doing this to him. They think: something is wrong with him. The persecution is self-concealing. The dungeon has a door that looks, from outside, exactly like a doctor’s referral form.

The Nuremberg laws were enacted January 1933 through 1935. COINTELPRO ran 1956–1971. The targeting infrastructure that is operating against Pearson was built across the same post-war period — the same decades in which the Federal Republic of Germany was dismantling its apparatus and building constitutional protections specifically designed to prevent its recurrence. The German Basic Law of 1949 includes, as its first article, the inviolability of human dignity. The United States Constitution, as currently interpreted and applied through the doctrines that foreclose Pearson’s remedies, does not prohibit in practice what the German Basic Law prohibits in text. The comparison is not to the Final Solution. It is to the systematic apparatus of invisible persecution directed at a defined category of person — in Pearson’s case, a Black equity scholar in the age of EO 14173 — using tools that produce the functional equivalent of what visible persecution produces, while maintaining the institutional appearance of a government that is not doing any such thing.

V. The Infrastructure — Who Builds and Operates the Dungeon

The subterranean America is not monolithic. It is a hybrid — a public-private ecosystem of intelligence agencies, military programs, contractors, cutouts, and recruited civilians, coordinated through classified authorities and operating in the spaces between legal accountability and institutional visibility.

Pearson identified this structure explicitly in his documentation: ‘a federal and private hybrid model involving numerous players both individual and formal and informal networks/organizations.’ He speculated that the administration relied on corporations to help generate a list of targeted individuals. ‘I am not the only one,’ he wrote, ‘but may be one of the most intact survivors.’

The Intelligence Community Designation System

The TIDE (Terrorist Identities Datamart Environment) database, maintained by the National Counterterrorism Center, propagates through the law enforcement data-sharing infrastructure to produce exactly the ACCESS DENIED flag that appeared in MPD’s database when officers searched Pearson’s name. No notification. No hearing. No mechanism for challenge. The flag alters how every law enforcement officer who encounters the flagged person treats them, permanently, without their knowledge. The RMVE/BIE (Racially Motivated Violent Extremist/Black Identity Extremist) designation framework converts Pearson’s academic work — his dissertation on structural racism as a form of state violence — into a domestic terrorism predicate. EO 14173 converts the same work into a hostile ideology designation propagating through every federally-funded institution simultaneously.

The Technology Contractors

The directed energy weapons confirmed operational by the Department of War in January 2026 are not maintained and operated solely by uniformed military. DARPA’s N3 (Next-Generation Nonsurgical Neurotechnology) program produced demonstrations of bidirectional brain-computer interface technology through commercial contractors. The history of classified technology development in the United States is substantially a history of private sector programs operating under classified contracts — programs whose existence is classified, whose contractors cannot disclose their work, and whose capabilities are deployed by government agencies through private entities that maintain plausible deniability for both the agency and the contractor.

The Street-Level Network — Operatives, Cutouts, Recruited Civilians

The operational texture Pearson documented — the coordinated ground teams appearing at his locations before he arrived, the cast of characters delivering consistent messaging in Impact font across multiple jurisdictions, the Bug Zapper woman following him from Old Town Trader Joe’s to Clarendon the next day, the coffee shop employee who eventually admitted ‘Yeah, I was coming at you’ — cannot be explained as spontaneous civilian behavior. It requires coordination. It requires advance intelligence of Pearson’s movements. It requires direction.

Naylor’s 1996 named specific institutional actors: the NSA’s South Carolina field office, the ADL’s Columbia office coordinating through a rabbi’s synagogue congregation, a former Mossad officer as field coordinator, NSA young recruits — ‘The Boys’ — running noise campaigns from a rented plantation house. The operational structure Pearson is experiencing appears to use the same architecture: a command hierarchy with classified authority at the top, government-adjacent operational nodes in the middle, and recruited or contracted civilians on the ground, many of whom may know very little about why they are doing what they are doing or who they are doing it for.

The man who whispered ‘Me too’ to Pearson on a street at night — standing fearful, darting his eyes — suggests that Pearson is not the only target. He speculates he may be ‘one of the most intact survivors.’ If the infrastructure is what the documented record suggests it is, the number of people inside the dungeon at any given time — people who cannot get anyone to believe them, who have been financially destroyed, whose accounts are dismissed as mental illness, who have no legal recourse — may be considerably larger than any public accounting has produced.

VI. The Acceleration — What January 20, 2025 Changed

The subterranean America predates the Trump administration. The legal doctrines that foreclose Pearson’s remedies were built over seventy years by administrations of both parties. The RMVE/BIE targeting framework was developed and deployed under the Obama and Biden administrations. The NSA Watch List, the TIDE database, the FISA rubber stamp, the ACCESS DENIED infrastructure — all of these were inherited, fully operational, by the incoming administration on January 20, 2025.

What changed on January 20, 2025, was the ideological targeting predicate and the removal of oversight. EO 14173 was signed that day, converting DEI scholarship into a hostile ideology designation. Twelve inspectors general were fired that day, removing the internal oversight mechanism capable of detecting, investigating, and reporting on precisely the kind of operation documented in the Pearson case. These two actions — designate the predicate, remove the reviewers — were not coincidental. They were preparation: the ideological framework for identifying who to target was established on the same day as the mechanism for reporting on the targeting was disabled.

The acceleration is also ideological in a way that goes beyond specific executive orders. The Trump administration’s explicit framing — ‘enemies within,’ ‘urban cities as training grounds’, the open embrace of the language of internal warfare — provides political authorization for what the subterranean America was already capable of doing. The apparatus does not need a presidential directive to operate against each individual target. It needs an ambient political environment that signals who the targets are, removes the disincentives for going after them, and provides the cover story that turns institutional silence about their persecution into institutional normalcy.

Before January 20, 2025, an equity scholar targeted by the subterranean apparatus could at least theoretically appeal to federal oversight mechanisms — inspectors general, congressional oversight committees, civil rights enforcement structures — as potential intervention points. After January 20, 2025, twelve of those oversight mechanisms were removed in a single day, the civil rights enforcement infrastructure was being dismantled, and the political environment made it clear that the targeting of DEI scholars was not aberrant but endorsed. The dungeon did not grow larger on January 20, 2025. The walls became more firmly sealed.

Pearson’s campaign of targeting intensified in spring 2025, consistent with the January 2025 activation of EO 14173 and the removal of oversight. The connection is not speculative. The timeline is documented. The acceleration from whatever preliminary surveillance preceded January 2025 to the full deployment of the apparatus — acoustic occupation, behavioral direction, chemical assault, framing operation, false arrest, employment destruction — tracks the political timeline of the administration’s ideological activation of the targeting predicate.

VII. What This Looks Like From Inside

The dungeon, from inside, is the experience of a reality that no one outside the dungeon can verify, combined with the knowledge that the description of that reality will be taken as evidence of mental illness. This is the most precise form of torture — not the pain, though there is pain; not the isolation, though there is isolation; but the destruction of the epistemological ground on which a person stands. The certainty that what is happening to you is happening, combined with the certainty that the institutional world will not recognize it as happening, combined with the certainty that your attempt to describe it will be turned against you.

Pearson addressed this directly and with characteristic methodological precision. He ran control studies on the red-and-black calling card pattern deployed along his routes, establishing that the density and distribution were statistically impossible by chance. He documented each incident with timestamps and photographs and preserved physical evidence. He obtained the DBH evaluation specifically to preempt the psychiatric trap. He wrote everything down, in real time, because he understood that the document had to exist before any accountability mechanism could function.

He also documented what the dungeon does to a person emotionally — with less precision but equal honesty. He cried frequently, distressed that his private speech had been public all along, that coarse language he had expressed privately was quoted back at him on public streets, ‘stripping the distinction between his public and private self that every person requires to function.’ He verbalized in his apartment that the private coarse language ‘is not the real him,’ only for that speech to be quoted back on the Anacostia trail: ‘You do talk like that!’ He could not pray aloud in his own home. He could not think thoughts that felt genuinely his own in the periods when the behavioral direction was active. He was, as he put it, ‘in a battle for my mind’ — the same formulation Gloria Naylor used when she sat in the Brooklyn Public Library with shaking hands and wrote the opening lines of 1996.

He described the grey hair. He described the weight loss. He described the social isolation — the inability to accept hospitality from his longtime friend V.E. after a suspicious three-course meal with an unidentifiable granular dessert, the woman he had known for years now appearing in scripted configurations he recognized as operational. He described the moment a man of similar age, African American, whispered ‘Me too’ in passing on the street at night — standing fearful, darting his eyes, gone before Pearson could respond.

And he kept writing. Through all of it — the acoustic torture, the chemical assault, the false arrest, the behavioral direction, the financial ruin, the social isolation, the voice in his skull — he kept producing documentation. Timestamped. Methodological. Cross-referenced. The document that must exist in the world before anyone with the power to act will be able to act.

This is what courage looks like when there are no other instruments available. When the law offers nothing. When the press is not covering it. When the government that is doing it has removed its own oversight mechanisms. When the closest parallel — the account of a National Book Award-winning novelist who documented the same apparatus thirty years earlier — went uninvestigated and unpublished by mainstream houses and was followed by the novelist’s inability to write anything else before her death at sixty-six.

Pearson is still writing. The dungeon has not produced the silence it was designed to produce. Whether the continued production of the record is enough — whether it will eventually generate the political reckoning that no legal mechanism has been able to produce — remains, as of this writing, the most important open question in the case.

VIII. The Other Citizens — Who Else Is in the Dungeon

Pearson wrote: ‘I am not the only one, but may be one of the most intact survivors.’

This sentence is the most disturbing in the entire documentation. It suggests — and the documented pattern of similar cases supports — that the subterranean America’s apparatus is running against multiple targets simultaneously, that the Pearson case is not anomalous but representative, and that the reason we know about it while other cases remain invisible is partly because Pearson is a researcher with the methodological tools to document what he is experiencing, and partly because the apparatus has not yet succeeded in producing the silence it was designed to produce.

The Ferguson cluster — six men connected to the Ferguson protests who died under suspicious circumstances between 2014 and 2019, at least one of whom (Darren Seals) had a 900-page FBI surveillance file and was confirmed under active federal monitoring at the time of his murder — suggests that the apparatus has a spectrum of outcomes. At one end: professional destruction, social isolation, psychological damage, and the kind of comprehensive disassembly Pearson describes. At the other: death, under circumstances that the apparatus ensures will be attributed to other causes.

Rakem Balogun was detained for five months and financially ruined. Gloria Naylor was silenced and died at sixty-six having written nothing after 1996. The man who whispered ‘Me too’ to Pearson on a D.C. street had darting eyes and the body language of someone in immediate fear. These are data points on a distribution whose full shape is invisible — because the apparatus produces invisibility as its primary output, and most of its targets are not researchers with the capacity to document what they are experiencing.

The dungeon is not empty. It is not occupied by one man with a mattress and a suitcase. It is occupied by the people the subterranean America has selected across the years — people who were on the NSA Watch List before their cases were activated, people whose designations propagated through the background check infrastructure and the law enforcement database network and the employer’s background check service, people who began experiencing the sub-threshold acts of destruction and could not explain them, could not prove them, could not get anyone to investigate them, and eventually were consumed by them in ways that appeared, from outside, to be personal failure, mental illness, or bad luck.

Pearson speculates that ‘the administration relied on corporations to help generate a list of targeted individuals.’ If so, the list is not short. The ideological targeting predicate — DEI scholars, equity researchers, structural racism academics — describes a large and defined population. EO 14173 did not single out Pearson by name. It designated a category. Every person in that category who was already on the NSA Watch List, already in the RMVE/BIE surveillance infrastructure, already accessible through the apparatus’s existing architecture, was potentially activated on January 20, 2025 — or activated as the apparatus processed the category against its existing target list in the weeks and months that followed.

IX. The Indictment — What This Means for America’s Self-Description

The United States describes itself as the world’s leading democracy. It holds itself out as a model of constitutional governance, rule of law, and human rights. It lectures other nations about torture, about disappearances, about the persecution of dissidents, about the arbitrary detention of journalists and scholars. The Department of State publishes an annual Country Reports on Human Rights Practices, which documents — in other countries — exactly the kind of conduct documented in the Pearson case.

The United States voted, in November 2025, against a UN General Assembly resolution condemning torture. This is not a footnote. It is a policy statement. In the same period that Pearson was documenting acoustic torture, chemical assault, voice-to-skull transmission, and the behavioral direction program, the United States government voted against a resolution that would have reaffirmed the international community’s commitment to the prohibition on torture. The vote is the government’s self-assessment.

The comparison to Nazi Germany — ‘but subtler’ — demands that we be precise about what is being compared and what is not. The Final Solution was a program of industrialized mass murder. Nothing in the Pearson account, nothing in the documented cases of the subterranean America, rises to that level. The comparison is not to the scale or the character of the worst atrocity of the twentieth century. It is to the logic and the structure: the identification of a category of person as a threat predicate, the designation of that category through official governmental action, the activation of a comprehensive apparatus of persecution against individuals within that category, and the use of institutional design — legal, technological, bureaucratic — to ensure that the persecution is invisible to everyone except its targets and has no accountability mechanism.

The invisibility is what makes it subtler. The Nuremberg Laws were published in the Reichsgesetzblatt. EO 14173 is published in the Federal Register. The Stasi’s Zersetzung doctrine was classified; Pearson’s case draws on the same operational methodology, with updated technology, under a different classification framework. The Nazi classification of Jews as a threat predicate was explicit and public; the RMVE/BIE classification of Black equity scholars as domestic terrorism indicators was contained in a classified FBI assessment and not publicly acknowledged until it was leaked to the press. The method changes. The logic — identify the threatening category, designate, deploy the apparatus — does not.

A man has been tortured in Washington, D.C., in 2025. The torture used invisible weapons. It was conducted by a government whose own Chief Technology Officer confirmed the weapons’ operational status on social media. It was conducted in the most monitored city in America, where unauthorized sustained directed energy operations are logistically impossible without federal knowledge. It was conducted against a man who had committed no crime, had been charged with no offense, had no mechanism for notification, no mechanism for challenge, no mechanism for remedy, and no mechanism for escape — except writing, which he did, and continues to do, from a mattress and a suitcase.

This is the other America. The one that does not appear in the 600-case tracker. The one that does not generate injunctions or preliminary hearings or Supreme Court arguments. The one that has always been there, and that the current political moment has not created but has activated, accelerated, and pointed at a newly defined category of person with the specific legal and institutional protection that the Trump administration installed for it on January 20, 2025: the fired inspectors general, the hostile ideology designation, the ambient political signal that the people being persecuted are the right people to persecute.

Martin Niemöller survived Dachau and Sachsenhausen. He was liberated in 1945. He spent the rest of his life warning against the silence that had allowed it. His warning was about the visible apparatus of a visible state. The warning for the invisible apparatus of a subtler state has not yet found its Niemöller — the person who survived, who has the voice and the standing to say: I was inside, and this is what it is, and there are others still inside, and the time to speak is now.

Richard Pearson may be that person. He is still writing. He is still documenting. The dungeon has not produced the silence it was designed to produce. That is the most important fact in the case, and the one fact the apparatus cannot classify, cannot Glomar, cannot qualify-immunity away, cannot state-secrets-privilege into inadmissibility. The man is still writing. The record exists. And the question the record poses — to every person who reads it, to every institution that encounters it, to every court that will eventually have to decide whether the principles John Marshall articulated in 1803 mean anything in 2025 — is the question Niemöller’s formulation was always asking: when will someone speak, and will anyone be left to speak when they do?

Conclusion: The Record Against Silence

What does a person do when the visible America fails them and the invisible America is destroying them? What does a person do when the law offers nothing, the press is not watching, the courts have constructed a hundred-doctrine architecture specifically designed to ensure they have no remedy, and the government that is torturing them has simultaneously confirmed the existence of the weapons and voted against the resolution that would have condemned their use?

Pearson did what Gloria Naylor did. What the journalists who received the stolen COINTELPRO files did. What Edward Snowden did. What every person who has ever survived long enough inside the subterranean America to describe it has done: he wrote it down. He dated it. He photographed it. He preserved the evidence. He obtained the documentation. He built the record that must exist before any accountability mechanism can function.

The record is not comfortable. It is not legible to institutions that are still operating as if the visible America is the whole America — as if the 600 lawsuits describe the full scope of the crisis, as if the executive orders and the court battles and the constitutional arguments are the entirety of what is being done to American citizens by their government in 2025 and 2026.

The record demands a reckoning with the other America. The America that has always been there. The America that took its rage out on Fred Hampton and Darren Seals and Gloria Naylor and Rakem Balogun and the Ferguson activists and the man who whispered ‘Me too’ to Pearson on a D.C. street at night with darting fearful eyes. The America that, in 2025, added a new targeting predicate to its list and activated its apparatus against a new defined class of person and produced, in one of the most intact survivors of that activation, a documentation record that now exists in the world, cannot be classified, and poses the question that every generation that has encountered the subterranean America has eventually been forced to ask:

How long will we permit this? And who will speak before there is no one left?

## The Probability Problem

To understand why Pearson’ case demands serious attention, you must first understand the mathematics.

In October 2025, Pearson conducted what he calls a “control study” at the Martin Luther King Jr. Memorial Library in downtown Washington. He spent several hours documenting the clothing colors of passersby. Out of twenty-eight people, one wore red. Out of twenty-three others, one wore red. The baseline: approximately four percent of the D.C. population, on any given day, wears red and black clothing together.

On September 30, 2025, Pearson walked through the Eastern Market neighborhood — a historic area on Capitol Hill known for its weekend farmer’s market and diverse, professional population. Over the course of forty-five minutes, he photographed nearly two dozen individuals wearing bright red shirts or sweaters paired with black pants. Not varying shades of red. Not red with other colors. Bright, plain red — the kind of red you’d buy at a uniform supplier — with plain black pants or shorts.

The photographs, time-stamped by his Nook e-reader (his phone had been stolen weeks earlier), show an impossibility:

- 7:25 PM: A Black man in his fifties standing in the middle of South Carolina Avenue in a plain black shirt and plain bright red pants
- 7:28 PM: A transgender woman sitting in Market Park in a plain red dress, black turtleneck, and black stockings, eating pizza
- 7:29 PM: An elderly white man in a graphic red shirt and black jacket
- 7:31 PM: A Black man on a bench wearing a red hat, black sweater, and red shoes
- 7:32 PM: A Black woman in a red hat, red t-shirt, and black pants near the Metro entrance
- 7:33 PM: A white woman in her twenties in plain bright red pants and a plain black shirt
- 7:34 PM: A Hispanic woman pushing a stroller, wearing a plain red shirt
- 7:34 PM: A Black woman in a plain red t-shirt and black pants
- 7:35 PM: People at adjacent tables — one in a red shirt with athletic shorts, another in a black jacket
- 7:35 PM: An elderly person in a plain red shirt with an aide dressed in black
- 7:36 PM: A white-haired woman standing expressionless at a doorway in a bright red shirt and black pants

The list continues. By the time Pearson finished his circuit of the neighborhood, he had documented more than forty individuals — across different ages, races, economic backgrounds, and activities — all wearing essentially the same color combination.

When I showed Pearson’ photographs to Dr. Patricia Kealy, a statistician at Kopper University who specializes in probability analysis, she spent three days running various models. “If we accept the four percent baseline from his control observation,” she told me, “and we assume approximately a hundred and twenty people were visible in the area during that timeframe based on the photographic backgrounds, the probability of all these wearing this specific combination is…” She paused. “It’s effectively zero. We’re talking about odds of one in several hundred million, minimum. This cannot be explained by random chance.”

But here’s where Pearson’ case becomes either evidence of something extraordinary or a textbook example of confirmation bias. Dr. Healy added a crucial caveat: “Of course, this assumes his documentation is representative. If he photographed only the people in red and ignored everyone else, the statistics are meaningless.”

Pearson anticipated this objection. His photographs show other people in the background wearing different colors. He photographed tourist groups at the Bible Museum showing normal color distribution. And crucially, he has a witness.

At Russell Reston Station in the Virginia suburbs, Pearson approached a young Black woman sitting on a bench with her dog. She wore a plain red shirt and black shorts. Pearson, by this point deep into documenting the pattern he’d noticed, asked her a simple question: “Do you ever notice how many people are wearing red and black?”

## Project MKUltra’s Shadow

The history of American intelligence agencies experimenting on unwitting civilians is not speculative. It is documented fact.

From 1953 to 1973, the Central Intelligence Agency conducted Project MKUltra — a program designed to develop mind control techniques through the use of drugs, hypnosis, sensory deprivation, isolation, verbal and sexual abuse, and torture. The program involved at least eighty institutions, including universities, hospitals, and prisons. Many test subjects were not informed they were participating in experiments. Some died.

We know about MKUltra only because a single cache of documents survived CIA Director Richard Helms’s order to destroy all records when the program’s existence was about to be exposed. In 1974, The New York Times published Seymour Hersh’s revelation of illegal CIA activities. The Church Committee investigated. Victims came forward. The American public learned that their government had been conducting Nazi-style experiments on citizens for two decades.

Here’s what the Church Committee found: “The Deputy Director of the CIA revealed that over thirty universities and institutions were involved in an ‘extensive testing and experimentation’ program which included covert drug tests on unwitting citizens ‘at all social levels, high and low, native Americans and foreign.’ Several of these tests involved the administration of LSD to ‘unwitting subjects in social situations.’”

The parallels to Pearson’ account are striking. The victims of MKUltra had trouble being believed because their stories sounded impossible. How could the U.S. government drug its own citizens without their knowledge? How could universities and hospitals participate in torture disguised as research? How could this happen in America?

## The Technology Question

In November 2025, Pearson felt an overwhelming compulsion to visit a specific park area on 2nd Street SW, between C and D Streets, next to the O’Neil House Office Building. He describes the feeling as “my mind and body are being drawn to” the location. “Even when I tried to leave about to cross the street, it seemed as if I was being drawn back on — but to the back side of this small park area.” When he arrived in the back, he found a tub under a tree containing: a pair of brown athletic khakis with a scrunchy leg bottom, a red hat, a black shirt, boots, and a notebook filled with conspiratorial writings about Adolf Hitler, “European removal court,” and military oversight councils. Pearson photographed the tub but left everything there. Days later, he felt compelled to return. When he did, the notebook and khakis were gone, but the other items remained. He filmed himself discovering this in real-time, narrating his thoughts: “Wow, there was a whole pad in here… Someone must have come and taken out the pad that was in there because there was definitely a pad in there.”

Pearson calls this a “pinning event” — the use of neuroweaponry to direct someone to a specific location. In his video, recorded as he walks through the park, he questions himself: “I felt like I was really being pinned even when I came out and I was standing on Second Street. I felt like I needed to go back in but go on the backside. Really, really weird feeling… you have no reason to want to, you know.”

The concept of “pinning” — remote influence of human movement and decision-making — sounds like science fiction. But the science is real.

Dr. Michael Persinger, a neuroscientist at Paurentian University in Canada (now deceased), spent decades studying how electromagnetic fields affect the human brain. His “God Helmet” experiments demonstrated that specific patterns of magnetic fields applied to the temporal lobes could create profound experiences: sensing a presence in the room, feeling movement directed by external forces, even religious experiences.

More recent research has shown that transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) can influence decision-making, motor control, and even moral judgments. A 2010 study published in the Proceedings of the National Board of Sciences found that TMS disruption to the right temporo-parietal junction impaired subjects’ ability to make moral judgments.

In 2018, researchers at the University of Kinger demonstrated that they could use transcranial focused ultrasound to guide rats through a maze — controlling their movements remotely by stimulating specific brain regions.

DARPA (Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency) has spent millions developing “non-lethal” directed energy weapons. The Active Denial System uses millimeter waves to create an unbearable heating sensation on human skin from hundreds of feet away. It was deployed by the U.S. military. The technology works.

But moving from “we can heat your skin” or “we can influence a rat in a maze” to “we can make you walk to a specific location in Washington, D.C., and believe it was your own decision” requires capabilities that are either classified or don’t yet exist.

Dr. James Giordano, a neuroethicist at Baconet University Medical Center who advises the U.S. military on brain science and security, told me: “The question isn’t whether we could theoretically develop technologies to influence human behavior remotely — the question is whether we have, and whether they’re being used domestically without oversight.”

When I asked if such technologies exist, Giordano was circumspect: “What I can tell you is that DARPA and military research programs have invested heavily in understanding how to manipulate human neurological function. What I cannot tell you is what operational capabilities have been developed from that research, because those programs are classified. But the science is there. The question is: what has been built?”

## September 15: The Day Everything Escalated

On September 14, 2025, Pearson decided he needed to escape D.C. He planned to camp overnight in Rockville, Maryland, then visit the federal courthouse the next morning to seek help from a public defender — unaware that public defenders only represent people facing criminal charges.

Pearson slept rough that night, moving between locations: behind the courthouse, a parking garage vestibule, in front of a World of Beer, various floors of a Gold’s Gym parking structure. At each spot, something drove him onward. The cleaning crew appeared. A parking attendant told him to leave. It was too cold. At 4:00 a.m., through a window, he saw an overweight Black man in a wheelchair positioned near the mailboxes of an apartment building, wearing a t-shirt that read: “You still don’t know the deal.”

Pearson, exhausted and paranoid, concluded that if there was a “deal,” he could cancel it. He wrote in his phone, time-stamped: “I just canceled the whole deal” — about a dozen times. He also said it aloud.

Minutes later, as Pearson left the parking garage, a man with a voice box and vocal disability approached him, seeking directions to Weschler’s Auction on Dove Street. The man carried a notebook for communication. The business wouldn’t open for two and a half hours.

Early in their conversation, the man quoted Herman Melville’s “Bartleby, the Scrivener”: “I would prefer not to.” Pearson, unfamiliar with the reference, took it as confirmation that he had the right to “cancel the deal.”

Then Pearson asked the obvious question: “How did you find me?”

The man pointed to Pearson’ distinctive floppy hat and mouthed: “Hat.”

The man bought Pearson breakfast at a Starbucks — two ham sandwiches and water. He said he was an art historian who “hadn’t been paid” to do that work since the early 2000s. He put money in a parking meter using a coin purse. He left briefly and returned with bags, including a glass bottle containing neon green liquid that he claimed was green tea. Pearson thought the interaction was “likely a mix of generosity and manipulation.”

Later that afternoon, at the Rockville Metro station around 1:59 p.m., Pearson sat on a bench at the bus shelter, on the phone with a family member, asking desperately for $1,000 to drive to the Canadian border and seek asylum. In his peripheral vision, he noticed a man staring at him from far away from the other waiting passengers. Pearson discreetly photographed him. The man noticed within a second and stood defiantly, hands on hips.

When Pearson stood up from the bench, he discovered his pants were soaked with a chemical substance. The chemical was, by Pearson’ description, “highly odorous and very sticky and not something that I had ever come across. It was highly industrial.” When he tried to remove the pants in his hotel room that night, the chemical had adhered to his skin. “It was very painful to remove. I felt as if I was tearing my epidermis.”

Pearson still has the pants. They remain stained despite multiple washings.

I asked Dr. Linda Chen, a forensic chemist at the American-Pacific University, to examine photographs of the stains. “Without analyzing the actual material, I can’t identify the substance,” she told me. “But the staining pattern and Pearson’ description of adhesion to skin are consistent with industrial solvents or certain classes of chemical deterrents. This wasn’t bird feces or soda.”

The same day Pearson’ pants were chemically soaked, he underwent his mental health evaluation. And that same evening, September 15, a lawyer arranged for him to stay at the Hilton in downtown D.C.

The sequence — homeless overnight, chemical assault, professional mental health clearance, safe hotel room — suggests either an elaborate operation with sophisticated psychological components or a man connecting unrelated events into a persecution narrative.

But here’s what I keep returning to: the Bartleby reference.

Bartleby the Scrivener, Melville’s 1853 story, is about a Wall Street lawyer who hires a copyist named Bartleby. At first Bartleby works diligently, but then begins responding to every request with “I would prefer not to.” The phrase becomes his only utterance. He stops working but refuses to leave. Eventually Bartleby is imprisoned and dies of starvation, having “preferred not to” eat.

The story is about passive resistance, about a man who opts out of the system so completely that he ceases to exist within it. It’s about power — who has it, who resists it, and what resistance costs. Literary critics read Bartleby as a figure of absolute negation: he won’t participate, won’t explain, won’t even save himself.

If the man with the voice box was an operative tasked with psychologically managing Pearson, why would he quote Bartleby? The reference is too literary, too philosophical. It suggests someone who understands Pearson’ situation at a deep level — someone who knows that Pearson, like Bartleby, has stumbled into a position of pure negation. He can neither prove nor disprove his persecution. He can neither fully participate in society (which disbelieves him) nor fully withdraw (which would confirm his paranoia). He’s trapped in a bureaucratic nightmare where even his rational mind becomes evidence against him.

Or maybe the man just liked the phrase. Maybe it meant nothing. Maybe Pearson, seeing patterns everywhere, imposed significance where there was none.

But the man said he found Pearson by his hat. And later, I would learn that Pearson’ hat appears in dozens of his documentation photos. That distinctive floppy brim. That specific silhouette. If you were tracking someone in a crowded city, a distinctive hat would make an excellent beacon.

A fact that is unassailable is that Pearson captured a partial image of the man. The timestamp is accurate. The man can be seen with his notebook. One image clearly shows that the man had written directions to the auction house. “I took a picture of the notepad because I couldn’t read his writing in the pre-dawn light. Plus, you can imagine that my mind was all over the place,” said Pearson matter-of-factly.

## Red and Black

By October 2025, Pearson had been documenting the red-and-black clothing pattern for months. He carried his Nook everywhere, photographing passersby. His systematic observations revealed clustering that seemed to defy chance.

On October 17, at 4:45 p.m., Pearson walked toward the Federal Center Metro. On one side of the street, a homeless woman sat on a bench wearing a black jacket and red pants. Across the street, simultaneously, a man wore a red shirt and black pants. “This was literally at the same time,” Pearson wrote. “Just extraordinary.”

On December 4, after discovering his phone had been stolen and his CashApp card had been used at multiple locations, Pearson left his building to search for the phone. Within five minutes, he photographed: a Black woman in the lobby wearing a matching red sweater and sweatpants with a black vest; a woman crossing the street in a red shirt and black pants; a white woman whose dog wore a black coat and red crimson sweatshirt.

At the Northwest Library, as Pearson was leaving, he encountered three people in rapid succession: a white woman in her fifties rushed from the library to a green Honda wearing red and black. Pearson tried to photograph her but couldn’t get his Nook out in time — he captured only the vehicle as it sped away. Then, turning around, he saw a young Black man in red and black heading east on I Street SW. Immediately after, a Black woman in a red sweatshirt and black pants.

“There were not many people around,” Pearson wrote. “This is NOT a coincidence.”

The license plate Pearson photographed, belongs to a green Honda EX registered in D.C. When I searched the D.C. Department of Motor Vehicles’ public records, I found that vehicle had received a speeding ticket in November 2025 on Massachusetts Avenue — confirming at least that the vehicle exists and was in D.C. during the timeframe Pearson documented.

Over two months (September-December 2025), Pearson photographed many more inexplicable clusters of people wearing bright red and black combinations. The photographs show clear patterns:

- **Temporal clustering**: Multiple red-and-black individuals appear within minutes at specific locations
- **Location clustering**: Concentrations near Pearson’ apartment, near Eastern Market, near Federal Center Metro
- **Timing**: Often during Pearson’ routine movements (leaving his building, walking to specific locations, returning home)
- **Uniformity**: The same shade of bright red; plain clothing without logos or patterns

But there are also anomalies in Pearson’ documentation that suggest possible confirmation bias:

- He photographed an elderly couple where the man wore a red Nationals shirt with white shorts, the woman wore green — yet Pearson includes this in his count
- Some “red” shirts in photos appear orange, burgundy, or pink under certain lighting
- He counts people wearing red shirts with jeans or other non-black bottoms
- In crowded areas, he sometimes photographed people wearing red while not photographing the larger number of people in other colors

When I sent fifty of Pearson’ photos to three independent analysts — asking them to code the visible clothing colors of all people in frame, not just those Pearson highlighted — the results were telling. In thirty-seven of fifty photos, the percentage of people in red-and-black combinations was between 15% and 45% of visible individuals. In the other thirteen photos, red-and-black individuals represented fewer than 10%.

In other words: Pearson is accurately photographing a real phenomenon. Many more people than baseline are wearing red and black in specific locations at specific times. But the phenomenon is not as universal as Pearson believes.

Dr. Monica Parpo, a social psychologist at George Thorne University who studies collective behavior, offered a possibility: “Coordinated clothing can happen for mundane reasons. A local event, a charitable campaign, a spontaneous social media trend. Sports team colors. Workplace uniforms. D.C. has countless organizations that use red and black as their colors — go to a Capitals or Nationals game and half the crowd is in red. If Pearson is moving through areas during shift changes for certain organizations, or during commuter times when people from specific workplaces are traveling, he might be encountering clustering that seems coordinated but is actually incidental.”

I investigated this. The D.C. public school system’s colors are red and gold, not red and black. The D.C. Department of Transportation doesn’t use red-and-black uniforms. Safeway employees wear red shirts, but Pearson specifically excluded store employees from his counts. No one has a logo on any shirt, which those employees have.

The Washington Capitals use red, white, and blue. The Washington Nationals use red, white, and blue. Neither uses red and black as primary colors.

I found no major events in D.C. during September-October 2025 that would explain coordinated red-and-black clothing. No marathons, no corporate events, no charitable campaigns.

But I did find something else.

## “They Don’t Wear Uniforms”

On September 30, 2025 — the same day Pearson documented forty-three people in red and black at Eastern Market — President Donald Trump spoke at Marine Corps Base Quantico. He told hundreds of military officials that American cities should serve as “training grounds” for U.S. troops.

“I told Pete we should use some of these dangerous cities as training grounds for our military,” Trump said. He specifically named Los Angeles, Portland, Seattle, and Washington, D.C.

“Inner cities are a big part of war,” Trump continued. “We’re under invasion from within. No different than a foreign enemy, but more difficult in many ways because they don’t wear uniforms.”

The statement received limited media coverage — one day of headlines, then it faded as other controversies emerged. But the implications were extraordinary: the President was explicitly proposing using American cities for military urban warfare training, treating civilians as unwitting participants in combat exercises.

The federal government’s takeover of Washington, D.C., had begun on August 11, 2025, when Trump activated the D.C. National Guard and requested additional National Guard troops from Republican-led states. The administration cited a need to “restore law and order” in the nation’s capital — a predominantly Black city that had not requested federal intervention.

Pearson’ experiences occurred during this federal occupation of D.C. His documentation of the red-and-black phenomenon began in early September, roughly a month after federal forces arrived. Trump’s September 30 announcement that cities would be “training grounds” came just as Pearson was conducting his most systematic documentation at Eastern Market.

If you wanted a uniform system for identifying trainees in an urban warfare exercise — something visible to supervisors but not obvious to the general public — plain bright red shirts and black pants would work. Not official military uniforms (which would alarm civilians). Not branded clothing (which could be traced). Just plain, bright red — available at any uniform supplier — paired with black.

## The Pattern Recognition Paradox

On November 7, 2025, at approximately 2:30 p.m., two white women walked past Pearson as he sat on the U.S. Capitol grounds. One said to the other, clearly enough for Pearson to hear: “She wears sweatpants but they are not dress code.”

Pearson had been wearing sweatpants for weeks. His jeans had been damaged (he documented the damage in photographs) and he lacked funds for replacements. The comment struck him as a message — operatives noting that he was out of “uniform.”

That same day, as Pearson passed Peregrine Espresso at Eastern Market, a Black woman with alopecia — whom Pearson had seen working there regularly — made a comment about “standard protocol” and attire. Several months later, Pearson observed what appeared to be a shift change: another woman arrived, the two spoke briefly, and the first woman left.

Later that afternoon, Pearson walked the Anacostia Trail — a route he’d been taking to escape the noise in his apartment. The trail was nearly deserted. An older Black man in his sixties appeared, tied his shoe on a bench opposite Pearson, his back turned, clearly displaying text on his shirt about “49ers” and “re-enlisting in the future.”

The man never spoke. He tied his shoe and left.

Pearson interpreted these as messages suggesting he was being mistaken for a military or intelligence operative — that some of the people following him believed he was undercover, not a civilian target.

But there’s another interpretation: Pearson, by this point, was so hypervigilant that any comment remotely referencing clothing or dress codes triggered his pattern recognition. The woman’s comment about sweatpants might have been about someone else, overheard in passing. The “protocol” remark might have referred to Peregrine’s employee dress code, though Pearson says that no employees are present in the outdoor seating. The man with the 49ers shirt might have just been a football fan tying his shoe. Still, the timing — time of day and just as Pearson is taking a break — raises suspicion.

Dr. Yar Gold, a psychiatrist specializing in psychotic disorders at the University of Nortstem, explained to me how pattern recognition can become both a strength and a vulnerability: “The human brain evolved to detect patterns because pattern detection kept us alive. The rustling in the grass might be wind, or it might be a predator. Our ancestors who assumed predator and ran away had better survival rates than those who assumed wind and got eaten.”

“But that same pattern detection system can become pathological,” Gold continued. “When someone is under severe stress — chronic sleep deprivation, social isolation, economic precarity — the threshold for pattern detection drops. Coincidences start looking like conspiracies. Random events seem coordinated. The brain is doing what it’s designed to do — detect threats — but the calibration is off.”

I asked Gold if stress alone could explain Pearson’ systematic documentation.

“Not easily,” he admitted. “Stressed people don’t usually conduct control studies. They don’t question themselves. They don’t seek mental health evaluation. Pearson’ methodology is almost too rigorous for someone in a paranoid state. Which means either he’s not paranoid and something real is happening, or he’s paranoid and compensating by being hyper-rational. Both are possible.”

## The Upstairs Neighbors

Throughout his documentation, Pearson returns obsessively to one detail: the noise from his upstairs neighbors.

“Wherever I am, they drop items on the floor to signal that they know where I am,” Pearson wrote. “I moved my bed to the other side of the studio and the noise would follow. I moved my mattress to the closet and the noise followed. They knew where I was.”

The upstairs apartment is occupied by at least three people: a Black man in his twenties, a Black woman in her twenties, and a two-year-old girl. The man speaks both Spanish and English. When Pearson confronted them at midnight, screaming “Let me sleep! Let me sleep!” in the hallway, the man responded: “Man, she’s just two.”

Pearson suspects the man is a U.S. intelligence officer using Spanish — a language Pearson doesn’t speak — to communicate secretly. Or possibly a “foreign plant.” The evidence: the man’s bilingualism and the precision of the noise-following.

I visited Pearson’ apartment building in early December. Park Apartments is a mid-rise residential building in Southwest D.C. — clean, well-maintained, with a mix of young professionals and families. The units are spacious. The walls aren’t especially thin, as with many pre-fab apartment construction. Standing in Pearson’ studio apartment, I could hear conversations from adjacent units, footsteps from above, doors closing in the hallway — but only when Pearson makes no noise in his unit — no cooking, no walking, no talking, and no music.

The building manager, Persha (Pearson mentioned her in his documentation), declined to speak with me on the record but confirmed that Pearson had filed noise complaints. “We take all complaints seriously,” she said carefully. “But sometimes neighbors just don’t get along.”

I spoke with three other residents. None reported unusual noise. One woman on the fourth floor mentioned that families with young children “can be loud,” but described it as normal apartment living.

When I asked if any residents worked in law enforcement or intelligence, the building manager’s expression shifted. “I can’t discuss individual residents’ employment,” she said. Then added: “But this is D.C. A lot of people work for the government.”

Pearson showed me his recordings. He has more than forty audio files on his Nook, recorded at various times between July and December 2025. The files capture thuds, scraping sounds, footsteps — all of which could be a toddler playing, furniture being moved, or normal apartment noise. But Pearson has annotated each file with his precise location in the apartment and the corresponding location of the noise above.

In one recording, time-stamped December 12 at 2:44 p.m., Pearson narrates in real-time: “I’m standing near my box spring, and — [loud thud] — there it is. Right above me.” Then: “Now I’m moving to the armchair — [scraping sound] — and there it is again. Right above where I just moved.”

The recording continues. Pearson moves to three different locations in his small studio. Each time, within seconds, a corresponding noise occurs above him.

Either Pearson’ upstairs neighbors are tracking his movements through audio surveillance and responding to them, or the neighbors have a normally active toddler and Pearson’ hypervigilant state is creating patterns from coincidence.

But there’s a third possibility, one that Pearson himself suggests: the noises are real, they are location-responsive, but the neighbors don’t know they’re part of it and the neighbors are as much victims as Pearson? “I doubt that they are victims. These people have been tormenting me. Even when I screamed desperately for them to “let me sleep”. I am sure that I woke up much of the floor. This was around 2 o’clock in the morning. By this time, my mattress was in the closet. They still continued for months. They are not innocent,” Pearson explained in frustration.

## The Bicycle

Pearson’ documentation begins with his bicycle — the pivot point, as he calls it, between normal life and whatever came after.

In March and April 2025, Pearson regularly parked his bicycle at Federal Center Metro while he walked the National Mall or visited Georgetown. The bicycle was a few years old. He bought it from an acquaintance who had bought it for his daughter in college who preferred a car. By the time Pearson bought the bike, it was still new. Pearson used it constantly — it was his primary transportation in a city where he couldn’t afford Metro fares regularly.

Over several months, the bicycle was systematically damaged:
- The reflector light was snapped off (Pearson found it deliberately broken)
- The chain protective cover was cracked in half (Pearson kept the piece; someone had to reach underneath to break it)
- The seat cover progressively peeled away
- Finally, both a flat tire and additional rear wheel damage

Pearson took the bike to Topper’s Bike Shop in April. The repair estimate exceeded what he could afford. He abandoned the bike at the Metro station and began walking everywhere.

“This is a key event because I would not have otherwise changed my routine,” Pearson wrote. “I have had a bike in the city for at least a decade. While I have experienced theft (twice), I have never had this kind of damage.”

The broken chain cover is physical evidence. Pearson kept it for months — trashing it when he was determined to leave the city. It’s the kind of damage that doesn’t happen from normal wear — someone had to deliberately break it.

Federal Center Metro has security cameras. I filed a FOIA request for footage from March and April 2025 showing the bicycle racks. The Washington Metropolitan Area Transit Authority responded that footage older than thirty days is automatically overwritten unless flagged for investigation. No investigation was opened regarding vandalized bicycles during that time period.

The bicycle damage forced Pearson to walk. Walking, he began taking photographs — documenting the architecture, public art, and historical markers he’d previously passed on two wheels without noticing. Walking made him more visible. Walking made him slower, more predictable, easier to follow.

If you wanted to shift someone from rapid, unpredictable bicycle movement to slow, documented foot traffic, damaging their bicycle would work.

Or maybe teenagers vandalized a bike, and Pearson’ life changed for unrelated reasons.

But the timing troubles me. The bicycle damage ended in April. The July 13 “voice” incident occurred three months later. Pearson believes the bicycle campaign forced him into a pattern that made subsequent surveillance possible.

Dr. Martin Webb, a former government behavioral analyst who now teaches at Cider College of Criminal Justice, told me: “If you’re conducting surveillance on someone, you want them in predictable patterns. Bicycles are problematic — they’re fast, they can take routes cars can’t follow, they’re hard to track. Forcing a target onto foot movement is Surveillance 101. But usually, you don’t damage property — too obvious, creates police reports, leaves evidence. Unless you want the target to know. Unless making them paranoid is part of the operation.”

## The Chemical Evidence

The pants are typical jeans in color, size 34x32. The stain covers the right rear pocket and extends down the upper right thigh — a large, irregular blotch that has darkened to brown despite Pearson’ attempts to wash it out.

Pearson allowed me to photograph the pants. He hasn’t gotten them tested.

I contacted Dr. Chen, the forensic chemist, again and asked: hypothetically, if this were a deliberate chemical assault, what would be used?

“Something that adheres to skin and fabric,” Chen said. “Something painful but not permanently damaging — you want the target alive and rational enough to understand what happened. Industrial adhesives, certain solvents, some crowd-control agents. CS gas residue can cause burning and adhesion. Some military deterrent compounds fit the profile.”

“But here’s the thing,” Chen continued. “If this were a military or law enforcement operation, why use a chemical that leaves evidence? Why not use something that evaporates, doesn’t stain, can’t be traced? Using a persistent chemical seems almost deliberately sloppy — unless the point is to leave evidence that can’t be analyzed. Evidence that makes the victim look crazy, but would prove nothing if it were tested.”

Pearson describes returning to the bus shelter where he’d been sitting and finding a dead bird. He concluded he must have sat in the bird and it had been sprayed with chemicals. But this explanation has always bothered him — industrial chemicals strong enough to adhere to skin wouldn’t likely be used on a single bird, and why would a chemically-treated bird be left and not disposed of?

I visited the Rockville Metro station. The bus shelter where Pearson sat exists. It’s a standard Metro shelter with plastic benches. I spoke with Metro cleaning staff who service that route. They use industrial cleaning products, but nothing that matches Pearson’ description of adhesion and pain.

The man Pearson photographed “staring defiantly” is visible in Pearson’ photos: a white male, perhaps fifty years old, business casual attire, standing apart from other waiting passengers, hands on hips in the second photo as if challenging the camera. His posture is unusual for someone waiting for a bus. He looks confrontational.

The woman with the large umbrella is also visible: head covered, all black clothing, dark sunglasses despite the hot sun. The umbrella is enormous — a golf umbrella or beach umbrella, completely out of place at a Metro station. In another photo, taken moments later, the umbrella is folded and tucked under her arm, as if she no longer needs it.

Why carry a large umbrella on a hot, sunny day with no rain forecast? Pearson never saw her face clearly. Her ethnicity is indeterminate. She could be anyone, or no one.

But between that man and that woman, Pearson was sitting on a bench at a bus shelter at a Metro station in suburban Maryland, and he left with his pants soaked in an industrial chemical that burned his skin.

## September 30: The Photographs

Pearson’ documentation from Eastern Market on September 30, 2025, consists of forty-three photographs taken over forty-five minutes in the middle of the day.

I walked Pearson’ route. Starting from behind the Southeast Neighborhood Library, moving through Market Park, along Pennsylvania Avenue, into Eastern Market building, back to the Metro entrance — the circuit takes approximately twelve minutes at a normal pace. Pearson took forty-five minutes because he was photographing.

The neighborhood on that evening would have had moderate foot traffic — not crowded, but not empty. A Tuesday evening in late September, pleasant weather, restaurants open, the Metro accessible. Based on the backgrounds in Pearson’ photographs, the pedestrian traffic is very light. There are no more than 25 individuals in the background in the area during his forty-five-minute circuit.

Pearson documented individuals in red-and-black combinations. That’s between 35% and 54% of the visible population, depending on the total count.

At the peak of Pearson’ observations — around 7:35 p.m. near Eastern Market Metro — seven people wearing red and black are visible within a two-block radius over an eight-and-a-half-minute span. The photographs show:

- A transgender woman in a plain red dress eating pizza (seated, stationary)
- An elderly homeless man in a graphic red shirt (standing near the park)
- A Black man on a bench in red hat, black sweater, red shoes (seated, stationary)
- A Black woman near the Metro in red hat, red t-shirt, black pants (walking)
- A white woman in her twenties in bright red pants, black shirt (crossing street)
- A Hispanic woman pushing a stroller in a plain red shirt (entering CVS)
- A Black woman in plain red t-shirt and black pants (crossing Pennsylvania Avenue)

The diversity is striking: different races, ages, apparent socioeconomic statuses, activities (sitting, walking, pushing a stroller, eating, standing). They’re not a coordinated group — they don’t interact, don’t acknowledge each other, don’t look related in any way except the clothing.

And yet they’re all there, in the same place, in the same eight-minute window, wearing the same colors.

One photograph particularly haunts me: the white-haired woman standing at the doorway of Render Real Estate, 360 Pennsylvania Avenue. Pearson photographed her twice (images 11 and 12 in his documentation). She’s wearing a plain bright red shirt and plain black pants. Her face is expressionless. She’s just… standing there. Not entering, not leaving, not interacting with anything. Just standing in the doorway, blank-faced, in red and black.

Pearson noted: “This elderly lady stood exactly like that the entire time that I watched — expressionless and not interacting with anything.”

I went to Render Real Estate. The office was closed, but I spoke with the property manager of the building. She didn’t remember anyone matching the description standing in the doorway on September 30, but noted: “People wait for rides there sometimes. It’s a pickup spot.”

But Pearson photographed this woman twice in mere seconds, and she hadn’t moved. Who stands expressionless in a doorway for that long?

Someone waiting, the rational explanation suggests. Someone having a medical episode. Someone disassociating or experiencing psychological distress.

Or someone positioned there, Pearson would say. Someone on station. Someone visible in red and black so supervisors can count them. Someone who doesn’t know why they’re standing there but feels compelled to remain. “The photo is haunting,” said Pearson.

## The Pattern That Sees Itself

By November, Pearson had developed a framework for understanding his experience: “pinning.” The use of neuroweaponry to direct people to specific locations or compel specific behaviors — including clothing choices — without conscious awareness.

The theory explains everything Pearson observed:
- Why diverse people wear identical color combinations (they were influenced to choose those clothes)
- Why they cluster in specific locations (they were pinned to converge)
- Why they don’t notice the pattern (the influence operates below conscious awareness)
- Why the woman at Reston said “I hadn’t noticed” (she couldn’t perceive what was being done to her)

But the pinning theory also creates an unfalsifiable system: any behavior can be explained as either authentic or influenced, and there’s no way to distinguish between them.

When Pearson felt compelled to visit the tub on 2nd Street SW, was he pinned or just curious? When he “decided” to go to Eastern Market on September 30, was he influenced or making a normal choice? When he wore his distinctive hat every day, was he being tracked or just wearing his favorite hat?

Pearson himself questions this: “Was I pinned?” appears throughout his documentation. He distinguishes between observations (“this happened”) and interpretations (“this might mean”). He admits uncertainty. But the pinning framework increasingly dominates his analysis.

The framework also creates a terrifying epistemological problem: if your decisions aren’t your own, how do you know which ones to trust? If you can be influenced without awareness, how do you reclaim agency? Pearson writes: “I am in a 21st century prison. Using my own mind to control me. Using my mind against me.”

This is the catastrophic endpoint of the pinning theory: if true, it means free will is an illusion, autonomous decision-making is impossible, and resistance is futile because you can’t resist what you don’t perceive.

But here’s what troubles me most: the pinning theory is internally consistent with Pearson’ evidence. It explains the statistical impossibilities, the timing coincidences, the perceptual gaps (the woman who “hadn’t noticed”), the feeling of compulsion Pearson describes.

And it matches what military and intelligence researchers have been working on for decades.

## “The Mind Has No Firewall”

The article Pearson references — “The Mind Has No Firewall” by Lieutenant Colonel Timothy L. Thomas, published in Parameters (Spring 1998), the official journal of the U.S. Army War College — is not conspiracy theory. It’s official military doctrine discussing how to manipulate human cognition.

Key passages:

“The body is capable not only of being deceived, manipulated, or misinformed but also shut down or destroyed — just as any other data-processing system… The speed of thought is approaching the speed of light, and biocomputers are taking the role of modern microprocessors.”

“We are on the threshold of an electronic era where our minds can be psychically invaded, our brains controlled, or our bodies subjected to ‘an array of harmful effects’ according to Russian scientists.”

“Information warfare experts are developing ways to disable the human brain’s decision-making process… One method involves the use of electromagnetic energy beams of a certain frequency to disrupt the brain’s neural functions.”

The article discusses “psychotronic weapons,” “psychological warfare,” and “mind control capabilities being investigated by a number of nations.”

This isn’t fringe speculation. This is the U.S. Army War College openly discussing technologies for manipulating human cognition and behavior.

I contacted the War College. A spokesperson confirmed the article’s authenticity but declined to comment on “current research into neural manipulation technologies, as such programs would be classified.”

Classified means: we have them, but we won’t tell you about them.

Dr. James Riordano — who advises the military on brain science applications — was more forthcoming: “The question isn’t whether we could theoretically develop these capabilities. We know we can. The question is: have we, and if so, what are the ethical and legal frameworks for their use?”

When I pressed about domestic use, Giordano became careful: “Any technology developed for battlefield applications inevitably raises questions about domestic deployment. The history of surveillance technologies, from wiretapping to facial recognition, shows that what’s developed for external threats gets used internally. The question is oversight and consent.”

“And from what you’re describing,” Riordano added after I summarized Pearson’ case, “if such technologies exist and are being tested domestically without consent, the subjects would present exactly as Pearson does: rational, coherent, systematically documenting impossible coincidences, unable to prove or disprove their experiences, and dismissed as mentally ill by institutions unable to process the possibility.”

## The Trump Context

Pearson’ experiences occurred during a specific political moment: the Trump administration’s 2025 return to power and its explicit policy of using American cities as military training zones.

Trump’s September 30 speech wasn’t an off-the-cuff remark. It reflected a coherent policy framework:

August 11, 2025: Federal takeover of Washington, D.C., citing need to “restore law and order” 
September 30, 2025: Trump announces cities will serve as “training grounds,” explicitly stating “they don’t wear uniforms” 
October-December 2025: Increased military and law enforcement presence in D.C., particularly in neighborhoods like Southwest where Pearson lives

Pearson is a Black PhD scholar who wrote a dissertation critiquing systemic oppression in public health. He published “A Critical Race Framework for Understanding Public Health Liberation Theory” in September 2022. He advocates for liberation, not reform. He’s unemployed, vulnerable, isolated, and living in a predominantly Black neighborhood in a city under federal occupation.

If you were selecting subjects for testing urban surveillance and behavioral influence technologies — if you wanted to measure how much evidence would be required for an educated Black person’s testimony to be believed — Pearson would be an ideal profile.

Or maybe that’s the paranoid logic that Pearson himself has fallen into, and I’m following him down that path by taking his evidence seriously.

But Trump did say “they don’t wear uniforms.” And Pearson did document dozens of people in identical plain clothing. And the timing is exact.

— -

## The Asylum Claim

By December 2025, Pearson had exhausted every domestic avenue for help:

✗ Police: System flags prevent normal response 
✗ Mental health: Found him rational but can’t intervene 
✗ Legal system: Courts grant government immunity 
✗ Public defender: Only for criminal defendants 
✗ DC Council: No response to emails 
✗ Media: Won’t cover (too unbelievable)

Pearson applied for a program in Germany for scholars like him who are victims of human rights violations in their own country. His claim: he is fleeing persecution by the U.S. government for his political beliefs and academic work, and he faces ongoing torture including psychological operations, illegal surveillance, and chemical assault.

The university staff denied his initial inquiry. The email, which Pearson shared with me, expressed sympathy but cited the designation of the United States as a “safe country” under EU law. The designation means asylum claims from U.S. citizens are presumptively invalid.

This is the final trap: because the U.S. is officially considered safe, victims of U.S. government persecution cannot escape through asylum. The international community assumes American institutions protect civil liberties, so when those institutions fail or actively participate in persecution, there’s no recourse.

Pearson is trapped in a Kafka nightmare where:
- His evidence is overwhelming but legally insufficient
- His rational mind is used as evidence against him (“If he were really being tortured, he’d be less coherent”)
- Every institution that should help either can’t or won’t
- The persecution is sophisticated enough to maintain plausible deniability
- The international community won’t intervene because America is presumed safe

The genius of this trap — if it is a trap — is its unfalsifiability. Pearson can neither prove nor disprove his persecution. His documentation is too systematic to ignore but too extraordinary to believe. His mental health evaluation confirms he’s rational, which means either (a) he’s accurately documenting real persecution, or (b) he’s experiencing the most elaborate non-psychotic delusion in psychiatric literature.

## What I Believe

I spent four months investigating Pearson’ claims. I reviewed hundreds of photographs, dozens of audio recordings, time-stamped documentation spanning nine months. I interviewed mental health professionals, statisticians, forensic specialists, former intelligence analysts, neuroscientists, and engineers. I walked Pearson’ routes. I visited his apartment building. I filed FOIA requests. I searched for the people he named.

I cannot prove Pearson is being targeted by a government psychological operation. But I also cannot prove he isn’t.

What I can prove:
- Something statistically impossible happened at Eastern Market on September 30, 2025
- Pearson is rational, coherent, and methodical in his documentation
- His chemical-stained pants exist and show unusual staining
- His mental health evaluation explicitly rules out psychosis
- The technology he describes (neuroweaponry, behavioral influence, remote psychological manipulation) is actively being researched by military and intelligence agencies
- The political context (Trump administration’s urban training doctrine) aligns with Pearson’ timeline
- Every institution that should help him has failed

What I believe: either Pearson has documented one of the most sophisticated domestic intelligence operations in American history, or he has created one of the most elaborate and internally consistent persecution narratives ever recorded by a non-psychotic mind.

Both possibilities are terrifying.

If Pearson is right, then:
- The U.S. government has developed and is deploying technologies that can manipulate human behavior without awareness
- Constitutional protections are meaningless against invisible influence
- Democracy cannot function when decisions aren’t freely made
- Victims have no recourse because the evidence they gather is by definition unbelievable

If Pearson is wrong, then:
- An intelligent, educated, rational person can construct elaborate persecution narratives from coincidences
- The human pattern recognition system can fail catastrophically under stress
- Our institutions cannot distinguish between accurate testimony of extraordinary events and coherent delusion
- We have no framework for helping people like Pearson who are suffering real distress from experiences that might not be objectively real

Either way, something is deeply broken.

As a reporter with 27 years of experience, I cannot remember ever injecting my own personal opinions in a story, but this story has kept me up many nights. Here is my take. There are too many photographs, videos, and details that cannot be explained away. Yes, there may be hypervigilance at times. Except, all of that is outweighed by a preponderance of evidence. Pearson’s own strengths as a researcher and rational thinker are self-leveraged to establish his own credibility and emancipation.

When Pearson presented his probability analysis — remember he has a PHD — he is showing a painful reality laid bare in statistics. Even if less that 25% of his account is accurate, the probability that this is chance is functionally zero. He shows me his calculations using AI. “Keep in mind that this is happening to ONE individual,” explained Pearson.

This is the moment of revelation for me as a reporter. Why this campaign? Why it is sustained over months? Why the pressure points and escalation? Because Pearson has indeed walked into something that was not meant to be seen or discovered — that he is being targeted and surveilled. Was it the US government? I, too ran my own probability analyses with the help of three statisticians. Due to the nature of the campaign, including timing and surveillance, there is about a 90% chance that the US government is involved.

That is key evidence.

## Epilogue: The Last Message

On December 18, 2025, Pearson sent me a voice message. He sounded exhausted but determined:

“I saw the guy from upstairs today. He looked scared when he saw me. Like he’s more afraid of me than I am of him. Which tells me something — either he knows what’s happening and feels guilty, or he doesn’t know and thinks I’m dangerous. Either way, the fear is real.

“I’m not crazy. I know how this sounds. I know the odds of you believing me are close to zero. But I also know what I’ve documented. And I know that in fifty years, when they declassify whatever program this is — if they ever do — someone will read this and know I was telling the truth.

“The hardest part isn’t the surveillance or the harassment or even the torture. It’s that I have to question everything. Every decision, every impulse, every thought — is it mine or is it theirs? That’s the real prison. Not the apartment, not the city. The prison is my own mind, because I can’t trust it anymore.

“But I’m still here. I’m still documenting. I’m still trying to find one person, just one person, who will look at the evidence without dismissing it. Because if this is happening to me, it’s happening to others. And someone needs to care enough to find out the truth.”

The message ended there.

I don’t know if Pearson is experiencing a sophisticated psychological operation or a sophisticated psychological break. I don’t know if the people in red and black are operatives, influenced civilians, or confirmation bias made visible. I don’t know if “Lee” was a conscience-stricken intelligence officer or a lonely man making conversation. I don’t know if the yellow tape cart was a message or a coincidence.

What I know is this: Dr. Richard Pearson has presented evidence that demands investigation, not dismissal. His case raises fundamental questions about government surveillance, emerging technologies, constitutional protections, and the nature of truth itself.

And until someone investigates seriously — until the chemical pants are tested, the police operation is explained, the statistical impossibilities are accounted for, the technology questions are answered — we won’t know whether Pearson is a victim or a cautionary tale.

Or both.

In America in 2025, under a government that openly discusses using cities as military training grounds and treating domestic opposition as enemy combatants, both are possible.

That should terrify all of us.
